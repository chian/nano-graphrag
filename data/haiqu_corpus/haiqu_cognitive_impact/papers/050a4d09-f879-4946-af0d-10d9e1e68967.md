Brain functional connectivity after Stroop task induced cognitive fatigue


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6.pdf)

## Abstract

We aim to investigate the changes in brain functional connectivity induced by cognitive fatigue from a whole-brain perspective and multiple angles to uncover the underlying mechanisms in healthy individuals, thereby enhancing high-quality cognitive activities and the effectiveness of cognitive rehabilitation training. This study involved 48 healthy adults aged 20–35 and was conducted in two phases: cognitive fatigue validation and whole-brain connectivity feature analysis. We used fMRI to compare Amplitude of Low-Frequency Fluctuations (ALFF), Regional Homogeneity (ReHo), Functional Connectivity (FC), and graph theory metrics before and after cognitive fatigue. The results indicated that changes in activity within the bilateral thalamus and Front-Parietal network were significantly correlated with subjective fatigue levels. Additionally, the left precuneus, cerebellum, and certain frontal regions may contribute to the neural regulation of cognitive fatigue through various mechanisms. These findings provide a reliable theoretical basis for developing new strategies to mitigate cognitive fatigue and offer valuable insights for optimizing rehabilitation plans for patients with cognitive impairments.

## Introduction

Cognitive fatigue (CF) is a cognitive state resulting from sustained cognitive activities, characterized by subjective sensations of “fatigue”,“lack of energy” and a diminished willingness to exert effort[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR1 "Marcora, S. M., Staiano, W. & Manning, V. Mental fatigue impairs physical performance in humans. J. Appl. Physiol. 106, 857–864 (2009)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR2 "Vogel, T. A. et al. Cognitive fatigue weakens the effects of distraction on pain. Pain 164, 2845–2851 (2023)."). Numerous studies have demonstrated the detrimental effects of cognitive fatigue on daily life, including reduced cognitive performance efficiency, attention, and alertness, as well as impairments in physical functioning and an increased susceptibility to anxiety and depression[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR1 "Marcora, S. M., Staiano, W. & Manning, V. Mental fatigue impairs physical performance in humans. J. Appl. Physiol. 106, 857–864 (2009)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR2 "Vogel, T. A. et al. Cognitive fatigue weakens the effects of distraction on pain. Pain 164, 2845–2851 (2023)."), [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR3 "Van Cutsem, J. et al. A drop in cognitive performance, whodunit? Subjective mental fatigue, brain deactivation or increased parasympathetic activity? It’s complicated!. Cortex; J. Devot. Study Nerv. Syst. Behav. 155, 30–45 (2022)."), [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR4 "Habay, J. et al. Mental fatigue and sport-specific psychomotor performance: A systematic review. Sports Med. 51, 1527–1548 (2021)."). For patients with cognitive impairments, susceptibility to cognitive fatigue is notably higher, which may adversely affect their adherence to rehabilitation training. Furthermore, some studies suggest that cognitive fatigue may impair the effectiveness of cognitive rehabilitation by disrupting these patients’ neural plasticity[5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR5 "García-Bouyssou, I. et al. Clinical and neuroanatomical predictors of post-stroke fatigue. J. Stroke Cerebrovasc. Dis. 33, 107708 (2024)."), [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR6 "Hechenberger, S. et al. Psychological factors and brain magnetic resonance imaging metrics associated with fatigue in persons with multiple sclerosis. J. Neurol. Sci. 454, 120833 (2023)."), [7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR7 "Chalah, M. A. et al. Neurophysiological, radiological and neuropsychological evaluation of fatigue in multiple sclerosis. Mult. Scler. Relat. Disord. 28, 145–152 (2019)."). Therefore, elucidating the underlying mechanisms by which cognitive fatigue influences cognitive performance and the rehabilitation training outcomes is essential for supporting healthy individuals in engaging in high-quality cognitive activities and for maintaining or improving cognitive rehabilitation outcomes in patients.

Research into the negative effects of cognitive fatigue on behavioral performance that dates back to the early twentieth century[3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR3 "Van Cutsem, J. et al. A drop in cognitive performance, whodunit? Subjective mental fatigue, brain deactivation or increased parasympathetic activity? It’s complicated!. Cortex; J. Devot. Study Nerv. Syst. Behav. 155, 30–45 (2022)."), and studies examining the underlying mechanisms continue to persist to this day. Many fMRI investigations have focused on specific brain regions, particularly the precuneus, which is a core area of the default mode network and is closely linked to higher cognitive functions such as self-referential thinking, memory, and spatial imagination[8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR8 "Dadario, N. B. & Sughrue, M. E. The functional role of the precuneus. Brain: A J. Neurol. 146, 3598–3607 (2023)."). Research has demonstrated a negative correlation between precuneus activity and levels of fatigue in healthy individuals[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR9 "Wylie, G. R. & Flashman, L. A. Understanding the interplay between mild traumatic brain injury and cognitive fatigue: Models and treatments. Concussion 2, CNC50 (2017)."), [10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR10 "Wylie, G. R. et al. Cognitive fatigue in individuals with traumatic brain injury is associated with caudate activation. Sci Rep 7, 8973 (2017)."). Additionally, Bruijel et al. found that reduced functional connectivity between the precuneus and the striatum is associated with an increase in subjective fatigue[11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR11 "Bruijel, J. et al. Task-induced subjective fatigue and resting-state striatal connectivity following traumatic brain injury. Neuroimage Clin 33, 102936 (2022)."). Furthermore, several studies have indicated that the thalamus plays a significant role in the perception of cognitive fatigue[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR12 "Guo, Y. et al. Thalamic network under wakefulness after sleep onset and its coupling with daytime fatigue in insomnia disorder: An EEG-fMRI study. J. Affect. Disord. 334, 92–99 (2023)."), [13](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR13 "Kluger, B. M., Krupp, L. B. & Enoka, R. M. Fatigue and fatigability in neurologic illnesses: Proposal for a unified taxonomy. Neurology 80, 409–416 (2013)."). Neuroimaging evidence has shown that thalamic atrophy and reduced activation are closely linked to self-reported levels of fatigue. The onset of cognitive fatigue is closely linked to the integrity of the striatal-thalamic-prefrontal cortex pathway[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR14 "Salihu, A. T., Hill, K. D. & Jaberzadeh, S. Neural mechanisms underlying state mental fatigue: A systematic review and activation likelihood estimation meta-analysis. Rev. Neurosci. 33, 889–917 (2022)."), [15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR15 "Lin, F. et al. Mental fatigability and heart rate variability in mild cognitive impairment. Am. J. Geriatr. Psychiatry: Off. J. Am. Assoc. Geriatr. Psychiatry 24, 374–378 (2016)."), where greater pathway integrity correlates with lower perceived fatigue. This theory is primarily based on chronic pathological fatigue resulting from basal ganglia dysfunction (e.g., in conditions such as multiple sclerosis and Parkinson’s disease) and is supported by neuroimaging evidence[16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR16 "Dobryakova, E., DeLuca, J., Genova, H. M. & Wylie, G. R. Neural correlates of cognitive fatigue: Cortico-striatal circuitry and effort-reward imbalance. J. Int. Neuropsychol. Soc.: JINS 19, 849–853 (2013)."), [17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR17 "DeLuca, J., Genova, H. M., Capili, E. J. & Wylie, G. R. Functional neuroimaging of fatigue. Phys. Med. Rehabil. Clin. N. Am. 20, 325–337 (2009)."). However, whether this theory extends to acute cognitive fatigue induced by prolonged cognitive activity remains to be explored further. Although these fMRI studies have provided valuable insights into the cognitive fatigue, most of these studies have employed seed-to-voxel analysis, selecting specific brain regions as regions of interest (ROIs) to calculate the functional connectivity patterns between those regions and other voxels. However, these studies often lack a systematic examination of whole-brain functional connectivity changes following cognitive fatigue. Such whole-brain, multi-perspective research can offer a more comprehensive and accurate view of changes in brain activity, thereby elucidating how cognitive fatigue contributes to the decline in cognitive performance and interferes with the effectiveness of cognitive rehabilitation training.

Resting-state functional magnetic resonance imaging (rs-fMRI) can compute various brain functional connectivity measures, each reflecting specific characteristics of brain networks[18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR18 "Veselinović, T. et al. Connectivity patterns in the core resting-state networks and their influence on cognition. Brain Connect. 12, 334–347 (2022)."). The Amplitude of Low-Frequency Fluctuations (ALFF) assesses the intensity of spontaneous activity in local brain regions within the low-frequency range, reflecting the functional activity of these areas and indirectly evaluating the functional connectivity among them. Regional Homogeneity (ReHo) evaluates the synchrony among adjacent voxels within a specific brain area, reflecting the coordinated activity of neuronal populations within that region. Functional Connectivity (FC) reflects the temporal correlations of neural activities across different brain areas, illustrating functional coordination among spatially separated regions and highlighting the dynamic interactions within brain networks[19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR19 "Raimondo, L. et al. Advances in resting state fMRI acquisitions for functional connectomics. Neuroimage 243, 118503 (2021)."), [20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR20 "Fingelkurts, A. A., Fingelkurts, A. A. & Kähkönen, S. Functional connectivity in the brain–is it an elusive concept?. Neurosci. Biobehav. Rev. 28, 827–836 (2005)."). Graph theory analysis conceptualizes the brain as a complex network, examining not only the functional connectivity among brain regions but also the overall topological structure of brain networks and the functions of individual regions, thereby uncovering both global and local characteristics of brain functional connectivity. These methods can enhance one another, reflecting various levels of brain connectivity—such as local activity, synchrony, inter-regional relationships, and network structure—from different perspectives. The global and regional characteristics of brain functional connectivity constitute fundamental neural substrates for cognition and behavior[21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR21 "Bassett, D. S. & Sporns, O. Network neuroscience. Nat. Neurosci. 20, 353–364 (2017)."), [22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR22 "Greicius, M. Resting-state functional connectivity in neuropsychiatric disorders. Curr. Opin. Neurol. 21, 424–430 (2008).").These metrics not only inform the determination of cognitive workloads in healthy individuals to maximize performance efficiency, but also serve as potential biomarkers for neurodegenerative risk assessment. In patients with cognitive impairments, these characteristics can be used to evaluate neural plasticity, select personalized rehabilitation targets, monitor treatment efficacy, and make necessary adjustments to rehabilitation plans.

This study aims to comprehensively assess changes in whole-brain functional connectivity before and after cognitive fatigue from multiple perspectives using fMRI. The Stroop task was selected as the cognitive fatigue induction paradigm due to its capacity to simultaneously engage inhibitory control and executive processing. This dual cognitive load simulates the fatigue accumulation process encountered in real-life situations. Furthermore, extensive empirical evidence has validated the effects of the Stroop task in inducing cognitive fatigue through behavioral and neuroscientific measures[23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR23 "Trecroci, A., Boccolini, G., Duca, M., Formenti, D. & Alberti, G. Mental fatigue impairs physical activity, technical and decision-making performance during small-sided games. PLoS ONE 15, e0238461 (2020)."), [24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR24 "Gergelyfi, M. et al. Mental fatigue correlates with depression of task-related network and augmented DMN activity but spares the reward circuit. Neuroimage 243, 118532 (2021)."), [25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR25 "Verschueren, J. O. et al. Does mental fatigue negatively affect outcomes of functional performance tests?. Med. Sci. Sports Exerc. 52, 2002–2010 (2020)."). To this end, we conducted subjective fatigue assessments, EEG measurements, and fMRI data collection before and after participants performed the Stroop cognitive fatigue task. We believe that this research not only adds to the existing findings on the relationship between cognitive fatigue and functional connectivity but also provides a systematic analysis of changes in whole-brain functional connectivity. This multidimensional analysis aids in uncovering the potential neural mechanisms underlying acute cognitive fatigue, offering theoretical support for the development of new strategies to mitigate cognitive fatigue in the future. Furthermore, it is of significant importance for enhancing the quality of cognitive activities in healthy individuals and optimizing cognitive rehabilitation training programs for patients with cognitive impairments.

## Methods

### Participants

Participants were recruited from the Beijing Rehabilitation Hospital Affiliated to Capital Medical University and its School of Medicine. Eligibility criteria included age between 20 and 35 years, right-handedness, self-reported normal or corrected vision, normal color perception, no history of neurological or psychiatric disorders, and no contraindications for magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). The recruitment period for participants is from January to June 2024. All participants signed a written informed consent form before their involvement in the study.

### Design and procedure

The study was divided into two phases: the first phase validated cognitive fatigue, while the second phase explored brain connectivity characteristics (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#Fig1)). These phases were conducted at least one week apart to maintain study rigor. To minimize the influence of circadian rhythms, all sessions were scheduled between 4 and 6 p.m, and participants were required to ensure adequate rest and avoid caffeine intake one hour before the study. Before the formal start of each phase, participants completed a 3-min practice task to familiarize themselves with the response keys. This study was approved by the Ethics Committee of Beijing Rehabilitation Hospital, Capital Medical University (No.: 2023bkky-079-001) and complies with the Declaration of Helsinki. It was also registered with the Clinical Trial Registry (No.: ChiCTR2400092212).

In the phase of cognitive fatigue validation, participants first underwent a 3-min EEG recording (pre-EEG) and then completed the VAS-F scale within one minute (pre-VAS-F). Then they performed the Stroop task, after which they completed the VAS-F scale again (post-VAS-F) and recorded the EEG (post-EEG).

In the stage of whole-brain functional connectivity analysis, all procedures were conducted in a magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scanner. Participants first underwent a resting-state fMRI scan (pre-fMRI), followed by the the Stroop task. After the task, they underwent an fMRI scan in a fatigued state (post-fMRI).

#### Induction tasks for cognitive fatigue

The Stroop task requires participants to ignore conflicting information (such as the mismatch between text color and its meaning) and to focus on the correct response, which places high demands on the brain’s executive function and inhibitory control. Given that this task involves multiple cognitive functions, including cognitive inhibition, attention control, and information processing speed—all of which can be negatively affected by fatigue—the Stroop task is widely used to induce cognitive fatigue[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR26 "Boksem, M. A. S., Meijman, T. F. & Lorist, M. M. Mental fatigue, motivation and action monitoring. Biol. Psychol. 72, 123–132 (2006)."), [27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR27 "Angius, L. et al. Physical and mental fatigue reduce psychomotor vigilance in professional football players. Int. J. Sports Physiol. Perform. 17, 1391–1398 (2022)."), [28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR28 "Baraniuk, J. N., Thapaliya, K., Inderyas, M., Shan, Z. Y. & Barnden, L. R. Stroop task and practice effects demonstrate cognitive dysfunction in long COVID and myalgic encephalomyelitis / chronic fatigue syndrome. Sci. Rep. 14, 26796 (2024)."). Previous studies have typically used cognitive tasks lasting 20 min or longer to induce cognitive fatigue[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR14 "Salihu, A. T., Hill, K. D. & Jaberzadeh, S. Neural mechanisms underlying state mental fatigue: A systematic review and activation likelihood estimation meta-analysis. Rev. Neurosci. 33, 889–917 (2022)."). Based on the research by Neil et al.[29](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR29 "Dallaway, N., Lucas, S. J. E. & Ring, C. Cognitive tasks elicit mental fatigue and impair subsequent physical task endurance: Effects of task duration and type. Psychophysiology 59, e14126 (2022)."), we employed a modified Stroop task lasting approximately 40 min to induce cognitive fatigue.

#### Subjective assessment of cognitive fatigue

The Visual Analog Scale for Fatigue (VAS-F)[30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR30 "Lee, K. A., Hicks, G. & Nino-Murcia, G. Validity and reliability of a scale to assess fatigue. Psychiatry Res. 36, 291–298 (1991)."), developed by Lee et al., is a psychological tool designed to measure subjective experiences of cognitive fatigue. It consists of 18 items split into two components: a fatigue subscale (13 items) and a vigor subscale (5 items). Each item includes a 10 cm line on which participants indicate their current level of subjective fatigue by marking a position on the line. Higer scores on the fatigue subscale and lower scores on the vigor subscale are indicative of increased subjective fatigue. This tool has been extensively validated for application across multiple clinical domains, including neurorehabilitation progress monitoring[31](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR31 "Nadarajah, M., Mazlan, M., Abdul-Latif, L. & Goh, H.-T. Test-retest reliability, internal consistency and concurrent validity of fatigue severity scale in measuring post-stroke fatigue. Eur. J. Phys. Rehabil. Med. 53, 703–709 (2017)."), longitudinal management of chronic conditions[32](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR32 "Chen, M. H., DeLuca, J., Genova, H. M., Yao, B. & Wylie, G. R. Cognitive fatigue is associated with altered functional connectivity in interoceptive and reward pathways in multiple sclerosis. Diagnostics 10, 930 (2020)."), and psychiatric symptom assessment[33](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR33 "Costa, T. et al. Autonomic dysregulation, cognition and fatigue in people with depression and in active and healthy controls: Observational cohort study. BJPsych. Open 9, e106 (2023).").The VAS-F is noted for its high internal consistency, ease of use, and conciseness, making it a popular choice for self-assessment of short-term fatigue[34](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR34 "Machado, M. O. et al. Measuring fatigue: A meta-review. Int. J. Dermatol. 60, 1053–1069 (2021).").

#### Objective assessment of cognitive fatigue

Changes in EEG α-band activity reflect the spontaneous activity of the cerebral cortex. Typically, α-band activity increases during states of rest or relaxation and decreases during focused attention or high cognitive load. Fatigue often disrupts spontaneous activity, resulting in increased alpha wave power, which indicates reduced cognitive alertness and may lead to declines in functions such as attention, reaction time, and working memory[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR26 "Boksem, M. A. S., Meijman, T. F. & Lorist, M. M. Mental fatigue, motivation and action monitoring. Biol. Psychol. 72, 123–132 (2006)."), [35](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR35 "Klimesch, W. EEG alpha and theta oscillations reflect cognitive and memory performance: A review and analysis. Brain Res. Brain Res. Rev. 29, 169–195 (1999)."), [36](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR36 "Proost, M. et al. How to tackle mental fatigue: A systematic review of potential countermeasures and their underlying mechanisms. Sports Med 52, 2129–2158 (2022)."), [37](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR37 "Käthner, I., Wriessnegger, S. C., Müller-Putz, G. R., Kübler, A. & Halder, S. Effects of mental workload and fatigue on the P300, alpha and theta band power during operation of an ERP (P300) brain-computer interface. Biol. Psychol. 102, 118–129 (2014)."). A meta-analysis has shown that shifts in α-band activity are reliable indicators of cognitive fatigue[38](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR38 "Tran, Y., Craig, A., Craig, R., Chai, R. & Nguyen, H. The influence of mental fatigue on brain activity: Evidence from a systematic review with meta-analyses. Psychophysiology 57, e13554 (2020).").

### Data acquisition and preprocessing

EEG data were collected in a quiet environment with participants keeping their eyes open, staying still, and remaining relaxed. Brain electrical signals were recorded using a 64-channel EEG system (Neuroscan), based on the 10–20 international standard. The date was sampled at a frequency of 500 Hz, with electrode impedance kept below 10 kΩ. Date preprocessing was conducted using MATLAB (version 2020B) and EEGLAB (version 2024) and involved segment rejection, re-referencing, filtering, interpolation of bad channels, and independent component analysis to remove artifacts. A total of 56 electrode signals were obtained.

MRI data were acquired with a 3 T MR scanner (SIGNA Pioneer; GE HealthCare, Milwaukee, WI, United States) equipped with a 24-channel head coil. Scanning sequences included 3D-T1 and resting-state fMRI(rs-fMRI). For 3D-T1, the parameters were as follows: repetition time (TR)/echo time (TE)/prep time (TP) = 8.1/3.1/450 ms, flip angle = 15°, field of view (FOV) = 300 mm × 300 mm, pixel size = 1.0 × 1.0 mm2, and slice thickness = 1.0 mm. For rs-fMRI , the parameters included: TR = 2000 ms, TE = 35 ms, flip angle = 90°, FOV = 280 mm, pixel size = 3.0 × 3.0 mm2, slice thickness = 4.5 mm, 32 slices, and an echo spacing of 0.8 mm. Data preprocessing was carried out using DPABISurf (Data Processing Assistant for Brain Imaging, version 8.2) and included multiple steps: (1) Checking the BIDS JSON-format image meta-data; (2) intensity non-uniformity correction and skull-stripping; (3) tissue segmentation of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), white matter (WM), and gray matter (GM); (4) brain surface reconstruction; (5) deleting initial 4 time points; (6) boundary-based registration of BOLD and T1 images; (7) BOLD image spatial normalization to fsaverage5 space; (8) Friston 24 head-motion, WM, and CSF signal and linear trend nuisance regression; (9) bandpass filtering (0.01–0.1 Hz); (10) spatial smoothing \[full-width at half-maximum (FWHM) of 6 mm\].

### Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses of questionnaire data were conducted using SPSS (version 23). Paired t-tests compared the changes in VAS-F scores before and after cognitive tasks, with _p_ < 0.05 set as the threshold for statistical significance.

Power spectral density of the α band (8–13 Hz) was conducted via the Welch method. Analysis was performed using MATLAB (version 2020B) and SPSS (version 23). One-tailed paired t-tests were used to compare EEG data before and after tasks, with statistical significance determined at _p_ < 0.05.

Preprocessed fMRI data were analyzed using DPABISurf (version 8.2) with the Automated Anatomical Labeling (AAL), dividing the brain into 116 regions of interest (ROIs). Paired t-tests were applied to whole-brain ALFF and ReHo values before and after cognitive fatigue to identify significant regions, with Gaussian random field (GRF) correction applied at a cluster-level _p_ < 0.05 and voxel-level _p_ < 0.001 (ReHo analysis was performed on pre-smoothed data, while ALFF analysis was conducted on post-smoothed data). Preprocessed data were then imported into Gretna software to compare functional connectivity (FC) as well as graph theory-based network metrics before and after cognitive fatigue, with significance levels following FDR correction reported as q values (uncorrected results are given as _p_ values).

### Cognitive fatigue validation

#### Fatigue scale

Figure [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#Fig2) A showed that after the Stroop tasks, scores on the VAS-F significantly increased (pre-task: 53.00 ± 3.16, post-task: 100.60 ± 3.84, _p_ < 0.001). ### Whole-brain functional connectivity features

#### ALFF

#### Post-hoc analysis

To further explain the results, we conducted Pearson correlation analysis to investigate the relationship between changes in whole-brain functional connectivity before and after cognitive fatigue and subjective fatigue. Figure [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#Fig6) showed that among the functional connections that showed significant changes, only the ReHo values of the bilateral thalamus (Left Thalamus, r = 0.302, _p_ = 0.039; Right Thalamus, r = 0.404, _p_ = 0.005) demonstrated a significant positive correlation with subjective fatigue. Furthermore, significant positive correlations with subjective fatigue were observed for the connectivity between the left middle frontal gyrus and the left inferior parietal lobule (r = 0.300, _p_ = 0.041), as well as between the left middle frontal gyrus and the right superior parietal lobule (r = 0.445, _p_ = 0.002).

## Discussion

This study aims to explore the characteristics of changes in whole-brain functional connectivity from a multidimensional perspective following cognitive fatigue. The results reveal several key findings: (1) Following a 40-min Stroop task, participants showed a significant increase in subjective fatigue and α band activity, consistent with psychological and physiological signs commonly associated with fatigue. These results confirm the task’s efficacy in inducing cognitive fatigue. (2) Following cognitive fatigue, whole-brain functional connectivity exhibited varying degrees of change. Specifically, ReHo values in the bilateral thalamus and the functional connectivity between the left middle frontal gyrus, the left inferior parietal lobule, and the right superior parietal lobule showed significant positive correlations with subjective fatigue levels. This suggests that these changes in functional connectivity may play an important role in the compensatory processes associated with cognitive fatigue. While changes in functional connectivity in the left precuneus, cerebellum, and certain frontal regions did not show significant correlations with subjective fatigue, they were nonetheless reflected in various multidimensional indices (e.g., local activity levels, regional synchronization, inter-regional functional connectivity, and network structure). This suggests that these brain regions may be involved in the complex neuroregulatory processes associated with cognitive fatigue through multiple mechanisms. Overall, these findings indicate that whole-brain functional connectivity characteristics undergo widespread changes following cognitive fatigue, providing new insights into the potential mechanisms by which cognitive fatigue impairs cognitive performance and disrupts cognitive rehabilitation training outcomes.

### Cognitive fatigue verification

This study aims to explore the characteristics of changes in whole-brain functional connectivity from a multidimensional perspective following cognitive fatigue, with a key focus on the effective induction of cognitive fatigue. The results showed that after the 40-min Stroop task, participants showed significantly higher VAS-F scores. As a validated quantitative measure of cognitive fatigue, the VAS-F directly captures subjective fatigue perception. Additionally, its high sensitivity and specificity enable it to effectively capture subtle changes in fatigue, thereby providing timely feedback for medical interventions[30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR30 "Lee, K. A., Hicks, G. & Nino-Murcia, G. Validity and reliability of a scale to assess fatigue. Psychiatry Res. 36, 291–298 (1991)."), [39](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR39 "Erani, F. et al. Investigating the influence of an effort-reward interaction on cognitive fatigue in individuals with multiple sclerosis. J. Neuropsychol. 17, 364–381 (2023)."), [40](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR40 "Ziino, C. & Ponsford, J. Vigilance and fatigue following traumatic brain injury. J. Int. Neuropsychol. Soc. 12, 100–110 (2006)."), [41](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR41 "Niemczak, C. E. et al. Neuroimaging markers of cognitive fatigue in individuals with post-acute sequelae of SARS-CoV-2 infection. Brain Cogn. 183, 106254 (2025).").The observed VAS-F elevation indicates a substantial increase in subjective fatigue perception, elevated cognitive load during task performance, and concomitant physical exertion. Additionally, we utilized changes in EEG alpha band power spectral density as an objective indicator for monitoring fatigue. Research has demonstrated that EEG signals are one of the most reliable and predictive biomarkers for assessing cognitive fatigue[38](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR38 "Tran, Y., Craig, A., Craig, R., Chai, R. & Nguyen, H. The influence of mental fatigue on brain activity: Evidence from a systematic review with meta-analyses. Psychophysiology 57, e13554 (2020)."). In particular, during states of cognitive fatigue, alpha band power often exhibits a significant increase. The psychological and physiological indicators observed in this study validate the occurrence of cognitive fatigue, confirming that the 40-min Stroop task effectively induces cognitive fatigue.

While the Stroop task has been widely employed in cognitive fatigue induction, its multifaceted engagement of cognitive functions (including inhibitory control, attention, and conflict monitoring) makes it particularly promising for cognitive rehabilitation applications. By assessing an individual’s fatigue levels, rehabilitation training protocols can be personalized (e.g., by optimizing task duration or adjusting the ratio of congruent/incongruent trials) to enhance cognitive performance while minimizing fatigue-related adverse effects. Furthermore, specific Stroop task paradigms (e.g., color-Stroop, numerical-Stroop, or spatial-Stroop tasks) can be selectively employed to target distinct impaired cognitive domains (such as attentional control, numerical processing, or spatial cognition), thereby achieving more focused rehabilitation outcomes.

### Characteristics of whole-brain functional connectivity

This study found that ReHo values in the bilateral thalamus significantly increased following cognitive fatigue, and this change was positively correlated with subjective feelings of fatigue. Most studies highlight the thalamus as a key player in fatigue[42](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR42 "Capone, F., Collorone, S., Cortese, R., Di Lazzaro, V. & Moccia, M. Fatigue in multiple sclerosis: The role of thalamus. Mult. Scler. (Houndmills, Basingstoke, England) 26, 6–16 (2020)."), research has shown that patients with multiple sclerosis, compared to non-fatigued individuals, display increased functional connectivity in the thalamus as a compensatory response aimed at alleviating fatigue symptoms. Our study similarly observed elevated bilateral thalamic ReHo following cognitive fatigue. This may reflect a mechanism by which healthy individuals enhance thalamic activity to alleviate discomfort caused by fatigue. Additionally, the thalamus is essential for various cognitive functions[43](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR43 "Sun, J. et al. Abnormal dynamic functional connectivity after sleep deprivation from temporal variability perspective. Hum. Brain Mapp. 43, 3824–3839 (2022)."), and its dysfunction may negatively affect attention, alertness, cognitive control, and executive functions[44](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR44 "Dehghani, N. & Wimmer, R. D. A computational perspective of the role of the thalamus in cognition. Neural Comput. 31, 1380–1418 (2019)."), [45](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR45 "Shine, J. M., Lewis, L. D., Garrett, D. D. & Hwang, K. The impact of the human thalamus on brain-wide information processing. Nat. Rev. Neurosci. 24, 416–430 (2023)."). Current evidence indicates that excessive thalamic activity occurs during early-stage cognitive impairment, potentially representing a neurocompensatory response to cortical functional decline[46](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR46 "Schoonheim, M. M. et al. Thalamus structure and function determine severity of cognitive impairment in multiple sclerosis. Neurology 84, 776–783 (2015)."), [47](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR47 "Li, M.-G. et al. Structural and functional thalamic changes in Parkinson’s disease with mild cognitive impairment. J. Magn. Reson. Imaging 52, 1207–1215 (2020)."), [48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR48 "Kaufmann, L. et al. An fMRI study of the numerical stroop task in individuals with and without minimal cognitive impairment. Cortex 44, 1248–1255 (2008)."), [49](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR49 "Wang, Z. et al. Changes in thalamus connectivity in mild cognitive impairment: Evidence from resting state fMRI. Eur. J. Radiol. 81, 277–285 (2012)."). In this study, the higher levels of thalamic synchrony may indicate the compensatory efforts of healthy individuals to sustain normal brain function and alertness following cognitive fatigue. Additionally, following cognitive fatigue, there was a significant enhancement in the functional connectivity between the left medial superior frontal gyrus and both the left inferior parietal lobule and the right superior parietal lobule. This enhancement was positively correlated with subjective feelings of fatigue. The fronto-parietal network, which facilitates attentional allocation and executive control in response to task demands[50](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR50 "Martín-Signes, M., Paz-Alonso, P. M. & Chica, A. B. Connectivity of frontoparietal regions reveals executive attention and consciousness interactions. Cereb Cortex 29, 4539–4550 (2019)."), [51](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR51 "Caldinelli, C. & Cusack, R. The fronto-parietal network is not a flexible hub during naturalistic cognition. Hum. Brain Mapp. 43, 750–759 (2022)."), displayed increased functional connectivity after cognitive fatigue. The increased functional connectivity within this network after cognitive fatigue may be related to an increase in task demands associated with fatigue, as well as an increase in compensatory activities related to fatigue[52](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR52 "Taya, F. et al. Fronto-parietal subnetworks flexibility compensates for cognitive decline due to mental fatigue. Hum. Brain Mapp. 39, 3528–3545 (2018)."), [53](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR53 "Shashidhara, S., Mitchell, D. J., Erez, Y. & Duncan, J. Progressive recruitment of the frontoparietal multiple-demand system with increased task complexity, time pressure, and reward. J. Cogn. Neurosci. 31, 1617–1630 (2019)."). These enhanced functional connectivity changes after cognitive fatigue strongly support the Compensation-Related Utilization of Neural Circuits Hypothesis (CRUNCH)[54](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR54 "Schneider-Garces, N. J. et al. Span, CRUNCH, and beyond: Working memory capacity and the aging brain. J. Cogn. Neurosci. 22, 655–669 (2010)."), [55](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR55 "Klaassen, E. B. et al. Working memory in middle-aged males: Age-related brain activation changes and cognitive fatigue effects. Biol. Psychol. 96, 134–143 (2014)."). Moreover, this hypothesis suggests that as cognitive fatigue increases, limited neural resources will gradually be depleted, ultimately leading to a decline in related cognitive performance.

Some changes in functional connectivity, while not significantly correlated with subjective fatigue, are still reflected in various indicators across different dimensions. This suggests that these brain regions may engage in the complex neural regulation of cognitive fatigue through multiple mechanisms. For example, following cognitive fatigue, the ALFF and ReHo values of the left precuneus decreased while node efficiency increased significantly. This indicates a reduction in the local processing activity in this region, alongside enhanced coordination within the global brain network. This adjustment, characterized by reduced local activity and energy consumption coupled with increased global network efficiency, may reflect a compensatory mechanism by the brain to adapt to task demands and maintain cognitive function during fatigued[56](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR56 "Lee, T.-W. & Xue, S.-W. Linking graph features of anatomical architecture to regional brain activity: A multi-modal MRI study. Neurosci. Lett. 651, 123–127 (2017)."), [57](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR57 "Ma, M. et al. Functional whole-brain mechanisms underlying effects of tDCS on athletic performance of male rowing athletes revealed by resting-state fMRI. Front Psychol. 13, 1002548 (2022)."), [58](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR58 "Cao, W. et al. Right-to-left shunt-associated brain functional changes in migraine: Evidences from a resting-state FMRI study. Front Hum. Neurosci. 18, 1432525 (2024)."). In contrast, following cognitive fatigue, the ALFF value of the cerebellum significantly increased, while node efficiency decreased. This may suggest enhanced local compensatory activity in this region, while its global information integration capacity was weakened, indicating an imbalance in resource allocation that reflects the inhibitory effects of cognitive fatigue on cerebellar function[56](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR56 "Lee, T.-W. & Xue, S.-W. Linking graph features of anatomical architecture to regional brain activity: A multi-modal MRI study. Neurosci. Lett. 651, 123–127 (2017)."), [57](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR57 "Ma, M. et al. Functional whole-brain mechanisms underlying effects of tDCS on athletic performance of male rowing athletes revealed by resting-state fMRI. Front Psychol. 13, 1002548 (2022)."), [58](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-08330-6#ref-CR58 "Cao, W. et al. Right-to-left shunt-associated brain functional changes in migraine: Evidences from a resting-state FMRI study. Front Hum. Neurosci. 18, 1432525 (2024).").

This study employed voxel-to-voxel analysis to investigate changes in functional connectivity across the whole brain before and after cognitive fatigue. Notably, these changes were centered on the left medial superior frontal gyrus, which showed significantly increased functional connectivity with other frontal and parietal regions. This interesting phenomenon suggests that the left medial superior frontal gyrus may play a crucial role in cognitive fatigue induced by the Stroop task. However, further research is necessary to verify whether these functional connectivity changes also apply to cognitive fatigue induced by other cognitive tasks.

### Limitations

However, this study has several limitations. First, it lacks control conditions, as no comparison group was included that did not perform cognitive tasks under similar circumstances. This limits the ability to account for potential effects of MRI and other external factors on the results. Second, the study did not incorporate behavioral measures (e.g., reaction time and accuracy). We speculate that compensatory brain mechanisms during fatigue may counterbalance potential declines in behavioral performance; moreover, participants’ strong learning and adaptive abilities may reduce observable behavioral changes as indicators of cognitive fatigue. Previous studies have reported task-on-time (TOT) effects, where cognitive fatigue leads to slower reaction times and reduced accuracy. Future research should incorporate experimental paradigms with appropriate task difficulty and duration to more effectively induce cognitive fatigue. Third, all participants completed data collection in two phases, both conducted at the same time on a day. While this design minimizes the potential impact of time-related variables on the results, it is still challenging to completely eliminate potential physiological and psychological differences between participants across the two phases. Therefore, the correlation between changes in functional connectivity and subjective fatigue requires further validation. Fourth, this study identified various compensatory functional connectivity changes associated with cognitive fatigue; however, these changes may be effective only in the short term. Prolonged fatigue has the potential to cause brain network dysfunction or even neural damage. This phenomenon merits further investigation, particularly in examining the dynamic changes of these indicators during the fatigue recovery process. Finally, as the participants in this study were all healthy individuals, the generalizability of the findings may be limited. Prior research has shown varying degrees of structural and functional connectivity impairments in patients with cognitive disorders, which may lead to increased perceptions of fatigue and reduced compensatory capacity. Future research should focus on patients with cognitive impairments to better understand the mechanisms of cognitive fatigue in this population, as this knowledge is crucial for developing effective treatment and rehabilitation strategies.

## Conclusion

These changes of brain functional connectivity suggest that certain brain regions may participate in the intricate neuroregulatory processes of cognitive fatigue through various mechanisms. This study provides a solid theoretical foundation for developing new approaches to alleviate cognitive fatigue and for guiding the design of rehabilitation treatment plans for affected patients.
