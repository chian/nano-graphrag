## ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT ARTICLE IN PRESS

**ABSTRACT** Sleep problems are common in anxiety especially around the onset of adolescence (“peri-adolescence”) when symptoms escalate and sleep changes. The pineal gland is a compelling target given its role in sleep behavior and associations with multiple disorders that report problems with sleep. We investigated pineal gland volume and anxiety severity in relation to objective measures of sleep derived from polysomnography in a group of peri-adolescents. Youth (N=200; 10-13 years) were sampled across a continuum of anxiety severity, with special emphasis on social, generalized, and separation anxiety disorders, to capture variability in key risk profiles and behaviors. This study uses structural scans obtained from participants with polysomnography data (n=135). Pineal glands were manually segmented into parenchymal and cyst compartments. Linear models were used to assess the interaction between anxiety severity and pineal parenchymal volume (PPV) on sleep onset latency (SOL), sleep efficiency (SE), and non-rapid eye movement (NREM) and REM duration. Analyses revealed significant interactions between anxiety severity and PPV on SOL, SE, and NREM duration but not REM duration; whereby elevated anxiety was associated with larger PPV and longer SOL and reduced SE and NREM

**INTRODUCTION** Sleep problems are commonly observed across psychopathologies and prospectively relate to the emergence or escalation of internalizing symptoms, especially during the transition from childhood to adolescence, or “peri-adolesence” (1,2). Our mechanistic understanding of these sleep problems and their association with pathology remains poorly understood. This lack of clarity may be due to variability in sleep related problems within and across disorders (e.g., problems with duration, quality, transitions), a limited number of studies examining sleep staging and neurophysiology in clinical samples, and the flux of sleep related behaviors and neurophysiology across development and contexts (e.g., shift of circadian rhythm in adolescence) (3,4). Here we aim to address these limitations to drive new mechanistic discovery. We deeply characterize clinical symptoms and sleep via multimethod assessment focused on a narrow developmental window at the cusp of adolescence. Although other brain structures such as the amygdala, brainstem, and hypothalamus are implicated in sleep regulation, we focus the current study on the relation between the pineal gland, a region important for melatonin synthesis, a neurohormone essential for establishing and maintaining circadian rhythms among

Sleep problems and anxiety are bidirectionally related, escalate during adolescence, and may be influenced by neurodevelopmental changes unique to this developmental window. Subjective sleep problems are reported in approximately 85% of anxious youth (5). Similarly, anxiety symptoms and mental health comorbidities are a common feature in people suffering from sleep disturbances like

Moreover, reduced sleep duration and sleep related problems, such as poor quality or increased sleep onset latency, have been prospectively linked to increased symptoms of anxiety and depression (9). Despite the associations between sleep problems and internalizing disorders like anxiety, neurobiological mechanisms have not been established. Smaller pineal glands have consistently been observed in other psychopathologies that exhibit sleep problems (10–12). However, previous studies have been performed in adults with no studies to our knowledge examining the role of the pineal gland in peri- adolescent youth, a developmental window when internalizing symptom severity sharply escalates and melatonin secretion demonstrates significant changes. Anxiety disorders are characterized by excessive worry and fear consistent with chronic stress (13) – pineal volume has been shown to be sensitive to acute and chronic stress (14,15). Melatonin synthesis in the pineal gland is dependent on serotonin availability (16,17); notably, anxiety has been linked to serotonin

The pineal gland is an oval-shaped structure, part of the diencephalon in a region known as the epithalamus. The gland sits above the superior colliculus, inferior to the splenium aspect of the corpus callosum, dorsal to the habenular commissure, and ventral to the posterior commissure with connection to the third ventricle through what is known as the pineal recess (19). The primary function of the pineal gland is the production and distribution of melatonin, a hormone important to overall health. In humans, melatonin is distributed in the central nervous system through release into cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) via the pineal recess (21). Pineal cysts, widely varying in their prevalence in magnetic resonance imaging (2% to 57.4% (22,23)) and observed in 21-41% of autopsy specimens, are fluid filled compartments that present as hypointense in T1-weighted images compared to parenchyma (24). Volume of the pineal parenchyma has been found to positively correlate with melatonin levels (25). Melatonin plays a critical role in the regulation of sleep initiation and maintenance. The first phase includes reductions or involutive structural changes that emerge initially but are followed by a stimulated phase of activity where hyperplasia and hypertrophy are dominant (14,15); however, subsequent studies have not observed similar hyperplasia and hypertrophy (46,47). Increased PPV may also arise due to altered serotonin levels, a proposed mechanism in anxiety disorders (48–53), contributing to reduced melatonin availability. All variables of interest were significantly positively correlated (all p < 0.001) and Bartlett’s test of sphericity (χ2(6) = 354.89, p <

0.001) suggested the measures were sufficiently correlated to justify a PCA approach. Further, the Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin measure of sampling adequacy was in a range (0.78) indicating pair-wise correlations can be explained by other common factors – necessary for PCA. The resultant PCA included a single component with an eigenvalue of 2.71 accounting for 67.8% of the variance in anxiety scores. This first component was extracted for each participant and used as the primary measure of anxiety severity. Please see Baker et al., (90) for a more detailed description of this approach using the same data.
