Logistic regression analysis of risk factors for anxiety and depression in patients with coronary heart disease and subclinical hypothyroidism

## Abstract

We sought to explore the risk factors for anxiety and depression in patients with coronary heart disease and subclinical hypothyroidism through logistic regression analysis. A retrospective analysis was conducted on 168 patients with coronary heart disease and subclinical hypothyroidism admitted to the Department of Cardiology of our hospital from February 2020 to November 2022. Patients were categorized into the control group, anxiety group, and depression group based on the Hamilton Anxiety Scale (HAMA) and Hamilton Depression Rating Scale (HAMD) scores. All participants were informed about the protocol and provided signed informed consent upon inclusion. The study examined influencing factors for anxiety and depression in patients with coronary heart disease and subclinical hypothyroidism. Collect patients’ gender, age, presence or absence of chronic diseases (including Diabetes, hypertension and hyperthyroidism), sleep quality, dietary habits, psychosocial stress, living environment, social support, education level, and blood TSH levels. The linear relationship between anxiety, depression, and each influencing factor was quantified using the Pearson correlation coefficient. Blood level of TSH and free T4 were detected by chemiluminescence immunoassay. Multiple logistic regression was applied to analyze the factors influencing anxiety and depression in these patients. Various factors were identified as significant influencers of anxiety and depression in patients with coronary heart disease and subclinical hypothyroidism. For anxiety, presence or absence of chronic diseases, sleep quality, dietary habits, psychosocial pressure, living environment, and blood TSH levels were found to be influential ( _P_ < 0.05). Similarly, for depression, presence or absence of chronic diseases, sleep quality, social support, quality of life, social support, education level, and blood TSH levels were identified as significant factors ( _P_ < 0.05). The study revealed positive correlations between presence or absence of chronic diseases, psychosocial stress, and TSH levels with anxiety symptoms in patients with coronary heart disease and subclinical hypothyroidism ( _P_ < 0.05). Conversely, sleep quality, dietary patterns, and living environment showed negative correlations with anxiety symptoms ( _P_ < 0.05).Gender and age had no correlation with anxiety levels ( _P_ > 0.05). Presence or absence of chronic diseases and TSH levels were positively correlated with depressive symptoms in patients with coronary heart diseaseand subclinical hypothyroidism ( _P_ < 0.05). On the other hand, sleep quality, social support, quality of life, and educational level were negatively correlated with anxiety symptoms ( _P_ < 0.05). Gender and age had no correlation with depression ( _P_ > 0.05). Notably, TSH levels in both the anxiety and depression groups were higher than those in the control group ( _P_ < 0.05), with no significant difference in free T4 levels among the groups ( _P_ > 0.05). The combination of chronic illness types, living habits (sleep quality, dietary habits), psychosocial pressure, living environment, and TSH levels emerged as risk factors for anxiety in patients with coronary heart disease and subclinical hypothyroidism ( _P_ < 0.05). Similarly, the combination of chronic illness types, sleep quality, social support, quality of life, education level, and TSH levels were identified as risk factors for depression in these patients ( _P_ < 0.05). This logistic regression analysis underscores the significant impact of factors such as types of chronic illness, sleep quality, social support, living environment, education level, and TSH levels on anxiety and depression symptoms in patients with coronary heart disease and subclinical hypothyroidism. These findings highlight the importance of considering these multiple risk factors collectively when devising treatment and management strategies to reduce the risk of mental health issues in this patient population.

## Introduction

Coronary heart disease, a predominant form of cardiovascular illness globally, has emerged as a leading cause of mortality across the world[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR1 "1. Shangguan F, Chen Z, Feng L, Lu J, Zhang XY: The prevalence and clinical correlates of suicide attempts in comorbid subclinical hypothyroidism in patients with never-treated major depressive disorder in China. Journal of affective disorders 2022, 312:54–60."). Typically arising from inadequate myocardial blood supply, this condition poses a significant health threat. Simultaneously, subclinical hypothyroidism, characterized by elevated blood level of TSH and normal peripheral thyroid hormone levels, has garnered increasing attention in clinical settings. The rising incidence of individuals with coronary heart disease complicated by subclinical hypothyroidism has become a subject of concern among clinicians and public health experts worldwide[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR2 "2. Elzanaty AM, Patel N, Sabbagh E, Eltahawy EA: Patent foramen ovale closure in the management of cryptogenic stroke: a review of current literature and guideline statements. Current medical research and opinion 2021, 37(3):377–384.").

As research delves deeper into the psychological ramifications of chronic diseases, it has become evident that anxiety and depression symptoms are prevalent, particularly among patients with cardiovascular diseases and thyroid dysfunction[3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR3 "3. Feldman AZ, Shrestha RT, Hennessey JV: Neuropsychiatric manifestations of thyroid disease. Endocrinology and metabolism clinics of North America 2013, 42(3):453–476."). These mental health challenges not only profoundly affect individuals’quality of life but may also adversely influence diseases prognoses[4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR4 "4. Wu Z, Wang P, Pan D, Zeng X, Guo Y, Zhao G: Effect of adzuki bean sprout fermented milk enriched in γ-aminobutyric acid on mild depression in a mouse model. Journal of dairy science 2021, 104(1):78–91.").

Despite this, there is a scarcity of comprehensive research systematically discussing the risk factors associated with anxiety and depression in individuals with coronary heart disease complicated by subclinical hypothyroidism. Against this backdrop, conducting a “logistic regression analysis of risk factors for anxiety and depression in coronary heart disease complicated by subclinical hypothyroidism” assumes great significance. The aim of this study was to elucidate the pivotal factors influencing the mental health status of this specific patient group and their correlation with anxiety and depression symptoms. By identifying these pertinent risk factors, the study sought to furnish clinicians with more comprehensive guidance for managing the mental health aspects of patients with coronary heart disease complicated by subclinical hypothyroidism. In the field of cardiovascular diseases, mental health concerns have consistently constituted an important part of chronic disease management. Anxiety and depression not only intertwine with the occurrence and development of chronic cardiovascular diseases but can also affect patients’ adherence to treatment and overall quality of life. In addition, thyroid dysfunction, especially hypothyroidism, is recognized as having implications for mental health. In the case of coronary heart disease complicated by subclinical hypothyroidism, this association may be even more complicated.

In this study, we used a logistic regression model to analyze the relationship between various potential risk factors and symptoms of anxiety and depression. By identifying and understanding these key risk factors, the aim was to provide more personalized and effective psychological support for these patients, thereby enhancing their overall health and quality of life.

## Materials and methods

### General information

A retrospective analysis was conducted on 168 individuals with coronary heart disease complicated by subclinical hypothyroidism at our hospital from February 2020 to November 2022. The study participants, aged between 36 and 76 years, had an average age of 64.37 ± 6.41 years, including 92 males and 76 females. Patients were divided into three groups: the control group (HAMA < 14, HAMD < 10, _n_ = 95), anxiety group (HAMA ≥ 14, _n_ = 33), and depression group (HAMD ≥ 10, _n_ = 47), based on Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale (HAMA) and Hamilton Depression Rating Scale (HAMD) scores. ## Inclusion criteria

Participants were required to be at least 18 years old, regardless of gender. The diagnosis of coronary heart disease was established based on clinical criteria such as electrocardiogram and coronary angiography results. Thyroid function assessment revealed elevated thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) levels. Additionally, patients needed to possess sufficient cognitive ability and to understand the research content and procedures and had to sign the informed consent form.

## Exclusion criteria

Exclusion criteria encompassed patients with severe schizophrenia and bipolar disorder, advanced tumors, liver cirrhosis, or chronic renal failure. Those with recent acute cardiac events such as myocardial infarction or heart failure, visibly abnormal thyroid function, such as clinically obvious hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism, ongoing thyroid hormone replacement therapy or specific heart medications, pregnant or lactating women, and those unable to comprehend the research process or provide informed consent were excluded.

## Medical ethics issues

This study obtained approval from the relevant ethics review committee at the First Hospital of Hebei Medical University. Inclusion in the research project was contingent upon providing informed consent, and all procedures complied with ethical norms and regulations.

## Methods

Participants were stratified into a control group ( _n_ = 95), an anxiety group ( _n_ = 33), and a depression group ( _n_ = 47) based on the scoring criteria of the Hamilton Anxiety Scale (HAMA) and Hamilton Depression Scale (HAMD). The HMDA scale is the Hamilton Depression Scale, developed by Hamilton in 1960, and is one of the most commonly used scales for assessing depression status in clinical practice. The majority of items in this scale are scored on a 5-level scale ranging from 0 to 4, with the following criteria for each level: 0 indicates none; 1 point indicates mild; 2 points indicate moderate; 3 points indicate severity; A score of 4 indicates extremely severe. A small number of projects adopt a three-level rating system of 0–2 points (0: none; 1: suspicious or mild; 2: obvious symptoms). The HMDA scale is suitable for adult patients with depressive symptoms. The evaluator should observe the patient, and the time range for evaluation is generally the previous week’s situation. A single evaluation takes 15–20 min. The results of this scale can reflect the severity of the condition through the total score: less than 8 points indicate normal, 8–20 points may indicate depression, 21–35 points indicate diagnosed depression, and more than 35 points indicate severe depression. The analysis of HMDA scale results is very important. A total score below 8 indicates no symptoms of depression, 8–20 indicates possible depression, 21–35 indicates diagnosed depression, and above 35 indicates severe depression. When evaluating, attention should be paid to having trained evaluators examine patients, score them based on their performance, and ultimately determine the patient’s level of depression through the total score. The Hamilton Depression Scale (HAMD) was developed by Hamilton in 1960 and is the most commonly used scale for assessing depression in clinical practice. This scale has three versions: 17 items, 21 items, and 24 items, mainly used to evaluate patients’ depressive symptoms and their severity. Two trained assessors will conduct a joint examination of the patient and score them through conversation and observation. Scoring method: Adopting a 5-level scoring method ranging from 0 to 4 points, with a few projects using a 3-level scoring method ranging from 0 to 2 points. Pre - and post-treatment scoring: Scoring is performed before and after treatment to assess the severity of the condition and the effectiveness of treatment.

Compilation of comprehensive patient data: encompassing chronic disease comorbidities, living conditions, and educational background details.

Utilize the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI)[5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR5 "5. Zitser J, Allen IE, Falgàs N, Le MM, Neylan TC, Kramer JH, Walsh CM: Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) responses are modulated by total sleep time and wake after sleep onset in healthy older adults. PloS one 2022, 17(6):e0270095.") score for assessing the sleep quality of patients: PSQI score is utilized for the evaluation of participants’ sleep quality, comprising 19 self-assessment and 5 other assessment items, with the 19th self-assessment item and the 5 other assessment items being non-scored. The 18 items are categorized into seven components, namely sleep quality, sleep onset latency, sleep duration, habitual sleep efficiency, sleep disturbances, use of sleeping medication, and daytime dysfunction. Each component is rated on a scale from 0 to 3, and the sum of these ratings yields the total PSQI score. This composite score ranges from 0 to 21 points; higher scores indicate poorer sleep quality. The homogeneity reliability of the PSQI scale ranges from 0.537 to 0.846, with a Cronbach’s alpha coefficient of 0.833 and a split half reliability of 0.853. The seven principal components obtained have a cumulative contribution rate of 58.42%, and their loadings are all between 0.475 and 0.868.

Utilize the Food Frequency Questionnaire (FFQ)[6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR6 "6. El Kinany K, Garcia-Larsen V: Adaptation and validation of a food frequency questionnaire (FFQ) to assess dietary intake in Moroccan adults. Nutrition journal 2018, 17(1):61.") for evaluating the dietary quality of patients: The FFQ is a questionnaire designed to assess the frequency of consumption of specific foods over a defined period, such as one day, one week, or one month. It aims to capture dietary patterns within the population for research purposes and does not comprehensively evaluate overall dietary intake. The FFQ focuses on individuals’ habitual food intake, providing data on their past nutrient and food consumption while also shedding light on their dietary habits and patterns. The relevant validity coefficients of FFQ range from 0.4 to 0.7 and 0.3–0.6, respectively.

Utilize the Perceived Social Support Scale (PSSS)[7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR7 "7. Zimet GD, Powell SS, Farley GK, Werkman S, Berkoff KA: Psychometric characteristics of the Multidimensional Scale of Perceived Social Support. Journal of personality assessment 1990, 55(3–4):610–617.") for the collection of social support data: This scale serves as an assessment tool for gauging an individual’s subjective comprehension and perception of social support. Comprising 12 items, each utilizing a 7-point scoring system to ascertain the perceived level of social support from sources such as family, friends, and others, the total score reflects the individual’s perceived level of social support. This scale has been adapted for local use and has demonstrated effective application in Chinese cancer patients. The Cronbach’s alpha coefficients of the PSSS total scale and three subscales are 0.923, 0.909, 0.866, and 0.789, respectively, with a half fold reliability of 0.801 ~ 0.915, indicating excellent reliability of the scale.

Utilize the Short Form-36 (SF-36)[8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR8 "8. Su CT, Ng HS, Yang AL, Lin CY: Psychometric evaluation of the Short Form 36 Health Survey (SF-36) and the World Health Organization Quality of Life Scale Brief Version (WHOQOL-BREF) for patients with schizophrenia. Psychological assessment 2014, 26(3):980–989.") for evaluating the patients’ quality of life: This table was derived from the Boston Health Study in the United States, utilizing the Medical Outcome Study Scale developed by Stewart in 1988. In 1991, the Chinese version of SF-36 was translated by the Social Medicine Teaching and Research Office of Zhejiang University School of Medicine, encompassing eight domains: physical functioning, role limitations due to physical health, bodily pain, general health perceptions, vitality, social functioning, role limitations due to emotional problems, and mental health. The test-retest reliability of SF-36 in various fields is as follows: physiological function (PF) 0.78, physiological problem limiting function (RP) 0.85, physical pain (BP) 0.92, overall health evaluation (GH) 0.82, vitality (VT) 0.77, social function (SF) 0.71, psychological problem limiting function (RE) 0.79, and mental health (MH) 0.66; The Cronbach’s alpha coefficients for each field are: PF 0.89 RP 0.75、BP 0.84、GH 0.86、VT 0.78、SF 0.72、RE0.86、MH 0.50.

Assessment of living environment: The process of conducting a ward environment safety risk assessment encompasses the following key elements. (1) Identifying potential risk factors involves thorough observation and investigation of the ward environment to pinpoint issues such as improper equipment placement, electrical hazards, and inadequate protective facilities. (2) Assessing the severity of risks entails evaluating each potential risk factor based on its likelihood and impact in order to gauge its effect on patients and healthcare workers. Evaluating the effectiveness of existing control measures involves assessing whether current measures are adequate in reducing the likelihood and severity of risks. Developing improvement plans is based on evaluation results, with clear delineation of necessary actions and improvement objectives including enhancements in equipment configuration, environmental remediation, training, and education. Implementing improvement measures requires executing corresponding control strategies outlined in the improvement plan such as equipment upgrades, employee training, and environmental remediation. Monitoring and evaluating improvement effectiveness necessitates regular assessment to refine improvement plans iteratively while ensuring continuous enhancement.

Assess the TSH level in the patient’s peripheral blood: In the morning, the patient was awakened and 5 ml of venous blood was collected on an empty stomach. The TSH concentration in the patient’s peripheral blood was detected using a standard chemiluminescence immunoassay, with the reagent kit obtained from Shanghai Biyun Tian Company. It is best to collect blood samples at the same time point (fasting in the morning). Venous blood collection is used, and sterile blood collection tubes can be selected with or without anticoagulants, depending on subsequent processing and analysis needs. Samples typically require 1–2 mL of blood. Centrifuge the blood sample at 4 ° C (e.g. 3000 g, 10 min) to separate plasma or serum. Transfer the separated serum or plasma to a new sterile tube and label it. The separated plasma or serum should be stored at -80 ° C under low temperature conditions.

### Statistical analysis

All data in this study were analyzed using SPSS28.0 statistical analysis software (IBM, USA). The measurement data was presented as “mean ± standard deviation” (± s), and intergroup comparisons were conducted using independent sample t-tests. Count data were expressed as percentages (%), and intergroup comparisons were analyzed using chi-square analysis. We explored the correlation between various variables and symptoms of anxiety and depression, utilizing Pearson correlation coefficient analysis to quantify the linear relationship between anxiety and depression symptoms and various influencing factors. Multiple logistic regression was employed to investigate the influencing factors of anxiety and depression in patients. A significance level of _P_ < 0.05 indicates statistical significance.

## Discussion

In our investigation of the factors influencing anxiety and depression in patients with coronary heart disease and subclinical hypothyroidism, we have identified a series of significantly correlated factors. Key influencing factors for anxiety include types of comorbid chronic diseases, sleep quality, dietary habits, psychosocial stress, living environment, and blood TSH levels. Significant influencers of depressive symptoms encompass the type of comorbid chronic diseases, sleep quality, social support, quality of life, educational level, and blood TSH levels. These findings underscore the profound impact of chronic illnesses on mental well-being. The increase in comorbid chronic diseases often leads to higher treatment complexity and reduced quality of life which may contribute to elevated symptoms of anxiety and depression.

Firstly, it is imperative to acknowledge the profound influence of sleep quality and dietary habits on mental health. Optimal sleep and a well-balanced diet are essential for maintaining both physical and mental well-being, whereas irregular sleep patterns and poor dietary habits may exacerbate symptoms of anxiety and depression. Psychological and social stressors, along with living conditions, exert a particularly significant impact on anxiety in patients with coronary heart disease and subclinical hypothyroidism. Prolonged states of stress and adverse living environments can lead to the accumulation of psychological stress responses, thereby intensifying symptoms of anxiety[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR9 "9. Celli BR, Fabbri LM: Differential Diagnosis of Suspected Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease Exacerbations in the Acute Care Setting: Best Practice. American journal of respiratory and critical care medicine 2023, 207(9):1134–1144."), [10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR10 "10. Yuan L, Luan D, Xu X, Yang Q, Huang X, Zhao S, Zhang Y, Zhou Z: Altered attention networks in patients with thyroid dysfunction: A neuropsychological study. Hormones and behavior 2020, 121:104714."), [11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR11 "11. Qiao D, Liu H, Zhang X, Lei L, Sun N, Yang C, Li G, Guo M, Zhang Y, Zhang K et al: Exploring the potential of thyroid hormones to predict clinical improvements in depressive patients: A machine learning analysis of the real-world based study. Journal of affective disorders 2022, 299:159–165.") .

Secondly, empirical evidence has demonstrated that the absence of social support constitutes a significant risk factor for depressive symptoms. Social support plays a crucial role in offering emotional solace and practical aid, mitigating the psychological distress experienced by individuals. Conversely, the absence of such support may engender feelings of isolation and helplessness, thereby exacerbating depressive symptoms[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR12 "12. Zhao X, Zhang D, Wu M, Yang Y, Xie H, Li Y, Jia J, Su Y: Loneliness and depression symptoms among the elderly in nursing homes: A moderated mediation model of resilience and social support. Cuijpers P, Karyotaki E: The effects of psychological treatment of perinatal depression: an overview. Archives of women’s mental health 2021, 24(5):801–806.").

Furthermore, the educational attainment of individuals significantly influences the manifestation of depressive symptoms in patients with both coronary heart disease and subclinical hypothyroidism[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR14 "14. Chlapecka A, Kagstrom A, Cermakova P: Educational attainment inequalities in depressive symptoms in more than 100,000 individuals in Europe. European psychiatry : the journal of the Association of European Psychiatrists 2020, 63(1):e97."). This association may be attributed to the influence of education on health literacy, adaptive coping mechanisms, and access to resources[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR14 "14. Chlapecka A, Kagstrom A, Cermakova P: Educational attainment inequalities in depressive symptoms in more than 100,000 individuals in Europe. European psychiatry : the journal of the Association of European Psychiatrists 2020, 63(1):e97."). Those with higher levels of education are likely to exhibit greater proficiency in self-management of their illnesses and resilience in dealing with life stressors, thereby mitigating the risk of developing depressive symptoms.

Ultimately, fluctuations in blood TSH levels exhibited a significant association with symptoms of anxiety and depression, underscoring the intricate connection between thyroid function and mental well-being. The journal of nutrition, health & aging 2009, 13(4):317–321."), [17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR17 "17. Beydoun MA, Beydoun HA, Shroff MR, Kitner-Triolo MH, Zonderman AB: Serum leptin, thyroxine and thyroid-stimulating hormone levels interact to affect cognitive function among US adults: evidence from a large representative survey. Neurobiology of aging 2012, 33(8):1730–1743.").

Pearson correlation coefficient was used to analyze the relationship between anxiety and depression symptoms and various possible influencing factors in patients with coronary heart disease complicated by subclinical hypothyroidism. The analysis results reveals numerous factors affecting anxiety symptoms in these patients, shedding light on the intricate interplay between chronic disease management and mental health. The increase of chronic diseases not only heightens treatment complexity, but may also instigate persistent health-related anxiety and a significant decline in quality of life. This accumulated burden places significant pressure on patients, fostering heightened anxiety levels. Psychosocial stress emerges as another pivotal factor affecting anxiety. Long-term psychological stress and adverse effects of social environment, such as social isolation, occupational instability, or family conflicts, can amplify the psychological burden on patients, leading to increased anxiety[18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR18 "18. Wang C, Wu W, Yang H, Ye Z, Zhao Y, Liu J, Mu L: Mendelian randomization analyses for PCOS: evidence, opportunities, and challenges. Trends in genetics : TIG 2022, 38(5):468–482."), [19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR19 "19. Finsrud I, Nissen-Lie HA, Ulvenes P, Melsom L, Vrabel K, Wampold B: Confidence in the therapist and confidence in the treatment predict symptomatic improvement week by week in therapy: A latent curve modeling approach. Journal of counseling psychology 2022, 69(6):823–834."). This pressure may stem from the inherent burden of the disease and the social and family environment surrounding the patient. Furthermore, an increase TSH level indicates that thyroid dysfunction may have a negative impact on emotional regulation[20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR20 "20. Du FM, Kuang HY, Duan BH, Liu DN, Yu XY: Effects of thyroid hormone and depression on common components of central obesity. The Journal of international medical research 2019, 47(7):3040–3049."). Given the crucial role of thyroid hormones in mental health, their involvement in regulating mood and behavior suggests that any abnormality of thyroid function, including subclinical changes, may heighten anxiety. On the other hand, maintaining good sleep quality and a balanced diet plays a critical role in sustaining emotional stability and reducing anxiety. Inadequate or poor sleep quality can induce mood swings and cognitive decline, while a balanced diet aids in stabilizing blood sugar levels and providing sufficient energy—essential elements for effective emotional regulation. Simultaneously, a supportive and comfortable living environment provides a sanctuary for patients, offering relief from stress and fostering emotional recovery. This aspect holds significant importance in relieving anxiety symptoms.

In the development of depressive symptoms, the presence or absence of chronic diseases, sleep quality, social support, quality of life, education level, and TSH levels collectively exert significant influences[21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR21 "21. Qiu QW, Qian S, Li JY, Jia RX, Wang YQ, Xu Y: Risk factors for depressive symptoms among older Chinese adults: A meta-analysis. Journal of affective disorders 2020, 277:341–346."). These factors interact, jointly shaping an individual’s psychological state, a phenomenon particularly pronounced in patients with coronary heart disease complicated by subclinical hypothyroidism. The burden caused by the combination of multiple chronic diseases may lead to psychological stress, fostering feelings of helplessness and despair, thereby increasing the risk of depression[22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR22 "22. Dai W, Liu J, Xie H, Teng Z, Luo W, Yuan H, Chen J, Liu M, Zhang X: Association between subclinical hypothyroidism and psychotic features in Chinese young adults with first-episode and untreated major depressive disorder. Journal of affective disorders 2023, 333:209–215."), [23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR23 "23. Zhan L, Yin H, Gao Y, Li Y, Ma J: Prevalence and Factors Associated with Subclinical Hypothyroidism in Major Depressive Disorder Patients with Dyslipidemia. Neuropsychiatric disease and treatment 2023, 19:2309–2318."). Each disease introduces unique challenges and management requirements, and their cumulative impact may exceed the coping abilities of patients, leading to emotional decline and the development of depression. Sleep quality emerges as a crucial determinant of mental health[24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR24 "24. Scott AJ, Webb TL, Martyn-St James M, Rowse G, Weich S: Improving sleep quality leads to better mental health: A meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. Sleep medicine reviews 2021, 60:101556."). Inadequate or poor sleep quality not only negatively affects physical health but also heightens the risk of depressive symptoms by influencing emotional well-being[24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR24 "24. Scott AJ, Webb TL, Martyn-St James M, Rowse G, Weich S: Improving sleep quality leads to better mental health: A meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. Sleep medicine reviews 2021, 60:101556."). Conversely, good sleep contributes to the recovery of the body and brain, improving emotional regulation. Social support proves to be a vital role with depression[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR25 "25. Wu S, Wang H, Zhou Y, Xia X, Yue Y, Wu Y, Peng R, Yang R, Li R, Yuan N et al: Clinical correlates of autoimmune thyroiditis and non-autoimmune hypothyroidism in treatment-naïve patients with major depressive disorders. Journal of affective disorders 2023, 323:755–761."). The support of family, friends, and community provides emotional comfort and practical assistance, reduces psychological burdens, and improve scoping abilities. On the contrary, a lack of social support may lead to feelings of isolation and helplessness, aggravating depression symptoms[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR26 "26. Gariépy G, Honkaniemi H, Quesnel-Vallée A: Social support and protection from depression: systematic review of current findings in Western countries. The British journal of psychiatry : the journal of mental science 2016, 209(4):284–293."). In addition, quality of life and education level affect an individual’s cognition and coping strategies. Educational background and cultural influences impact how individuals comprehend and process health information, shaping their approach to health challenges. The decline in quality of life directly affects emotional state and may precipitate the occurrence of depression. Changes in thyroid function, especially the elevation of TSH levels, can reflect the influence of potential endocrine disorders on emotional states[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR27 "27. Zhao S, Zhang X, Zhou Y, Xu H, Li Y, Chen Y, Zhang B, Sun X: Comparison of thyroid function in different emotional states of drug-naïve patients with bipolar disorder. BMC endocrine disorders 2021, 21(1):210."). Imbalances in thyroid hormone levels may lead to mood swings and cognitive dysfunction, increasing the risk of depression[20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-77516-1#ref-CR20 "20. Du FM, Kuang HY, Duan BH, Liu DN, Yu XY: Effects of thyroid hormone and depression on common components of central obesity. The Journal of international medical research 2019, 47(7):3040–3049."). Chemiluminescence immunoassay results, revealing elevated TSH levels in the anxiety and depression groups compared to the control group, with no significant difference in free T4 levels, further emphasize the association between thyroid function and mental health. Considering the compound risk factors of anxiety and depression through this analysis method, which accounts for the interaction of multiple variables, provides a more comprehensive risk assessment. The results highlight that the presence or absence of chronic diseases, living habits (sleep quality, dietary pattern), psychosocial stress, living environment, and TSH levels are primary risk factors for anxiety. Similarly, the presence or absence of chronic diseases, sleep quality, social support, quality of life, education level, and TSH levels stand out as key influencing factors for depression.

In summary, this logistic regression analysis of patients with coronary heart disease complicated by subclinical hypothyroidism unveils the substantial influence of chronic diseases, sleep quality, social support, living environment, education level, and TSH levels on anxiety and depression symptoms. These findings emphasize the necessity of a comprehensive consideration of these risk factors in the treatment and management of such patients, aiming to reduce the risk of mental health problems.
