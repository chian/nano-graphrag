Cognition and the menopause transition: cross-sectional evidence from a large community cohort


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z.pdf)

## Abstract

Subjective cognitive symptoms (brain fog, memory problems) are common in menopause, but whether these self-reported symptoms correspond to measurable deficits in cognition remains unclear. In 14,234 females (aged 45–55) from the REACT-Long Covid Study, we examined self-reported cognitive symptoms and objective cognitive performance in premenopausal, perimenopausal, postmenopausal participants. Global cognitive performance was derived from eight online tasks (‘Cognitron’). Perimenopausal (OR = 1.31 \[1.18, 1.35\], _p_ = 0.015) and postmenopausal participants (OR = 1.13 \[1.08, 1.32\], _p_ = 0.014) had higher odds of reporting cognitive symptoms than premenopausal participants. Objective cognitive performance differed minimally across menopause status groups, with perimenopausal participants showing marginally higher accuracy than premenopausal and postmenopausal participants (0.03–0.06 SD, _p_ < 0.023). Across all menopause status groups, cognitive symptoms were weakly associated with objective performance but moderately related to psychological symptoms. These findings underscore recognising cognitive symptoms as a key component of menopausal care and integrating patient-reported outcomes with objective and biological measures of cognitive health.

## Introduction

Menopause is a major neuroendocrine event for females, with spontaneous menopause occurring at the average age of 51[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR1 "Brinton, R. D., Yao, J., Yin, F., Mack, W. J. & Cadenas, E. Perimenopause as a neurological transition state. Nat. Rev. Endocrinol. 11, 393–405 (2015)."). Cognitive symptoms, which are subjective experiences of cognitive difficulty like forgetfulness and ‘brain fog’ (relating to memory lapses, mental slowing, or attention difficulties[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR2 "Maki, P. M. & Jaff, N. G. Menopause and brain fog: how to counsel and treat midlife women. Menopause 31, 647–649 (2024).")), have been reported in 40–50% of females[3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR3 "Woods, N. F., Mitchell, E. S. & Adams, C. Memory functioning among midlife women: observations from the Seattle Midlife Women’s Health Study. Menopause 7, 257–265 (2000)."), [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR4 "Mitchell, E. S. & Woods, N. F. Cognitive symptoms during the menopausal transition and early postmenopause. Climacteric 14, 252–261 (2011)."), [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR5 "Zhu, Y., Yang, X., Wang, Y. & Zhu, X. Assessment of knowledge, understanding and awareness of Chinese women clinical staff towards menopause hormone therapy: a survey study. J. Obstet. Gynaecol. 43, 2171779 (2023)."), and up to 80% of females attending private clinics[6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR6 "Betti, S. et al. Attitudes towards menopause in a group of women followed in a public service for menopause counseling. Aging 13, 331–338 (2001)."). Moreover, perimenopausal and postmenopausal females are more likely than premenopausal females to report cognitive symptoms[7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR7 "Gold, E. B. et al. Relation of demographic and lifestyle factors to symptoms in a multi-racial/ethnic population of women 40-55 years of age. Am. J. Epidemiol. 152, 463–473 (2000)."), [8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR8 "Devi, G., Hahn, K., Massimi, S. & Zhivotovskaya, E. Prevalence of memory loss complaints and other symptoms associated with the menopause transition: a community survey. Gend. Med. 2, 255–264 (2005)."), [9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR9 "Coslov, N., Richardson, M. K. & Woods, N. F. Symptom experience during the late reproductive stage and the menopausal transition: observations from the women living better survey. Menopause 28, 1012–1025 (2021)."). Consequently, cognitive symptoms during menopause may negatively affect quality of life and perceived health[10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR10 "Griffiths, A., MacLennan, S. J. & Hassard, J. Menopause and work: an electronic survey of employees’ attitudes in the UK. Maturitas 76, 155–159 (2013)."), [11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR11 "Nappi, R. E. et al. Prevalence and quality-of-life burden of vasomotor symptoms associated with menopause: a European cross-sectional survey. Maturitas 167, 66–74 (2023).").

The biological basis of these cognitive symptoms is yet to be fully elucidated, but fluctuations in oestrogen in menopause are thought to play a role due to disruption to oestrogen receptor networks, which subsequently alter i) intracellular signalling, such as acetylcholine and dopaminergic systems[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR12 "Russell, J. K., Jones, C. K. & Newhouse, P. A. The role of estrogen in brain and cognitive aging. Neurotherapeutics 16, 649–665 (2019)."), and ii) neural function in brain regions critical to cognition, memory and emotion, namely the hippocampus, amygdala, and prefrontal cortex[13](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR13 "Merchenthaler, I. & Shughrue, P. J. Estrogen Receptor alpha versus beta: new estrogen responsive tissues and new potentials for HRT. in Biol. Menopause (ed. Bellino, F. L.) 259–272 (Springer, 2000)."), [14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR14 "Hara, Y., Waters, E. M., McEwen, B. S. & Morrison, J. H. Estrogen effects on cognitive and synaptic health over the lifecourse. Physiol. Rev. 95, 785–807 (2015)."). These pathways provide useful context for understanding cognitive symptom reported across the menopause transition.

Evidence of measurable impairments in objective cognitive performance during menopause is contradictory. Some studies have reported subtle cognitive decline during perimenopause[15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR15 "Maki, P. M. et al. Cognitive changes during the menopausal transition: a longitudinal study in women with and without HIV. Menopause 28, 360–368 (2021).") which resolves postmenopause[16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR16 "Greendale, G. A. et al. Effects of the menopause transition and hormone use on cognitive performance in midlife women. Neurology 72, 1850–1857 (2009)."), [17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR17 "Epperson, C. N., Sammel, M. D. & Freeman, E. W. Menopause effects on verbal memory: findings from a longitudinal community cohort. J. Clin. Endocrinol. Metab. 98, 3829–3838 (2013)."), while others have reported no significant change[18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR18 "Fuh, J. L., Wang, S. J., Lee, S. J., Lu, S. R. & Juang, K. D. A longitudinal study of cognition change during early menopausal transition in a rural community. Maturitas 53, 447–453 (2006)."), [19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR19 "Kilpi, F. et al. Changes in six domains of cognitive function with reproductive and chronological ageing and sex hormones: a longitudinal study in 2411 UK mid-life women. BMC Women’s Health 20, 177,                    https://doi.org/10.1186/s12905-020-01040-3                                     (2020)."). Similarly, some cross-sectional studies have shown differences in cognitive performance based on menopausal status[20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR20 "Kaur, M. & Kaur, M. Cognitive performance of women at various stages of reproductive aging and associated risk factors. Climacteric 25, 278–285 (2022)."), while others have not[21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR21 "Luetters, C. et al. Menopause transition stage and endogenous estradiol and follicle-stimulating hormone levels are not related to cognitive performance: cross-sectional results from the study of women’s health across the nation (SWAN). J. Women’s Health 16, 331–344 (2007)."). These discrepancies may result from methodological differences, such as characterisation of menopause, objective measures of cognition (global, task-specific), follow-up duration[22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR22 "Greendale, G. A., Karlamangla, A. S. & Maki, P. M. The menopause transition and cognition. JAMA 323, 1495–1496 (2020)."), and the nature of population-based samples. For example, those attending private clinics for menopause may report more menopausal symptoms, which may introduce bias.

Whilst some menopause research shows cognitive symptoms are linked to poorer working memory and slower reaction times[23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR23 "Drogos, L. L. et al. Objective cognitive performance is related to subjective memory complaints in midlife women with moderate to severe vasomotor symptoms. Menopause 20, 1236–1242 (2013)."), [24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR24 "Schaafsma, M., Homewood, J. & Taylor, A. Subjective cognitive complaints at menopause associated with declines in performance of verbal memory and attentional processes. Climacteric 13, 84–98 (2010)."), [25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR25 "Jaff, N. G., Rubin, L. H., Crowther, N. J., Norris, S. A. & Maki, P. M. Menopausal symptoms, menopausal stage and cognitive functioning in black urban African women. Climacteric 23, 38–45 (2020)."), but not verbal memory, or attention[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR26 "Weber, M. T., Mapstone, M., Staskiewicz, J. & Maki, P. M. Reconciling subjective memory complaints with objective memory performance in the menopausal transition. Menopause 19, 735–741 (2012)."), [27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR27 "Chen, L. et al. Menopausal hormone therapy does not improve some domains of memory: a systematic review and meta-analysis. Front. Endocrinol. 13, 894883 (2022).") other studies have linked cognitive symptoms to poorer verbal memory and attention-mediated processes alongside more dominant influences of negative affect and severe vasomotor symptoms[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR26 "Weber, M. T., Mapstone, M., Staskiewicz, J. & Maki, P. M. Reconciling subjective memory complaints with objective memory performance in the menopausal transition. Menopause 19, 735–741 (2012)."). Brain fog in disorders like COVID-19 has been weakly associated with objective cognitive impairment[28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR28 "Hampshire, A. et al. Cognition and memory after Covid-19 in a large community sample. N. Engl. J. Med. 390, 806–818 (2024)."), yet despite brain fog being increasingly recognised in healthcare professional terminology in menopause[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR2 "Maki, P. M. & Jaff, N. G. Menopause and brain fog: how to counsel and treat midlife women. Menopause 31, 647–649 (2024)."), [29](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR29 "Maki, P. M. & Jaff, N. G. Brain fog in menopause: a health-care professional’s guide for decision-making and counseling on cognition. Climacteric 25, 570–578 (2022)."), [30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR30 "Johnson, H. & Ogden, J. Much more than a biological phenomenon: A qualitative study of women’s experiences of brain fog across their reproductive journey. J. Health Psychol. 30, 1963–1976 (2025)."), its relationship with objective cognitive performance likely varies across populations[31](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR31 "Denno, P., Zhao, S., Husain, M. & Hampshire, A. Defining brain fog across medical conditions. Trends Neurosci. 48, 330–348 (2025)."). This variability may reflect differences in study design, cognitive assessment tools, sample characteristics (e.g., age, education, health status), and the classification of menopause[32](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR32 "Bangle, A., Williams, D., Walters, J., & Nguyen, L. Cognitive functioning in perimenopause: an updated systematic review and meta-analysis. Psychol. Aging.                    https://doi.org/10.1037/pag0000946                                     (2025)."). Therefore, the relationship between cognitive symptoms and cognitive performance deficits in menopause requires further investigation.

While subjective cognitive symptoms reflect individuals’ lived experiences, objective cognitive assessments can provide a global measure of cognitive ability through performance of challenging tasks. Using a large, community-based sample in England from the Real-Time Assessment of Community Transmission (REACT) study, this study aimed to investigate how cognitive symptoms and cognitive performance relate during premenopause, perimenopause, and postmenopause. We hypothesised that perimenopausal participants would report a higher incidence and greater frequency of cognitive, physical, and psychological symptoms compared with both premenopausal and postmenopausal participants. Although some studies have reported the objective cognitive decline in postmenopause[33](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR33 "Rentz, D. M. et al. Sex differences in episodic memory in early midlife: impact of reproductive aging. Menopause 24, 400–408 (2017)."), we hypothesised that perimenopausal participants would exhibit slightly lower objective cognitive performance relative to premenopausal and postmenopausal participants, reflecting the expected impact of elevated menopausal symptoms, including cognitive symptoms, on global cognition.

## Results

### Cohort characteristics

A total of 148,583 REACT-LC participants started the cognitive assessment, with 121,964 completing it. Analyses focused on individuals aged 45–55 years ( _n_ = 29,042) who completed the entire cognitive assessment ( _n_ = 14,234 females). Females were categorised by menopausal status (premenopausal _n_ = 2761, perimenopausal _n_ = 4044, postmenopausal _n_ = 7429). Demographic characteristics are reported in Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#Tab1). The average age of participants was 50.55 years. Participants were predominantly White and more likely to live in areas with lower levels of multiple deprivation (IMD, measures multiple dimension of neighbourhood deprivation at a small area level across England).

## Discussion

As expected, given past studies[3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR3 "Woods, N. F., Mitchell, E. S. & Adams, C. Memory functioning among midlife women: observations from the Seattle Midlife Women’s Health Study. Menopause 7, 257–265 (2000)."), [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR4 "Mitchell, E. S. & Woods, N. F. Cognitive symptoms during the menopausal transition and early postmenopause. Climacteric 14, 252–261 (2011)."), [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR5 "Zhu, Y., Yang, X., Wang, Y. & Zhu, X. Assessment of knowledge, understanding and awareness of Chinese women clinical staff towards menopause hormone therapy: a survey study. J. Obstet. Gynaecol. 43, 2171779 (2023)."), cognitive symptoms were elevated in perimenopausal and postmenopausal participants, with odds of reporting poor memory or brain fog up to 1.31 times higher than premenopausal participants. Furthermore, the frequency at which brain fog was reported to occur amongst those who experienced it in the past two weeks showed a linear trend from pre- to peri- to postmenopause, which accords with the hypothesis that hormonal transition contributes to subjective cognitive symptoms.

However, counter to our hypothesis, global cognitive performance differed minimally across menopause status groups as similar mean global cognitive scores were achieved, with only very small advantages in accuracy observed for perimenopausal participants. At the individual level, correlations between subjective cognitive symptoms and objective cognitive performance were weak but statistically significant and consistent across all menopause status groups. This indicates that global cognitive ability, as objectively measured using cognitive tasks, is not the major contributor to differences in subjective cognitive symptoms in these menopause status groups. This weak association between objective cognitive task performance and subjective cognitive symptoms accords with previous studies of menopause[34](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR34 "Dumas, J. A. Lifetime hormone exposure and cognition in postmenopausal women with breast cancer. Menopause 20, 883–885 (2013)."), [35](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR35 "Vega, J. N. et al. Altered brain connectivity in early postmenopausal women with subjective cognitive impairment. Front Neurosci. 10, 433 (2016)."), [36](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR36 "Conley, A. C. et al. Cognitive complaints are associated with smaller right medial temporal gray-matter volume in younger postmenopausal women. Menopause 27, 1220–1227 (2020).") and similar dissociations have been observed during other reproductive phases, such as during pregnancy[37](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR37 "Henry, J. D. & Rendell, P. G. A review of the impact of pregnancy on memory function. J. Clin. Exp. Neuropsychol. 29, 793–803 (2007)."), post-partum[38](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR38 "Orchard, E. R., Ward, P. G. D., Egan, G. F. & Jamadar, S. D. Evidence of subjective, but not objective, cognitive deficit in new mothers at 1-year postpartum. J. Women’s Health 31, 1087–1096 (2022)."), and menstruation[39](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR39 "Ronca, F. et al. Attentional, anticipatory and spatial cognition fluctuate throughout the menstrual cycle: Potential implications for female sport. Neuropsychologia 206, 108909 (2025).").

More broadly, cognitive symptoms can have a variety of underlying causes. Brain fog is an ambiguous term, and its underlying causes likely vary across individuals and conditions, and sociocultural factors may influence self-report rates[31](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR31 "Denno, P., Zhao, S., Husain, M. & Hampshire, A. Defining brain fog across medical conditions. Trends Neurosci. 48, 330–348 (2025)."). Accordingly, the association with objective cognition differs across conditions. For example, somewhat more robust associations between cognitive symptoms and cognitive impairments have been reported in post COVID-19 populations who were assessed using the same tasks, particularly those who had more severe or persistent illness[28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR28 "Hampshire, A. et al. Cognition and memory after Covid-19 in a large community sample. N. Engl. J. Med. 390, 806–818 (2024)."), [40](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR40 "Wood, G. K. et al. Posthospitalization COVID-19 cognitive deficits at 1 year are global and associated with elevated brain injury markers and gray matter volume reduction. Nat. Med. 31, 245–257 (2025)."), [41](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR41 "Zhao, S. et al. Long COVID is associated with severe cognitive slowing: a multicentre cross-sectional study. EClinicalMedicine 68,                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eclinm.2024.102434                                     (2024).").

In the context of menopause, our results suggest that perceived cognitive symptoms may not necessarily represent a deficit in global cognitive ability, but rather that these symptoms are multi-factorial, potentially arising from a combination of cognitive fluctuations, affective symptoms, and somatic changes. Hormonal changes are more likely to contribute through their effects on other physical and psychological processes[42](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR42 "Berent-Spillson, A. et al. Metabolic and hormone influences on emotion processing during menopause. Psychoneuroendocrinology 76, 218–225 (2017)."). For example, hot flushes and sleep disturbances in menopause are likely linked to changes in the hypothalamic preoptic nucleus and suprachiasmatic nucleus controlling thermoregulation and circadian rhythms, respectively[43](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR43 "Pinkerton, J. V., Abraham, L., Bushmakin, A. G., Cappelleri, J. C. & Komm, B. S. Relationship between changes in vasomotor symptoms and changes in menopause-specific quality of life and sleep parameters. Menopause 23, 1060–1066 (2016)."). This may be reflected in an elevation of fatigue and affective symptoms (apathy, anxiety, depression and dissociation). Accordingly, cognitive and psychological symptoms showed more robust correlations across all menopause status groups examined in this study.

Moreover, there may also be an elevation in fluctuations in cognitive performance and transient cognitive failures may meaningfully impact daily functioning, yet they are poorly captured within the focused timeframe of a cognitive assessment. These short-term issues, such as misplacing items or sudden lapses in attention and memory, may be more noticeable in everyday life than a typical test setting. This may explain why self-reported cognitive symptoms can be elevated when objective task performance appears largely intact in perimenopause.

Either way, cognitive symptoms have a substantial impact on quality of life, even in the absence of global cognitive dysfunction. The cognitive symptoms are real and are distressing, despite not being measurable as global cognitive ability. Therefore, greater precision in characterising and measuring cognitive symptoms is a priority for future research in menopause. We suggest that future studies fractionate cognitive symptoms using scales to subtype perceived cognitive difficulties, and to quantify related factors like fatigue, affective symptoms and dissociation. Although there is variability in existing menopause findings on how cognitive symptoms in menopause often affect specific domains[23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR23 "Drogos, L. L. et al. Objective cognitive performance is related to subjective memory complaints in midlife women with moderate to severe vasomotor symptoms. Menopause 20, 1236–1242 (2013)."), [24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR24 "Schaafsma, M., Homewood, J. & Taylor, A. Subjective cognitive complaints at menopause associated with declines in performance of verbal memory and attentional processes. Climacteric 13, 84–98 (2010)."), [25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR25 "Jaff, N. G., Rubin, L. H., Crowther, N. J., Norris, S. A. & Maki, P. M. Menopausal symptoms, menopausal stage and cognitive functioning in black urban African women. Climacteric 23, 38–45 (2020)."), [26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR26 "Weber, M. T., Mapstone, M., Staskiewicz, J. & Maki, P. M. Reconciling subjective memory complaints with objective memory performance in the menopausal transition. Menopause 19, 735–741 (2012)."), [27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR27 "Chen, L. et al. Menopausal hormone therapy does not improve some domains of memory: a systematic review and meta-analysis. Front. Endocrinol. 13, 894883 (2022)."), this study used a global cognitive composite to provide a robust overall measure of cognition. This may obscure subtle domain-specific differences, but it is appropriate for assessing group-level patterns in this large community-based sample. Future research may wish to explore domain-specific cognitive performance to clarify how particular symptoms relate to specific cognitive abilities. This might facilitate understanding of the impact of sex steroid hormones in cognitive symptoms on a neural level.

It is also important to note that HRT use was not explored in the current sample, but our findings provide insight into menopause symptom reporting for effective treatment. Our findings emphasise a critical issue: evaluating the effectiveness of treating menopause symptoms should not neglect meaningful subjective experience. Patient-reported outcomes should also be used to evaluate treatment effectiveness. Although some research has explored the neurobiology of subjective cognitive symptoms in menopause[44](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR44 "Reuben, R., Karkaby, L., McNamee, C., Phillips, N. A. & Einstein, G. Menopause and cognitive complaints: Are ovarian hormones linked with subjective cognitive decline? Climacteric 24, 321–332 (2021)."), including associations with serum oestradiol, oestrone[4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR4 "Mitchell, E. S. & Woods, N. F. Cognitive symptoms during the menopausal transition and early postmenopause. Climacteric 14, 252–261 (2011)."), [16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR16 "Greendale, G. A. et al. Effects of the menopause transition and hormone use on cognitive performance in midlife women. Neurology 72, 1850–1857 (2009)."), [45](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR45 "Koyama, A. K. et al. Endogenous sex hormones and cognitive function in older women. Alzheimers Dement. 12, 758–765 (2016)."), structural changes in grey matter and altered functional connectivity[35](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR35 "Vega, J. N. et al. Altered brain connectivity in early postmenopausal women with subjective cognitive impairment. Front Neurosci. 10, 433 (2016)."), [36](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR36 "Conley, A. C. et al. Cognitive complaints are associated with smaller right medial temporal gray-matter volume in younger postmenopausal women. Menopause 27, 1220–1227 (2020)."), [46](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR46 "Zhang, S. et al. Aberrant cerebral activity in early postmenopausal women: a resting-state functional magnetic resonance imaging study. Front. Cell Neurosci. 12, 454 (2018)."), it is critical that subjective experience is incorporated when evaluating the efficacy of hormonal treatment for menopause. Healthcare providers should foster more open discussions about cognitive symptoms in menopause, helping to validate menopausal experiences, and promote appropriate support strategies which can be fostered from future research.

This study has limitations. It is important to note that the study focused on the relationship between cognitive task performance and cognitive symptoms, leaving the broader implications of hormonal changes on cognition across time beyond the scope of this study. There are clear findings on the impact of oestrogens on maintaining cognitive and brain function[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR12 "Russell, J. K., Jones, C. K. & Newhouse, P. A. The role of estrogen in brain and cognitive aging. Neurotherapeutics 16, 649–665 (2019)."), [47](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR47 "McEwen, B. Estrogen actions throughout the brain. Recent Prog. Horm. Res. 57, 357–384 (2002)."). Moreover, cognitive symptoms have been associated with the onset of Alzheimer’s disease[48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR48 "Jessen, F. et al. Prediction of dementia by subjective memory impairment: effects of severity and temporal association with cognitive impairment. Arch. Gen. Psychiatry 67, 414–422 (2010)."), and sex-specific differences have been observed, with females showing stronger links between midlife cognitive symptoms and later dementia risk[49](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR49 "Heser, K. et al. Subjective cognitive decline may be a stronger predictor of incident dementia in women than in men. J. Alzheimers Dis. 68, 1469–1478 (2019)."). From around age 65, cognitive symptoms in females appear to become particularly predictive of subsequent dementia onset. The current study poses questions about the transient nature of hormonal fluctuations during menopause, rather than the long-term trajectory of cognitive decline in menopause. Longitudinal designs require clear categorisation of menopausal stages and potential biomarkers of change in the brain and ovarian hormones that may underscore these subjective experiences of cognitive symptoms.

Also, menopause status groups were characterised using self-reported menstrual cycle information, which may be affected by recall bias leading to misclassification[50](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR50 "Metcalf, C. A., Duffy, K. A., Page, C. E. & Novick, A. M. Cognitive problems in perimenopause: a review of recent evidence. Curr. Psychiatry Rep. 25, 501–511 (2023)."). The primary criteria for reproductive aging in the STRAW + 10[51](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR51 "Harlow, S. D. et al. Executive summary of the stages of reproductive aging workshop + 10: addressing the unfinished agenda of staging reproductive aging. J. Clin. Endocrinol. Metab. 97, 1159–1168 (2012).") is based on cycle regularity and the cessation of menstrual periods, yet this information may be susceptible to misremembering. Length of amenorrhea was not documented, meaning that postmenopausal participants were characterised based on self-reported cessation of menstrual periods. This is a limitation may lead to misclassification of postmenopausal status, such as with late perimenopause, where hormonal volatility may impact cognitive symptom reporting. It is also important to note that cognitive symptom reporting may vary in relation to type of loss of ovarian hormones, such as premature ovarian insufficiency which occurs before 40, where cognitive symptom reporting is underexplored. Thus the implications of these findings relate to spontaneous menopause only. Additionally, the predominantly White sample would not illustrate the disparities where cognitive symptoms in menopause may be influenced by cultural attitudes towards aging, gender, and menopause. This could vary significantly across populations and affect how those transitioning through menopause report or interpret cognitive difficulties. Future research should prioritise studying more diverse populations to better understand these factors across different communities.

Our findings indicate that in perimenopause and postmenopause, females experience elevated cognitive symptoms including brain fog and, to a lesser extent, poor memory; however, these symptoms do not correlate with poorer global cognitive task performance. Although no global cognitive dysfunction was observed, the reported cognitive symptoms still significantly impact quality of life, correlate with psychological symptoms, pointing to the need for more precise measures of subjective cognitive difficulties in future research. Furthermore, these symptoms may reflect transient fluctuations in cognitive ability that are not captured in cross-sectional cognitive assessments, a hypothesis which requires investigation via short-term longitudinal designs. Finally, investigation of the long-term trajectory of cognitive function in menopause, and how hormonal changes may contribute to cognitive aging and dementia risk, remains a priority for better support through this phase.

## Methods

All procedures were conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. This study received ethical approval from the South-Central Berkshire B Research Ethics Committee (REC ref. [21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR21 "Luetters, C. et al. Menopause transition stage and endogenous estradiol and follicle-stimulating hormone levels are not related to cognitive performance: cross-sectional results from the study of women’s health across the nation (SWAN). J. Women’s Health 16, 331–344 (2007).")/SC/0134).

### Cohort

The REACT study in England collected data about infection rates of SARS-CoV-2 infection from 1st May 2020 to 31st March 2022[52](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR52 "Elliott, P. et al. Design and implementation of a national SARS-CoV-2 monitoring program in England: REACT-1 study. Am. J. Public Health 113, 545–554 (2023)."). The database consisted of a randomly selected community sample of 3,099,386 adults (≥18 years of age), of which 2,494,309 participants (80.50%) consented to be recontacted and allowed their data linkage to National Health Service (NHS) records. Between 1st August and 30th December 2022, approximately 800,000 (32.10%) individuals were invited to participate in a follow up survey and cognitive assessments[53](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR53 "Atchison, C. J. et al. Long-term health impacts of COVID-19 among 242,712 adults in England. Nat. Commun. 14, 6588 (2023)."). The current study used a subset of the 800,000 participants which was based on full completion of all eight cognitive tasks, and being aged between 45 to 55 (0.70%, _n_ = 21,789). Females were identified based on self-reported biological sex.

### Menopausal status

The REACT-LC survey collected self-reported menstrual cycle information, including cycle length and regularity, from all participants who reported female as their biological sex. Using the STRAW + 10 criteria for reproductive aging[51](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR51 "Harlow, S. D. et al. Executive summary of the stages of reproductive aging workshop + 10: addressing the unfinished agenda of staging reproductive aging. J. Clin. Endocrinol. Metab. 97, 1159–1168 (2012)."), we categorised perimenopausal participants based on self-reported irregular menstrual cycles, and postmenopausal participants as those who reported their menstrual periods had stopped. The duration of menstrual period cessation was not recorded. Premenopausal participants were those reporting regular menstrual cycles. The survey did not capture history of chemotherapy, or hormone therapy details, and therefore these factors were not accounted for in the current analyses.

### Menopausal symptoms

The REACT-LC survey included general health questions, focusing on symptom reporting and their frequency over the past two weeks. The current study focused on symptoms commonly associated with menopause, as measured by the Greene Climacteric Scale[54](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR54 "Greene, J. G. Constructing a standard climacteric scale. Maturitas 61, 78–84 (2008)."). These included cognitive symptoms (e.g., brain fog, poor memory), physical symptoms which are somatic or bodily experiences (e.g., sleep difficulties, severe fatigue, palpitations) and psychological symptoms that relate to changes in mood or emotional state (e.g., mood swings, anxiety, low mood).

For each reported symptom, participants were asked to indicate how often they experienced it during the past two weeks. The response options were ‘Daily’, ‘Most days’, ‘Occasionally’, or ‘Once’.

The questionnaires used for REACT-LC are available on the study website ( [https://www.imperial.ac.uk/medicine/research-and-impact/groups/react-study/studies/react-long-covid/react-long-covid-materials/](https://www.imperial.ac.uk/medicine/research-and-impact/groups/react-study/studies/react-long-covid/react-long-covid-materials/)).

### Cognitive assessment

Participants were invited to complete eight computerised online tasks on the Cognitron platform, as reported in Hampshire et al.[28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR28 "Hampshire, A. et al. Cognition and memory after Covid-19 in a large community sample. N. Engl. J. Med. 390, 806–818 (2024)."). The entire battery takes approximately 15–20 min and is typically completed within a single session, but it can be extended over multiple sessions. Tasks were presented in a fixed order and accessed via their personal devices, such as laptop computer or smartphone. The tasks were selected from the broader Cognitron library with each task representing a distinct cognitive domain with minimal overlap to provide robust global cognitive composite scores[55](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR55 "Hampshire, A., Highfield, R. R., Parkin, B. L. & Owen, A. M. Fractionating human intelligence. Neuron 76, 1225–1237 (2012)."), [56](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR56 "Daws, R. E. & Hampshire, A. The negative relationship between reasoning and religiosity is underpinned by a bias for intuitive responses specifically when intuition and logic are in conflict. Front. Psychol. 8, 2191 (2017)."). Previous studies have reported the robust nature of the selected tasks across devices[57](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR57 "Hampshire, A., Ballard, C. & Williams, G. Computerized neuropsychological tests undertaken on digital platforms are cost effective, achieve high engagement, distinguish and are highly sensitive to longitudinal change: Data from the PROTECT and GBIT studies: advancing clinical trial design and methods in a digital age. Alzheimers Dement. 16, e041122 (2020)."), their expected sensitivity to population characteristics including sex, age, and education level in both healthy[58](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR58 "Giunchiglia, V. et al. An iterative approach for estimating domain-specific cognitive abilities from large scale online cognitive data. NPJ Digit. Med. 7, 328,                    https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-024-01327-x                                     (2024)."), and good discriminability in clinical populations with cognitive deficits[59](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR59 "Jolly, A. E., Scott, G. T., Sharp, D. J. & Hampshire, A. H. Distinct patterns of structural damage underlie working memory and reasoning deficits after traumatic brain injury. Brain 143, 1158–1176 (2020)."), [60](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR60 "Hampshire, A. et al. Cognitive deficits in people who have recovered from COVID-19. EClinicalMedicine 39,                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eclinm.2021.101044                                     (2021)."). They included measures of immediate recognition memory, two-dimensional mental manipulation, spatial working memory, spatial planning, verbal analogical reasoning, word definitions, information sampling, and delayed recognition memory. The latter is not completed if the battery is completed outside of a single session.

Each task outputs a primary accuracy-based score. Additional secondary task scores included reaction times and error types. The raw, unadjusted data were processed by applying a rank-based transformation to normality, outputting z-scores for each participant on each task metric. Global cognition scores for accuracy and reaction time were computed by averaging the standardised scores across all eight tasks for participants aged 45–55 (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#Fig1)). We did not exclude any participants based on performance metrics.

### Statistical analyses

Cohort data and statistical figures were processed in R, MATLAB 2024a and Microsoft Excel 2016. Statistical analyses were conducted in Jamovi version 2.3.28. Effect sizes of reported associations correspond to the extended version of Cohen’s classification[61](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44294-026-00132-z#ref-CR61 "Brydges, C. R. Effect size guidelines, sample size calculations, and statistical power in gerontology. Innov. Aging 3. (2019)."). Analyses were conducted as pairwise comparisons between menopause status groups to facilitate clear interpretation of differences in symptom reporting and cognitive performance. Adjustments for multiple comparisons were made (FDR-corrected _p_ values).

Logistic regression was used to compare the likelihood of reporting cognitive (brain fog, poor memory), psychological (anxiety, low mood, mood swings), and physical symptoms (sleep difficulties, severe fatigue, and palpitations) across menopausal status (premenopausal, perimenopausal, postmenopausal). Education, ethnicity, index of multiple deprivation (IMD), age, and COVID-19 status at the time of participation were included as covariates (see Supplementary Information). IMD measures multiple dimensions of neighbourhood deprivation at a small area level across England, with quintile 1 being the most deprived and quintile 5 being the least deprived. Estimates represent the log odds of reporting cognitive symptoms (reference level: presence) vs not reporting cognitive symptoms (absence).

Ordinal logistic regression was performed on individuals reporting each symptom presence to model the odds of being at a higher frequency level (i.e. from ‘Once’ to ‘Daily’) of symptom reporting across premenopausal, perimenopausal, postmenopausal participants. Estimates represent the log-odds of reporting symptoms at different frequency levels, ranging from the least (e.g., ‘Once’ or ‘Occasionally’) to most frequent (e.g., ‘Daily’).

Across the entire sample, a linear regression model was conducted to measure the relationship between global cognition (accuracy, reaction time) and predictor variables, with the main effect of menopause status group reported here. Standardised coefficients were calculated indicating the difference in global cognition for a one standard deviation change based on menopause status group. Details of the model outputs, including estimates and significance, are provided in the Results section. Other predictor variables (education, ethnicity, IMD, age, and COVID-19 status at the time of participation), which are known to be associated with cognition, were included in the model but are not the primary focus of the results reported here, but in the Supplementary Information.

Spearman’s rank correlation was used to determine the relationship between cognitive symptoms (brain fog, poor memory) and global cognition (accuracy, reaction time) as cognitive symptoms have two levels (presence/absence), and global cognition variables are continuous. These correlations were conducted across the entire sample (premenopausal, perimenopausal, postmenopausal).

Additionally, psychological symptoms, created by summing the binary variables (presence/absence) for anxiety, low mood, and mood swings, was included in correlation analyses with cognitive symptoms.
