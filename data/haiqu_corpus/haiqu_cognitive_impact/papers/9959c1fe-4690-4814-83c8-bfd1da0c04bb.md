Childhood emotional maltreatment is linked to healthy dietary behavior through depression, anxiety, and subjective well-being


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y.pdf)

## Abstract

This research investigated how childhood emotional maltreatment (CEM) is associated with healthy dietary behavior among university students and explored whether emotional distress and subjective well-being (SWB) serve as intermediary variables in these associations. A cross-sectional design was applied, involving 3,007 university students recruited between October and December 2024. Key measures included CEM, depression, anxiety, SWB, and healthy dietary behavior. Correlation analysis was performed to identify associations among variables, and a chain mediation model was tested using the PROCESS macro for SPSS (Model 80). CEM showed positive links with depression ( _r_ = 0.356) and anxiety ( _r_ = 0.371), but negative links with SWB ( _r_ = -0.317) and dietary quality ( _r_ = -0.217). Both depression and anxiety were inversely related to SWB ( _r_ = -0.386; _r_ = -0.378) and healthy dietary behavior ( _r_ = -0.210; _r_ = -0.206), while depression and anxiety were strongly correlated with each other ( _r_ = 0.785). In contrast, SWB was positively associated with healthy dietary behavior ( _r_ = 0.185). Mediation testing indicated that emotional distress and SWB partly explained the pathway between CEM and healthy dietary behavior. The findings highlight potential psychological associations between adverse childhood experiences and eating behaviors. Addressing emotional difficulties and promoting SWB may be valuable directions for dietary and mental health programs targeting university students. This study provides theoretical implications and practical references for university mental health services and nutrition education initiatives.

## Introduction

Childhood emotional maltreatment (CEM) refers to the persistent verbal aggression, emotional neglect, control, humiliation, threats, or disregard by parents or other caregivers, which results in children feeling hurt, devalued, or unloved on an emotional level[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR1 "Xu, W. & Zheng, S. Childhood emotional abuse and cyberbullying perpetration among chinese university students: the chain mediating effects of self-esteem and problematic social media use. Front Psychol 13, (2022)."). Although the effects of emotional maltreatment may not be as immediately apparent as those of physical maltreatment, they can have profound and long-lasting impacts on a child’s self-esteem, emotional security, and psychological well-being. These adverse effects may persist into adulthood, influencing mental health, social functioning, and the ability to establish intimate relationships[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR2 "Demir Kaya, M., Kaya, F. & Eroglu, Y. Interpersonal cognitive distortions: What is the role of childhood trauma and attachment? J. Educ. Sci. Environ. Health. 9, 292–309 (2023)."), [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR3 "Kwok, S. Y. C. L., Gu, M. & Kwok, K. Childhood emotional abuse and adolescent flourishing: a moderated mediation model of self-compassion and curiosity. Child. Abuse Negl. 129, 105629 (2022)."), [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR4 "Thompson, A. E. & Kaplan, C. A. Childhood emotional abuse. Br. J. Psychiatry: J. Ment Sci. 168, 143–148 (1996)."). A meta-analysis conducted in 2012, which included 29 studies with a total of 7,082,279 participants, reported a global prevalence of CEM at 36.3%[5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR5 "Stoltenborgh, M., Bakermans-Kranenburg, M. J., Alink, L. R. A. & van IJzendoorn, M. H. The universality of childhood emotional abuse: a meta-analysis of worldwide prevalence. J. Aggress. Maltreatment Trauma. 21, 870–890 (2012)."). In 2018, another meta-analysis encompassing 32 studies found that the prevalence of CEM among Chinese university students was 36.7%[6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR6 "Fu, H. et al. Reported prevalence of childhood maltreatment among chinese college students: a systematic review and meta-analysis. PLOS One. 13, e0205808 (2018)."). Previous studies have shown that CEM is associated with post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)[7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR7 "Watts, J., Leeman, M., O’Sullivan, D., Castleberry, J. & Baniya, G. Childhood emotional maltreatment and post-traumatic stress disorder in the context of centrality of the event and intrusive rumination. Rehabil Couns. The relationship between childhood emotional abuse and depressive symptoms among chinese college students: the multiple mediating effects of emotional and behavioral problems. J. Affect. Disord. 288, 129–135 (2021)."), impaired immune function[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR9 "Jiang, W. et al. Associations between childhood maltreatment, peripheral immune biomarkers, and psychiatric symptoms in adults: a cohort study of over 138,000 participants. Brain Behav. Immun. 123, 840–850 (2025)."), reduced self-esteem[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR1 "Xu, W. & Zheng, S. Childhood emotional abuse and cyberbullying perpetration among chinese university students: the chain mediating effects of self-esteem and problematic social media use. Front Psychol 13, (2022)."), as well as physical symptoms such as chronic pain and headaches[10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR10 "Karimov-Zwienenberg, M., Symphor, W., Peraud, W. & Décamps, G. Childhood trauma, PTSD/CPTSD and chronic pain: a systematic review. PLOS One. 19, e0309332 (2024)."). The investigation sheds light on potential associations between CEM and later outcomes while supplying essential evidence to guide counseling strategies and mental health promotion among undergraduates.

In recent years, research has begun to explore the potential impacts of emotional maltreatment on physical health, particularly its effects on diet and nutritional status. This line of research plays a crucial role in clarifying how early adverse experiences shape long-term health outcomes, with particular attention to the links between CEM and dietary behaviors. A balanced diet provides the body with essential nutrients, including macronutrients such as proteins and fluids, micronutrients like vitamins, and adequate fiber[11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR11 "Lean, M. E. J. Principles of human nutrition. Med. S. R. et al. The burden of psychological distress and unhealthy dietary behaviours among 222,401 school-going adolescents from 61 countries. Sci. The role of diet for prevention and management of hypertension. Curr. Opin. Cardiol. 33, 388–393 (2018)."), diabetes[15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR15 "Zheng, Y., Ley, S. H. & Hu, F. B. Global aetiology and epidemiology of type 2 diabetes mellitus and its complications. Nat. Rev. Endocrinol. 14, 88–98 (2018)."), and cancer[16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR16 "Wang, Y. et al. Associations between plant-based dietary patterns and risks of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, cancer, and mortality - a systematic review and meta-analysis. Nutr. J. 22, 46 (2023)."). As emerging adults, university students face rapid developmental changes, making healthy dietary behavior especially crucial. According to self-determination theory, intrinsic motivation and autonomy are key factors influencing individuals’ behavioral choices[17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR17 "Ten Cate, T. J., Kusurkar, R. A. & Williams, G. C. How self-determination theory can assist our understanding of the teaching and learning processes in medical education. AMEE guide 59. Med. Teach. 33, 961–973 (2011)."). Theoretically, earlier emotional maltreatment may be linked to lower perceived autonomy and self-regulatory difficulties in later years, which could be associated with a greater tendency toward convenience- or reward-driven food choices (e.g., energy-dense options)[18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR18 "Koçak, Z. & Çağatay, S. E. Childhood traumas and emotional eating: the mediating role of self-esteem, and emotion dysregulation. Curr. Psychol. 43, 21783–21791 (2024)."). Consequently, CEM is negatively correlated with healthy dietary behavior. Previous studies have shown that childhood trauma, especially emotional maltreatment, can adversely affect emotional regulation abilities. Difficulties in emotional regulation are a significant factor contributing to unhealthy dietary behavior in adulthood, such as binge diet, anorexia, and bulimia. In one study with 461 adults, the cross-sectional analysis revealed a significant positive link between childhood maltreatment and unhealthy eating patterns[19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR19 "Dawson, D., Strodl, E. & Kitamura, H. Childhood maltreatment and disordered eating: The mediating role of emotion regulation. Appetite 172, 105952 (2022)."). Based on these findings, we propose the following hypothesis. Hypothesis H1: CEM is negatively associated with healthy dietary behavior.

Emotional distress is defined as a psychological state characterized by persistent anxiety, sadness, depression, and overall emotional turmoil, with anxiety and depression being its core components[20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR20 "Demir Kaya, M. School burnout in adolescents: What are the role of emotional autonomy and setting life goals? ResearchSquare Preprint                    https://doi.org/10.21203/rs.3.rs-956772/v1                                     (2022)."). This distress is particularly pronounced under significant stress and is associated with adverse outcomes, including impaired mental health, academic decline, and reduced quality of life[21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR21 "Kuehner, C. Why is depression more common among women than among men? Lancet Psychiatry. 4, 146–158 (2017)."). Given their high comorbidity and central role in mental health, anxiety and depression are widely used to operationalize emotional distress in empirical research[22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR22 "Liao, Z., Wang, W., Ding, L. & Wen, C. Clinical characteristics and related factors of comorbid anxiety disorders in patients with depression. Chin. J. Public. Health. 30, 552–555 (2014)."), [23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR23 "Carpallo-González, M., Muñoz-Navarro, R., González-Blanch, C. & Cano-Vindel, A. Symptoms of emotional disorders and sociodemographic factors as moderators of dropout in psychological treatment: a meta-review. Int. J. Clin. Health Psychol. 23, 100379 (2023)."), [24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR24 "Kessler, R. C. Epidemiology of women and depression. J. Affect. Disord. 74, 5–13 (2003)."). In this study, they serve as its primary indicators. Emotional distress affects not only emotional well-being but also behavioral regulation and lifestyle choices[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR25 "Hartley, C. A. & Phelps, E. A. Anxiety and decision-making. Biol. Psychiatry. 72, 113–118 (2012)."). Among university students, emotional distress has become an increasingly pressing issue, exacerbated by academic pressures, social adaptation challenges, and personal growth demands[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR26 "Misra, R., McKean, M., West, S. & Russo, T. Academic stress of college students: comparison of student and faculty perceptions. Coll. Stud. J. 34, 236–245 (2000)."). A recent meta-analysis conducted in 2022, which included 64 studies with a total of 100,187 participants, found a global prevalence of anxiety symptoms among university students to be 39.0%[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR27 "Li, W., Zhao, Z., Chen, D., Peng, Y. & Lu, Z. Prevalence and associated factors of depression and anxiety symptoms among college students: a systematic review and meta-analysis. J. Child. Psychol. Psychiatry. 63, 1222–1230 (2022)."). Another 2022 meta-analysis focusing on anxiety symptoms among Chinese university students during the COVID-19 pandemic, which analyzed 25 studies involving 1,003,743 participants, reported an overall prevalence of 25.0%[28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR28 "Wang, X. & Liu, Q. Prevalence of anxiety symptoms among Chinese university students amid the COVID-19 pandemic: A systematic review and meta-analysisCOVID-19. Heliyon 8, e10117 (2022)."). Depression is a common and serious mental health disorder characterized by persistent low mood, loss of interest and pleasure, and a decline in engagement with daily activities[29](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR29 "Duarte, A. C., Matos, A. P. & Marques, C. Cognitive emotion regulation strategies and depressive symptoms: gender’s moderating effect. Procedia - Soc. Behav. Sci. 165, 275–283 (2015)."). It not only affects emotional well-being but also has profound implications for cognition, behavior, and physical health[30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR30 "Cui, R. Editorial: a systematic review of depression. Curr. Neuropharmacol. 13, 480 (2015)."). According to a 2022 meta-analysis encompassing 64 studies and involving 100,187 university students, the prevalence of depression was found to be 33.6%[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR27 "Li, W., Zhao, Z., Chen, D., Peng, Y. & Lu, Z. Prevalence and associated factors of depression and anxiety symptoms among college students: a systematic review and meta-analysis. J. Child. Psychol. Psychiatry. 63, 1222–1230 (2022)."). A meta-analysis conducted in 2015, which reviewed 32,694 university students over a span of 39 years, reported a prevalence of depression among Chinese university students at 23.8%[31](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR31 "Lei, X. Y., Xiao, L. M., Liu, Y. N. & Li, Y. M. Prevalence of depression among chinese university students: a meta-analysis. PLOS One. 11, e0153454 (2016)."). The ecological systems theory posits that an individual’s development is shaped within multiple levels of environmental systems. Within this framework, CEM is regarded as a critical negative factor in the ecosystem, with interactions among parents, family, school, and community are closely associated with children’s psychological well-being[32](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR32 "Belsky, J. Etiology of child maltreatment: a developmental-ecological analysis. Psychol. Bull. 114, 413–434 (1993)."). Emotional maltreatment is not merely an issue confined to parents or primary caregivers; it often reflects broader family system dysfunctions, such as impaired family functioning, socioeconomic stress, and ineffective communication among family members. Such family circumstances have been linked to higher levels of emotional distress in children, as shown in previous empirical research[33](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR33 "Cicchetti, D. & Toth, S. L. Child maltreatment and developmental psychopathology: a multilevel perspective. in Developmental psychopathology: Maladaptation and psychopathology, Vol. 3, 3rd ed 457–512 (John Wiley & Sons, Inc., Hoboken, NJ, US, (2016).                    https://doi.org/10.1002/9781119125556.devpsy311                                    ."). Therefore, CEM is associated with higher levels of emotional distress. Prior studies have consistently reported significant associations between CEM and indicators of emotional distress such as depression and anxiety in adulthood[34](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR34 "Yang, L. et al. The relationship between childhood psychological abuse and depression in college students: a moderated mediation model. Abstract BMC Psychiatry. 24 (1)                    https://doi.org/10.1186/s12888-024-05809-w                                     (2024)."), [35](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR35 "Yang, L. et al. The relationship between childhood psychological abuse and depression in college students: internet addiction as mediator different dimensions of alexithymia as moderator. BMC Public. Health. 24 (1)                    https://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-024-20232-2                                     (2024)."), [36](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR36 "Liyuan, Y., Ying, T., Ning, W., Yichi, Z. & Yang, L. Child psychological maltreatment depression psychological inflexibility and difficulty in identifying feelings a moderated mediation model. Sci. Rep. 15 (1).                    https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-025-92873-1                                     (2025)."), [37](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR37 "Yang, L. et al. The mediating effect of social network sites addiction on the relationship between childhood psychological abuse and depression in college students and the moderating effect of psychological flexibility Abstract Psychology and Psychotherapy: Theory Research and Practice 98(2) 534-548                    https://doi.org/10.1111/papt.12580                                     (2025)."). For example, a cross-sectional study involving 1,116 adults found a positive relationship between retrospectively reported CEM and current symptoms of anxiety and depression[38](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR38 "Alnassar, J. S., Juruena, M. F., Macare, C., Perkins, A. M. & Young, A. H. Effect of childhood emotional abuse on depression and anxiety in adulthood is partially mediated by neuroticism: Evidence from a large online sample. J. Affect. Disord. 359, 158–163 (2024)."). Similarly, both cross-sectional and longitudinal findings in the literature suggest that individuals with higher levels of CEM exposure tend to report more emotional difficulties later in life[39](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR39 "Hovens, J. G. F. M., Giltay, E. J., Spinhoven, P., van Hemert, A. M. & Penninx, B. W. J. H. Impact of childhood life events and childhood trauma on the onset and recurrence of depressive and anxiety disorders. J. Clin. Psychiatry. 76, 931–938 (2015)."). These observations support the view that emotional distress may play a mediating role in the link between CEM and other psychological or behavioral outcomes. Furthermore, according to dynamic systems theory[40](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR40 "Karimi-Aghdam, S. Zone of proximal development (ZPD) as an emergent system: a dynamic systems theory perspective. Integr. Psychol. Behav. Sci. 51, 76–93 (2017)."), individual behavior results from the interaction of multiple factors that continuously evolve over time. Applying this theory, the relationship between depression, anxiety, and healthy dietary behavior choices can be viewed as a dynamic interplay among various subsystems. Specifically, depression and anxiety may be associated with a greater likelihood of choosing unhealthy food as a temporary coping mechanism, which may coincide with a reinforcing pattern over time[41](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR41 "Dakanalis, A. et al. The association of emotional eating with overweight/obesity, depression, anxiety/stress, and dietary patterns: a review of the current clinical evidence. Nutrients 15, 1173 (2023)."). However, such unhealthy dietary behavior habits may exacerbate physical discomfort and weight-related issues, further intensifying emotional distress. Previous research has found a significant positive correlation between anxiety and depression symptoms and daily beverage consumption among Chinese university students. For instance, a cross-sectional study involving 8,226 Chinese university students revealed that high sugar and caffeine content in beverages could negatively impact the nervous system, and depressive moods may be associated with an increased preference for high-sugar foods and drinks, potentially forming unhealthy dietary behavior habits that further exacerbate mental health problems[42](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR42 "Zhang, X. et al. Daily intake of soft drinks is associated with symptoms of anxiety and depression in chinese adolescents. Public. Health Nutr. 22, 2553–2560 (2019)."). Based on these findings, we propose the following hypothesis. Hypothesis H2: Emotional distress is associated with the relationship between CEM and healthy dietary behavior, serving as a potential mediating variable.

In recent years, the field of psychology has increasingly recognized the crucial role of subjective well-being (SWB) in individual health behaviors, particularly its close association with emotional regulation and behavioral choices. SWB typically refers to an individual’s overall evaluation of their quality of life, encompassing emotional well-being, life satisfaction, and positive self-evaluation[43](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR43 "Diener, E. Subjective well-being. Psychol. Bull. 95, 542–575 (1984)."). According to Attachment Theory, the emotional bond between children and their primary caregivers is critical to their emotional and social development. Emotional maltreatment has been found to be related to disruptions in attachment security, which are associated with difficulties in emotional regulation and lower levels of perceived social support, thereby correlating with lower SWB[44](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR44 "Zukauskiene, R. Interpersonal Development. Routledge                    https://doi.org/10.4324/9781351153683                                     (2017)."). Empirical evidence indicates that CEM is closely related to mental health problems in adulthood. For instance, a meta-analysis including 79 English and 11 Chinese studies reported that emotional maltreatment is linked to poorer emotional regulation and greater experience of negative emotions, which are in turn associated with a higher likelihood of unhealthy dietary behaviors used to cope with distress. These maladaptive behaviors have been observed to co-occur with disordered eating patterns such as binge eating and bulimia[45](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR45 "Xiao, Z. et al. The impact of childhood psychological maltreatment on mental health outcomes in adulthood: a systematic review and meta-analysis. Trauma. Violence Abuse. 24, 3049–3064 (2023)."). Emotional maltreatment therefore shows consistent correlations with both mental health difficulties and unhealthy dietary patterns. Furthermore, according to Self-Determination Theory, SWB is generally viewed as reflecting the fulfillment of basic psychological needs—autonomy, competence, and relatedness. When these needs are more fully satisfied, individuals tend to report higher levels of SWB and are more likely to engage in healthier lifestyle patterns, including dietary behaviors[46](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR46 "Ryan, R. M. & Deci, E. L. Self-determination theory and the facilitation of intrinsic motivation, social development, and well-being. Am. Psychol. 55, 68–78 (2000)."). Individuals with higher SWB are often found to report more consistent healthy dietary habits, suggesting a close link between SWB and self-regulated health behaviors[47](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR47 "Sheldon, K. M. & Elliot, A. J. Goal striving, need satisfaction, and longitudinal well-being: the self-concordance model. J. Pers. Soc. Psychol. 76, 482–497 (1999)."). Previous research has established a positive correlation between SWB and healthy dietary behavior. For example, a cross-sectional study involving 338 university students from ten cities in East Kalimantan, Indonesia, found that SWB was significantly positively correlated with healthy dietary habits. Moreover, healthy dietary behavior was associated with better psychological and physical functioning through greater emotional stability and perceived social support[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR27 "Li, W., Zhao, Z., Chen, D., Peng, Y. & Lu, Z. Prevalence and associated factors of depression and anxiety symptoms among college students: a systematic review and meta-analysis. J. Child. Psychol. Psychiatry. 63, 1222–1230 (2022)."). Based on these findings, we propose the following hypothesis. SWB is associated with the relationship between CEM and healthy dietary behavior, serving as a potential mediating variable (H3).

CEM is closely associated with long-term patterns of emotional regulation and mental health, which may in turn relate to subsequent levels of SWB and health-related behaviors[48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR48 "Mehta, D. et al. Child maltreatment and long-term physical and mental health outcomes: an exploration of biopsychosocial determinants and implications for prevention. Child. Psychiatry Hum. Dev. 54, 421–435 (2023)."). According to emotional regulation theory and self-determination theory[49](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR49 "Ryan, R. M. & Deci, E. L. Self-determination theory. in Encyclopedia of Quality of Life and Well-being Research 1–7 (Springer, Cham, doi:                   https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-319-69909-7_2630-2                                    . (2022)."), [50](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR50 "Gratz, K. L., Dixon, L. J., Kiel, E. J. & Tull, M. T. Emotion regulation: theoretical models, associated outcomes and recent advances. SAGE Handb. Personality Individual Differences: Appl. Personality Individual Differences. 63–89.                    https://doi.org/10.4135/9781526451248.n3                                     (2018). Sage Reference."), emotional distress reflects a more immediate psychological response to perceived threats—typically expressed as transient negative emotional states such as anxiety and depression—whereas SWB represents a broader and more stable psychological construct, encompassing overall life satisfaction and positive functioning. Persistent emotional distress is often linked to diminished autonomy and competence, which are negatively associated with SWB. The university period represents a key developmental transition from adolescence to adulthood, during which individuals face academic, interpersonal, and career-related challenges[51](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR51 "Rapee, R. M., Creswell, C., Kendall, P. C., Pine, D. S. & Waters, A. M. Anxiety disorders in children and adolescents: A summary and overview of the literature. Behav. Res. Ther. 168, 104376 (2023)."). These pressures and uncertainties are frequently correlated with heightened anxiety and depression, both of which show a strong negative relationship with SWB. Prior studies have reported that depressive symptoms are significantly and negatively correlated with SWB; students experiencing higher levels of depression tend to report lower life satisfaction and reduced positive emotional experiences. Moreover, the association between anxiety, depression, and SWB can be interpreted through the framework of emotional regulation. Emotional regulation refers to how individuals manage their emotional experiences, expressions, and the behavioral consequences of emotions. When emotional regulation is compromised, individuals may struggle to manage negative affect, which is often accompanied by emotional fluctuations, distress, and reduced life satisfaction[52](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR52 "Aldao, A., Nolen-Hoeksema, S. & Schweizer, S. Emotion-regulation strategies across psychopathology: a meta-analytic review. Clin. Psychol. Rev. 30, 217–237 (2010)."). Consistent with this framework, previous cross-sectional studies have found significant correlations between emotional distress and SWB among university students. For instance, one study of 783 university students reported a significant negative correlation between depressive symptoms and SWB, showing that higher depression levels were associated with lower life satisfaction and emotional well-being. Depressive emotions were also linked to weaker self-identity and social engagement, which corresponded to lower levels of SWB[53](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR53 "Song, Y., Xiao, Z., Zhang, L. & Shi, W. Trait depression and subjective well-being: the chain mediating role of community feeling and self-compassion. Behav. Sci. 13, 448 (2023)."). Similarly, another cross-sectional study involving 305 university students found that anxiety was negatively correlated with SWB, with self-esteem and emotional regulation ability partially accounting for this association[54](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR54 "Öztürk, A. & Mutlu, T. The relationship between attachment style, subjective well-being, happiness and social anxiety among university students’. Procedia - Soc. Behav. Sci. 9, 1772–1776 (2010)."). Furthermore, numerous studies have supported the association between SWB and healthy lifestyle behaviors. Individuals with higher SWB tend to exhibit stronger self-regulation and intrinsic motivation, which are associated with maintaining balanced diets and other health-promoting habits[55](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR55 "Ifroh, R. H., Gai, X., Rabiautsani, M. A. & Han, X. The social support, healthy lifestyle, subjective well-being, and health-related quality of life among university students. J. Educ. Health Promot. 13, 427 (2024)."). Conversely, lower SWB is often linked to emotional eating and irregular dietary patterns[56](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR56 "Lin, J., Yang, F., Lan, M., Ding, Y. & Yin, K. Adhere to the chinese dietary guidelines associated with better subjective well-being: evidence from a cross-sectional survey and a daily diary investigation. BMC Public. Health. 24, 445 (2024)."). Although the present study adopts a cross-sectional design and therefore cannot establish causal directionality, the proposed sequence of “CEM → emotional distress → SWB → healthy dietary behavior” is theoretically and temporally coherent. This model represents a potential psychological pathway through which earlier emotional experiences may be related to current emotional states and lifestyle choices, consistent with developmental psychology and prior empirical research. Depression, anxiety, and SWB are sequentially associated within the relationship between CEM and healthy dietary behavior, forming a potential chain mediation model (H4).

Based on the above evidence, CEM is negatively associated with healthy dietary behavior. This association may be further understood through a potential chain mediation mechanism involving emotional distress (operationalized as anxiety and depression) and SWB. Accordingly, the present study proposes a chain mediation model in which CEM (X) is associated with emotional distress (M1 and M2), which is further related to SWB (M3), and these variables are jointly associated with healthy dietary behavior (Y). The hypothesized model is illustrated in Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#Fig1), and the analysis was conducted using the PROCESS macro (Model 80).

## Methods

### Participants

This study employed a combination of convenience sampling and teacher-mediated snowball sampling to recruit university students from six universities in mainland China, located in Hunan, Sichuan, Shaanxi, Shanxi, and Anhui provinces. Data collection was conducted between October and December 2024, with participant recruitment commencing in October 2024. The survey was administered online via an electronic questionnaire platform. A total of 3301 university students were invited to participate, and 3007 valid questionnaires were retained for analysis, yielding an effective response rate of 91.1%. Participation was entirely voluntary and anonymous, and no incentives were provided. The electronic questionnaire was distributed through course instructors and student communication networks. All participants were informed that their responses would remain anonymous and confidential, and that participation or withdrawal would involve no negative consequences. The study protocol received ethical approval from the authors’ institutional review board prior to data collection. The first page of the questionnaire included a detailed explanation of the study purpose, statements regarding anonymity, confidentiality, and data use, as well as an electronic informed consent form. Only participants who provided consent by clicking “Agree” were able to proceed with the survey. On average, participants completed the questionnaire in approximately 10 min. To ensure data quality, responses were screened prior to analysis, and cases with abnormally short completion times or highly patterned responding were excluded. The final analytic sample consisted of 3,007 participants, including 1267 males (42.1%) and 1,740 females (57.9%), with a mean age of 19.03 ± 1.18 years. Among them, 596 participants (19.8%) were only children, and 2411 (80.2%) were non-only children. Regarding academic year, 1371 (45.6%) were freshmen, 1431 (47.6%) sophomores, 194 (6.5%) juniors, and 11 (0.4%) seniors. With respect to parental education, maternal education levels were reported as unknown for 176 participants (5.9%), primary or below for 880 (29.3%), secondary education for 1659 (55.2%), university education for 273 (9.1%), and graduate-level education for 19 (0.6%). Paternal education levels were unknown for 159 participants (5.3%), primary or below for 652 (21.7%), secondary education for 1868 (62.1%), university education for 305 (10.1%), and graduate-level education for 23 (0.8%). Overall, the majority of parents had attained secondary education.

Psychol. By employing these refined instruments, this study ensures a balance between data quality and participant convenience, ultimately contributing to more accurate and meaningful findings.

#### Childhood emotional maltreatment

This study adopted the emotional abuse and emotional neglect subscales from the revised short form of the Childhood Trauma Questionnaire (CTQ-SF) by Zhao Xingfu[59](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR59 "Zhao, X. et al. Reliability and validity of the Chinese version of the Childhood Trauma Questionnaire. Chin. J. Clin. Rehabilitation, 105–107. (2005)."), to assess CEM among university students. The number of items was further simplified for the present study, resulting in a total of four items, with two items for each subscale. The emotional neglect items were reverse scored. A five-point Likert scale was used, ranging from 1 (never) to 5 (always). The total score ranged from 4 to 20, with higher scores indicating greater levels of emotional maltreatment. In this study, the Cronbach’s α coefficients for the two subscales were 0.755 and 0.838, respectively, and the Cronbach’s α coefficient for the overall CEM scale was 0.845. The Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin (KMO) value was 0.772, indicating adequate sampling adequacy. This measurement tool has been widely applied in previous research and has demonstrated good reliability and validity[60](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR60 "Yu, T., Liu, W. & Liu, F. The relationship between psychological maltreatment and anhedonia in children: The mediating role of cognitive reappraisal. Psychol. Behav. Res. 20, 361–367 (2022)."). The confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) indicated an excellent model fit: χ2/df = 0.499, CFI = 1.000, TLI = 0.999, RMSEA <0.001, and SRMR = 0.001. All indices met the established psychometric criteria for good model fit. All scales demonstrated satisfactory convergent validity. For the emotional abuse factor, standardized loadings ranged from 0.754 to 0.805, with a composite reliability (CR) of 0.76 and an average variance extracted (AVE) of 0.61. For the emotional neglect factor, standardized loadings ranged from 0.844 to 0.855, with CR = 0.86 and AVE = 0.72. Both CR and AVE values exceeded conventional psychometric thresholds (CR ≥ 0.70, AVE ≥ 0.50)[61](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR61 "Fornell, C. & Larcker, D. F. Evaluating structural equation models with unobservable variables and measurement error. J. Mark. Res. 18, 39–50 (1981)."), indicating that the scales possessed good reliability and convergent validity.

Both scales consist of two items and use a 4-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (not at all) to 4 (nearly every day). The total score ranges from 2 to 8, with higher scores indicating more severe depression and anxiety. In this study, the Cronbach’s α coefficients for depression and anxiety were 0.783 and 0.852, respectively. This measurement tool has been widely applied in previous research and has shown good reliability and validity[64](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR64 "Zhou, C. et al. Emotional distress was modeled as a higher-order construct comprising two latent variables—depression and anxiety. All scales demonstrated good convergent validity and reliability. ### Statistical analysis

Descriptive statistics (including means, standard deviations, skewness, and kurtosis) and correlation analyses were conducted using SPSS 29.0 to examine the distributions and associations among the study variables. Following Kim’s (2013) criteria, absolute skewness values less than 2 and absolute kurtosis values less than 7 were considered indicative of approximate univariate normality[73](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR73 "Kim, H. Y. Statistical notes for clinical researchers: assessing normal distribution (2) using skewness and kurtosis. Restor. Dent. Endod. 38, 52–54 (2013)."). All variables in the present study met these standards. To examine group differences across demographic variables, independent-sample t-tests and one-way analyses of variance (ANOVA) were performed. To control for inflated Type I error rates due to multiple comparisons, p-values were adjusted using the Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) correction, thereby improving the robustness of statistical inferences. Prior to hypothesis testing, the assumptions of regression analysis were assessed. Tolerance and variance inflation factor (VIF) values were used to examine multicollinearity. All tolerance values exceeded 0.20, and all VIFs were below 5, suggesting no severe multicollinearity issues[74](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR74 "Hair, J. F. Multivariate data analysis: an overview. in International Encyclopedia of Statistical Science (ed Lovric, M.) 904–907 (Springer, Berlin, Heidelberg, doi:                   https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-642-04898-2_395                                    . (2011)."). Subsequently, the PROCESS macro (Model 80) for SPSS was employed with 5,000 bootstrap resamples to test the proposed chain mediation model[75](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR75 "Hayes, A. F. Partial, conditional, and moderated moderated mediation: quantification, inference, and interpretation. Commun. Monogr. 85, 4–40 (2018)."). In this model, CEM was treated as the independent variable, healthy dietary behavior as the dependent variable, and depression, anxiety, and SWB as mediators. The bias-corrected bootstrap method was used to estimate 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for the indirect effects. Following the recommendations of Preacher and Hayes[76](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR76 "Preacher, K. J. & Hayes, A. F. Asymptotic and resampling strategies for assessing and comparing indirect effects in multiple mediator models. Behav. Res. Methods. 40, 879–891 (2008)."), the proportion of mediation was calculated by dividing the total indirect effect by the total effect (i.e., total indirect effect / total effect), reflecting the extent to which mediators account for the overall association between the independent and dependent variables. The significance level was set at α = 0.05. In addition, to verify the robustness and theoretical specificity of the hypothesized model, AMOS 29.0 was used to construct and compare several competing models. The results indicated that all models demonstrated satisfactory fit indices, confirming the theoretical soundness and stability of the primary model’s pathway structure. Given that this study employed a cross-sectional design, the temporal ordering among variables could not be verified. Therefore, all mediation pathways were interpreted as associative relationships rather than causal links, aiming to examine the correlations among CEM, emotional distress, SWB, and healthy dietary behavior, rather than to infer causality.

### Missing data and statistical power analysis

A total of 3,301 university students were invited to participate, and 3,007 valid questionnaires were retained for analysis, yielding an effective response rate of 91.1%. Data were collected using an online questionnaire format, and a small pilot test ( _n_ = 120) was conducted prior to the formal survey to determine the average completion time (M = 218.4 s, SD = 32.1 s). Based on this pilot test, questionnaires completed in less than three standard deviations below the mean (< 120 s) were considered invalid. The data screening criteria included: (1) completion time of less than 120 s; (2) failure to pass attention-check questions (e.g., “Please select ‘Strongly Agree’ for this item”); and (3) patterned or inconsistent responses on reverse-scored items. All invalid responses were automatically identified by the program and manually verified before exclusion. The final dataset contained less than 1% missing data, which met the criteria for Missing Completely at Random (MCAR); therefore, listwise deletion was applied. According to Cohen’s (1988) sample size estimation formula, assuming a medium effect size (f² = 0.15) at a significance level of α = 0.05 with 10 predictors, a minimum sample size of approximately 118 would be required to achieve a statistical power of 0.80. The present study’s sample size of 3,007 far exceeded this threshold, indicating adequate statistical power and robustness of the results.

### Testing the stability of the hypothesized model

To examine the robustness and theoretical specificity of the hypothesized model (M1), four competing models were further constructed for comparison: a reverse model (M2: CEM → SWB → anxiety and depression → healthy dietary behavior), a parallel mediation model (M3: CEM → anxiety and depression, SWB → healthy dietary behavior), a single mediation model (M4: CEM → anxiety and depression → healthy dietary behavior), and a moderation model (M5: SWB moderating the relationship between emotional distress and dietary behavior). Although M3 exhibited the most optimal statistical performance, M1 was retained as the primary model for interpretation and discussion, given that it aligns with the theoretical assumption of a chain mediation pathway (CEM → anxiety and depression → SWB → healthy dietary behavior) and reflects a psychologically and developmentally reasonable temporal sequence. ### Mediation model analysis

After adding emotional distress variables, the association between CEM and healthy dietary behavior weakened slightly but remained significant (β = −0.149, _p_ < 0.001); both depression (β = −0.102, _p_ < 0.001) and anxiety (β = −0.072, _p_ < 0.05) were also negatively related to healthy dietary behavior. Overall, higher levels of CEM were associated with greater emotional distress and lower SWB, which in turn were linked to poorer healthy dietary behavior. To improve interpretability, standardized regression coefficients were reported to reflect practical significance: for each one standard deviation increase in CEM, healthy dietary scores decreased by 0.132 SDs; for each one SD increase in depression and anxiety, healthy dietary scores decreased by 0.084 and 0.060 SDs, respectively; conversely, for each one SD increase in SWB, healthy dietary scores increased by 0.087 SDs. ## Discussion

This study examined the associations among CEM, emotional distress, SWB, and healthy dietary behavior in a sample of Chinese university students, with a particular focus on the mediating roles of depression, anxiety, and SWB. The results showed that CEM was positively correlated with depression and anxiety, both of which were negatively correlated with SWB, whereas SWB was positively correlated with healthy dietary behavior. Further analyses indicated that depression, anxiety, and SWB partially mediated the relationship between CEM and healthy dietary behavior. In addition, the correlations between SWB and emotional distress in this study ( _r_ = − 0.371 to − 0.356) were comparable to those reported in previous studies employing multi-item standardized scales ( _r_ ≈ − 0.55 among American university students[79](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR79 "Liu, Y., Beauparlant, E. This consistency provides theoretical support for the validity of the current measurement tools and confirms the cross-cultural stability of the negative association between SWB and emotional distress. The confirmatory factor analysis further demonstrated excellent model fit, with satisfactory reliability and convergent validity for all scales, indicating that the single-item SWB measure and the abbreviated anxiety and depression scales were psychometrically appropriate for the present research context. Although all indirect effects reached statistical significance, their magnitudes were relatively small (standardized indirect effects = 0.02–0.08), corresponding to small-to-medium effects according to Cohen’s criteria[81](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR81 "Cohen, J. This suggests that while the directions of the psychological pathways are consistent with theoretical expectations, the overall magnitudes are modest, reflecting stable theoretical associations rather than strong behavioral or clinical effects. Therefore, the present findings should primarily be interpreted as theoretical support for the proposed psychological framework. Future research should employ longitudinal or experimental designs to examine whether these pathways strengthen over time or in high-risk populations, thereby enhancing their practical and clinical significance.

### Test of hypothesis 1: the direct effect of childhood emotional maltreatment on healthy dietary behavior

The present study found that CEM was negatively correlated with healthy dietary behaviors among university students, consistent with previous findings[82](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR82 "Yuksel, H. Prior research has shown that emotional maltreatment in childhood is often associated with difficulties in emotional regulation and maladaptive health behaviors. Such experiences may impair individuals’ ability to manage emotions effectively, which in turn relates to unhealthy eating patterns[83](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR83 "Heim, C. & Nemeroff, C. ### Test of hypothesis 2: emotional distress mediates the relationship between childhood emotional maltreatment and healthy dietary behavior patterns

Prior studies have shown that emotional maltreatment during childhood is often associated with difficulties in emotional regulation, self-evaluation, and perceived social support—factors that are closely linked to depressive and anxious symptoms[83](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR83 "Heim, C. & Nemeroff, C. In this study, emotional distress was significantly negatively correlated with healthy dietary behavior, suggesting that individuals with poorer emotional states tended to report lower levels of healthy eating. ### Test of hypothesis H3: subjective well-being mediates the relationship between childhood emotional maltreatment and healthy dietary behavior

The results of the present study showed a significant negative correlation between CEM and SWB, indicating that individuals who reported higher levels of emotional maltreatment tended to exhibit lower levels of SWB. This finding is consistent with previous cross-sectional studies[44](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR44 "Zukauskiene, R. Interpersonal Development. For example, the prefrontal cortex, which is involved in emotional regulation and self-control, and the amygdala, which is linked to emotional responses, have been found in prior studies to show structural differences among individuals with high levels of early emotional adversity. In the present study, SWB was positively correlated with healthy dietary behavior. This result aligns with emerging evidence suggesting that psychological well-being and health-related behaviors are interconnected. For instance, research on the gut–brain axis indicates that individuals with higher SWB often exhibit better emotional regulation and healthier eating patterns, whereas frequent consumption of high-fat or high-sugar foods is associated with lower SWB and potential gut microbiota imbalance[90](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR90 "Foster, J. CEM was found to be significantly associated with both SWB and healthy dietary behavior, and SWB may play a potential mediating role in this relationship. Future studies could further examine the temporal sequence and causal mechanisms of this association within longitudinal or experimental frameworks.

### Test of hypothesis H4: emotional distress and subjective well-being jointly mediate the relationship between childhood emotional maltreatment and healthy dietary behavior in a chain mediation model

The current study found that emotional distress negatively predicts SWB, and that depression and anxiety partially mediate the relationship between CEM and healthy dietary behavior via SWB. These findings support Hypothesis 4 of the present study and are consistent with previous research[54](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR54 "Öztürk, A. & Mutlu, T. The relationship between attachment style, subjective well-being, happiness and social anxiety among university students’. Procedia - Soc. Behav. Sci. 9, 1772–1776 (2010)."). According to social ecological theory, the development of an individual’s mental health and well-being is embedded within interactions across multiple ecological systems[92](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR92 "Crandon, T. J., Scott, J. G., Charlson, F. J. & Thomas, H. J. A social–ecological perspective on climate anxiety in children and adolescents. Nat. Clim. Change. 12, 123–131 (2022)."). This theory provides a broad contextual perspective for the present study rather than serving as a direct explanation of individual-level psychological mechanisms. Within this ecological framework, the association between emotional distress and SWB primarily occurs at the individual level, but it is also indirectly related to environmental factors such as family support, social relationships, and community resources[93](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR93 "Ford, B. Q., Shallcross, A. J., Mauss, I. B., Floerke, V. A. & Gruber, J. Desperately seeking happiness: valuing happiness is associated with symptoms and diagnosis of depression. J. soc. clin. psychol. 33, 890–905 (2014)."). Research has shown that CEM is often associated with social isolation, lack of intimate relationships, and emotional support[94](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR94 "Briere, J. & Elliott, D. M. Prevalence and psychological sequelae of self-reported childhood physical and sexual abuse in a general population sample of men and women. Child. Abuse Negl. 27, 1205–1222 (2003)."). Chronic deprivation of emotional support and a sense of security can lead to increased vulnerability to feelings of depression, helplessness, and hopelessness in adulthood, which are core symptoms of emotional distress[95](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR95 "Teicher, M. H. & Samson, J. A. Childhood maltreatment and psychopathology: a case for ecophenotypic variants as clinically and neurobiologically distinct subtypes. Am. J. Psychiatry. 170, 1114–1133 (2013)."). Emotional maltreatment is closely linked to low self-esteem, excessive self-blame, and feelings of helplessness in individuals, which in turn promote the emergence of depression and anxiety[94](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR94 "Briere, J. & Elliott, D. M. Prevalence and psychological sequelae of self-reported childhood physical and sexual abuse in a general population sample of men and women. Child. Abuse Negl. 27, 1205–1222 (2003)."). Higher levels of depression and anxiety are typically associated with lower life satisfaction and lower self-worth, which are indicators of low SWB[96](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR96 "Swinburn, B. A. et al. The global obesity pandemic: shaped by global drivers and local environments. Lancet (Lond Engl). 378, 804–814 (2011)."). More critically, low SWB may impair the motivational system and executive functioning, both of which are crucial for goal-directed behavior, such as making consistent healthy dietary behavior choices[97](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR97 "The impact of social change on the decreasing trend. of subjective well-being in Chinese adolescents: A cross-temporal meta-analysis. Child. Youth Serv. Rev. 150, 106988 (2023)."). When SWB is compromised, individuals may experience a decline in intrinsic motivation to engage in health-promoting behaviors and a reduced ability to resist impulsive or emotionally driven diet[98](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR98 "Relations between negative. affect, coping, and emotional eating. Appetite 48, 368–376 (2007)."). This is especially relevant given that emotional distress often sensitizes the brain’s reward system, increasing the appeal of high-calorie, sugary, or processed foods as temporary mood regulators[99](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR99 "Herman, C. P. & Polivy, J. External cues in the control of food intake in humans: The sensory-normative distinction. Physiol. Behav. 94, 722–728 (2008)."). Furthermore, the findings of this study suggest that the role of SWB in the model may be more complex than a simple chain mediation pathway. Comparable chain mediation structures have been observed in prior studies examining emotional distress and behavioral outcomes [100](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-41669-y#ref-CR100 "Yan, C., Liu, Y., Xiao, T. & Zhang, T. This indicates that, at a cross-sectional level, SWB and emotional distress may be simultaneously associated with healthy dietary behavior rather than following a fixed temporal or causal order. In other words, SWB and emotional distress may reflect different but interrelated psychological processes that are jointly linked to individuals’ healthy dietary patterns. Future studies using longitudinal or experimental designs could further explore the dynamic relationship between these psychological factors to clarify their temporal mechanisms.

### Theoretical and practical implications and future research directions

#### Theoretical implications

The present study found that the chain mediation pathway (CEM → emotional distress → SWB→ healthy dietary behavior) showed a relatively weak effect, whereas a more simplified two-step pathway (CEM → emotional distress → healthy dietary behavior) demonstrated stronger statistical significance. This suggests that the association between CEM and healthy dietary behavior may primarily operate through the more direct psychological pathway of emotional distress, with the mediating role of SWB being relatively limited. From a psychological mechanism perspective, emotional distress (e.g., anxiety and depression) represents a more immediate emotional response to early adverse experiences, and its link with dietary behavior is often characterized by short-term functional reactivity. Prior research has indicated that individuals under stress or negative emotional states tend to engage in emotional eating or prefer high-sugar and high-fat foods as a means of emotion regulation, forming short-term maladaptive coping patterns. Such mechanisms are relatively direct, which may explain their stronger statistical effects. In contrast, SWB reflects a more stable and long-term psychological experience, whose influence may be more evident in the maintenance of sustained healthy lifestyle habits rather than in immediate, emotion-driven eating behaviors. Furthermore, from a methodological standpoint, SWB and emotional distress share conceptual overlap and may interact with each other. Both reflect broader dimensions of emotional regulation and psychological well-being, leading to shared variance that may attenuate the independent mediating effect of SWB (effect attenuation). Given the cross-sectional design of this study, temporal dynamics among these variables cannot be fully captured, which may also contribute to the underestimation of the sequential mediation effect. Overall, these findings suggest that the link between CEM and dietary behavior in emerging adulthood is more likely to be explained by the direct association through emotional distress, while SWB may serve a complementary or buffering role rather than a necessary mediating pathway. Future studies should employ longitudinal or ecological momentary assessment (EMA) designs to further examine the temporal and dynamic mechanisms linking emotional distress, SWB, and healthy behavioral patterns.

#### Practical implications

Grounded in theoretical frameworks from psychology and behavioral medicine, the present study examined the associations between CEM and healthy dietary behavior among university students, with particular attention to the mediating roles of emotional distress and SWB. The study was theoretically informed by the social ecological theory and emotion regulation theory. The results demonstrated a significant negative association between CEM and healthy dietary behavior, with emotional distress and SWB serving as important mediators. These findings provide a theoretical basis for psychological intervention—specifically, addressing emotional distress and enhancing individuals’ SWB may help mitigate the negative associations between CEM and healthy dietary patterns. Although the cross-sectional design of this study does not permit causal inference, the hypothesized chain mediation model is supported by both theoretical and empirical evidence. Previous research has shown that CEM is associated with higher levels of emotional distress, lower SWB, and less healthy dietary behaviors. Accordingly, the proposed mediation pathway in this study reflects an established theoretical temporal sequence among these psychological constructs, while interpretations remain correlational in nature.

#### Limitations and future research directions

First, this study employed a cross-sectional design, which precludes the establishment of causal relationships among CEM, emotional distress, SWB, and healthy dietary behavior. The proposed pathways should therefore be interpreted as theoretically driven associations rather than empirical causal chains. Future research should employ longitudinal or experimental designs to verify the temporal sequence and causal directions among these variables. Second, CEM was assessed retrospectively based on self-report. As with all retrospective measures, participants’ current emotional states may influence the recall and interpretation of past experiences, potentially introducing recall bias. This limitation should be considered when interpreting the observed associations. Third, the study utilized abbreviated scales and single-item indicators to reduce participant burden. Although these measures demonstrated acceptable reliability and validity, their brevity may have limited the comprehensiveness and precision of construct assessment. For example, dietary behavior in this study was assessed based on the frequency of fruit, vegetable, and soft drink consumption, which should be regarded as a proxy indicator of general dietary health tendencies, rather than a comprehensive nutritional evaluation. Future studies could adopt multidimensional and validated dietary assessment tools (e.g., the Healthy Eating Index, dietary screening questionnaires, or emotional eating scales) to examine broader aspects of dietary behavior, including processed food intake, portion control, and meal regularity. Fourth, the study used convenience and snowball sampling, with participants drawn from six universities in central and southwestern China, which may introduce limitations in representativeness and potential selection bias. Although the sample covered multiple regions and academic disciplines and demonstrated some heterogeneity, the findings should be generalized with caution. Future studies should consider probability or stratified sampling and replicate the model across community, clinical, and non-student samples to test its robustness and cross-group applicability. Fifth, cultural and social contexts may influence the observed associations. Cultural factors such as collectivist orientation, mental health stigma, and familial expectations in Chinese society may moderate the relationship between childhood experiences and health behaviors. Future research should conduct cross-cultural validation across different regions and cultural contexts to enhance the external validity and cultural generalizability of the findings. In addition, from a methodological standpoint, the PROCESS macro used in this study cannot explicitly account for measurement error or potential school-level clustering effects. Future research could adopt latent variable structural equation modeling (SEM) or multilevel modeling approaches (HLM/ML-SEM) to more accurately estimate measurement error and hierarchical variance. Given that some variables were ordinal in nature, future studies may also consider robust estimation methods (e.g., WLSMV) to improve the precision of results. Finally, although some of the indirect effects were relatively small in magnitude, their directions were consistent and aligned with theoretical expectations, suggesting that CEM may be indirectly associated with SWB and healthy lifestyle behaviors in emerging adulthood through multilevel psychological mechanisms. Future longitudinal and intervention-based studies could further explore the clinical and applied implications of this model, including psychological interventions, emotion regulation training, and health behavior promotion strategies.

## Conclusion

This study examined the psychological mechanisms underlying the associations between CEM and healthy dietary behavior among university students, with a particular focus on the mediating roles of emotional distress and SWB. By applying a chain mediation framework, this research extends previous correlational studies and provides a more nuanced understanding of how early emotional experiences are statistically linked to later health-related behaviors. From a practical perspective, the findings suggest that psychological factors should be integrated into university-based dietary health programs. Specifically, reducing emotional distress and enhancing SWB may help support more positive dietary tendencies among students. It should be noted that healthy dietary behavior in this study was assessed using a brief three-item composite, which serves as a proxy indicator of general dietary tendencies rather than a comprehensive nutritional assessment, and the findings should be interpreted within this measurement scope. Although the cross-sectional design limits causal interpretation, the observed associations offer meaningful theoretical insights into the interconnectedness of early emotional experiences, mental health, and health behaviors. Future research should adopt longitudinal or experimental designs, employ multi-item and multidimensional measures to improve reliability, and explore potential moderating variables—such as self-control, social support, or coping styles—to further clarify and expand understanding of these complex psychological pathways.
