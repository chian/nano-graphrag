Anxiety severity moderates the relation between pineal parenchymal volume and objective sleep problems in peri-adolescent youth


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y.pdf)

## Abstract

Sleep problems are common in anxiety especially around the onset of adolescence (“peri-adolescence”) when symptoms escalate and sleep changes. The pineal gland is a compelling target given its role in sleep behavior and associations with multiple disorders that report problems with sleep. We investigated pineal gland volume and anxiety severity in relation to objective measures of sleep derived from polysomnography in a group of peri-adolescents. Youth ( _N_ = 200; 10–13 years) were sampled across a continuum of anxiety severity, with special emphasis on social, generalized, and separation anxiety disorders, to capture variability in key risk profiles and behaviors. This study uses structural scans obtained from participants with polysomnography data ( _n_ = 135). Pineal glands were manually segmented into parenchymal and cyst compartments. Linear models were used to assess the interaction between anxiety severity and pineal parenchymal volume (PPV) on sleep onset latency (SOL), sleep efficiency (SE), and non-rapid eye movement (NREM) and REM duration. Analyses revealed significant interactions between anxiety severity and PPV on SOL, SE, and NREM duration but not REM duration; whereby elevated anxiety was associated with larger PPV and longer SOL and reduced SE and NREM duration. Pineal morphology may be a contributing factor to sleep problems in anxiety during peri-adolescence.

## Introduction

Sleep problems are commonly observed across psychopathologies and prospectively relate to the emergence or escalation of internalizing symptoms, especially during the transition from childhood to adolescence, or “peri-adolesence”[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR1 "McMakin, D. L. & Alfano, C. A. Sleep and anxiety in late childhood and early adolescence. Curr. Opin. Psychiatry. 28 (6), 483–489 (2015)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR2 "Stepan, M. E., Franzen, P. L., Teresi, G. I., Rode, N. & Goldstein, T. R. Sleep quality predicts future mood symptoms in adolescents with bipolar disorder. J. Affect. Disord. 361, 664–673 (2024)."). Our mechanistic understanding of these sleep problems and their association with pathology remains poorly understood. This lack of clarity may be due to variability in sleep related problems within and across disorders (e.g., problems with duration, quality, transitions), a limited number of studies examining sleep staging and neurophysiology in clinical samples, and the flux of sleep related behaviors and neurophysiology across development and contexts (e.g., shift of circadian rhythm in adolescence)[3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR3 "Carskadon, M. A. Sleep in adolescents: the perfect storm. Pediatr. Clin. 58 (3), 637–647 (2011)."), [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR4 "Harvey, A. G., Murray, G., Chandler, R. A. & Soehner, A. Sleep disturbance as transdiagnostic: consideration of Neurobiological mechanisms. Clin. Psychol. Rev. 31 (2), 225–235 (2011)."). Here we aim to address these limitations to drive new mechanistic discovery. We deeply characterize clinical symptoms and sleep via multimethod assessment focused on a narrow developmental window at the cusp of adolescence. Although other brain structures such as the amygdala, brainstem, and hypothalamus are implicated in sleep regulation, we focus the current study on the relation between the pineal gland, a region important for melatonin synthesis, a neurohormone essential for establishing and maintaining circadian rhythms among other mechanisms, and anxiety severity on objective measures of sleep derived from polysomnography (PSG) – a systematic process to collect physiological data (e.g., electroencephalogram, electrooculogram) during sleep.

Sleep problems and anxiety are bidirectionally related, escalate during adolescence, and may be influenced by neurodevelopmental changes unique to this developmental window. Subjective sleep problems are reported in approximately 85% of anxious youth[5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR5 "Willis, T. A. & Gregory, A. M. Anxiety disorders and sleep in children and adolescents. Sleep. Med. Clin. 10 (2), 125–131 (2015)."). Similarly, anxiety symptoms and mental health comorbidities are a common feature in people suffering from sleep disturbances like insomnia[6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR6 "Glidewell, R. N., McPherson Botts, E. & Orr, W. C. Insomnia and anxiety: diagnostic and management implications of complex interactions. Sleep. Med. Clin. 10 (1), 93–99 (2015)."), [7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR7 "Jansson-Fröjmark, M. & Lindblom, K. A bidirectional relationship between anxiety and depression, and insomnia? A prospective study in the general population. J. Psychosom. Res. 64 (4), 443–449 (2008)."). Objective PSG measures of sleep in anxious youth have been somewhat sparse and inconsistent but in some cases have identified longer sleep onset latencies (SOL), decreased sleep efficiency (SE), and decreases in sleep depth[8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR8 "Cox, R. C. & Olatunji, B. O. Sleep in the anxiety-related disorders: A meta-analysis of subjective and objective research. Sleep. Med. Rev. 51, 101282 (2020)."). Moreover, reduced sleep duration and sleep related problems, such as poor quality or increased sleep onset latency, have been prospectively linked to increased symptoms of anxiety and depression[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR9 "Akbar, S. A., Mattfeld, A. T., Laird, A. R. & McMakin, D. L. Sleep to internalizing pathway in young adolescents (SIPYA): A proposed neurodevelopmental model. Neurosci. Biobehav Rev. 140, 104780 (2022).").

Despite the associations between sleep problems and internalizing disorders like anxiety, neurobiological mechanisms have not been established. Smaller pineal glands have consistently been observed in other psychopathologies that exhibit sleep problems[10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR10 "Atmaca, M. et al. Reduced pineal gland volume in patients with obsessive-compulsive disorder. Int. J. Psychiatry Med. 60 (5), 508–516 (2024)."), [11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR11 "Takahashi, T. et al. Pineal gland volume in major depressive and bipolar disorders. Front. Psychiatry. 11, 450 (2020)."), [12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR12 "Zhao, W. et al. Pineal gland abnormality in major depressive disorder. Psychiatry Res. Neuroimaging. 289, 13–17 (2019)."). However, previous studies have been performed in adults with no studies to our knowledge examining the role of the pineal gland in peri-adolescent youth, a developmental window when internalizing symptom severity sharply escalates and melatonin secretion demonstrates significant changes. Anxiety disorders are characterized by excessive worry and fear consistent with chronic stress[13](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR13 "Shin, L. M. & Liberzon, I. The neurocircuitry of Fear, Stress, and anxiety disorders. Neuropsychopharmacology 35 (1), 169–191 (2010).") – pineal volume has been shown to be sensitive to acute and chronic stress[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR14 "Milin, J., Martinovic, J. & Demajo, M. Morphodynamic response of the pineal gland to initial stress attack. Arch. Anat. Microsc Morphol. Exp. 73 (3), 159–180 (1984)."), [15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR15 "Miline, R. The role of the pineal gland in stress. J. Neural Transm. 47 (3), 191–220 (1980)."). Melatonin synthesis in the pineal gland is dependent on serotonin availability[16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR16 "Ganguly, S., Coon, S. L. & Klein, D. C. Control of melatonin synthesis in the mammalian pineal gland: the critical role of serotonin acetylation. Cell. Tissue Res. 309 (1), 127–137 (2002)."), [17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR17 "Lee, B. H., Hille, B. & Koh, D. S. Serotonin modulates melatonin synthesis as an autocrine neurotransmitter in the pineal gland. Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. 118 (43), e2113852118 (2021)."); notably, anxiety has been linked to serotonin dysregulation[18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR18 "Murphy, D. L. et al. Anxiety and affective disorder comorbidity related to serotonin and other neurotransmitter systems: obsessive-compulsive disorder as an example of overlapping clinical and genetic heterogeneity. Philos. Trans. R Soc. Lond. B Biol. Sci. 368 (1615), 20120435 (2013)."). Thus, pineal volume may be impacted by common features (e.g., stress) or mechanisms of anxiety (e.g., serotonin availability) and negatively impact sleep problems in anxious youth.

The pineal gland is an oval-shaped structure, part of the diencephalon in a region known as the epithalamus. The gland sits above the superior colliculus, inferior to the splenium aspect of the corpus callosum, dorsal to the habenular commissure, and ventral to the posterior commissure with connection to the third ventricle through what is known as the pineal recess[19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR19 "Arendt, J. & Aulinas, A. Physiology of the pineal gland and melatonin. In: (eds Feingold, K. R., Anawalt, B., Blackman, M. R., Boyce, A., Chrousos, G., Corpas, E. et al.) Endotext. South Dartmouth (MA): MDText.com, Inc. (2000)."). Development begins from birth to two years of age after which point further development is minimal[20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR20 "Sumida, M., Barkovich, A. J. & Newton, T. H. Development of the pineal gland: measurement with MR. AJNR Am. J. Neuroradiol. 17 (2), 233–236 (1996)."). The primary function of the pineal gland is the production and distribution of melatonin, a hormone important to overall health. In humans, melatonin is distributed in the central nervous system through release into cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) via the pineal recess[21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR21 "Leston, J. et al. Melatonin is released in the third ventricle in humans. A study in movement disorders. Neurosci. Lett. 469 (3), 294–297 (2010)."). Pineal cysts, widely varying in their prevalence in magnetic resonance imaging (2% to 57.4%[22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR22 "Al-Holou, W. N., Garton, H. J. L., Muraszko, K. M., Ibrahim, M. & Maher, C. O. Prevalence of pineal cysts in children and young adults. J. Neurosurg. Pediatr. 4 (3), 230–236 (2009)."), [23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR23 "Bumb, J. M., Brockmann, M. A., Groden, C., Al-Zghloul, M. & Nölte, I. TrueFISP of the pediatric pineal gland: volumetric and microstructural analysis. Clin. Neuroradiol. 22 (1), 69–77 (2012).") and observed in 21–41% of autopsy specimens, are fluid filled compartments that present as hypointense in T1-weighted images compared to parenchyma[24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR24 "Barboriak, D. P., Lee, L. & Provenzale, J. M. Serial MR imaging of pineal cysts. Am. J. Roentgenol. 176 (3), 737–743 (2001)."). Volume of the pineal parenchyma has been found to positively correlate with melatonin levels[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR25 "Nölte, I. et al. Pineal volume and circadian melatonin profile in healthy volunteers: an interdisciplinary approach. J. Magn. Reson. Imaging. 30 (3), 499–505 (2009).").

Melatonin plays a critical role in the regulation of sleep initiation and maintenance. For instance, aralkylamine _N_-acetyltransferase (AANAT)−/− knockout zebrafish larvae exhibit significantly delayed sleep onset and reduced sleep duration[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR26 "Gandhi, A. V., Mosser, E. A., Oikonomou, G. & Prober, D. A. Melatonin is required for the circadian regulation of sleep. Neuron 85 (6), 1193–1199 (2015)."). Melatonin influences neurons in the suprachiasmatic nucleus, important for establishing circadian rhythms and the promotion of sleep[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR27 "Liu, C. et al. Molecular dissection of two distinct actions of melatonin on the Suprachiasmatic circadian clock. Neuron 19 (1), 91–102 (1997)."), [28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR28 "Slawik, H. et al. Prospective study on salivary evening melatonin and sleep before and after pinealectomy in humans. J. Biol. Rhythms. 31 (1), 82–93 (2016)."). It also has a regulatory effect on gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH), a neurohormone essential for pubertal onset[29](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR29 "Dubocovich, M. L., Rivera-Bermudez, M. A., Gerdin, M. J. & Masana, M. I. Molecular pharmacology, regulation and function of mammalian melatonin receptors. Front. Biosci-Landmark. 8 (4), 1093–1108 (2003)."), [30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR30 "Herbison, A. E. Control of puberty onset and fertility by gonadotropin-releasing hormone neurons. Nat. Rev. Endocrinol. 12 (8), 452–466 (2016)."). Melatonin synthesis changes across development, with production peaking in late childhood, followed by steep declines around the onset of puberty[31](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR31 "Andersen, C. C. et al. Melatonin secretion across puberty: A systematic review and meta-analysis. Psychoneuroendocrinology 173, 107281 (2025)."), [32](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR32 "Poza, J. J., Pujol, M., Ortega-Albás, J. J. & Romero, O. Melatonin in sleep disorders. Neurol. Engl. Ed. 37 (7), 575–585 (2022)."), playing an important role in the normative circadian shift observed during this developmental period[33](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR33 "Hagenauer, M. H., Perryman, J. I., Lee, T. M. & Carskadon, M. A. Adolescent changes in the homeostatic and circadian regulation of sleep. Dev. Neurosci. 31 (4), 276–284 (2009)."). Melatonin also exhibits properties of anti-inflammatory, antioxidative, anxiolytic, antinociceptive, anti-depressive, amongst others[34](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR34 "Emet, M. et al. A review of Melatonin, its receptors and drugs. Eurasian J. Med. 48 (2), 135–141 (2016)."). While much evidence has supported melatonin’s role in the onset of sleep, its impact on sleep staging has been more variable, with some studies showing no impact on the percentage of rapid eye movement (REM) sleep, but an increase in non-REM (NREM) stage 2[35](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR35 "Rajaratnam, S. M. W., Middleton, B., Stone, B. M., Arendt, J. & Dijk, D. J. Melatonin advances the circadian timing of EEG sleep and directly facilitates sleep without altering its duration in extended sleep opportunities in humans. J. Physiol. 561, 339–351 (2004).").

Here we sought to investigate whether pineal morphology was related to sleep problems in peri-adolescents with differing levels of anxiety severity. We hypothesized that pineal morphology would negatively correlate with anxiety severity and correspond to worse objective sleep outcomes. Participants were recruited from both clinic and community sources to ensure rich sampling across anxiety severity with a focus on primary disorders that are commonly observed in this age range of social phobia, generalized, and separation anxiety disorders, to capture variability in key risk profiles and behaviors. Youth between the ages of 10 and 13 years ( _N_ = 200) completed a clinical intake and were randomized to either a sleep ( _n_ = 135) or wake ( _n_ = 65) condition to support the primary project’s focus on the role of sleep versus wake on memory. Structural magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans were collected prior to overnight PSG recording in participants randomized to the Sleep condition. Each participant’s pineal gland was manually segmented into parenchymal and cyst compartments (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#Fig1)). Interactions between anxiety severity and pineal parenchymal volume (PPV) on sleep initiation and quality – SOL, SE, and both NREM and REM duration – were assessed.

## Results

### No significant association between pineal compartment volumes and anxiety severity

We examined the relation between pineal tissue compartment volume and anxiety severity. No significant associations between anxiety severity and total pineal volume combining cyst and parenchyma (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#Fig2) A; anxiety PCA: β = 9.6, bootstrapped BCa 95% CI \[-0.65, 0.2\], bootstrapped SE = 5.3, _p_ = 0.06), cyst volume (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#Fig2) B; anxiety PCA: β = 4.0, bootstrapped BCa 95% CI \[-0.03, 8.6\], bootstrapped SE = 2.2, _p_ = 0.06), nor PPV (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#Fig2) C; anxiety PCA: β = 5.6, bootstrapped BCa 95% CI \[-1.3, 0.13\], bootstrapped SE = 3.6, _p_ = 0.11) were observed. Given our assumption that melatonin production is principally afforded by pineal parenchyma, we limited our subsequent analyses to the PPV compartment.

### SOL is longer in high anxious participants with large PPV

To examine the moderation between anxiety severity and PPV on SOL we used a bootstrap procedure with 10,000 resamples (with replacement) to estimate the bias-corrected and accelerated (BCa) confidence interval for the moderation effect. We found that SOL – the time in minutes from when they got into bed to sleep onset – was differentially associated with PPV in the context of anxiety severity (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#Fig3) A; PPV \* anxiety PCA: β = 0.04, bootstrapped BCa 95% CI \[0.02, 0.07\], bootstrapped SE = 0.01, _p_ = 0.002). Specifically, when examining the simple slopes _post hoc_, high anxiety participants, captured by the upper standard deviation (+ 1SD), showed a positive linear relation between PPV and SOL, such that participants with larger PPV exhibited longer durations in their SOL (anxiety PCA \[+ 1SD\]: β = 0.14, t = 4.12, _p_ < 0.001). No relation between PPV and SOL was observed in our low anxious group (anxiety PCA \[-1SD\]: β = 0.00, t = 0.05, _p_ = 0.96).

### SE is lower in anxious participants with large PPV

Similar bootstrapped procedures were used to examine the moderation between anxiety severity and PPV on SE. We observed a significant moderation of PPV by anxiety severity on SE (\[Time spent asleep (min)/Total recording period (min)\] \* 100) (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#Fig3) B; PPV \* anxiety PCA: β = -0.02, bootstrapped BCa 95% CI \[-0.03, -0.004\], bootstrapped SE = 0.007, _p_ = 0.012). _Post hoc_ simple slopes analyses, while not significant, exhibited patterns consistent with high anxiety participants (+ 1SD) showing a negative relation between PPV and SE (anxiety PCA \[+ 1SD\]: β = -0.02, t = -0.88, _p_ = 0.38). For these individuals, SE was lower for those with larger PPV. In contrast, in lower anxiety severity participants (-1SD), the trend was reversed (anxiety PCA \[-1SD\]: β = 0.04, t = 1.98, _p_ = 0.05), with PPV positively associated with SE.

### NREM sleep is shorter in duration in anxious participants with large PPV

Lastly, we examined the relation between PPV and anxiety severity on NREM and REM duration. In the context of anxiety severity, PPV was differentially associated with NREM duration (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#Fig3) C; PPV \* anxiety PCA: β = -0.11, bootstrapped BCa 95% CI \[-0.16, -0.04\], bootstrapped SE = 0.03, _p_ = 0.002). Participants with elevated anxiety (+1SD) exhibited a nonsignificant negative trend between NREM duration and PPV (anxiety PCA \[+1SD\]: β = -0.09, t = -1.06, _p_ = 0.29). Specifically, larger PPV was associated with shorter NREM durations. The opposite significant association between PPV and NREM duration was observed in our low anxious subset of participants (-1SD) (anxiety PCA \[-1SD\]: β = 0.26, t = 2.8, _p_ = 0.01); NREM duration was greater in participants with larger PPV. No significant interaction between parenchymal volume and anxiety severity was observed when examining REM duration (PPV \* anxiety PCA: β = -0.006, bootstrapped BCa 95% CI \[-0.04, 0.04\], bootstrapped SE = 0.02, _p_ = 0.85).

## Discussion

Problems with sleep are prospectively associated with the development and maintenance of internalizing disorders in peri-adolescence[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR9 "Akbar, S. A., Mattfeld, A. T., Laird, A. R. & McMakin, D. L. Sleep to internalizing pathway in young adolescents (SIPYA): A proposed neurodevelopmental model. Neurosci. Biobehav Rev. 140, 104780 (2022)."). We observed that anxiety significantly moderated the effect of PPV on objective sleep measures. In participants with high anxiety (+ 1SD), larger PPV was associated with longer SOL, lower SE, and shorter NREM duration. In contrast, participants with low anxiety (-1SD) showed the opposite pattern – larger PPV was associated with shorter SOL, higher SE, and longer NREM duration – concordant with expectations. Our findings indicate that PPV is associated with sleep abnormalities in anxious youth and may reflect a pathway through which features common to anxiety (e.g., excessive worry or stress) or potential mechanisms (e.g., serotonin dysregulation) interact with neural regions involved in sleep regulation and potentially further contribute to sleep problems in anxious youth.

Our primary results showed that larger PPV in our most anxious youth was associated with delayed SOL, reduced SE, and shorter NREM duration. Sleep abnormalities, such as disrupted onset, efficiency, and subjective sleep quality, have consistently been reported in individuals with anxiety[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR1 "McMakin, D. L. & Alfano, C. A. Sleep and anxiety in late childhood and early adolescence. Curr. Opin. Psychiatry. 28 (6), 483–489 (2015)."), [8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR8 "Cox, R. C. & Olatunji, B. O. Sleep in the anxiety-related disorders: A meta-analysis of subjective and objective research. Sleep. Med. Rev. 51, 101282 (2020)."), [36](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR36 "Alfano, C. A., Zakem, A. H., Costa, N. M., Taylor, L. K. & Weems, C. F. Sleep problems and their relation to cognitive factors, anxiety, and depressive symptoms in children and adolescents. Depress. Anxiety. 26 (6), 503–512 (2009)."), [37](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR37 "Alfano, C. A., Reynolds, K., Scott, N., Dahl, R. E. & Mellman, T. A. Polysomnographic sleep patterns of non-depressed, non-medicated children with generalized anxiety disorder. J. Affect. Disord. 147 (1), 379–384 (2013)."), [38](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR38 "Fuller, K. H., Waters, W. F., Binks, P. G. & Anderson, T. Generalized anxiety and sleep architecture: A polysomnographic investigation. Sleep 20 (5), 370–376 (1997)."). Mechanistic implications of the larger PPV observed in our participants with greater anxiety severity may include elevated melatonin production[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR25 "Nölte, I. et al. Assessing the mechanisms that contribute to larger PPV in anxious individuals also warrants discussion. For example, larger PPV may reflect a response to elevated stress in anxious youth[44](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR44 "Gyles, T. M., Nestler, E. J. & Parise, E. M. Past studies have shown that the pineal gland is characterized by two distinct phases following environmental stress. The first phase includes reductions or involutive structural changes that emerge initially but are followed by a stimulated phase of activity where hyperplasia and hypertrophy are dominant[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR14 "Milin, J., Martinovic, J. & Demajo, M. The synthesis of melatonin originates from the aromatic amino acid tryptophan, commencing with the same chemical pathway of serotonin[54](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR54 "Hardeland, R. Melatonin metabolism in the central nervous system. Curr. Neuropharmacol. 8 (3), 168–181 (2010)."), [55](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR55 "Tordjman, S. et al. In fact, serotonin and norepinephrine both act as checkpoints for the stimulation of melatonin production[17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR17 "Lee, B. H., Hille, B. & Koh, D. S. Serotonin modulates melatonin synthesis as an autocrine neurotransmitter in the pineal gland. Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. 118 (43), e2113852118 (2021)."). Altered serotonin availability in anxious youth could therefore affect the regulation of AANAT, the rate limiting enzyme, and hinder serotonin’s function as a precursor to melatonin production, potentially causing a decrease of endogenous melatonin supply. Due to this dysregulation of melatonin synthesis, we posit that children with high anxiety levels will have decreased melatonin production, impacting sleep neurophysiology. A similar model of melatonin pathway dysregulation has been considered for the decreased melatonin levels in Alzheimer’s patients[56](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR56 "Wu, Y. H. et al. Molecular changes underlying reduced pineal melatonin levels in alzheimer disease: alterations in preclinical and clinical stages. J. Clin. Endocrinol. Metab. 88 (12), 5898–5906 (2003)."). It is important to note that our sample consisted of peri-adolescents, with a significant difference in pubertal maturation observed between our males and females (Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#Tab1)). On average, females begin puberty before males[64](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR64 "Breehl, L., Caban, O. & Physiology Puberty. In: StatPearls. Treasure Island (FL): StatPearls Publishing (2025)."). At puberty’s onset, children experience increased release of gonadotropin releasing hormone and growth hormone. Results of the current study should be interpreted considering important limitations. First, the study did not directly measure melatonin. Previous research has shown that pineal volume is associated with melatonin availability[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR25 "Nölte, I. et al. Pineal volume and circadian melatonin profile in healthy volunteers: an interdisciplinary approach. J. Magn. Reson. Imaging. 30 (3), 499–505 (2009)."). Future research to elucidate a potential interaction between melatonin dysregulation and anxiety severity would benefit from direct measures of melatonin synthesis. Second, pineal morphology was assessed using T1-weighted structural MRI scans, which are insensitive to pineal calcifications, a common incidental finding impacting pineal function. While CT scans are a superior methodology for detecting pineal calcifications[79](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR79 "Beker-Acay, M. et al. Radiol. 100 (1), 13 (2016)."), [80](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR80 "Turgut, A. T. et al. Age-related changes in the incidence of pineal gland calcification in turkey: A prospective multicenter CT study. Pathophysiol 15 (1), 41–48 (2008)."). Finally, PSG data were collected for one night, introducing the possibility of the first night effect[81](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR81 "Byun, J. H., Kim, K. T., Moon, H. J., Motamedi, G. K. & Cho, Y. W. The first night effect during polysomnography, and patients’ estimates of sleep quality. Psychiatry Res. 274, 27–29 (2019)."), [82](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR82 "Newell, J., Mairesse, O., Verbanck, P. & Neu, D. ## Conclusion and future directions

To conclude, anxiety severity moderated associations between PPV and key measurements of sleep neurophysiology derived from PSG. Specifically, amongst our most severely affected anxious youth, larger PPV was associated with longer SOL, lower SE, and shorter NREM duration. These results highlight the importance of pineal gland morphology in pediatric internalizing disorders. Future studies should directly examine melatonin availability and work in non-human animal models should manipulate noradrenergic and serotonergic tone to evaluate impact on melatonin synthesis assessing causal mechanisms. More directed investigations of pineal morphology in developmental anxious populations at the cusp of puberty are necessary for elucidating further mechanistic understandings and developing novel treatment plans.

## Methods

### Participant assent and consent

Before initiation of the study, informed child assent and parental consent were obtained from all participants. After study completion, participants were compensated for their time, receiving a 3D model of their brain and up to $300. All procedures and protocols were approved and performed in accordance with the relevant guidelines and regulations detailed by the Florida International University Institutional Review Board.

### Participants

Data were collected from peri-adolescents, ages 10–13 years, recruited from the Miami area through behavioral health clinics, events in the community, public schools, study flyers, and social media. The study was designed to recruit across a range of anxiety symptom severity stratified across low, medium, and high anxiety tertiles determined by the Pediatric Rating Scale (PARS)[83](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR83 "Caporino, N. E. et al. Defining treatment response and remission in child anxiety: signal detection analysis using the pediatric anxiety rating scale. J. Am. Acad. Child. Adolesc. Psychiatry. 52 (1), 57–67 (2013)."). Diagnoses were determined by detailed case presentation and expert consensus on more than 90% of cases (single expert was used for the remaining cases).

In total, 200 participants completed a clinical intake session (Table [S1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#MOESM1)), with 83 participants assessed prior to the onset of the Coronavirus Disease pandemic. Upon completion of the clinical intake, participants were randomized (2:1 ratio) to the Sleep ( _n_ = 135) and Wake ( _n_ = 65) groups. Only data from the Sleep group will be included given the focus on sleep neurophysiology. Participants were excluded if they met any of the following criteria: left-handedness, severe visual impairments, difficulties with speaking/reading English, conditions which lead to brain function impediments (e.g., traumatic brain injury), MRI contraindications, an IQ less than 80, ongoing intake of psychotropic medications, and the following psychiatric diagnoses: past or current depressive disorder, ADHD, specific phobia, post-traumatic stress disorder, schizophrenia and other psychotic disorders, autism spectrum disorder, bipolar disorder, OCD, oppositional defiant disorder, conduct disorder, and a panic disorder without comorbid separational anxiety, generalized anxiety disorder, and/or social phobia. Of note, regarding medication usage, if a candidate was on stimulant medications, participation in the study was allowed if the child and family (in consultation with the doctor) were able to do a 24-hour wash-out period.

Of the participants randomized to the sleep condition ( _n_ = 135), eight were lost to follow up between their clinical intake and MRI sessions, seven withdrew from the study prior to their scheduled MRI visit (2 refused randomization to the Sleep group, 1 expressed fear of the MRI, 3 families encountered logistical impediments that precluded their ability to complete the study, and 1 participant indicated taking an exclusionary medication). One participant was randomized to the Sleep group but not included due to a complication at the scanning facility, and an additional participant was removed due to an incidental finding. The final sample consisted of 118 participants (Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#Tab1)).

### Anxiety severity measures and ratings

#### General clinical intake overview

A battery of measures were administered to establish diagnoses and interference severity according to Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (5th ed.)[84](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR84 "Diagnostic and statistical manual of mental disorders: DSM-5™ 5th edn (American Psychiatric Publishing, Inc., 2013)."). These data were integrated and reviewed in a weekly clinical meeting where discrepancies in diagnoses and/or severity ratings were resolved by consensus among experts in child/adolescent anxiety, including a licensed clinical psychologist with board certification in child and adolescent clinical psychology (DM) and a licensed mental health counselor. If both experts were not available, one expert rated the case (~ 10% of cases). Complex cases waited for both experts.

#### Anxiety disorders interview schedule

The Anxiety Disorders Interview Schedule (ADIS) is a semi-structured interview administered during the clinical intake by a team of trained and supervised clinical interviewers[85](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR85 "Silverman, W. & Albano, A. Anxiety Disorders Interview Schedule (ADIS-IV) Child/Parent (Oxford University Press, 1996)."). The parent and child were separately interviewed. Although the interview derives diagnoses based on parent and child report, we relied on the process described above that integrated all available clinical data to arrive at diagnosis and severity ratings.

#### Pediatric anxiety rating scale

The PARS[83](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR83 "Caporino, N. E. et al. Defining treatment response and remission in child anxiety: signal detection analysis using the pediatric anxiety rating scale. J. Am. Acad. Child. Adolesc. Psychiatry. 52 (1), 57–67 (2013).") was administered during the semi-structured interview to both child and parent separately to the identify frequency, severity, and level of interference of symptoms. Following the interview, clinical interviewers met to integrate both reports and derive a final PARS-6 score ranging between 0 (no symptoms) and 30 (highest severity). To sample across the full distribution of anxiety symptom severity, recruitment was stratified by PARS-6 tertiles: low (0–3), medium[4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR4 "Harvey, A. G., Murray, G., Chandler, R. A. & Soehner, A. Sleep disturbance as transdiagnostic: consideration of Neurobiological mechanisms. Clin. Psychol. Rev. 31 (2), 225–235 (2011)."), [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR5 "Willis, T. A. & Gregory, A. M. Anxiety disorders and sleep in children and adolescents. Sleep. Med. Clin. 10 (2), 125–131 (2015)."), [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR6 "Glidewell, R. N., McPherson Botts, E. & Orr, W. C. Insomnia and anxiety: diagnostic and management implications of complex interactions. Sleep. Med. #### Screen for child anxiety related emotional disorders

The Screen for Child Anxiety Related Emotional Disorders (SCARED) questionnaire was administered to derive a total anxiety score. Scores can range between 0 and 82 with scores of 25 or greater associated with a high likelihood of an anxiety disorder diagnosis.

#### Principal components analysis

Given that each measure and source (i.e., parent versus youth respondent) regarding anxiety provides important information and peri-adolescence is a time in development when there is significant variability in terms of which informant may be able to provide the most accurate summary, a single composite score of anxiety severity was calculated by combining primary diagnoses (i.e., binary output yes or no diagnosis of anxiety) with PARS-6 as well as both parent and child SCARED scores using Principal Components Analysis (PCA). This approach captures latent components reflecting common variance across measures, providing a data driven method that minimizes informant-specific biases while at the same time integrating across multiple informant perspectives. The ‘FactoMineR’ package[89](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR89 "Lê, S., Josse, J. & Husson, F. FactoMineR: an R package for multivariate analysis. J Stat. Softw 25(1). (2008).") in R was used to calculate the PCA. All variables of interest were significantly positively correlated (all _p_ < 0.001) and Bartlett’s test of sphericity ( _χ_ 2[6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR6 "Glidewell, R. N., McPherson Botts, E. & Orr, W. C. Insomnia and anxiety: diagnostic and management implications of complex interactions. Sleep. Med. Clin. 10 (1), 93–99 (2015).") = 354.89, _p_ < 0.001) suggested the measures were sufficiently correlated to justify a PCA approach. Further, the Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin measure of sampling adequacy was in a range (0.78) indicating pair-wise correlations can be explained by other common factors – necessary for PCA. The resultant PCA included a single component with an eigenvalue of 2.71 accounting for 67.8% of the variance in anxiety scores. This first component was extracted for each participant and used as the primary measure of anxiety severity. Please see Baker et al.,[90](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR90 "Baker, A. E. et al. A pubertal shift in anxiety reporting: Parent–child discrepancies intensify during peri-adolescence. Res Child Adolesc Psychopathol. (in press).") for a more detailed description of this approach using the same data.

### Pubertal development

Participants completed the Pubertal Development Scale (PDS)[92](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR92 "Petersen, A. C., Crockett, L., Richards, M. & Boxer, A. A self-report measure of pubertal status: Reliability, validity, and initial norms. J. Youth Adolesc. 17 (2), 117–133 (1988).") to assess their pubertal status. The PDS consists of six items assessing physical changes related to puberty, including growth spurt, body hair growth, skin changes, breast development (girls only), and voice changes (boys only). Each item is accompanied by a 4-point Likert scale ranging from 1 ( _not yet started_) to 4 ( _seems completed_). Items were converted to approximate Tanner stages[93](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR93 "Emmanuel, M. & Bokor, B. R. Tanner Stages. In: StatPearls. Treasure Island (FL): StatPearls Publishing. (2024).") using the Shirtcliff method[94](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR94 "Shirtcliff, E. A., Dahl, R. E. & Pollak, S. D. Pubertal development: correspondence between hormonal and physical development. Child. Dev. 80 (2), 327–337 (2009).") wherein scores range from 1 (prepubertal) to 5 (pubertal development completed).

### MRI procedures

All imaging was conducted at Florida International University, Center for Imaging Science (CIS) using a 3 Telsa Siemens MAGNETOM Prism using a 32-channel head coil. The imaging was performed as part of a larger study examining negative overgeneralization in peri-adolescent youth. Participants completed a T1-weighted anatomical scan (TE = 29 ms, flip angle = 8°, TR = 2500 ms, FoV = 256 × 256 mm2, slices = 176 interleaved, voxel size = 1 mm3). These anatomical images were subsequently used for manual segmentations of the pineal gland.

### Pineal gland manual segmentation protocol

The pineal gland was manually segmented using the coronal plane, however, all three planes were utilized to ensure consistency and accuracy upon final review. All pineal gland measurements were performed blinded to diagnostic materials and personally identifying health information by two research assistants (FF and ML). The gland was first located using key anatomical markers: inferior to the splenium aspect of the corpus callosum, superior to the quadrigeminal plate, medial to the pulvinars of the thalamic heads, and caudal to the third ventricle. Beginning anteriorly, the gland was segmented slice by slice moving posteriorly. The borders were identified as separate from other structures as the pineal gland is bathed in CSF and is surrounded laterally by ambient cistern and posteriorly by the quadrigeminal cistern. Segmentation was continued until the habenula was encountered. Non-pineal parenchyma voxels (e.g., hypointensities in the T1-weighted image \[i.e., cysts\]) were labeled separately (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#Fig1)). These areas of hypointensities were then confirmed by a PhD neuroscientist (A.T.M.). Voxels labeled as pineal parenchyma had an average volume of 98.7 mm3 while voxels labeled as cysts had an average volume of 20.4 mm3. Parenchymal voxels had a significantly greater T1-weighted signal intensity than voxels labeled as cysts (mean (SD): pineal parenchyma, 906.3 (102.7); cysts, 622.5 (123.5); t = 23.03, _p_ < 0.001). Inter-rater reliability was assessed on a set of 61 randomly selected T1-weighted anatomical images assigned to each research assistant after completion of the first manual segmentation. Dice similarity coefficient of spatial overlap was calculated obtaining a score of 0.79, which is considered a moderate-high to high overlap between raters.

### Polysomnography

PSG data were collected using the Respironics Alice ( _n_ = 43) and Natus Sleepworks PSG ( _n_ = 75) systems. Immediately following their MRI scan participants travelled to Nicklaus Children’s Hospital (NCH) for a single evening of PSG nocturnal data collection. Participants were measured and fitted with the LDE Headbox EEG for scalp recording of electroencephalographic signals at the following electrodes: C3, C4, F3, F4, O1, O2, PZ, FZ all referenced to linked mastoids A1/A2. PSG wiring also included two electrooculographic and two electromyographic leads. EEG was sampled at 500 Hz. PSG preprocessing included the removal of artifacts in 4-second epochs using an automated algorithm for electromyographic-twitches and manual removal of eye-movement and pulse artifacts. Scoring followed standard procedures[95](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR95 "Kales, A., Rechtschaffen, A. & University of California, L. A. B. I. S. NINDB Neurological Information Network. A manual of standardized terminology, techniques and scoring system for sleep stages of human subjects. Allan Rechtschaffen and Anthony Kales, editors. Bethesda, Md: U. S. National Institute of Neurological Diseases and Blindness, Neurological Information Network (1968).").

### Statistical analysis

Hypotheses were tested using general linear models with bootstrapped confidence intervals implemented in R (v.4.5.2)[96](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR96 "R Core Team. The R Project for Statistical Computing. R: A Language and Environment for Statistical Computing. (2023).                    https://www.r-project.org/                                    "). In each model, we examined the interaction between anxiety severity, as captured by our PCA measure, and PPV on the following winsorized (5th and 95th percentiles) dependent measures: SOL, SE, and NREM and REM duration. In each model we included covariates for biological sex, WASI IQ, intracranial volume (ICV) obtained from passing the T1-weighted structural scan through Freesurfer (v.7.1)[97](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39349-y#ref-CR97 "Fischl, B. & FreeSurfer NeuroImage 62(2), 774–781. (2012)."), pubertal development, cyst volume, and differences in PSG system.
