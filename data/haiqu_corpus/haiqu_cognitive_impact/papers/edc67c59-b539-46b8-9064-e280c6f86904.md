**METHODS**

In a single-blinded, randomized, sham-controlled pilot trial (NCT05563298), we evaluated the safety, feasibility, and biological effects of home-based tPBM in individuals aged ≥50 with MCI. Participants received either active (n = 10) or sham (n = 10) treatment using visually identical NIR devices targeting default mode network regions and the olfactory bulb. Active devices emitted pulsed 810 nm light for 20 minutes per session, six days per week for six weeks; sham devices emitted light for only 2 seconds per session. No serious adverse events occurred; three mild-to-moderate events were reported, and adherence exceeded 98%.

**RESULTS**

Active tPBM led to greater improvements in global cognition, as measured by the Mini-Mental State Examination, and in episodic memory, as measured by the delayed recognition test of the California Verbal Learning Test–Second Edition. Blood analyses showed increased serum pyruvate and lactate, a reduced lactate-to-pyruvate ratio, and lower plasma IL-6. Neuroimaging revealed enhanced default mode network connectivity and focal cortical volume and thickness gains.

**DISCUSSION**

These data demonstrate safety, preliminary efficacy, and support future definitive clinical studies.

**Background** Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and related dementias (ADRD) affect over 55 million individuals worldwide, with approximately 10 million new cases annually, posing immense social and economic burdens1. By the time clinical symptoms emerge, extensive synaptic loss and neuronal death have already occurred, limiting the efficacy of existing therapies2. Recently approved anti-amyloid agents, such as lecanemab, provide modest clinical benefit in early AD3, and their use is constrained by high costs, infrastructure requirements (e.g., PET imaging, infusion protocols), and the risk of adverse events such as amyloid-related imaging abnormalities4.

Importantly, up to 45% of dementia cases may be preventable through early intervention 5,6 . This underscores the urgent need for scalable, noninvasive strategies, particularly in high-risk populations such as those with mild cognitive impairment (MCI). The aim is to stabilize or improve cognitive function during this critical window of disease modification.

MCI involves measurable cognitive decline, typically in episodic memory, without major functional impairment7. Approximately 15% of individuals with MCI progress to dementia within two years, and one-third convert to AD within five years7. Mitochondrial dysfunction is increasingly recognized as an early feature of AD8. Cortical neurons are highly energy-dependent, making them vulnerable to impaired oxidative phosphorylation9. Studies in MCI show reduced cytochrome-c oxidase activity, lower ATP production, and elevated reactive oxygen species 10–12,13 , contributing to redox imbalance and accelerating amyloid and tau pathology 14,15 . Nonetheless, most therapies continue to focus on amyloid, tau, or inflammation16, while mitochondria-directed approaches remain scarce—highlighting the need for more effective interventions that address mitochondrial dysfunction.

Transcranial photobiomodulation (tPBM) is a noninvasive neuromodulatory approach that delivers near-infrared (NIR) light (~700-1100 nm) to stimulate cytochrome-c-oxidase (CCO) and enhance mitochondrial respiration in cortical tissue17. NIR light wavelengths align with the CCO absorption peak18, facilitating efficient photon capture. tPBM has been shown to increase ATP production, reduce ROS, and promote nitric oxide release 19–21 . In AD mouse models (e.g., 5×FAD, APP/PS1), repeated tPBM reduces Aβ burden, preserves synaptic integrity, and improves memory performance 22–24 . Preliminary human studies using tPBM in individuals with MCI25 or AD 26,27 , have reported improvements in attention, executive function, memory, regional cerebral blood flow25, and default mode network (DMN) functional connectivity27. However, these studies are limited in number and often lack rigorous design elements such as sham controls and adequate sample sizes 25-29 .

To address these limitations, we conducted a single-blinded, randomized, sham-controlled feasibility trial of tPBM in individuals with MCI (n = 20; [https://www.clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT05563298](https://www.clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT05563298)). **Methods**

## Participants and Study Design

Between March 2023 and December 2024, we enrolled twenty community-dwelling adults aged 60–85 years who met the 2011 NIA–AA clinical criteria for MCI due to Alzheimer’s disease (NCT05563298). This criteria is based on clinical assessment, without biomarker confirmation63.

Candidates were referred from the St. Michael’s Hospital Memory Clinic or responded to community advertisements. A single geriatric psychiatrist screened every participant in the Memory Clinic. All cognitive tests, blood draws, and MRI scans were carried out at St. Michael’s Hospital. Written informed consent was obtained under a protocol approved by the St. Michael’s Hospital Research Ethics Board. Inclusion criteria: a subjective cognitive concern (self, informant, or clinician); MoCA score 19–25 with memory impairment; CDR 0.5 with preserved activities of daily living; and at least eighth-grade education. Exclusion criteria: any contraindication to MRI or venipuncture; current psychiatric or other neurological disease; uncontrolled systemic illness; history of stroke or seizures; scalp photosensitivity; use of major anticoagulants; significant functional impairment (CDR ≥ 1.0); or inability to provide informed consent.

A single research coordinator handled every aspect of the trial—screening, enrollment, randomization, weekly check-in calls, and outcome assessments—and therefore was necessarily unblinded. Participants themselves remained unaware of their assigned condition (active vs. sham), making this a single-blind study.

**Intervention**

Each participant received a portable Class II research-use-only device (Health Canada ITA #347303) equipped with five near-infrared LEDs (810 nm) targeting DMN hub regions: one frontal LED targeting the medial prefrontal cortex (positioned along the midline at the hairline), one posterior LED targeting the posterior cingulate cortex (placed at the crown of the head), and two lateral LEDs targeting the bilateral temporal/angular regions (which aligned naturally with the headset design). An additional intranasal LED, targeting the olfactory bulb, was inserted into the nostril through which the participant reported easier breathing (Figure 6). In the active group, when turned on, the frontal LED delivered 75 mW/cm², each posterior/temporal LED delivered 100 mW/cm², and the intranasal LED delivered 25 mW/cm²; all were pulsed at 40 Hz with a 50 % duty cycle. Heat generation was minimal (< 0.5 °C). The sham device was identical in appearance and emitted NIR light only for the first two seconds of each twenty-minute session, then powered off for the remainder. Both active and sham units produced the same audible beep if dislodged, preserving participant blinding. All devices complied with Class II Health Canada safety requirements.

## Device Training

At baseline, the research coordinator provided a thirty-minute, in-person training session on device placement (scalp landmarks), cleaning, and diary completion.

## Home-Based Treatment and Monitoring

Participants completed 20-minute home sessions six days a week for six weeks (36 sessions total). Adherence and safety were tracked through (1) participant diaries, (2) weekly calls from the study coordinator, and (3) an adverse-event log.

During each call, the coordinator: a) verified the number of sessions completed, b) resolved any technical problems, and 3) documented side effects or safety concerns.

Compliance was defined a priori as ≥30 of 36 sessions (≈ 83 %).

## Cognitive Function Assessments (n = 10 active, n = 10 sham)

Cognitive assessments were conducted at baseline and following the six-week intervention (week 7) by the same coordinator, who was trained in psychometric testing. In a fixed order to minimize variability, participants completed the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) 66 was used to assess global cognition, with total scores serving as the primary outcome measures. The Trail Making Test (TMT) 67 Parts A and B recorded completion times in seconds as primary outcomes, reflecting processing speed (Part A) and executive set-shifting ability (Part B). TMT B/A ratio was also calculated to account for individual differences in processing speed and more specifically isolate executive control. Verbal learning and memory were assessed using the California Verbal Learning Test–Second Edition (CVLT-II) 68 , administered using the Standard Form at baseline and the Alternate Form at week 7 to minimize practice effects. Primary outcomes for the CVLT-II included total recall across the five learning trials (Trials 1–5), twenty-minute delayed free recall, and long-delay recognition hits, capturing verbal learning, long-term memory, and recognition memory, respectively. The CVLT-II was selected for its sensitivity to episodic memory decline in MCI 69 (Kramer et al., 2020). We used the standard/alternate form pairing, which offers acceptable reliability (r = 0.61–0.73) and minimizes practice effects (Cohen’s d = –0.01 to 0.18), making it suitable for longitudinal designs 70 . Two participants in the active group did not complete the post-intervention CVLT-II assessment due to time constraints and reported stress associated with the task.

## Secondary Outcomes

Sleep quality was assessed using the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) 71 , with the global PSQI score as the primary outcome, where higher scores indicate poorer sleep quality.

Neuropsychiatric symptoms were evaluated using the Mild Behavioral Impairment Checklist (MBI-C) 72 , with the total MBI-C severity score as the primary measure, capturing the overall burden of behavioral symptoms.

## rs-fMRI Processing (n = 9 active, n = 10 sham)

Resting-state fMRI data were processed using a hybrid pipeline that incorporated ANTs ( [http://stnava.github.io/ANTs](http://stnava.github.io/ANTs)), AFNI ( [https://afni.nimh.nih.gov](https://afni.nimh.nih.gov/)), FSL ( [https://fsl.fmrib.ox.ac.uk/fsl](https://fsl.fmrib.ox.ac.uk/fsl)), and custom in-house scripts. Full details of the acquisition and preprocessing procedure have been published previously83. Anatomical T1-weighted images were first skull-stripped, segmented, and non-linearly registered to the MNI152 template. First, volumes with framewise displacement > 0.5 mm were identified and removed, followed by slice-timing correction and motion correction. Functional images were then non-linearly normalized to MNI152 space and resampled to 3 mm isotropic resolution. Spatial smoothing was applied using a 6 mm full-width at half-maximum (FWHM) Gaussian kernel. Subsequent preprocessing steps included linear detrending, regression of six motion parameters, and removal of nuisance signals derived from white matter (WM) and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) time series. We applied the Schaefer 200-parcel cortical atlas (Yeo’s seven networks) 84 plus the Tian 16-region subcortical atlas (eighth network) 85 to extract mean time series from 216 ROIs. ## Statistical Analysis

Statistical analyses were performed in R v4.2.2. For each outcome, change scores (Δ = week 7 − baseline) were tested for normality using the Shapiro–Wilk test (α = 0.05). Primary analyses focused on the between-group differences in change scores. Within-group, pre–post comparisons are reported in the Supplementary Information for descriptive purposes only. If both groups met normality, independent two-sample t-tests were used (with Welch’s correction if variances were unequal), and effect sizes were expressed as Cohen’s d. If normality was violated in either group, Mann–Whitney U tests were used with rank-biserial correlation (r = Z/√N) as the effect size. This analytic strategy was applied uniformly across cognitive, imaging, and biomarker outcomes. No correction for multiple comparisons was applied, given the hypothesis-generating nature and limited sample size of this pilot study. A priori hypotheses focused on core mitochondrial and network-level endpoints (e.g., lactate, pyruvate, lactate/pyruvate ratio, DMN connectivity), with other comparisons considered exploratory. Missing data were handled via pairwise deletion.

**Discussion**

This study is the first randomized, sham-controlled trial to evaluate the effects of transcranial photobiomodulation (tPBM) in individuals with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using a multimodal framework that integrates cognitive, blood-based metabolic —which cross the blood–brain barrier or tightly mirror central levels— and neuroimaging biomarkers. tPBM was associated with greater gains in global cognition (MMSE) and episodic memory (CVLT-II delayed recognition) compared to the sham group, along with peripheral shifts in mitochondrial-related metabolites, including increased serum pyruvate and lactate, and a reduced lactate-to-pyruvate ratio. Additionally, functional connectivity (FC) within the default mode medRxiv preprint doi: [https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.08.19.25333989](https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.08.19.25333989); this version posted August 24, 2025. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted medRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

network (DMN) increased in the active group, suggesting enhanced network-level brain function.

The primary outcome was improvement in cognitive function. Global cognition improved significantly on the MMSE in the active group, inline with other studies30. The mean gain exceeded the 1- to 3-point threshold which is considered clinically meaningful31. Episodic memory also improved on the CVLT-II long-delay recognition task, showing more hits and fewer false positives. This is an especially meaningful finding given that recognition memory deficits emerge early in the AD trajectory (e.g., MCI) 32,33 .

Blood-based metabolic markers pointed to a parallel bioenergetic shift in the active tPBM group vs. sham. Serum pyruvate increased more than lactate leading to a reduced lactate-to-pyruvate (L/P) ratio, signalling a move toward oxidative phosphorylation through the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle34 and increased mitochondrial engagement34. Given that MCI is often characterised by elevated lactate35, normalisation of the L/P ratio suggests partial restoration of cerebral energy metabolism. As both metabolites cross the blood–brain barrier via monocarboxylate transporters36, their serum levels serve as accessible proxies of brain metabolic status.

Neurometabolic imaging with proton magnetic resonance spectroscopy (¹H-MRS) revealed a trend toward increased N-acetylaspartate to creatine ratio (NAA/Cr) in the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC). PCC is a key DMN hub and early site of degeneration in AD 28,29 . Although not statistically significant, this trend may reflect enhanced neuronal viability or mitochondrial support37.

FC within the DMN, measured by resting-state fMRI, increased in the active tPBM group, suggesting that tPBM may enhance communication between brain regions critical for memory, attention, and self-referential processing. The DMN was selected a priori due to its central role in memory and early Alzheimer’s pathology38, and its spatial overlap with tPBM stimulation sites. This finding is consistent with prior studies reporting tPBM-induced increases in DMN connectivity in MCI27.

The absence of significant change in brain blood flow, as measured by pseudo-continuous arterial spin labeling (PCASL), aligns with previous null findings in healthy adults39. Our exploratory endpoints revealed several systemic and brain-level effects of tPBM. Metabolically, active tPBM modulated serum markers linked to nitrogen and one-carbon metabolism. Elevated L-alanine may reflect increased pyruvate transamination41, while higher L-carnitine suggests enhanced mitochondrial import of fatty acids, favoring β-oxidation42. Reductions in urea and sarcosine point to decreased amino acid catabolism43 with possible effects on NMDA receptor signaling44. Lower glycerol levels may indicate reduced lipolysis or decreased reliance on peripheral lipid stores45. Together, these serum metabolite shifts likely reflect improved brain energy metabolism. Alongside increased pyruvate and lactate and a reduced lactate-to-pyruvate ratio, the pattern suggests that tPBM promotes aerobic ATP production, efficient substrate handling, and metabolic homeostasis.

Active tPBM also lowered plasma IL-6– a cytokine implicated in systemic inflammation and AD progression46. This aligns with in vitro evidence showing PBM-mediated suppression of IL-6 and IL-8 at higher doses, and with clinical trials reporting PBM-induced reductions in IL-6, IL-8, and TNF-α in patients with COVID-19 47 . These findings support the hypothesis that tPBM may attenuate inflammation via mitochondrial mechanisms48.

Exploratory analysis of rs-fMRI data, showed increased FC between the caudate nucleus and DMN, and decreased FC within the limbic network. The former may reflect enhanced network integration related to memory and cognitive control 49,50 , while the latter may signal reduced emotional reactivity—a feature often elevated in early AD51.

Structural MRI showed greater volume increases in the active tPBM vs sham in the left nucleus accumbens and right thalamus— subcortical regions involved in motivation, attention, and executive function that undergo early atrophy in AD 52,53 . These changes parallel findings from other neuromodulatory interventions, such as cognitive training54 and repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) 55 . In addition, modest increases in cortical thickness were also observed in the right frontal pole and right entorhinal cortex—regions implicated in working memory56 and episodic memory57, and among the earliest affected in AD57. These region-specific effects may reflect subtle structural plasticity, warranting further investigation.

A unique feature of our protocol was intranasal LED stimulation targeting the olfactory bulb—an early site of AD pathology 58,59 . Though speculative, this route may influence subcortical and limbic circuits via olfactory projections60, potentially amplifying downstream neuromodulatory effects. Notably, the olfactory bulb, entorhinal cortex, frontal pole, nucleus accumbens, and thalamus form a functionally interconnected network involved in memory, motivation, and executive function61— domains impacted early in AD62. The structural and functional changes observed in these regions may reflect convergent tPBM effects on this distributed neurocircuitry.

## Mechanistic synthesis of findings

In summary, the current study provides preliminary evidence that tPBM may modulate cognitive function and physiological processes relevant to the early stages of neurodegenerative disease. Our findings suggest that tPBM enhances mitochondrial bioenergetics, as indicated by increased serum pyruvate and lactate levels, a reduced lactate-to-pyruvate ratio, elevated L-carnitine and L-alanine, and decreased concentrations of urea, glycerol, and sarcosine. These shifts are consistent with more efficient oxidative metabolism and reduced reliance on amino acid and lipid catabolism. A concurrent reduction in plasma IL-6 further suggests potential anti-inflammatory effects. In parallel, increased functional connectivity within the DMN points to enhanced stability in memory-related brain circuits. Additionally, we speculate that the intranasal LED component may contribute to these effects by reaching deep brain structures through the olfactory bulb, which is uniquely accessible via the nasal cavity due to the absence of intervening bone. This pathway may allow light to engage limbic and subcortical regions more directly, complementing transcranial stimulation. Taken together, these results support a model in which tPBM promotes cognitive improvement by modulating mitochondrial and immunometabolic pathways that influence large-scale brain networks.

## Strength & Limitations

This study is the first randomized, sham-controlled trials to evaluate tPBM in individuals with MCI, integrating cognitive assessments with blood-based and multimodal neuroimaging biomarkers. The use of a comprehensive metabolomics and inflammatory panel allowed for a multidimensional evaluation of mitochondrial and systemic effects, with pre-specified targets providing mechanistic insight. evaluation of tPBM’s biological effects. Finally, high adherence rates and the use of visually indistinguishable sham devices further support the feasibility, scalability, and internal validity of home-based neuromodulation in this population.

Limitations include the small sample size (n = 10 per group), which limited power and precluded correction for multiple comparisons. The single-blind design and involvement of a single coordinator may have introduced bias. No PET or CSF biomarkers were included to confirm AD pathology. Although the design captured sustained pre–post effects, it did not assess treatment durability over time post study. Findings should be considered preliminary and hypothesis-generating.

**Conclusion**

This feasibility trial demonstrates that home-based tPBM is safe, well-tolerated, and shows preliminary efficacy across cognitive and biomarker outcomes in individuals with MCI. These data support the plausibility of a noninvasive, mitochondrial-targeted intervention to modulate brain structure, connectivity, and systemic metabolism. As anti-amyloid agents like lecanemab enter clinical use, therapies such as tPBM may offer additive benefit by addressing non-amyloid targets. In sum, tPBM may represent a promising candidate for noninvasive, mechanism-based intervention in early AD. Larger, double-blind, multicenter trials with longitudinal cognitive endpoints are warranted to confirm therapeutic potential.
