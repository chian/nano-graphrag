ABSTRACT

Background: Cognitive impairment is among the most frequently reported and disabling
persistent symptoms following SARS-CoV-2 infection. Despite growing evidence of its
prevalence, the characterization of domain-specific cognitive profiles and the identification of
modifiable factors influencing cognitive outcomes in post-COVID-19 condition (PCC)
remains scarce. Digital cognitive assessment tools have emerged as scalable and examinerindependent alternatives to traditional neuropsychological testing, but their validation in postinfectious contexts is still limited.

Objective: The DigiCog study aims to characterize cognitive profiles associated with PCC,
to evaluate a novel digital cognitive assessment technology (ViewMind, VMTech) against
standardized neuropsychological measures, and to explore the potential role of cognitive
reserve (CR) proxies in shaping cognitive outcomes following SARS-CoV-2 infection.

Methods: DigiCog is a single-center cross-sectional study conducted within the
Luxembourgish Predi-COVID cohort (NCT04380987). Participants aged 25–65 years, with
and without persistent COVID-19 symptoms, are invited to participate. Cognitive
assessments are performed during face-to-face sessions by trained nurses and
neuropsychologists using both the VMTech device and standardized neuropsychological
tests. Additional data on PCC symptom status, CR, sociodemographic characteristics,
fatigue, and psychological factors are also collected. Agreement between digital and
standard cognitive assessments will be evaluated using Cohen’s kappa coefficient, with
sensitivity, specificity, and receiver operating characteristic analyses as secondary
measures. Cognitive performance will be compared between participants with and without
PCC, and associations with CR proxies will be explored.

Conclusions: The DigiCog study will provide new insights into cognitive functioning in PCC
and evaluate the validity of a novel digital cognitive assessment approach. By integrating
neuropsychological testing, digital assessment, and cognitive reserve, the study may inform
scalable and more personalized approaches to cognitive screening and monitoring in postinfectious conditions.

INTRODUCTION

The prevalence of Post-COVID-19 Condition (PCC), also referred to as Long COVID,
represents a substantial and ongoing public health challenge. A recent meta-analysis of
worldwide data estimated that approximately 45% of COVID-19 survivors report at least one
1
persistent symptom four months after infection, regardless of hospitalization status . The
World Health Organization (WHO) defines PCC as symptoms that occur or persist at least
2
three months after acute SARS-CoV-2 infection . However, increasing evidence indicates
3–7
that symptoms may persist for one year or longer , imposing substantial medical,
psychosocial and economic burden worldwide. Similarly, a large international study shows
that 65.2% of patients remain with at least one persistent symptom after six months, with
8
80% of those reporting fatigue and 58.4% reporting cognitive issues .

Neuropsychological investigations confirm that cognitive alterations represent a significant
component of PCC. One year after infection, approximately 18% of individuals exhibit
7
cognitive impairment, while 33% report lingering cognitive complaints . Reported
impairments include difficulties in attention, memory, processing speed, verbal fluency, and
9,10
learning . Longitudinal findings suggest that cognitive changes may persist for up to 18
7,11 11–12
months in some individuals . Available evidence indicates that cognitive outcomes
following SARS-CoV-2 infection are heterogeneous, with some individuals showing
improvement and others exhibiting persistent deficits, while overall recovery patterns remain
incompletely understood.

In parallel, research in aging and neurodegenerative disorders has shown that interindividual
variability in cognitive outcomes may partly reflect differences in Cognitive Reserve (CR), the
brain's capacity to cope with pathological changes, shaped by lifetime experiences such as
15,16
education, occupational complexity, and engagement in stimulating activities . Higher CR
has been associated with less severe or delayed cognitive manifestations in conditions such

* * *

17,18 19,20 21,22
as Alzheimer's disease , Parkinson's disease , traumatic brain injury , and multiple
23–25
sclerosis . Recent evidence suggests that CR may also moderate PCC cognitive deficits,
26
with less severe impairments observed in individuals with higher reserve . However, the
specific CR proxies that may account for variability in PCC cognitive outcomes remain
largely unexplored.

Digital cognitive assessment tools have emerged as complementary approaches to
traditional neuropsychological testing. Evidence suggests that computerized cognitive tests
can perform comparably to conventional pen and paper assessments, and in some
27–29
contexts may enhance sensitivity to subtle changes . Eye-tracking-based paradigms, in
particular, capture oculomotor behavior that is closely linked to attentional control, working
30–31
memory, and executive processes . Although such approaches have been investigated
32,33
in neurodegenerative conditions , their validation in post-infectious cognitive syndromes
remains limited. Improving the accuracy and scalability of cognitive assessment, particularly
in contexts where individuals are not yet engaged in specialized neurological care,
represents an important methodological and public health challenge.

In this context, the DigiCog study was developed within the well-characterized longitudinal
34,35
Predi-COVID cohort to investigate cognitive functioning in adults following SARS-CoV-2
infection. The study combines standardized neuropsychological assessment with a digital
eye-tracking-based cognitive assessment device developed by ViewMind (hereafter referred
to as VMTech) within a cross-sectional evaluation framework and includes the examination
of potentially modifiable neuroprotective behaviors relevant to population brain health.
Specifically, the key objectives of this study are to: (1) characterize cognitive performance
profiles in individuals with and without persistent PCC symptoms; (2) evaluate the level of
agreement between digital cognitive measures and standard neuropsychological outcomes;
and (3) explore associations between CR indicators (including multilingualism, education,
occupational history, and leisure activities) and cognitive performance.

METHODS

Project Overview

The prospective Predi-COVID cohort study was launched in 2020 during the pandemic and
targeted individuals in Luxembourg with laboratory-confirmed COVID-19. It aimed to
characterize deep phenotypes of participants, during the acute phase of infection.

* * *

Participants were subsequently followed remotely for at least 1 year to document the
34
evolution of their health status and persistent symptoms .

Building on this infrastructure, the DigiCog project was later established to specifically
investigate the neurocognitive dimension of PCC. DigiCog includes exclusively participants
from the Predi-COVID cohort, both with and without PCC, enabling cross-sectional
neurocognitive assessments in the context of well-characterized longitudinal symptom data.
This protocol integrates an observational study with a medical device validation component.

Validation Strategy

The validation component of this project involves the evaluation of a novel digital device
compared with established gold-standard assessment instruments (see the Study
Parameters and Measurements section). Each participant completes both assessments in
a predefined sequence, allowing within-subject comparisons in which participants serve
as their own controls. This design reduces interindividual variability and enhances
statistical power by enabling direct comparison of outcomes obtained under different
assessment conditions within the same participant.

The device was developed by ViewMind (ViewMind Sàrl, Luxembourg), whose proprietary
modeling processes and algorithms constitute protected intellectual property and
therefore cannot be disclosed in this protocol. To ensure neutrality and objectivity in the
validation study, the Luxembourg Institute of Health (LIH) research team conducting the
DigiCog study does not have access to the underlying algorithms or processing methods
used to generate the digital outcomes. Results are provided by ViewMind in the same
standardized format that would be used in a clinical implementation setting.

Study Population

The study population will be composed of adults (aged 25-65 years) who are nonhospitalized and living in Luxembourg. Exclusion criteria include traumatic brain injury,
stroke, epilepsy, and chronic fatigue syndrome/myalgic encephalomyelitis, particularly if
these occurred over the past seven years, that is, five years prior to the Predi-COVID study.
Participants must also be able to complete the tests independently, visual impairments such
as double vision and color blindness are checked as part of the eligibility criteria.

Participants and Recruitment

The PCC symptomatology reported by participants during the Predi-COVID cohort followup is used to guide a preliminary group allocation for the DigiCog study. Potential control
participants are defined as individuals who remained asymptomatic throughout the Predi-
COVID follow-up (i.e., asymptomatic during the acute phase and at 12-, 15-, and 24-
months), or who reported no symptoms at their most recent Predi-COVID assessment
(i.e., the assessment closest to the time of DigiCog enrollment).

This preliminary classification is intended to achieve an approximately balanced
distribution between groups. Final group allocation will be determined based on
predefined classification criteria applied after completion of data collection.

Setting

The study is primarily conducted at the Luxembourg Clinical and Translational Research
Centre (LCTR), a clinical site partially managed by the LIH. Assessments at the LCTR
take place in quiet, controlled rooms to minimize external disturbances. If individuals
condition their participation on home-based assessments, measurements will be
conducted at their residence, as long as it meets LCTR’s quality standards.

Contextual and Psychosocial Factors

Sociodemographics

The collected sociodemographic variables include age, sex, education level, occupational
status, country of birth, marital status, family structure, and type of residence. Education is
documented both as years of formal schooling and highest degree obtained. Occupational
status captures both current and previous professional engagement.

CR is assessed using multiple complementary indicators as proxies. These include
educational attainment, occupational history, and engagement in cognitively stimulating
leisure and social activities. Questions address the nature of the activity as well as its
frequency. Particular attention is given, for example, to the number of languages learned,
36
currently practiced, and frequency of use. Participants report activity engagement both
retrospectively (prior to the COVID-19 pandemic) and at the time of assessment. The
37
validated Cognitive Reserve Index Questionnaire (CRIq) is additionally administered to
provide a standardized composite measure. The CRIq quantifies educational attainment,
occupational complexity, and leisure activities according to established scoring procedures.

Mental Health

Symptoms of anxiety and depression are assessed using the Beck Anxiety Inventory (BAI)
38,39 40
and the Beck Depression Inventory (BDI-II) , two validated self-report instruments.
Subjective cognitive concerns are measured using the Questionnaire of Cognitive
41 42
Complaints (QPC) and the Cognitive Difficulty Scale (CDS) . These instruments provide
dimensional measures of emotional distress and perceived cognitive functioning at the time
of assessment.

Fatigue

Fatigue severity is assessed using the 13-item Functional Assessment of Chronic Illness
43,44
Therapy-Fatigue (FACIT-Fatigue) scale. This instrument evaluates fatigue experienced
during the seven days preceding assessment. Items are rated on a four-point Likert scale
ranging from 0 (“very much”) to 4 (“not at all”). Total scores are calculated according to
standardized scoring procedures, with lower scores indicating greater fatigue severity.

PCC Symptom Status

An updated symptom questionnaire is completed by participants to document the current
PCC. The questionnaire assesses symptoms experienced since the initial COVID-19
diagnosis. For each item, participants indicate whether they currently experience the
symptom, experienced it previously but no longer do, or have never experienced it. The
questionnaire includes general questions about overall health and common symptoms (eg,
fatigue, fever, pain), followed by items covering specific symptom domains, including earnose-throat (ENT), neurological and ocular, cardiorespiratory, gastrointestinal, vascular and
lymphatic, urinary, and dermatological symptoms. PCC status will be defined according to
2
the WHO criteria : participants reporting at least one ongoing symptom not attributable to an
alternative medical diagnosis will be classified as having PCC.

The assessment procedure is standardized to ensure consistency across participants (Table
1). The interval between immediate and delayed recall tasks is fixed at approximately 10
minutes. During this interval, participants complete the Stroop task and the SDMT, thereby
maintaining a controlled delay period while minimizing interference with memory processes.
The Stroop Color and Word Test is administered following the standardized procedure
outlined by Golden (1978), using fixed 45-second trials for each of the three conditions
51, 52
(word, color, and color-word interference ).

In Luxembourg’s multilingual context, all neuropsychological tests are administered either in
French or German according to participants’ language preference to ensure valid cognitive
assessment. The language of administration is recorded and considered in the analyses
when appropriate.

Digital cognitive performance is assessed using the VMTech headset, a digital medical
60
device equipped with integrated eye-tracking technology . Participants are seated during
testing while stimuli are presented along the central visual axis of the display. Eye
movements are recorded at a sampling rate of 120 Hz. Recordings are binocular by default;
in cases of unilateral visual impairment, a monocular recording mode is used. Prior to task
initiation, gaze calibration is performed using a standardized 9-point calibration procedure to

After a brief practice session to ensure task comprehension, participants complete three
eye-tracking–based cognitive tasks: (1) a Go/No-Go task, (2) a spatial N-Back task, and (3)
a Visual Short-Term Memory Binding task (VSTMB). These paradigms assess executive
control, working memory, sustained attention, processing speed, visual scanning, and shortterm memory binding. Eye-movement-based performance metrics are automatically
recorded by the VMTech system ( [https://www.viewmind.ai/](https://www.viewmind.ai/); see Data Management section).

The Go/No-Go task assesses sustained attention, inhibitory control, and processing speed

. Participants respond to directional stimuli while inhibiting responses in incongruent
conditions. Outcome measures include trial duration and gaze-based parameters such as

Data Collection and Management Analysis

Data Protection

All data are processed in accordance with applicable national and European data protection
regulations, including the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Participants are
assigned a unique study identifier, and no directly identifiable information is included in the
analytical database. Distinct identifiers are used for the Predi-COVID and DigiCog studies.
Access to identifiable information and to the correspondence between study identifiers is
restricted to authorized personnel. All staff involved in data handling are trained in Good
Clinical Practice (GCP) and data security procedures.

Data storage

Data from the standard neuropsychological assessment and the FACIT questionnaire (Table

1. are initially recorded in paper case report forms (CRFs) and stored in locked cabinets
   when not in use. These data are subsequently entered into an electronic CRF (eCRF) using
   Ennov Clinical software. Quality control procedures are implemented to identify potential
   entry errors or inconsistencies, and the database includes an audit trail documenting all
   modifications. Electronic data are stored on secure institutional servers with restricted
   access and regular backups.

The DigiCog study leverages participants enrolled in the Predi-COVID cohort and classifies
them into 2 groups, those without PCC and those with symptoms compatible with PCC. The
initial planning, based on anticipated recruitment and dropout rates in Predi-COVID,
suggested that a total sample of approximately 300 participants would be feasible. However,
slower than expected enrollment made it clear that a total sample larger than 200
participants with more than 12 month data completed would not be realistic within the
project timeframe. A revised power evaluation was therefore conducted under the constraint

Sample Size and Power of a maximum total sample size of 200 participants.

Sample size estimation for the agreement between digital and standard cognitive
68
assessments is based on the method proposed by Hong et al. using Cohen’s kappa.
Assuming a marginal prevalence of cognitive impairment of approximately 19% (derived
69
from previous population-based data in Luxembourg ), and a null hypothesis of 70%
agreement between the 2 methods, we considered as clinically relevant an alternative
hypothesis of 80% agreement (difference of 0.10 in agreement proportions). Under these
assumptions, evaluating 100 participants with both methods provides 80% power to detect a
kappa of about 0.71 at a two sided α of 0.05. A total sample of up to 200 participants
therefore ensures sufficient precision for the validation of the digital tool against the
pen and paper reference.

For group comparisons of cognitive impairment, prevalence estimates from a previous
Luxembourg-based study were used, in which 19.13% of older adults had cognitive
36
impairment without dementia . Given the younger age distribution in Predi-COVID, we
conservatively assume a prevalence of cognitive impairment of at most 10% in the
non PCC group. Under the constraint of a maximum total sample size of 200 participants,
power calculations (continuity corrected χ² test, two sided α = .05) indicate that with
approximately 100 participants per group the study will have ≥80% power to detect a
prevalence of cognitive impairment of about 26–28% or higher in the PCC group compared
with 10% in the non PCC group. Smaller between group differences will be associated with
lower statistical power.

Cognitive profiling between PCC and non-PCC participants

Characterization of cognitive performance profiles in individuals with and without PCC will be
based on these neuropsychological scores. Group differences will be examined using
analysis of covariance (ANCOVA) models controlling for age and educational level. Effect
sizes will be reported to facilitate interpretation of the magnitude of observed differences.

Validation study: digital tool versus pen and paper reference

Analyses exploring CR will assess how cognitive outcomes relate to proxies of CR, adjusting
for acute phase symptom severity, current symptom burden, fatigue severity, and
psychological distress. Correlations and multivariable regression models will be constructed
to examine the independent contribution of these variables to cognitive performance.
Interaction effects will be explored where theoretically justified.

All statistical analyses will be conducted using R (latest stable version). Additional software
may be used where appropriate. Linear mixed-effects models will be estimated using the
lme4 package in R. Statistical significance will be set at α = .05 (two-tailed), and corrections
for multiple comparisons will be applied where appropriate.

To ensure methodological independence, all statistical analyses will be performed
exclusively by the LIH research team according to pre-specified analysis plans, and eyetracking technology-derived outputs provided after data extraction.

RESULTS

Participant recruitment for the DigiCog study began in May 2023. As of March 2025, a total
of 199 participants from the Predi-COVID cohort had been enrolled, including individuals
with and without PCC. Following completion of data cleaning, quality control procedures, and
integration of the different data sources, the final study database was locked in December
2025\. Statistical analyses based on the prespecified analysis plan are currently ongoing, and
outcome data are not available for presentation at this stage. Results are expected to be
reported in late 2026.

DISCUSSION

Growing evidence indicates that cognitive difficulties are among the most frequently reported
9,10
neurological manifestations of PCC . However, the pattern of cognitive alterations
11,12
appears heterogeneous , with domain-specific deficits more commonly reported than
uniform global impairment. By retaining condition-specific neuropsychological subtest scores
rather than relying exclusively on global cognitive indices, the DigiCog study seeks to
improve sensitivity to subtle cognitive alterations that may characterize PCC. This approach
could help refine the characterization of post-infectious cognitive profiles and contribute to
the broader understanding of neurological manifestations associated with SARS-CoV-2
infection. This approach may also have implications for the development of targeted
cognitive rehabilitation and stimulation strategies.

Beyond the descriptive characterization of cognitive functioning, the study also evaluates the
performance of a digital eye-tracking–based cognitive assessment technology. If adequate
validity is demonstrated in comparison with standardized neuropsychological measures,
such tools may offer several practical advantages. Digital assessments may allow medRxiv preprint doi: [https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.14.26350862](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.14.26350862); this version posted April 16, 2026. The copyright holder for this preprint

automated data acquisition, reduce examiner-related variability, and facilitate scalable
27–29
cognitive screening in clinical and research settings . In particular, validated digital tools
28
could support early identification of cognitive difficulties in primary care contexts , where
access to comprehensive neuropsychological evaluation is often limited. Such approaches
could therefore complement traditional assessments by providing accessible first-line
screening or monitoring tools, particularly in conditions such as PCC, where cognitive
complaints are common. In a multilingual country, at the heart of Europe such as
Luxembourg, a robust cognitive assessment tool independent of the language used would
be particularly valuable.

Another objective of the DigiCog study is to explore the potential role of CR in post-infectious
cognitive outcomes. CR reflects the capacity of the brain to cope with pathological processes
through pre-existing cognitive resources shaped by lifelong experiences such as education,
occupational complexity, multilingualism, and engagement in cognitively stimulating activities
15,16
. In the context of PCC, identifying factors associated with greater resilience to cognitive
26
impairment may help explain interindividual variability in cognitive outcomes and could
inform future prevention or rehabilitation strategies targeting modifiable lifestyle factors.

Several limitations should also be acknowledged. The final sample size is smaller than
initially anticipated in the protocol power calculations. As a result, the study is primarily
powered to detect moderate to large effects, and smaller effects may remain undetected. In
addition, cognitive performance is assessed at a single post-acute time point, which limits
the ability to draw conclusions regarding the temporal evolution of cognitive functioning
following SARS-CoV-2 infection. Finally, the study is conducted in Luxembourg, a
multilingual context that may introduce variability in cognitive test performance. To minimize
this risk, assessments are administered in either French or German according to participants’ language preference, and test administration procedures were harmonized
across languages to ensure procedural equivalence.

CONCLUSION

The DigiCog study is expected to provide new insights into cognitive functioning in PCC and
to evaluate a novel eye-tracking–based digital cognitive assessment approach. By
integrating neuropsychological testing, digital cognitive assessment, and the investigation of
CR, the study may contribute to the understanding of post-infectious cognitive
manifestations. In the context of emerging infectious diseases and their long-term
neurological consequences, scalable cognitive assessment tools may play an important role
in future clinical monitoring strategies.
