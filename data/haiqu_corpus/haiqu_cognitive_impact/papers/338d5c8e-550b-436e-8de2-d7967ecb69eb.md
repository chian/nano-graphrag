Efficacy of heat-treated _Lacticaseibacillus paracasei_ PS23 for individuals with long coronavirus disease-19 syndrome: a double-blinded randomized control pilot study


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3.pdf)

## Abstract

Long coronavirus disease (long COVID) refers to symptoms that persist beyond the acute phase of SARS-CoV-2 infection. This study applied the World Health Organization (WHO) definition, which identifies post-COVID-19 condition as symptoms lasting at least two months and beginning around three months after confirmed infection. Given limited evidence on probiotic use for long COVID, we conducted a double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial to evaluate the effects of heat-treated _Lacticaseibacillus paracasei_ PS23 (HT-PS23) in individuals with confirmed COVID-19 and self-reported prolonged symptoms. Thirty-nine eligible participants were randomly assigned to receive either the heat-treated _Lacticaseibacillus paracasei_ PS23 (HT-PS23) or placebo for six weeks. Assessments included validated neuropsychological tests (including executive function and working memory using Color Trails 1&2 and Digit Span and Coding), self-reported symptom scales of depression, anxiety, and quality of life, and blood biomarkers related to inflammation, immune response, and stress (e.g. cortisol, IFN-γ zonulin). After the six-week trial, HT-PS23 group showed notable improvements in cortisol level ( _p_ = 0.006), a reduction in errors of CTT2 ( _p_ = 0.018), and symptom severity related to breathing difficulties ( _p_ = 0.016) and appetite loss ( _p_ = 0.030) compared to the placebo group. HT-PS23 may be a feasible and well-tolerated intervention for relieving cognitive or physical symptoms associated with long COVID. Further studies with larger samples are warranted.

Given the overlap between long COVID symptoms and the known effects of HT-PS23, this pilot double blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial was designed to evaluate its efficacy in alleviating prolonged symptoms after the COVID infection. The study also aims to explore underlying biochemical mechanisms related to inflammation and oxidative stress.

## Methods

### Study design and participants

This double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled pilot trial was conducted at National Taiwan Sports University. Participants aged 20 ~ 65 were recruited via public advertisements. Inclusion criteria required a confirmed COVID-19 diagnosis (PCR or antigen test) at least four weeks prior and persistent symptoms lasting at least two months post-infection, consistent with WHO’s definition of long COVID. Exclusion criteria included recent antibiotic or probiotic use, immunodeficiency, allergy to lactic acid bacteria, dementia, psychiatric medication use, or any condition deemed unsuitable by the Principal Investigator. Participants who reported any acute infectious disease (Eg. influenza, gastroenteritis) during the intervention period were also excluded. No such cases were identified during the study. Participants could withdraw voluntarily due to adverse reactions or personal reasons.

### Randomization and intervention

Eligible participants were randomly assigned to receive either heat-treated _Lacticaseibacillus paracasei_ PS23 (HT-PS23) or placebo capsules for six weeks. Randomization was performed using an online generator by an independent researcher. Each HT-PS23 capsule contained 10 billion heat-treated cells; participants took two capsules daily. The selected dosage was based on a previous human clinical trial in which a daily intake of 20 billion HT-PS23 cells demonstrated significant improvements in stress-related symptoms and cortisol levels among nurses under high occupational stress[28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR28 "Wu, S. I. et al. Psychobiotic supplementation of HK-PS23 improves anxiety in highly stressed clinical nurses: a double-blind randomized placebo-controlled study. Food Function. 13, 89078919 (2022)."). Both HT-PS23 and placebo capsules were identical in appearance and contained the same excipients to ensure blinding. The HT-PS23 capsules consisted of 62.5% HT-PS23 powder, 35% microcrystalline cellulose, 1.25% magnesium stearate, and 1.25% silicon dioxide. The placebo capsules contained 97.5% microcrystalline cellulose, 1.25% magnesium stearate, and 1.25% silicon dioxide. All products were provided by Bened Biomedical Ltd. and stored at 4 °C. Adverse events were monitored throughout the study, with no intervention-related events reported.

### Assessments

At baseline (V0) and post-intervention (V1), participants underwent blood sampling and evaluations on neuropsychological functions, physical activities, and gastrointestinal symptoms. Compliance was tracked by capsule return and adverse event reporting.

### Biomarker analysis

Blood samples were collected at consistent times to control for circadian variation. Biomarkers analyzed included (Supplementary Table [S1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#MOESM1)):

- Inflammation and immune markers: IFN-γ, IFN-α2, MCP-1, LBP, hs-CRP, D-dimer, cortisol.

- Gut permeability and viral response: Zonulin, SARS-CoV-2-RBD-IgG.


Serum samples were analyzed to evaluate liver and renal functions, including measurements of glutamic oxaloacetic transaminase (GOT), glutamic pyruvic transaminase (GPT), blood urea nitrogen (BUN), and creatinine (CRE). All assays followed manufacturer protocols.

### Ethics

The study was approved by the Institutional Review Board of Mackay Memorial Hospital (IRB no. 22CT054be) and registered at ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT05813899), in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki.

### Objective neuropsychological assessments

Validated Chinese versions of standardized neuropsychological tests were used to assess cognitive function at baseline (V0) and post-intervention (V1) to detect changes in cognitive performance:

- **Executive function** was assessed using the Color Trails Test (CTT 1 & 2)[29](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR29 "Color Trails Test."), which measures processing speed and cognitive flexibility. Good reliability and validity have been shown in the Chinese-translated version[30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR30 "Kuo, H. Y. A Normative Study of the Color Trails Test in Normal middle-aged and Elderly People in Taiwan (Taipei, 2009)."). We used the CTT1 to evaluate processing speed, and CTT2 to examine changes in cognitive flexibility and executive functioning[31](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR31 "Tyburski, E. et al. Color trails test: a new set of data on cognitive flexibility and processing speed in schizophrenia. Front. Psychiatry. 11, 521 (2020)."), [32](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR32 "Chang, J. G., Ha, E. H., Lee, W. & Lee, S. Y. Cognitive impairments in patients with subacute coronavirus disease: initial experiences in a post-coronavirus disease clinic. Front. Aging Neurosci. 14, 994331 (2022).") before and after the trial.

- **Working memory and processing speed** were evaluated using the Digit Span and Coding subtests from the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale-IV (WAIS-IV). The Chinese version of WAIS-IV has good reliability and validity[33](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR33 "Fan, H. Z. et al. Four-subtest index-based short form of WAIS-IV: psychometric properties and clinical utility. Arch. Clin. Neuropsychol. 34 (1), 81–88 (2019)."), [34](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR34 "Chen, J., Chen, H. Y. & Taipei Wechsler adult intelligence scale - third edition (Chinese). Taiwan.: Chinese Behavioral Science Corp., Taipei, ; 2002. (2002)."). Herein, we assessed participants’ responses to Digit Span and Coding from the WAIS-IV to compare their working memory and processing speed before and after the interventions[32](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR32 "Chang, J. G., Ha, E. H., Lee, W. & Lee, S. Y. Cognitive impairments in patients with subacute coronavirus disease: initial experiences in a post-coronavirus disease clinic. Front. Aging Neurosci. 14, 994331 (2022).").

- **Global clinical status**: The Clinical Global Impression Scale (CGI) is a widely used clinician-rated measure of severity (CGI-S) and improvement (CGI-I)[35](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR35 "Busner, J. & Targum, S. ### Statistical analysis

At an a _priori_ determined α level of 0.05 and a power of 0.80, a total sample size of 24 is required[28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR28 "Wu, S. I. et al. Psychobiotic supplementation of HK-PS23 improves anxiety in highly stressed clinical nurses: a double-blind randomized placebo-controlled study. Food Function. 13, 89078919 (2022)."), [50](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR50 "Sample Size Calculator. ClinCalc:                    https://clincalc.com/stats/samplesize.aspx                                    "). For data not following a normal distribution, the non-parametric analysis of Mann-Whitney U test was applied. The baseline characteristics and subjective or objective neuropsychological measures and changes over time between V0 and V1 and between the HT-PS23 and the placebo groups were compared using the Pearson Chi-Squared and Mann-Whitney U tests for categorical and continuous variables, respectively.

## Results

The study process is illustrated in Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#Fig1). Thirty-nine participants met the inclusion criteria and signed the informed consent forms. Randomization was performed to separate the participants into placebo and HT-PS23 groups. One participant in the placebo group and three in the HT-PS23 group dropped out after being infected with COVID again during the intervention. Two participants in the HT-PS23 group dropped out due to loss of follow-up for personal reasons, and one person dropped out due to taking antibiotics during the intervention period. One participant in the placebo group was not included in the analysis because of low compliance noted (taken < 80% upon trial completion).

Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#Tab1) reveals that the baseline characteristics were not significantly different between the two groups after randomization, except that the HT-PS23 group had significantly more people with more than one COVID infection ( _p_ = 0.018). Sore throat, fatigue, cough, and phlegm were the four most common symptoms, and none of the symptoms during acute COVID-19 infection were significantly different between the groups. No patients were admitted for hospitalization during the COVID episode. The results of the baseline neuropsychological measures (including depression, anxiety, activities, quality of life, and cognitive functions) were also similar in both groups. ## Discussion

This double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled pilot trial is the first to report improvements in cortisol levels, specific physical symptoms, and executive function of long COVID following a 6-week intervention with heat-treated _Lacticaseibacillus paracasei_ PS23 (HT-PS23). Despite baseline differences in COVID history and cognitive performance, the HT-PS23 group showed significant reductions in cortisol, greater improvements in breathing difficulty and appetite, as well as fewer errors on the CTT2, compared to placebo. These findings suggest that HT-PS23 may have unique benefits in relieving long-lasting cognitive or physical symptoms possibly related to COVID infections.

In this study, HT-PS23 administration for six weeks resulted in a significant decrease in the blood cortisol level among participants with long COVID. This finding aligns with previous research showing that HT-PS23 reduced cortisol in individuals under psychological stress[28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR28 "Wu, S. I. et al. Psychobiotic supplementation of HK-PS23 improves anxiety in highly stressed clinical nurses: a double-blind randomized placebo-controlled study. Food Function. 13, 89078919 (2022)."). Cortisol is a key biomarker of stress and hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis activity, which may be dysregulated in long COVID due to chronic inflammation or psychological burden[51](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR51 "Dziurkowska, E. & Wesolowski, M. Cortisol as a biomarker of mental disorder severity. J. Clin. Med. 10(21), 5204 (2021)."). The observed decrease suggests that HT-PS23 may help modulate stress-related physiological responses, potentially through microbiota–gut–brain axis interactions[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR26 "Huang, S. Y. et al. Lactobacillus paracasei PS23 delays progression of age-related cognitive decline in senescence accelerated mouse prone 8 (SAMP8) mice. Nutrients 10(7), 894 (2018)."). Recent evidence further supports the role of heat-killed probiotics, when combined with n-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids, led to notable improvements in behavioral and biological markers of depression in rodent model of chronic mild stress. These effects were attributed to changes in gut microbiota composition and reductions in systemic inflammation, underscoring the influence of the gut-brain axis[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR25 "Cho, H. & Park, Y. Synergistic antidepressant-like effects of biotics and n-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids on dopaminergic pathway through the brain-gut axis in rats exposed to chronic mild stress. Probiotics Antimicrob. Proteins. 17 (4), 1769–1781 (2025)."). Their findings support the broader application of probiotics like HT-PS23 to influence neuroimmune pathways and psychological outcomes, particularly in conditions characterized by chronic stress and inflammation such as long COVID. However, further studies are needed to confirm these effects and clarify the underlying mechanisms.

Greater improvement in self-reported breathing difficulty was noted in the HT-PS23 group than in the placebo group. Although direct evidence linking probiotics to respiratory symptoms in long COVID is limited, literature suggests that probiotics might help influence respiratory health through inhibiting thrombotic destructions on lung permeability at the recovery or repair stage after COVID[52](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR52 "Xiang, M., Jing, H., Wang, C., Novakovic, V. A. & Shi, J. Persistent lung injury and prothrombotic state in long COVID. Front. Immunol. 13, 862522 (2022)."), [53](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR53 "Xiang, M., Wu, X., Jing, H., Novakovic, V. A. & Shi, J. The intersection of obesity and (long) COVID-19: Hypoxia, thrombotic inflammation, and vascular endothelial injury. Front. Cardiovasc. Med. 10, 1062491 (2023)."). Some theories have suggested that interactions between the lung immune system and gut microbiome may affect respiratory conditions[54](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR54 "Enaud, R. et al. The Gut-Lung axis in health and respiratory diseases: A place for Inter-Organ and Inter-Kingdom crosstalks. Front. Cell. Infect. Microbiol. 10, 9 (2020)."). For example, probiotic intake has been associated with a 2-fold decreased risk of respiratory tract infections[55](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR55 "Hao, Q., Dong, B. R. & Wu, T. Probiotics for preventing acute upper respiratory tract infections. Cochrane Database Syst. Rev. 8(8), CD006895 (2022).") and reduced the duration of symptoms, including dyspnea or ear pain. The observed benefit in breathing difficulty may reflect HT-PS23’s potential to modulate immune responses[56](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR56 "Maldonado Galdeano, C., Cazorla, S. I., Lemme Dumit, J. M., Velez, E. & Perdigon, G. Beneficial effects of probiotic consumption on the immune system. Ann. Nutr. Metab. 74 (2), 115–124 (2019)."), [57](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR57 "Clua, P. et al. Peptidoglycan from immunobiotic Lactobacillus rhamnosus improves resistance of infant mice to respiratory syncytial viral infection and secondary Pneumococcal pneumonia. Front. Immunol. 8, 948 (2017).") through microbial competition[58](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR58 "Chong, H. X. et al. Lactobacillus plantarum DR7 improved upper respiratory tract infections via enhancing immune and inflammatory parameters: A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study. J. Dairy Sci. 102 (6), 4783–4797 (2019)."), [59](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR59 "Selle, K. & Klaenhammer, T. R. Genomic and phenotypic evidence for probiotic influences of Lactobacillus gasseri on human health. FEMS Microbiol. Rev. 37 (6), 915–935 (2013).") or alter the functions of the respiratory tract[60](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR60 "Chakraborty, M. & Munshi, S. K. The prospects of employing probiotics in combating COVID-19. Tzu Chi Med. J. 34 (2), 148–159 (2022)."), [61](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR61 "d’Ettorre, G. et al. Challenges in the management of SARS-CoV2 infection: the role of oral bacteriotherapy as complementary therapeutic strategy to avoid the progression of COVID-19. Front. Med. (Lausanne). 7, 389 (2020)."), [62](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR62 "Morrow, L. E., Gogineni, V. & Malesker, M. A. Probiotics in the intensive care unit. Nutr. Clin. Pract. 27 (2), 235–241 (2012)."). It has also been suggested that interactions between probiotics and formations of granulocyte-microphages might strengthen the epithelial barrier to defend against respiratory infections. Gohil et al.[63](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR63 "Gohil, K., Samson, R., Dastager, S. & Dharne, M. Probiotics in the prophylaxis of COVID-19: something is better than nothing. 3 Biotech. 11 (1), 1 (2021).") hypothesized that by expressing ACE2, _L. paracasei_ entering the host cell may become a receptor for the spike protein of SARS-CoV-2 to reduce the influence viral infection in humans. These mechanisms might also explain the effects of probiotics on prolonged respiratory symptoms after COVID.

Improvements in loss of appetite were significantly greater in the HT-PS23 group compared to the placebo group. Loss of appetite is among the most commonly reported nutrition-related complaints during and after COVID hospitalization[64](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR64 "Wierdsma, N. J. et al. Poor nutritional status, risk of sarcopenia and nutrition related complaints are prevalent in COVID-19 patients during and after hospital admission. Clin. Nutr. ESPEN. 43, 369–376 (2021)."), [65](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR65 "Yusuf, F. et al. Global prevalence of prolonged Gastrointestinal symptoms in COVID-19 survivors and potential pathogenesis: A systematic review and meta-analysis. F1000Research 10, 301 (2021)."), yet few studies have explored targeted interventions for this symptom[65](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR65 "Yusuf, F. et al. Global prevalence of prolonged Gastrointestinal symptoms in COVID-19 survivors and potential pathogenesis: A systematic review and meta-analysis. F1000Research 10, 301 (2021)."). Previous research has suggested that probiotics may influence appetite regulation, including a randomized trial showing benefits in patients with major depressive disorder[66](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR66 "Kazemi, A., Noorbala, A. A. & Djafarian, K. Effect of probiotic and prebiotic versus placebo on appetite in patients with major depressive disorder: post hoc analysis of a randomised clinical trial. J. Hum. Nutr. Diet. 33 (1), 56–65 (2020)."). A case report described that a combination of high-fiber formula, Chinese herbal foods, and prebiotics helped alleviate symptoms of decreased appetite, palpitations, and anxiety by modifying the gut flora and microbial fermentation activities in a patient with severe long-lasting post-COVID syndrome[67](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR67 "Wang, Y., Wu, G., Zhao, L. & Wang, W. Nutritional modulation of gut microbiota alleviates severe Gastrointestinal symptoms in a patient with Post-Acute COVID-19 syndrome. mBio 13 (2), e0380121 (2022)."). Notably, HT-PS23 has been shown to increase ghrelin levels- a key appetite-regulating hormone- in animal models[68](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR68 "Cheng, L-H. et al. Lactobacillus paracasei PS23 dietary supplementation alleviates muscle aging via Ghrelin stimulation in d-galactose-induced aging mice. J. Funct. Foods. 85, 104651 (2021)."), [69](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR69 "Sato, T. et al. Structure, regulation and function of Ghrelin. J. Biochem. 151 (2), 119–128 (2012)."), which may partially explain the observed effects, although ghrelin was not measured in this study. The dysbiotic gut microbiota has been linked to long COVID outcomes[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR12 "Zuo, T. et al. Alterations in gut microbiota of patients with COVID-19 during time of hospitalization. Gastroenterology 159 (3), 944–955 (2020). e948."), [21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR21 "Yeoh, Y. K. et al. Gut microbiota composition reflects disease severity and dysfunctional immune responses in patients with COVID-19. Gut 70 (4), 698–706 (2021)."), [67](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR67 "Wang, Y., Wu, G., Zhao, L. & Wang, W. Nutritional modulation of gut microbiota alleviates severe Gastrointestinal symptoms in a patient with Post-Acute COVID-19 syndrome. mBio 13 (2), e0380121 (2022)."), [70](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR70 "Wang, W. et al. Detection of SARS-CoV-2 in different types of clinical specimens. JAMA 323 (18), 1843–1844 (2020)."). Probiotics may help restore microbial balance, enhance intestinal barrier integrity, and regulate metabolic responses[65](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR65 "Yusuf, F. et al. Global prevalence of prolonged Gastrointestinal symptoms in COVID-19 survivors and potential pathogenesis: A systematic review and meta-analysis. F1000Research 10, 301 (2021)."), [71](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR71 "Engevik, M. A. & Versalovic, J. Biochemical features of beneficial microbes: foundations for therapeutic microbiology. Microbiol. Spectr. 5(5),                    https://doi.org/10.1128/microbiolspec.bad-0012-2016                                     (2017)."), [72](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR72 "Parada Venegas, D. et al. Short chain fatty acids (SCFAs)-Mediated gut epithelial and immune regulation and its relevance for inflammatory bowel diseases. Front. Immunol. 10, 277 (2019)."). Given the potential consequences of prolonged appetite loss, including malnutrition and sarcopenia, probiotic supplementation may offer a supportive strategy for managing gastrointestinal symptoms in long COVID, though further research is warranted.

Analysis of cognitive performance showed that participants in the HT-PS23 group made significantly fewer color errors on the CTT2, indicating improved executive function compared to the placebo group. This finding indicates that _L. paracasei_ might improve prolonged cognitive dysfunction related to executive function. Such finding may be in line with previous animal or human studies showing that probiotics can increase brain-derived neurotropic factors (BDNF) and may help prevent cognitive decline[73](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR73 "Corpuz, H. M. et al. Long-Term Diet Supplementation with Lactobacillus paracasei K71 Prevents Age-Related Cognitive Decline in Senescence-Accelerated Mouse Prone 8. Nutrients 10(6). (2018).") or memory impairments[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR26 "Huang, S. Y. et al. Lactobacillus paracasei PS23 delays progression of age-related cognitive decline in senescence accelerated mouse prone 8 (SAMP8) mice. Nutrients 10(7), 894 (2018)."), [74](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR74 "Huang, H. J. et al. Lactobacillus plantarum PS128 prevents cognitive dysfunction in alzheimer’s disease mice by modulating propionic acid levels, glycogen synthase kinase 3 beta activity, and gliosis. BMC Complement. Med. Ther. 21 (1), 259 (2021)."), [75](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR75 "Mohammadi, A. A. et al. The effects of probiotics on mental health and hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis: A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in petrochemical workers. Nutr. Neurosci. 19 (9), 387–395 (2016)."), [76](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR76 "Den, H., Dong, X., Chen, M. & Zou, Z. Efficacy of probiotics on cognition, and biomarkers of inflammation and oxidative stress in adults with alzheimer’s disease or mild cognitive impairment - a meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials. Aging 12 (4), 4010–4039 (2020)."). Animal studies have shown that _L. paracasei_ improves spatial learning and memory, potentially through elevated levels of dopamine, serotonin, and BDNF in the brain[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR26 "Huang, S. Y. et al. Lactobacillus paracasei PS23 delays progression of age-related cognitive decline in senescence accelerated mouse prone 8 (SAMP8) mice. Nutrients 10(7), 894 (2018)."), [77](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR77 "Cheng, L. H. et al. Lactobacillus paracasei PS23 improves cognitive deficits via modulating the hippocampal gene expression and the gut microbiota in D-galactose-induced aging mice. Food Funct. 13 (9), 5240–5251 (2022)."). These mechanisms may help explain the cognitive benefits observed in individuals with long COVID. Although the average improvement in CTT2 color errors was modest, the result suggests that HT-PS23 might contribute to maintaining or enhancing executive function. While no statistically significant changes were observed in blood biomarkers related to inflammation and oxidative stress, the improvements in symptom severity and cognitive function suggest that HT-PS23 may exert its effects through alternative pathways. One possibility is the modulation of the HPA axis, which plays a central role in stress regulation and has been implicated in long COVID-related fatigue and cognitive symptoms. Additionally, psychobiotics may influence neuroendocrine signaling and neurotransmitter synthesis via microbial metabolites such as short-chain fatty acids, which can act locally or systemically to affect brain functions. These mechanisms may operate independently of measurable changes in peripheral inflammatory markers, and warrant further investigation in future studies.

The absence of significant differences in GI symptoms between groups or across time points supports the feasibility and tolerability of HT-PS23 supplementation, consistent with previous findings[28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR28 "Wu, S. I. et al. Psychobiotic supplementation of HK-PS23 improves anxiety in highly stressed clinical nurses: a double-blind randomized placebo-controlled study. Food Function. 13, 89078919 (2022)."). The IPAQ results showed a lower proportion of participants in the HT-PS23 group engaging in health-enhancing physical activity post-intervention, which may reflect lifestyle factors or individual preferences rather than treatment effects. It is also possible that improved cognitive function in this group facilitated engagement in more sedentary, cognitively demanding tasks. In addition, no significant changes were observed in mood, sleep, or quality of life measures, contrasting with previous studies suggesting potential benefits of probiotics or nutritional supplements for fatigue and neurological symptoms in long COVID[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR14 "Stefanou, M. I. et al. Neurological manifestations of long COVID syndrome: a narrative review. Therapeutic Adv. Chronic Disease. 13, 20406223221076890 (2022)."), [16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-27036-3#ref-CR16 "Rathi, A., Jadhav, S. B. & Shah, N. A randomized controlled trial of the efficacy of systemic enzymes and probiotics in the resolution of post-COVID fatigue. Med. (Basel) 8(9), 47 (2021)."). This may be due to the relatively mild symptom burden in our sample, or the short duration of intervention. While probiotics may exert anti-inflammatory or antioxidant effects, these mechanisms may not translate into immediate improvements in subjective well-being. External factors such as stress, life events, or post-pandemic adjustments could also influence mood-related outcomes. Further research is needed to clarify the role of HT-PS23 in managing broader aspects of long COVID.

### Strengths and limitations

This prospective, double-blinded, randomized, placebo-controlled pilot trial was designed to investigate the possible effects of _L. paracasei_ on long COVID symptoms, and to extensively evaluate physical or neuropsychological functions before and after the 6-week probiotic versus the placebo. Participants in this study were a homogenous group of people in their mid-20s, whose COVID infection was not severe enough to require hospitalization. However, the small sample size was a major limitation, and a larger sample size is required to confirm the efficacy of probiotics on prolonged symptoms after COVID. Nevertheless, the current pilot study can still be viewed as an exploration of probiotic acceptability and tolerability among those possibly suffering from long COVID syndrome. Second, while we demonstrated significant differences in changes over time in physical or cognitive functions among young college students, further generalizations on the possible effects of HT-PS23 on long COVID symptoms in different age ranges or other physical conditions may still be needed due to study confinement. Third, although we demonstrated improvements in CTT2 before and after the trial, we could not determine whether they could help those with serious cognitive impairments (z-scores < −1.5, standard deviation compared to the norm). This may be because our participants were primarily young people without significant cognitive impairment at baseline. Fourth, symptoms of long COVID indeed encompass a diverse array of symptoms, reflecting the complex pathophysiology of the condition. The heterogeneity in how long COVID may be coded clinically complicates the standardization of the outcome measures. Most of our participants did not have records on their clinical symptoms. Reasons may be that they perceived their symptoms as mild or manageable and did not seek medical attention; there could be barriers to access healthcare; or there may be lack of awareness and recognitions. We used the main outcomes that were surrogate markers and may not be able to proof their clinical significance. However, these surrogate markers, like difficulty breathing and poor appetite, are directly related to the patient’s daily experience and could have a significant impact on their quality of life. Whenever possible, complement surrogate markers with hard endpoints, such as hospitalization rates or recovery times, to strengthen the argument for clinical significance may be needed in future studies. Finally, we employed a wide range of assessments, including blood biological markers that may be linked to physical or mental health, daily activities, inflammation, or metabolism; however, other uncertain mechanisms and confounders remain, including individual dietary habits or stressors. Future evaluations, such as dietary records or assessment of clinical implications based on changes in microorganism distribution, may reveal the possible causal relationships.

## Conclusions

This pilot randomized controlled trial provides preliminary evidence supporting the feasibility and safety of using the psychobiotic _Lacticaseibacillus paracasei_ PS23 (HT-PS23) to manage selected symptoms associated with long COVID. Participants receiving HT-PS23 showed improvements in cortisol levels, executive function, breathing difficulty, and appetite compared to placebo. While these findings are promising, they should be interpreted with caution due to the small sample size and short intervention period. Further research with larger, more diverse populations and extended follow-up is needed to confirm these effects and explore the underlying biological mechanisms, including inflammation, immunomodulation, and microbiota interactions.
