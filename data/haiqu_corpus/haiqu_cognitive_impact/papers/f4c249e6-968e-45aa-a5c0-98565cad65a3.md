Assessing the mental health implications of carbon trading policies: evidence from urban China


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6.pdf)

## Abstract

Despite robust evidence demonstrating its efficacy in reducing carbon emissions, the impact of carbon emission trading (CET) policies on mental health remains largely unexplored. Based on a difference-in-differences methodology, this study uses the four waves of 2011, 2013, 2015, and 2018 of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS) dataset to ascertain the causal effect of introducing pilot CET schemes on depression among middle-aged and elderly individuals in urban China. The findings reveal a significant increase in the risk of depression following policy implementation, with varying effects observed across demographic groups and regions, especially among older adults and relatively higher socioeconomic status. Furthermore, a mechanistic analysis suggests that immediate word recall, a measure of memory deficits, serves as a mediator in the relationship between policy implementation and depression, while stressful life events exacerbate the policy’s impact. The study concludes with several recommendations aimed at mitigating the unintended mental health consequences associated with the implementation of similar policies.

## Introduction

Depression poses a pervasive challenge among middle-aged and older adults, characterized by its high prevalence, prolonged duration, and propensity for recurrence, thereby significantly impeding health and welfare worldwide (Chisholm et al. [2001](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR7 "Chisholm D, Amir M, Fleck M et al. (2001) Longitudinal Investigation of Depression Outcomes (the LIDO study) in primary care in six countries: Comparative assessment of local health systems and resource utilization. Int J Methods Psychiatr Res 10(2):59–71.                    https://doi.org/10.1002/mpr.101                                    ")). According to the latest Report on National Mental Health Development in China, an alarming 95 million Chinese individuals were grappling with depression in 2022, with approximately 280 thousand suicides occurring annually. The prevalence rates stand at 5.7% and 6.3% for middle-aged and elderly populations, respectively. Notably, well-documented micro-level factors like stressful life events substantially contribute to adult depression, while macro-social pressures also serve as pivotal triggers for mental health issues (Schneiderman et al. [2005](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR58 "Schneiderman N, Ironson G, Siegel SD (2005) Stress and health: psychological, behavioral, and biological determinants. Annu Rev Clin Psychol 1(1):607–628.                    https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.clinpsy.1.102803.144141                                    ")). Given their diminished social adaptability, the middle-aged and elderly demographic emerges as particularly vulnerable to depression, often stemming from the confluence of macro and micro social stressors. Furthermore, individuals coping with common psychological illnesses may encounter memory deficits and executive dysfunction disorders (Alexopoulos et al. [2008](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR1 "Alexopoulos GS, Raue PJ, Kanellopoulos D et al. (2008) Problem solving therapy for the depression-executive dysfunction syndrome of late life. Int J Geriatr Psychiatry 23(8):782–788.                    https://doi.org/10.1002/gps.1988                                    ")), leading to cognitive disarray, impaired judgment, and delayed reactions (Friedrich et al. [2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR16 "Friedrich F, Hinz A, Bjelopavlovic M et al. (2024) Chronic stress in old age: psychometric properties of the Trier Inventory of Chronic Stress (TICS), based on a general population sample aged 60 and over. Arch Gerontol Geriatr 116:105138.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.archger.2023.105138                                    "); Marshall et al. [2015](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR42 "Marshall AC, Cooper NR, Segrave R et al. (2015) The effects of long-term stress exposure on aging cognition: a behavioral and EEG investigation. Neurobiol Aging 36(6):2136–2144.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neurobiolaging.2015.02.026                                    "); Straussner and Calnan, [2014](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR66 "Straussner SLA, Calnan AJ (2014) Trauma through the life cycle: a review of current literature. Clin Soc Work J 42(4):323–335.                    https://doi.org/10.1007/s10615-014-0496-z                                    ")). Hence, prioritizing depression prevention among middle-aged and older adults in China, especially amidst rapid social transitions, emerges as an imperative.

Climate change continues to accelerate at a pace surpassing previous forecasts, presenting formidable obstacles to human development (Haskett et al. [2010](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR20 "Haskett J, Schlamadinger B, Brown S (2010) Land-based carbon storage and the European Union emissions trading scheme: the science underlying the policy. Mitig Adapt Strateg Glob Chang 15(2):127–136.                    https://doi.org/10.1007/s11027-009-9202-9                                    ")). In response to national carbon emission targets, the Chinese government has introduced economic and environmental policies that blend market mechanisms with environmental regulations to address the situation (Zhou and Wang, [2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR82 "Zhou F, Wang X (2022) The carbon emissions trading scheme and green technology innovation in China: a new structural economics perspective. Econ Anal Policy 74:365–381.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eap.2022.03.007                                    ")). Beginning in 2011, major cities including Beijing, Shanghai, Shenzhen, and Guangzhou initiated the establishment of carbon emission trading markets as a proactive measure to optimize resource allocation and promote low-carbon economic growth (Cong and Wei, [2010](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR9 "Cong RG, Wei YM (2010) Potential impact of (CET) carbon emissions trading on China’s power sector: a perspective from different allowance allocation options. Energy 35(9):3921–3931.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.energy.2010.06.013                                    ")). Subsequently, since 2013, the pilot carbon emission trading policy has been rolled out in eight cities, encompassing Beijing, Shanghai, Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Tianjin, Wuhan, Chongqing, and Fujian (Chen et al. [2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR6 "Chen Z, Song P, Wang B (2021) Carbon emissions trading scheme, energy efficiency and rebound effect—evidence from China’s provincial data. Energy Policy 157:112507.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.enpol.2021.112507                                    ")). Participation in this market enables high-energy-consuming enterprises to mitigate their emission reduction costs, foster clean production practices, and drive sustainable industrial development through the adoption of innovative green technologies (Ma, [2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR39 "Ma B (2022) Assessment of the impact of China’s carbon emissions trading pilot policy on the export product quality. Discret Dyn Nat Soc 2022:1–10.                    https://doi.org/10.1155/2022/6458398                                    "); Wu et al. [2014](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR72 "Wu L, Qian H, Li J (2014) Advancing the experiment to reality: perspectives on Shanghai pilot carbon emissions trading scheme. Energy Policy 75:22–30.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.enpol.2014.04.022                                    "); Wu and Wang, [2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR73 "Wu Q, Wang Y (2022) How does carbon emission price stimulate enterprises’ total factor productivity? Insights from China’s emission trading scheme pilots. Energy Econ 109:105990.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eneco.2022.105990                                    ")).

This market-driven policy is pivotal in facilitating China’s transition to a low-carbon economy by stimulating technological innovation and encouraging energy conservation (Daskalakis, [2018](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR10 "Daskalakis G (2018) Temporal restrictions on emissions trading and the implications for the carbon futures market: lessons from the EU emissions trading scheme. Energy Policy 115:88–91.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.enpol.2018.01.008                                    ")). Residents are likely to shape positive attitudes toward improved air quality, and it may contribute to good psychological well-being in turn. However, unforeseen regulatory adjustments and new policy introductions may exert intangible pressure on individuals navigating uncertain social and economic landscapes, eliciting feelings of anxiety, insecurity, and even severe depression (Meyer and Lobao, [2003](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR44 "Meyer K, Lobao L (2003) Economic hardship, religion and mental health during the midwestern farm crisis. J Rural Stud 19(2):139–155.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/S0743-0167(02)00069-4                                    ")). Middle-aged and elderly residents under the pressure of negative life events, due to their limited adaptability, are exposed to such stressors. With the rollout of the national CET system in 2021 and its anticipated impacts on economic restructuring and attitudinal shifts, it becomes imperative to recognize the subtle yet profound influence of intangible stressors stemming from CET policies on adult mental health.

Previous research has extensively documented the adverse and significant repercussions of pilot carbon emissions trading policies on economic and social welfare, notwithstanding their positive effects on air quality improvement and the advancement of a low-carbon economy (Wang and Watanabe, [2019](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR71 "Wang L, Watanabe T (2019) Effects of environmental policy on public risk perceptions of haze in Tianjin City: a difference-in-differences analysis. Renew Sustain Energy Rev 109(3):199–212.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.rser.2019.04.017                                    "); Chen and Lin, [2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR5 "Chen X, Lin B (2021) Towards carbon neutrality by implementing carbon emissions trading scheme: policy evaluation in China. Energy Policy 157:112510.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.enpol.2021.112510                                    "); Zhang et al. [2020](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR77 "Zhang W, Li J, Li G et al. (2020) Emission reduction effect and carbon market efficiency of carbon emissions trading policy in China. Energy 196:117117.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.energy.2020.117117                                    ")). However, the discussion on the policy’s impact on health status, considered a comprehensive indicator of welfare (Liu et al. [2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR37 "Liu Y, Liu K, Zhang X et al. (2024) Does digital infrastructure improve public Health? A quasi-natural experiment based on China’s Broadband policy. Soc Sci Med 344:116624.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.socscimed.2024.116624                                    ")), has been largely confined to its influence on psychological well-being, and is not been fully discussed. Moreover, while studies have established a connection between major depressive disorders and short-term memory impairment (Halvorsen et al. [2011](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR19 "Halvorsen M, Waterloo K, Sundet K et al. (2011) Verbal learning and memory in depression: a 9-year follow-up study. Psychiatry Res 188(3):350–354.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psychres.2011.02.022                                    ")), the potential mediating role of immediate or delayed word recall in the relationship between policy implementation and depression remains unexplored. Additionally, stressful life events such as retirement and child bereavement have been associated with psychological distress and prolonged grief among older adults (Hessel, [2016](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR23 "Hessel P (2016) Does retirement (really) lead to worse health among European men and women across all educational levels? Soc Sci Med 151:19–26.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.socscimed.2015.12.018                                    "); Heller-Sahlgren, [2017](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR22 "Heller-Sahlgren G (2017) Retirement blues. J Health Econ 54:66–78.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jhealeco.2017.03.007                                    "); Pohlkamp et al. [2019](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR51 "Pohlkamp L, Kreicbergs U, Sveen J (2019) Bereaved mothers’ and fathers’ prolonged grief and psychological health 1 to 5 years after loss—a nationwide study. Psycho-Oncol 28(7):1530–1536.                    https://doi.org/10.1002/pon.5112                                    ")). Nevertheless, the combined impact of life trauma and the implementation of pilot CET policies on depression remains unclear, warranting further investigation.

To address the abovementioned issues, we utilized data from four waves of the CHARLS study spanning from 2011 to 2018. Employing a difference-in-differences methodology, we aimed to elucidate the potential effects of the CET policy on psychological health. Additionally, we employed mediated and moderated effect analyses to delve into the potential mechanisms underlying the impact of the CET policy on psychological well-being. In summary, our objective was twofold: to investigate the influence of the pilot carbon emissions trading policy on depression and to examine the mediating role of memory impairment and the synergistic effect of stressful life experiences on depression among urban adults.

Our study contributes significantly to the existing literature in three key ways. Firstly, while prior research primarily examines the impact of the CET policy on social welfare losses, there exists a notable gap in understanding its effects on psychological well-being. Through a rigorous analysis employing a difference-in-difference approach and utilizing CES-D depression scores as a proxy variable for health well-being, our study unveils the detrimental impact of the CET policy on psychological health. This not only enriches existing theories but also furnishes valuable insights for governmental departments involved in policy formulation and implementation processes. Secondly, based on psychiatric theory, our research delves into the mediating role of cognitive memory deficits in the relationship between the CET policy and mental health. By elucidating the influence of immediate or delayed word recall as indicative signals, our study offers guidance for clinical interventions targeting depressive disorders. Lastly, to our knowledge, our study represents the first comprehensive exploration of the synergistic effects of the CET policy and stress life events on depression among middle-aged and older adults. This expands our understanding of the interplay between trauma stress theories and social transition in precipitating depression within a specific context.

The rest of this paper is conducted as follows. The first section is the introduction. The second section provides the institutional background and literature review. The third section describes the data and methodology. The fourth section presents empirical results. Then, in the last section, we emphasize the conclusions and implications of this study.

## Institutional background and literature review

### Institutional background

Since the 1980s, China has undergone a period of rapid economic growth and profound social transformation, bringing about significant changes across various aspects of life for its populace. However, alongside this progress, there have been adverse consequences (Peng et al. [2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR50 "Peng J, Xie R, Ma C et al. (2021) Market-based environmental regulation and total factor productivity: evidence from Chinese enterprises. Econ Mode 95:394–407.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.econmod.2020.03.006                                    ")). Traditional patterns of high energy consumption and expansive economic development have contributed to increased greenhouse gas emissions (Zhao et al. [2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR80 "Zhao S, Zhang L, Peng L et al. (2024) Enterprise pollution reduction through digital transformation? [2015](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR35 "Liu L, Chen C, Zhao Y et al. (2015) China’s carbon-emissions trading: overview, challenges and future. Renew Sustain Energy Rev 49:254–266.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.rser.2015.04.076                                    ")). Specifically, environmental degradation and air pollution stemming from climate change have emerged as substantial threats to human health (Wang and Watanabe, [2019](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR71 "Wang L, Watanabe T (2019) Effects of environmental policy on public risk perceptions of haze in Tianjin City: a difference-in-differences analysis. Therefore, China faces considerable social transition pressure to curb carbon emissions within resource constraints to meet international targets to reduce carbon emissions (Chen and Lin, [2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR5 "Chen X, Lin B (2021) Towards carbon neutrality by implementing carbon emissions trading scheme: policy evaluation in China. By 2023, China’s GDP reached 126058.2 billion Yuan, nearly forty-six times its 1992 figure. Notably, the peak growth rate of 13.4% was observed in 1993, while the lowest, 2.3%, was recorded in 2020 due to the impact of COVID-19. China became the world’s second-largest economy in 2010 marking a significant milestone since the inception of the reform and opening-up policy in 1978 (Lai, [2003](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR38 "Lai P (2003) China’s economic growth: new trends and implications. Resour Policy 85:103932")). Consequently, the Chinese government revised its annual economic target to balance energy conservation and sustainable development. This adjustment signifies a new normal, with GDP growth consistently below 8% since 2013, highlighting the necessity for structural reforms to sustain medium-high growth. To combat climate change, China actively promotes low-carbon technological innovation and implements carbon emission trading pilots to improve production efficiency (Qiu et al. [2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR53 "Qiu L, Yu R, Hu F et al. Nonetheless, economic structural reform and social transitions remain ongoing long-term processes.

### Literature review

## Model and data

### Data

The data utilized in this study were sourced from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), comprising four waves of data collection spanning the years 2011, 2013, 2015, and 2018. Spearheaded by the Institute of Social Science Survey (ISSS) at Peking University since 2008, this nationally representative panel survey targets individuals aged 45 and above across China. The primary objective of CHARLS is to comprehensively gather data at the individual, family, and community levels, facilitating the documentation of societal, economic, and health-related transformations, thus serving as a cornerstone for both academic inquiry and policy formulation. The inception of CHARLS dates back to 2008, with pilot surveys conducted in two provinces, culminating in a nationwide baseline survey in 2011. For further insights into CHARLS, interested parties are encouraged to consult previous studies (Scheffel and Zhang, [2019](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR57 "Scheffel J, Zhang Y (2019) How does internal migration affect the emotional health of elderly parents left-behind? J Popul Econ 32(3):953–980.                    https://doi.org/10.1007/s00148-018-0715-y                                    "); Zhao et al. [2014](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR81 "Zhao Y, Hu Y, Smith JP et al. (2014) Cohort profile: the China health and retirement longitudinal study (CHARLS). Int J Epidemiol 43(1):61–68.                    https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dys203                                    ")). The study’s robust design ensures the capture of essential facets of psychological health, demographic characteristics, and stressful life experiences among middle-aged and elderly individuals in China.

We excluded observations from rural areas in our analysis considering that most energy-intensive enterprises are situated in urban regions. Rural residents are primarily engaged in agricultural activities, which are less impacted by air pollution and the implementation of the CET policy (Wang and Watanabe, [2019](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR71 "Wang L, Watanabe T (2019) Effects of environmental policy on public risk perceptions of haze in Tianjin City: a difference-in-differences analysis. Renew Sustain Energy Rev 109(3):199–212.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.rser.2019.04.017                                    ")). Additionally, China’s unique urban-rural dual structure (Gao et al. [2018](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR18 "Gao L, Yan J, Du Y (2018) Identifying the turning point of the urban–rural relationship: evidence from macro data. China World Econ 26(1):106–126.                    https://doi.org/10.1111/cwe.12231                                    ")) results in distinct production modes and lifestyle norms between urban and rural areas, rendering the demographic characteristics of rural residents inconsistent with our research assumptions. Therefore, our focus remains on urban residents, as the CET policy holds greater relevance for them. Furthermore, we omitted observations with missing variables pertaining to demographic information. As a result, our study encompasses 10,170 observations sourced from 28 provinces across China.

$${Depressio}{n}\_{{ijt}}=\\alpha +\\beta {di}{d}\_{{ijt}}+\\delta {X}\_{{ijt}}+{\\nu }\_{t}+{\\gamma }\_{i}+{\\phi }\_{j}+{\\varepsilon }\_{{ijt}}$$

(1)


where in Eq. ( [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#Equ1)): \\({\\boldsymbol{Depressio}}{{\\boldsymbol{n}}}\_{{\\boldsymbol{ijt}}}\\) is the dependent variable which indicates that the depression scores of individual **_i_** in province **_j_** in the year of **_t_**. \\({\\boldsymbol{di}}{{\\boldsymbol{d}}}\_{{\\boldsymbol{ijt}}}\\) is the independent variable to identify the pilot carbon emission trade policy. The pilot provinces after policy implementation are 1, otherwise are 0. \\({{\\boldsymbol{X}}}\_{{\\boldsymbol{ijt}}}\\) is a vector of individual characteristic control variables. **_ν_** is the time fixed effect, **_γ_** is the individual fixed effect, **_ϕ_** is the regional fixed effect, and **_ε_** is the random error term.

### Variables

#### Psychological health measures

Psychological well-being was assessed using the CES-10 score, comprising 10 representative items reflecting depression symptoms. Participants rated negative emotions such as depression, hopelessness, and fear, along with other common symptoms, while scoring positive emotions in reverse. Each item was rated on a scale from 0 to 3, with 3 indicating “nearly every day” and 0 indicating “not at all”. The total score ranged from 0 to 30, with higher scores indicating more severe depression. The Cronbach’s alpha coefficient was calculated to be 0.618, indicating that the CES-D score is a reliable instrument for evaluating psychological health. It is widely acknowledged that moderate and severe depression can have profound impacts on both professional and personal aspects of life, potentially leading to a relapse into despair. Therefore, for this study, adults were categorized as experiencing moderate to severe depression if their CES-D score exceeded 15, while those with scores below 15 were classified as not experiencing moderate to severe depression.

#### Independent variables

We used the Carbon Emission Trade pilot cities after policy implementation as proxy variables for DID. The period after the implementation of the CET policy was considered the treatment group, and that before implementation was considered the control group. We used the interaction term of city and time to measure the exogenous impact of the CET policy.

#### Control variables

In addition to the implementation of the carbon emission trade policy, various factors may contribute to depression symptoms. To better understand these factors, individual-level information such as age (continuous variable), gender (dummy variable: male = 1, female = 0), education level (Primary school, Middle school, High school, College and above), and marital status (dummy variable: having a spouse = 1, no spouse = 0) were taken in account. We also evaluated stress-related information by assessing participation in social activities and smoking habits (Erdsiek and Brzoska, [2019](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR14 "Erdsiek F, Brzoska P (2019) Association between second-hand smoke exposure and depression and its moderation by sex: Findings from a nation-wide population survey in Germany. J Affect Disord 253:102–106.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2019.04.081                                    "); Shan et al. [2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR59 "Shan Y, Zhao W, Hao W et al. (2024) Five-year changes of social activity and incident long-term care needs among depressed older adults: A 15-year follow up. Arch Gerontol Geriatr 116:105163.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.archger.2023.105163                                    ")). Self-reported memory, rated on a scale from 1 (Excellent) to 5 (Poor), was used for cognitive function. Life satisfaction was also measured to gauge overall perception of life status. Household-level information was further examined, with wealth status evaluated based on cash holdings. It is well-documented that wealth status can have a significant impact on psychological health (Simou and Koutsogeorgou, [2014](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR62 "Simou E, Koutsogeorgou E (2014) Effects of the economic crisis on health and healthcare in Greece in the literature from 2009 to 2013: A systematic review. Health Policy 115(2-3):111–119.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.healthpol.2014.02.002                                    "); Zissi and Stalidis, [2017](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#ref-CR84 "Zissi A, Stalidis G (2017) Social class and mental distress in Greek urban communities during the period of economic recession. Int J Soc Psychiatry 63(5):459–467.                    https://doi.org/10.1177/0020764017712743                                    ")). Additionally, access to medical insurance, determined through external public resources, was considered to capture regional-level information.

To analyze the mediating effects of depression, immediate and delayed word recall tests were conducted. Furthermore, retired status and child bereavement were taken into account as potential stressors to analyze the moderating effects of depression. These factors provide a comprehensive framework for exploring the complex interplay between psychological health, life circumstances, and external stressors.

### Summary statistics

The results of the summary statistics are presented in Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#Tab1). The CES-D depression score was 7.37, which is lower than the threshold of 7.5, indicating that the overall mental health of middle-aged and older adults is relatively good. However, it is concerning that 14% of the sample reported moderate to severe depression, suggesting that more than 1 in 10 adults may be struggling with mental health issues. In terms of demographic characteristics, the majority of participants were over 60 years old, with a fairly even distribution of gender (46% male). Most participants only had completed middle school education (54%) and were currently married (85%). Only 40% of adults reported smoking, which profoundly increases the risk of health issues. ### Mechanism analysis

The research indicates a significant increase in depression risk among adults following CET policy implementation, yet the precise mechanism remains unclear. However, insights from related studies suggest potential explanations. Firstly, individuals with depression often exhibit memory deficits, which may manifest prior to depression onset, serving as crucial precursors. Secondly, common stressors such as retirement and child bereavement, particularly prevalent among individuals aged 45 and above, can exacerbate mental health challenges. In the context of policy implementation, these stressors may synergistically impact individuals’ mental well-being, triggering feelings of nervousness, sadness, and anxiety. Consequently, two key questions emerge: What types of memory deficits contribute to depression during new policy implementation? And, how do stressful life events influence depression during this period?

#### The mediating effect of memory deficits

The results of the medication effects are detailed in Table [7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05171-6#Tab7). While the policy implementation does not consistently have a direct impact on depression, there is evidence to suggest that memory deficits may exert a mediating effect. Immediate and delayed word recall tests were utilized to investigate this. In column (1), the coefficient of immediate word recall was −0.164, significant at the 1% level, indicating an increased risk of depression in adults. Column (2) revealed a coefficient of −0.261 at the 5% significance level, suggesting that policy implementation had a negative and significant effect on immediate word recall. This implies that the partial mediating effect of immediate word recall between policy implementation and depression was significant. However, in column (4), the coefficient of delayed word recall showed no significant effect on depression, indicating no mediating effect. Bootstrapping and Sobel tests were used to ensure the robustness of the mediating effect. Results confirmed that the CET policy and depression symptoms mediated immediate word recall, but not delayed word recall. These findings underscore the nuanced role of memory deficits in influencing the relationship between policy changes and depression, with immediate word recall exhibiting a notable mediating effect while delayed word recall does not.

## Conclusions and discussions

### Main findings

Utilizing a well-constructed longitudinal dataset encompassing data on depression symptoms, memory deficits, life stressors, and demographic characteristics across four waves spanning from 2011 to 2018, we conducted an empirical investigation into the impact of the CET policy on depression symptoms among adults residing in eight pilot cities. Employing a staggered DID identification framework and leveraging the parallel trend method, we observed no significant divergence between the treatment and control groups prior to policy implementation, thereby reinforcing the consistency of our DID results. To bolster the reliability of our findings, we further conducted placebo tests and several robust checks. Our analysis also encompassed heterogeneity assessments, probing how policy implementation affected depression symptoms across diverse socioeconomic strata. We employed mechanism analysis to elucidate the mediating role of memory deficits between the CET policy and depression symptoms. Furthermore, our study underscored the pivotal role of stressful life events, such as retirement and child bereavement, as significant contributors to depression, particularly within the context of social transitions. Additionally, we explored the intertwined influence of the CET policy and stressful life events on depression symptoms, shedding light on their synergistic impact.

We have several conclusions as follows. First, the findings employed by the DID approach compellingly indicate that the implementation of the CET policy significantly heightens the likelihood of depressive disorders among adults in these cities, particularly impacting older adults and those with comparatively higher socioeconomic status. Second, immediate word recall emerged as a partial mediator in the relationship between CET implementation and depression, with the policy negatively influencing immediate word recall, thereby contributing to depressive disorders. Third, stressful life events, such as retirement and child bereavement, exacerbated depressive disorders within the context of CET policy implementation. Stressful life events, such as retirement and child bereavement, exacerbate depressive disorders within the context of CET policy implementation.

In conclusion, our findings underscore the heightened susceptibility of adults to depressive disorders when confronted with stressors emanating from both macro and micro environments, emphasizing the multifaceted nature of depression etiology and the need for comprehensive intervention strategies addressing both policy-driven and personal stressors.

### Theoretical implications

Our research provides a beneficial supplement to the effect evaluation of CET policy from the perspective of depression symptoms. Firstly, our baseline regression analysis confirmed a notable increase in depression among adults in the eight pilot cities following the implementation of the CET policy. This policy, emblematic of broader socioeconomic transitions, introduces heightened uncertainty and insecurity, exerting substantial pressure on adults’ mental well-being and elevating the risk of depressive disorders. Furthermore, the impact of the CET policy on depression was more pronounced among adults with relatively high socioeconomic status and those aged over 60 years, indicating a greater likelihood of experiencing heightened depressive symptoms. We significantly contribute to extending the social cognition theory to incorporate environmental and economic policy, highlighting the detrimental psychological impact of social transitions on adults.

Secondly, our analysis identified immediate word recall as a partial mediator in the relationship between the CET policy and depression. In contrast, the mediated effect of delayed word recall was deemed insignificant. Immediate memory recall, which reflects the brain’s initial response to external stimuli, likely triggered anxiety in the presence of deficits, thereby mediating the link between CET policy implementation and depression symptoms. Conversely, delayed memory recall, involving processing and encoding of information after reception, had a negligible impact, likely due to limitations in information retention during the interview process. This finding is validated econometrically by integrating psychopathological theory, which breaks the boundaries of the knowledge of socioeconomic studies when encountering the psychopathology discipline.

Thirdly, our investigation revealed a significant synergistic effect between the CET policy and stressful life events in exacerbating depression symptoms. These major life stressors inflict severe psychological distress on middle-aged and elderly individuals, as evidenced by the significant and positive interaction terms of DID with retirement and child bereavement. Amid regulatory adjustments and societal changes, adults face compounded pressures, leading to an increased risk of depression. The evolving social and economic landscape resulting from new policy implementations has heightened feelings of instability and insecurity among adults, with concurrent stressful life experiences amplifying these challenges. By innovate integrating the macro social factors and the micro individual factors, we validate the synergistic effect on depression, which deepens the theoretical understanding of its multifaceted causes.

### Practical implications

Several policy implications arise from these findings. Firstly, the social transition can significantly exacerbate the negative feelings of anxiety, tension, and pressure, and eventually impact intangibly the psychological health of populations. Hence, mental health concerns should be prioritized during institutional adjustments and policy introductions, with a focus on social fairness alongside economic efficiency. Besides, there also exist heterogeneous effects among different attribute cohorts. Targeted social interventions are needed for relatively vulnerable cohorts, including older adults and susceptible individuals with higher socioeconomic status, to mitigate depressive disorders. Social workers should develop effective intervention strategies for these groups to forestall negative moods and depression symptoms.

Secondly, individuals should remain vigilant regarding memory deficits as potential precursors of depressive disorders. Immediate memory deficits have a mediating impact between CET policy and depression, but delayed memory deficits do not. Though delayed memory deficits do not have a mediation effect, people also focus on the issue of memory degradation among older adults. As a pivotal reminder of potential depression, short memory deficits play a vital role in psychological intervention, which means pre-prevention in the early stage can mainly focus on the subtle change of related memory deficit behaviors. Hence, timely psychological interventions and medical treatments are crucial in preventing memory deficits from progressing into severe psychological disorders, including Alzheimer’s disease.

Lastly, the compounding effects of micro and macro stressors underscore the necessity of addressing mental health concerns among vulnerable cohorts experiencing life changes and traumatic events. Policymakers should consider the vulnerable groups trapped in significant pressure and pain, and provide more social support not only in material but also in psychology. Policymakers should prioritize comprehensive social and psychological support for these groups, particularly during retirement and bereavement, when they are more susceptible to stress and negative emotions under rapid social transitions. Hence, timely social support and psychological intervention are invaluable resources for navigating adversity and fostering psychological resilience in challenging social contexts.

### Limitations and future research

While our study contributes valuable insights, it also has several limitations that warrant consideration. Firstly, the dataset’s initiation in 2011, coinciding with the pilot carbon emissions trading policy’s announcement, may restrict the depth of our analysis. Future research should incorporate datasets spanning extended periods to ensure more robust outcomes and better capture the policy’s long-term effects. Secondly, the CES-D depression scale, with its limited 10-item structure, demonstrated a Cronbach’s α coefficient below the critical value of 0.8. Subsequent studies should explore alternative depression scales better suited for assessing mental health among middle-aged and older adults in China, ensuring greater reliability and validity in measurement. Furthermore, future research efforts should adopt more nuanced measurements of mental health, including variables such as life anxiety, sleep quality, behavioral expressions, and physical conditions. This comprehensive approach would provide a more holistic understanding of individuals’ psychological states in the context of environmental policies like carbon emissions trading. Lastly, given the potential implications of compromised psychological health on healthcare utilization, future studies should investigate the relationship between pilot carbon emissions trading and individual healthcare service utilization. Understanding this link could offer valuable insights into the broader societal impacts of environmental policies on public health systems and resource allocation.
