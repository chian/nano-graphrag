3D modelling and simulation of thermal effects and dispersion of particles carrying infectious respiratory agents in a railway transport coach


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2.pdf)

## Abstract

Even though the COVID-19 pandemic now belongs to the long history of infectious diseases that have struck humanity, pathogenic biological agents continue to pose a recurring threat in private places, but also and mainly in places where the public congregates. In our recent research published in this journal in 2022 and 2023, we considered the illustrative example of a commuter train coach in which a symptomatic or asymptomatic passenger, assumed to be infected with a respiratory disease, sits among other travellers. The passenger emits liquid particles containing, for example, COVID-19 virions or any other pathogen. The size spectrum of particles varies depending on whether they are produced during breathing, speaking, coughing or sneezing. More specifically, droplets associated with breathing are in the range of 1–10 µm in aerodynamic diameter, while at the other end of the spectrum, drops associated with coughing can reach 100–1000 µm. In the first part of our research, we used Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) to model and simulate in 3D the transport and dispersion of particles from 1 µm to 1 mm in the turbulent flow generated by the ventilation of the railway coach. We used both the Eulerian approach and the Lagrangian approach and showed that the results were strictly similar and illustrated the very distinct aerodynamics, on one hand, of the aerosol of droplets suspended in the air and, on the other hand, of the drops falling or behaving like projectiles depending on their initial speed. In the second part of our research, we developed a model of filtration through a typical surgical mask and possible leaks around the mask if it is poorly adjusted. We resumed the twin experiment of the railway coach and compared the distribution of droplets depending on whether the passengers (including the infected one) wear masks or not and whether the masks are perfectly fitted or worn loosely. Our method made it possible to quantify the particles suspended in the air of the railway coach depending on whether the infected passenger wore their mask more or less well. In this third article, we specifically explore how thermal effects due to the presence of passengers influence the spatio-temporal distribution in the railway coach of aerosols produced by the breathing infected person. We demonstrate that the influence of thermal effects on aerodynamics is very significant and can be very favourable for air decontamination if the ventilation system is judiciously configured. Beyond its application to a commuter train, our work confirms the value of validated CFD tools for describing the airflow and dispersion of particles in complex spaces that do not always allow experimentation. The models that we have developed are applicable to any other semi-confined, ventilated public place, such as a classroom, a hospital room or a performance hall, and they enable the objective assessment of whether the occupation of these spaces could be critical with regard to infectious contamination and of how to limit this ubiquitous risk.

## Introduction

Infectious diseases transmitted by viruses or bacteria have accompanied humanity since its origin. Identified at the end of 2019, the COVID-19 disease caused by the SARS-CoV-2 virus has been responsible for millions of deaths, with some people still suffering its after-effects. More generally, COVID-19 has caused disorganisation and major economic and social problems across the entire planet. This disease now seems to be under control despite regular seasonal resurgences depending on the location on the globe. However, COVID-19 has reminded us of the urgent need to question the routes of airborne transmission of pathogens in order to slow or stop the propagation of epidemics throughout the population. This is all the more important given that diseases such as severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS), Middle East respiratory syndrome (MERS) or the many types of influenza (H1N1) and their variants still cause concern. This original publication is the continuation of two articles[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023).") published in this journal implementing physical modelling and numerical simulation to better understand the spread of infectious agents, particularly in places frequented by the public. We will have the opportunity to come back to this further on in the article.

In our first report[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), we studied the risk of airborne respiratory diseases such as COVID-19 among public transport passengers. We created a twin experiment by reproducing the numerical mock-up of a railway coach in which humanoid manikins were placed. This coach is one of a commuter train type used by millions worldwide for travel on suburban transportation networks. We assumed that an infected individual emitted droplets and drops while breathing or during a cough. The particles were transported and dispersed by the ventilation system of the coach and showed very distinct aerodynamics between droplets (which perfectly followed the streamlines) and drops (which separated from the carrier fluid by their inertia and tended to sediment in the immediate vicinity of the spreader). While the droplets adapted very quickly to the flow around them, this was not the case for the largest drops, which adopted a ballistic behaviour or settled quite rapidly depending on their initial relative velocity in relation to the airflow. This phenomenology was highlighted in our three-dimensional simulations, which examined the turbulent flow within the coach and the dispersion of particles of different sizes, using both an Eulerian approach and a Lagrangian approach that led to similar results. The study demonstrated that the models used for simulating the transport and dispersion of particles in an indoor environment were appropriate and could be re-employed with confidence in semi-enclosed ventilated spaces of different geometries. However, in this study, the railway coach passengers, including the infected passenger, were assumed not to wear masks.

In our second report[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023)."), we considered it interesting and useful to carry out CFD simulations featuring the wearing of masks by passengers. In fact, when the epidemic was active, many governments made it mandatory to wear masks on public transport; this remains the case in some parts of the world and could become widespread again in the event of new respiratory diseases including, for instance, variants of SARS-CoV-2 or other viruses. Thus, we sought to determine whether the use of masks by infected people could effectively reduce the dissemination of virions in a public space such as a railway coach. This led us to undertake simulations that once again involved a twin experiment in a railway coach, but this time with passengers wearing masks and, among them, one passenger assumed to be infected with the COVID-19 disease. We considered and compared three situations in which the coach passengers did not have masks, wore their masks tightly against the face or wore them loosely with the existence of leaks around the mask. The mask was not explicitly modelled but placed on the faces of the humanoid manikins, either perfectly or leaving spaces between the face and the mask. In addition, the mask was not a porous medium, yet it allowed air and a fraction of particles to pass through its filtering part (and through leaks where applicable). As there are many types of masks, special emphasis was placed on the surgical mask, which is very widespread due to its particularly low cost. We return to mask modelling in more detail later in this article. The study showed the limited dissemination within the railway coach of droplets produced by the breathing or coughing of the masked infected passenger, whether the mask was poorly or well fitted to the face. Beyond proposing models of the mask, the flow generated by ventilation and the dispersion of particles of all sizes, this study made it possible to obtain quantitative results on the reduction of the number of drops and droplets in the coach, opening perspectives for the use of these models in other semi-confined spaces.

Previous studies had the limitation of not considering the thermal effects generated by the difference between the temperature of the passengers and the temperature of the internal structures of the train. In this new research, we intend to examine the influence of thermal phenomena on airflow and particle dispersion while retaining the case study of the railway coach. We will highlight the impact of buoyancy on the spatial and temporal distribution of droplets produced by the breathing of an infected passenger. Moreover, we will revisit the situations of the passenger without a mask, with a perfectly fitted mask and with a mask worn loosely. The report is structured as follows. First, we recall results from the literature on the emission of particles expectorated by humans and on the effect of wearing a mask during respiratory events, particularly breathing. These elements are essential as they allow us to justify the hypotheses associated with our modelling of the mask worn more or less well. Then, we review articles published in the literature that report numerical results of CFD modelling of the dispersion of particles of different sizes, especially in semi-confined ventilated spaces. We thus underline the originality and relatively unique character of our study. After this, we present the results of our modelling and simulation with thermal effects taken into account. First, we consider the head and bust of a humanoid manikin, immersed in a still atmosphere; this allows us to examine situations in which the model wears a tight-fitting mask, a loose mask, or no mask at all. We then show the results obtained with full humanoid manikins wearing masks and placed on the commuter train, with one of the passengers infected with any respiratory disease. Finally, we present a general discussion on the results obtained and the perspectives offered by our approach in terms of scientific developments and operational applications. The rest of the article details the CFD tool implemented in the study, the hypotheses of the numerical study concerning the aerodynamic conditions in the railway car and the production of droplets by breathing, as well as the resources associated with the simulations and the computing times.

### Aerial transmission of infectious agents with or without a mask

As documented in numerous articles, pathogenic respiratory agents such as the SARS-CoV-2 virus are present in the liquid particles between 0.1 and 1000 µm in size[3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR3 "Mittal, R., Ni, R. & Seo, J. The flow physics of Covid-19. J. Fluid Mech. 894, F2 (2020).") expectorated by infected humans, who are also called “spreaders”[4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR4 "Liu, Y. et al. Aerodynamic analysis of SARS-CoV-2 in two Wuhan hospitals. Nature 582, 557–560 (2020)."), [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR5 "Van Doremalen, N. et al. Aerosol and surface stability of SARS-CoV-2 as compared with SARS-CoV-1. N. Engl. J. Med. 382, 1564–1567 (2020)."), [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR6 "Viola, I. M. et al. Face coverings, aerosol dispersion and mitigation of virus transmission risk. IEEE Open J. Eng. Med. Biol. 2, 26–35 (2021)."). The sputum of individuals is often categorised into drops of 100–1,000 µm crashing almost instantly onto accessible surfaces[7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR7 "Anfinrud, P., Bax, C. E. & Bax, A. Visualizing speech-generated oral fluid droplets with laser light scattering. N. Engl. J. Med. 382, 2061–2063 (2020)."), [8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR8 "Stadnytskyi, V., Bax, C. E., Bax, A. & Anfinrud, P. The airborne lifetime of small speech droplets and their potential importance in SARS-CoV-2 transmission. Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. USA 117(22), 11875–11877 (2020)."), and droplets of 0.1–10 µm known as aerosols, which can travel distances of 7 to 8 m depending on their initial velocity and the airflow[6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR6 "Viola, I. M. et al. Face coverings, aerosol dispersion and mitigation of virus transmission risk. IEEE Open J. Eng. Med. Biol. 2, 26–35 (2021).") and which remain airborne in the range of minutes to hours[5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR5 "Van Doremalen, N. et al. Aerosol and surface stability of SARS-CoV-2 as compared with SARS-CoV-1. N. Engl. J. Med. 382, 1564–1567 (2020)."), [9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR9 "Nicas, M., Nazaroff, W. W. & Hubbard, A. Toward understanding the risk of secondary airborne infection: Emission of respirable pathogens. J. Occup. Environ. Hygiene 2(3), 143–154 (2005)."), [10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR10 "Tellier, R. Review of aerosol transmission of influenza A virus. Emerg. Infectious Diseases 12(11), 1657–1662 (2006)."). Symptomatic and asymptomatic spreaders do not produce the same number or the same size of particles. In addition, symptomatic carriers do not necessarily excrete higher viral-load drops and droplets than do asymptomatic people[11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR11 "Bai, Y. et al. Presumed asymptomatic carrier transmission of Covid-19. J. Am. Med. Assoc. 323(14), 1406–1407 (2020)."). There are also “super-spreaders” producing up to 17 times more particles during a cough compared to other individuals[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR12 "Smith, S. H. et al. Aerosol persistence in relation to possible transmission of SARS-CoV-2. Phys. Fluids 32, 107108 (2020)."). The number of exhaled particles varies depending on whether they are generated by a low-frequency event or a cyclic event. Indeed, a sneeze produces around 1000–10,000 particles per second with a speed of up to 20 m s−1[13](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR13 "Han, Z. Y., Weng, W. G. & Huang, Q. Y. Characterizations of particle size distribution of the droplets exhaled by sneeze. J. R. Society Interface. 10, 20130560 (2013).") and a cough 100–1000 particles per second with a speed of around 10 m s−1[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR14 "Xie, X., Li, Y., Sun, H. & Liu, L. Exhaled droplets due to talking and coughing. J. R. Society Interface 6, S703–S714 (2009)."), [15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR15 "Asadi, S., Bouvier, N., Wexler, S. & Ristenpart, W. D. The coronavirus pandemic and aerosols: Does Covid-19 transmit via expiratory particles?. Aerosol Sci. Technol. 54(6), 635–638 (2020)."). Breathing or speaking produces a minimum of 3000 and up to 20,000 droplets per minute with diameters between 0.8 and 5.5 µm[6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR6 "Viola, I. M. et al. Face coverings, aerosol dispersion and mitigation of virus transmission risk. IEEE Open J. Eng. Med. Biol. 2, 26–35 (2021).") up to 1 mm[16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR16 "Asadi, S. et al. Aerosol emission and super-emission during human speech. Increase with voice loudness. Nat. Sci. Rep. 9(1), 2348 (2019)."), with a speed of the order of 1 m s−1[17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR17 "Tang, J. W. et al. Airflow dynamics of human jets: sneezing and breathing. Potential sources of infectious aerosols. PLoS One. 8(4), e59970 (2013)."). Since breathing and speaking are recurrent phenomena, they are the most common source of aerosol that can be inhaled by other people[17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR17 "Tang, J. W. et al. Airflow dynamics of human jets: sneezing and breathing. Potential sources of infectious aerosols. PLoS One. 8(4), e59970 (2013)."), [18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR18 "Fiegel, J., Clarke, R. & Edwards, D. A. Airborne infectious disease and the suppression of pulmonary bioaerosols. Drug Discov. Today 11(1–2), 51–57 (2006)."), [19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR19 "Atkinson, M. P. & Wein, L. M. Quantifying the routes of transmission for pandemic influenza. Bull. Math. Biol. 70(3), 820–867 (2008).") and are ten times more important in contamination than coughing or sneezing[19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR19 "Atkinson, M. P. & Wein, L. M. Quantifying the routes of transmission for pandemic influenza. Bull. Math. Biol. 70(3), 820–867 (2008).").

Liquid particles are subject to evaporation. The volume of the particles decreases down to a certain threshold below which they are considered to be non-evaporable solid nuclei[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR14 "Xie, X., Li, Y., Sun, H. & Liu, L. Exhaled droplets due to talking and coughing. J. R. Society Interface 6, S703–S714 (2009)."), [20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR20 "Wells, W. F. Airborne contagion and air hygiene. An ecological study of droplet infections. J. Am. Med. Assoc. 159(1), 90 (1955)."), [21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR21 "Bourouiba, L., Dehandschoewercker, E. & Bush, J. W. M. Violent expiratory events: On coughing and sneezing. J. Fluid Mech. 745, 537–563 (2014)."), [22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR22 "Vejerano, E. P. & Marr, L. C. Physico-chemical characteristics of evaporating respiratory fluid droplets. J. R. Society Interface 15(139), 20170939 (2018)."). The evaporation rate depends on the difference between the saturation vapour pressure at the surface of the particles and the vapour pressure of the ambient air, which is a function of the temperature and humidity and is influenced by the outdoor or indoor airflow[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR12 "Smith, S. H. et al. Aerosol persistence in relation to possible transmission of SARS-CoV-2. Phys. Fluids 32, 107108 (2020)."). High temperature and low relative humidity lead to higher evaporation rates[3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR3 "Mittal, R., Ni, R. & Seo, J. The flow physics of Covid-19. J. Fluid Mech. 894, F2 (2020)."). On the contrary, low temperature and high relative humidity can lead to a phenomenon of condensation on the particles[23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR23 "Liu, L., Wei, J., Li, Y. & Ooi, A. Evaporation and dispersion of respiratory droplets from coughing. Indoor Air 27(1), 179–190 (2017)."), [24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR24 "Liu, F. et al. Evaporation and dispersion of exhaled droplets in stratified environment. IOP Conf. Series Mater. Sci. Eng. 609, 042059 (2019)."). Exhaled saturated warm air entraining liquid particles forms a plume in front of the nose and mouth of individuals, especially during breathing. In the buoyant plume, particle evaporation can be limited due to the reduced heat and mass transfer between the particles and the air[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR25 "Li, X., Shang, Y., Yan, Y., Yang, L. & Tu, J. Modelling of evaporation of cough droplets in inhomogeneous humidity fields using the multi-component Eulerian-Lagrangian approach. Building Environ. 128, 68–76 (2018)."). As for dynamics, the evaporation process differs for drops and droplets (as defined in the previous paragraph). The drops, whose characteristic diameter is greater than 100 μm, settle very quickly on the ground without being significantly affected by evaporation[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR26 "Yang, Y., Wang, Y., Song, B., Fan, J. & Cao, Y. Stability and accuracy of numerical investigation of droplet motion under local ventilation airflow. Building Environ. 140, 32–42 (2018)."). Droplets of 1–10 µm remain suspended in the air while evaporating more or less rapidly depending on the temperature and relative humidity of the ambient air[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR27 "Marr, L. C., Tang, J. W., van Mullekom, J. & Lakdawala, S. S. Mechanistic insights into the effect of humidity on airborne influenza virus survival, transmission and incidence. J. R. Society Interface 16, 20180298 (2019)."), [28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR28 "Wang, J. et al. Short-range exposure to airborne virus transmission and current guidelines. Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. USA 118(37), e2105279118 (2021)."). For example, at a typical indoor relative humidity of 50%, 5 and 20 μm droplets shrink to 1 µm in less than 3 s and more than 5 s respectively[29](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR29 "Ai, Z. T. & Melikov, A. K. Airborne spread of expiratory droplet nuclei between the occupants of indoor environments: A review. Indoor Air 28(4), 500–524 (2018)."), [30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR30 "Sakharov, A. S. & Zhukov, K. Study of an air curtain in the context of individual protection from exposure to coronavirus (SARSCoV-2) contained in cough-generated fluid particles. Physics 2(3), 340–351 (2020)."). In humid air close to water vapour saturation, the evaporation process can last longer[31](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR31 "Morawska, L. et al. Size distribution and sites of origin of droplets expelled from the human respiratory tract during expiratory activities. J. Aerosol Sci. 40, 256–269 (2009)."), [32](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR32 "Holmgren, H. et al. Relation between humidity and size of exhaled particles. J. Aerosol Med. Pulmonary Drug Delivery. 24, 253–260 (2011)."). After evaporation, the nuclei of the dried droplets can remain in suspension and virulent for minutes or hours[5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR5 "Van Doremalen, N. et al. Aerosol and surface stability of SARS-CoV-2 as compared with SARS-CoV-1. N. Engl. J. Med. 382, 1564–1567 (2020)."). In the simulations presented in this report, we neglected the evaporation process of droplets generated by breathing individuals because the droplets stay suspended in the flow regardless of the evaporation rate and the evolution of their size.

During the COVID-19 epidemic or during other acute episodes of respiratory diseases, the surgical mask has been widely recommended around the world to protect individuals surrounding a possibly infected person wearing this kind of mask. It is a single-use respiratory mask whose purpose is to limit to the immediate environment the spread of bacteria and viruses exhaled from the respiratory tract (mouth and nose) of the wearer. When used correctly, the surgical mask quite successfully contains the dispersion of respiratory drops produced during a sneeze or a cough. It is commonly accepted to be an effective device for blocking drops of several tens of microns projected by the wearer. It has been shown to greatly limit the transmission of airborne viruses (influenza, coronavirus, etc.) by infected people[33](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR33 "Leung, N.H.L., Chu, D.K.W., Shiu, E.Y.C., Chan, K.H., McDevitt, .J.J., Hau, B.J.P., Yen, H.L., Li, Y., Ip, D.K.M., Peiris, J.S.M., Seto, W.H., Leung, G.M., Milton, D.K. & Cowling, B.J. Respiratory virus shedding in exhaled breath and efficacy of face masks. Nat. Med. 26(5), 676–680 (2020)."). That said, it is not as effective in stopping the transmission of the finest aerosols smaller than 5 µm[34](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR34 "Milton D.K., Fabian M.P., Cowling B.J., Grantham M.L & Mc Devitt J.J. Influenza virus aerosols in human exhaled breath: particle size, culturability, and effect of surgical masks. PLOS Pathogens. 9(3), e1003205 (2013)."), and its effectiveness depends on its design, the materials used in its manufacture, its dimensions and its fit on the face. A number of studies have examined the effectiveness of surgical masks, though most of them have not been performed in the context of the COVID-19 pandemic, but instead for other infections (especially influenza). In addition, most of these studies have been experimental, solely qualitative and with different areas of focus (samples of filtering materials, masks placed on humanoid manikins or patient cohorts). They were extremely rarely supplemented by CFD simulations. Due to differences among the protocols and the challenges involved in making the various measurements, the conclusions of these studies can be contradictory. Nevertheless, the following information can be derived about surgical masks.

Several authors have studied the filtration efficiency of surgical masks for various particle sizes, including fine particles. Different types of masks were tested with the assumption that they were perfectly fitted, i.e., without leakage between the mask and the wearer’s face[35](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR35 "Akhtar, J. et al. Can face masks offer protection from airborne sneeze and cough droplets in close-up, face-to-face human interactions? A quantitative study. Phys. Fluids 32, 127112 (2020)."). Drops with a diameter greater than 10 µm were generally filtered by the different masks, as were particles with a diameter less than 200 nm, due to the Brownian effect. Still, none of the surgical masks tested could filter 100% of the droplets of intermediate size whose diameter was between 1 and 5 μm. In this experiment, even with a perfectly tight fit, aerosol leakage through the surgical mask represented 0.1–0.2% of the exhaled particles. Another experiment involving different types of perfectly fitting surgical masks gave even poorer overall aerosol filtration efficiency results for surgical masks, with about 50% of particles with a diameter between 1 and 8 µm being retained[36](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR36 "Ueki, H. et al. Effectiveness of face masks in preventing airborne transmission of SARS-CoV-2. Clin. Sci. Epidemiol. 5(5), e00637-e720 (2020).").

In practice, leakage can be very significant at the wearer’s face because the mask lets a large quantity of air pass around its perimeter. Recently, the problem of leakage from surgical masks has been presented experimentally in a relatively large number of scientific publications. For instance, an experimental reconstitution[37](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR37 "Brooks, J. T. et al. Maximizing fit for cloth and medical procedure masks to improve performance and reduce SARS-CoV-2 transmission and exposure. Morbidity Mortality Weekly Rep. 70(7), 254–257 (2021).") of the cough of a humanoid manikin showed that only 56% of aerosols between 0.1 and 7 µm were filtered by a surgical mask due to leaks around the edge of the mask. CFD simulation work performed on the subject in 2021 by the Riken Scientific Research Institute (Japan)[38](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR38 "                   https://www.hpcwire.com/2020/11/09/its-fugaku-vs-covid-19-how-the-worlds-top-supercomputer-is-shaping-our-new-normal                                    . RIKEN Institute, Japan (2020).") showed that the fraction of aerosols passing through different types of fabric masks without being filtered was larger than 70%, with a variable fraction of aerosols leaking through the spaces between the wearer’s face and the mask.

Apart from its filtration properties, a surgical mask can significantly reduce the airflow velocity during a sneeze, during a cough or within the respiratory cycle[39](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR39 "Tang, J. W., Liebner, T. J., Craven, B. A. & Settles, G. S. A Schlieren optical study of the human cough with and without wearing masks for aerosol infection control. J. R. Society Interface 6, S727–S736 (2009)."), [40](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR40 "Verma, S., Dhanak, M. & Frankenfield, J. Visualizing the effectiveness of face masks in obstructing respiratory jets. Phys. Fluids 32, 061708 (2020)."), [41](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR41 "Verma, S., Dhanak, M. & Frankenfield, J. Visualizing droplet dispersal for face shields and masks with exhalation valves. Phys. Fluids 32, 091701 (2020)."), [42](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR42 "Bhagat, R. K., Wykes, M. S. D., Dalziel, S. B. & Linden, P. F. Effects of ventilation on the indoor spread of COVID-19. J. Fluid Mech. 903, F1 (2020)."), [43](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR43 "Li, Y. et al. Probable airborne transmission of SARS-CoV-2 in a poorly ventilated restaurant. Building Environ. 196, 107788 (2021)."). That said, the inhalation and exhalation phases also increase leaks on the perimeter of the mask due to “pumping” effects. The jets resulting from these leaks can be highly turbulent and directional, which increases the effects of aerosol dispersion in the transverse directions but redirects the aerosols in directions that are less problematic than a breath of air emitted directly from the mouth or nose of the wearer[44](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR44 "Lei, Z., Yang, J., Zhuang, Z. & Roberge, R. Simulation and evaluation of respirator face seal leaks using Computational Fluid Dynamics and infrared imaging. Ann. Occup. Hygiene 57(4), 493–506 (2013)."), [45](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR45 "Lei, Z. & Yang, J. Computing carbon dioxide and humidity in filtering face piece respirator cavity during breathing cycles. in Proceedings of the ASME 2014 International Design Engineering Technical Conferences and Computers and Information in Engineering Conference. Volume 1A: 34th Computers and Information in Engineering Conference, V01AT02A077. Buffalo, New York, USA. August 17–20, 2014 (2014)."). The direction and range of the exhaled airflow are modified according to the type of mask worn[6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR6 "Viola, I. M. et al. Face coverings, aerosol dispersion and mitigation of virus transmission risk. IEEE Open J. Eng. Med. Biol. 2, 26–35 (2021)."). Instead of passing through the filtering part of the mask, the air flows partially around the filtering part through leaks. Leakage can be so significant as to represent two thirds of the total airflow through all parts of the mask. CFD can very effectively complement experiments by providing precise access to airflow velocities and particle trajectories. For instance, the air and droplet leakage through and around the surgical mask worn by a humanoid manikin were evaluated for a five-second cyclical cough (with 1,008 droplets per cycle and a maximum expectoration velocity of 5 m s−1)[46](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR46 "Dbouk, T. & Drikakis, D. On respiratory droplets and face masks. Phys. Fluids 32(6), 063303 (2020)."). The particles were distributed between 1 and 300 µm, and the filtration efficiency of the modelled surgical mask was assumed to be 91%. The simulations made it possible to identify the main locations of the leaks and evaluated the airflow velocities through these leaks to be 0.2–0.4 m s−1. The results also provided an estimate of the proportions of droplets blocked by the mask, passing through the mask and escaping through leaks. Unfortunately, the authors did not attempt to relate particle size to the nature of the leakage.

There is almost no information on wearer protection against incoming aerosols (that can also penetrate inside the mask by passing through the spaces between the wearer’s face and the mask). Between 20 and 80% of aerosols less than 1 µm[47](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR47 "Chen, C. C. & Willeke, K. Aerosol penetration through surgical masks. Am. J. Infection Control 20(4), 177–184 (1992)."), [48](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR48 "Weber, A. et al. Aerosol penetration and leakage characteristics of masks used in the health care industry. Am. J. Infection Control 21(4), 167–173 (1993)."), [49](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR49 "Bałazy, A. et al. Do N95 respirators provide 95% protection level against airborne viruses, and how adequate are surgical masks?. Am. J. Infection Control 34(2), 51–57 (2006).") or between 1 and 3 µm[50](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR50 "Oberg, T. & Brosseau, L. M. Surgical mask filter and fit performance. Am. J. Infection Control 36(4), 276–282 (2008).") pass through the mask, depending on its design, the number of layers, the material used for filtration and the airflow imposed through the mask. Very few CFD simulations have supplemented these data. For instance, a humanoid manikin limited to its head inhaled aerosols through a perfectly fitted surgical mask[51](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR51 "Xi, J., Si, X. A. & Nagarajan, R. Effects of mask-wearing on the inhalability and deposition of airborne SARS-CoV-2 aerosols in human upper airway. Phys. Fluids 32, 123312 (2020)."). The aerosols were between 1 and 20 µm, and the filtration efficiency of the mask was set to 65% for all particle sizes. The results showed that mask use clearly alters the flow near the nose and mouth. The air velocities were significantly lower and the particles entered less deeply into the respiratory tract, favouring the deposition of aerosols in the nose. Overall, wearing the mask reduced the quantity inhaled by a factor of three and five, respectively, for the 3–10 µm and 15 µm aerosols. For the aerosols of 1–3 µm in diameter, the quantity inhaled was almost the same regardless of whether a mask was worn or not. Another CFD and experimental study that featured the head of a humanoid manikin equipped with a mask[45](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR45 "Lei, Z. & Yang, J. Computing carbon dioxide and humidity in filtering face piece respirator cavity during breathing cycles. in Proceedings of the ASME 2014 International Design Engineering Technical Conferences and Computers and Information in Engineering Conference. Volume 1A: 34th Computers and Information in Engineering Conference, V01AT02A077. Buffalo, New York, USA. August 17–20, 2014 (2014).") enabled the assessment of leak sites around the perimeter of the mask and the way in which these leaks were distributed. The results indicated that, on average, leaks occurred mainly at the level of the nose (35–50%), to a lesser extent near the cheeks (20–25%) and least of all near the chin (6–12%).

Different types of masks were compared regarding their filtration efficiency during inhalation and exhalation[52](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR52 "Pan, J., Harb, C., Leng, W. & Marr, L. C. Inward and outward effectiveness of cloth masks, a surgical mask, and a face shield. Aerosol Sci. Technol. 55(6), 718–733 (2020)."). The aerosol diameters were 0.04–1 µm in the first case, and 2–5 µm in the second. The filtration efficiency was evaluated by tests on a specific test bench using a sample of the mask material (without leaks). In addition, the inhalation and exhalation protection efficiencies were determined using tests on manikins (accounting for leaks). For diameters less than 5 µm, the results depend on the diameters, with the exhalation and inhalation filtration efficiencies found to be between 25 and 75%. There is a strong deficiency in the effectiveness of the surgical mask for diameters below 2 µm, whether the wearer is inhaling or exhaling. Above 5 µm, the exhalation and inhalation efficiencies of the surgical mask were comparable (around 75%).

Finally, the air and particles expectorated by a human, which are warmer than the ambient atmosphere, may be drawn into a buoyant plume that is also generated by the warm, uncovered head of the individual. The changes in the airflows due to these thermal effects have been demonstrated in experiments involving real humans (volunteers) wearing a mask or not[39](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR39 "Tang, J. W., Liebner, T. J., Craven, B. A. & Settles, G. S. A Schlieren optical study of the human cough with and without wearing masks for aerosol infection control. J. R. Society Interface 6, S727–S736 (2009)."), [40](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR40 "Verma, S., Dhanak, M. & Frankenfield, J. Visualizing the effectiveness of face masks in obstructing respiratory jets. Phys. Fluids 32, 061708 (2020)."), [41](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR41 "Verma, S., Dhanak, M. & Frankenfield, J. Visualizing droplet dispersal for face shields and masks with exhalation valves. Phys. Fluids 32, 091701 (2020)."), [42](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR42 "Bhagat, R. K., Wykes, M. S. D., Dalziel, S. B. & Linden, P. F. Effects of ventilation on the indoor spread of COVID-19. J. Fluid Mech. 903, F1 (2020)."). They were visualised using the Schlieren process, which is based on density variations caused by the temperature differences between the lukewarm exhalation and the ambient air. The Schlieren process very clearly shows the buoyant plume and any possible leaks through a surgical mask for different behaviours of the wearer of the mask and in different situations, such as when two people are breathing or talking face to face. This makes it possible to identify the origin and extent of the leaks. Experiments were also carried out in which the particles produced by an individual wearing a surgical mask were visualised with a laser beam[39](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR39 "Tang, J. W., Liebner, T. J., Craven, B. A. & Settles, G. S. A Schlieren optical study of the human cough with and without wearing masks for aerosol infection control. J. R. Society Interface 6, S727–S736 (2009).").

### CFD simulations of the dispersion of particles expectorated by humans

Studies based on CFD simulations and devoted to the dispersion of particles loaded with infectious agents have multiplied since the COVID-19 epidemic began. They complement the much more numerous works dealing with the ventilation and thermal comfort of premises. Without being exhaustive, we propose to summarise below some of the most recent numerical studies dedicated to the dissemination of pathogenic biological agents. Although these studies are interesting, they nevertheless present limitations, which we show and which seem to us to fully justify the performance of our own CFD simulations.

Mohamadi and Fazeli[53](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR53 "Mohamadi, F. & Fazeli, A. A review on applications of CFD modeling in Covid-19 pandemic. Arch. Comput. Methods Eng. 29, 3567–3586 (2022).") have carried out a fairly exhaustive review of the applications of CFD modelling during the COVID‑19 epidemic. They emphasised that in general, CFD work aims to answer questions about social distancing and the use of face masks to limit the dispersion of virion-containing particles or about the effectiveness of ventilation and disinfection devices to prevent transmission of COVID-19. They also highlighted the wide variety of locations in which dispersion simulation applications have been carried out, mentioning an elevator, a classroom, a supermarket, a restaurant, various hospital rooms and an airplane cabin. In what follows, we take up some examples of applications of the transport and dispersion of particles loaded with virions in different types of premises by completing and updating the bibliographical review by Mohamadi and Fazeli. We first comment on articles in which no real environment, even simplified, is taken into account, before considering more or less realistic indoor environments where interpersonal contamination can occur.

Thanigaiarasu et al.[54](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR54 "Thanigaiarasu, S., Balamani, G., Bakiya, A., Immaculyne, F., Kamalanand, K. &. De Britto, R.L.J. Numerical modeling and simulation of the droplet transmission of SARS-CoV-2 in the ambient environment and its relevance to social distancing. Math. Problems Eng. 6881712 (2016).") used ANSYS Fluent to simulate the movement of particles expelled into still ambient air during a cough, sneeze or conversation with an initial velocity of 1, 5 or 10 m s−1. Additionally, the maximum distance travelled by particles with higher velocities was analysed using a linear regression model. They stated that a distance of at least 2.5 m should be maintained for effective social distancing. Dbouk and Drikakis[55](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR55 "Dbouk, T. & Drikakis, D. On coughing and airborne droplet transmission to humans. Phys. Fluids 32, 053310 (2020).") were also interested in the distance travelled by the spectrum of particles emitted during a coughing episode in an airflow at 0, 1 or 4 m s−1. They considered a Weibull distribution of particles (80 µm in average diameter) at the exit of a mouth whose shape was modelled. The simulations were carried out with Open FOAM in the Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes (RANS) formalism with the k-omega turbulence model. The dispersion of the particles was evaluated by a Lagrangian method, taking into account the phase changes of the particles and their interactions. As the authors show, the smallest particles were still suspended 6 m beyond their point of origin when carried away by the strongest airflow. There are other studies, not reported here, which, like these two examples, ignore any real geometry of indoor or outdoor space and its obstacles as well as human presence and its influence on the airflow and the dispersion of particles. Furthermore, the length of the last simulation domain was 6 m while, under certain conditions, the particles continue to progress in the air well beyond this distance. This seems to confirm that the notion of “safe distance” is difficult to define.

The following articles considered the spreading limits and pathways of drops and droplets carrying an infectious agent in a single unfurnished and ventilated room representative of an office. The simulation domains included one or two very simplified humanoid manikins with a parallelepipedic or cylindrical body and head, except in the first publication mentioned, in which the head was more finely represented. None of these manikins wore masks. Most of the time, unless otherwise stated, the flow computations were performed in the RANS formalism with k-epsilon turbulence modelling. The transport and dispersion of the particles were evaluated using a Lagrangian approach. Al-Baghdadi et al.[56](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR56 "Al-Baghdadi, M. A. R. S. CFD analysis of spread COVID-19 with air conditioning systems. Int. J. Energy Environ. 12(2), 63–72 (2021).") studied the case of a single person coughing or sneezing in an office equipped with an air-conditioning unit recirculating the same air inside the room. They insisted that the airflow can increase the spreading of particles and create a virion-laden environment. Existing ventilation systems should thus include extraction and air filtration systems and/or germicidal processes. Mills and Hamad[57](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR57 "Mills, I. & Hamad, F.A. A review and CFD case study: The effect of temperature, humidity, aerodynamics on corona virus transmission, mitigation in open and enclosed environments. Biomed. J. Sci. Tech. Res. 33(4), BJSTR. MS.ID.005444 (2020).") were interested in an office-type room occupied by one or two occupants. Their simulations with ANSYS Fluent showed that the positioning of air-conditioning systems or open windows and doors could create beneficial or harmful airflow patterns in regard to the dissemination of the virion-laden particles. Mariam et al.[58](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR58 "Mariam, A. M. et al. CFD simulation of the airborne transmission of Covid-19 vectors emitted during respiratory mechanisms: Revisiting the concept of safe distance. ACS Omega 2021(6), 16876–16889 (2021).") carried out simulations with the ANSWER CFD code, considering two humanoid manikins seated or standing face to face, with dissemination events being either discrete (coughs and sneezes emitting particles of 70–80 µm) or continuous (speaking normally and loudly at different flow rates producing particles of 1 and 5 µm). They pointed out that, unlike rapidly settling drops, the droplets were able to reach any receptor point for certain airflow patterns in the room and persist for durations of up to 30 min at certain receptor points. Thus, “safe distancing” cannot be a general infection control mitigation strategy. They also argued that a higher ventilation rate did not guarantee better removal of particles and that a ceiling-to-side wall ventilation design might be a better choice compared to a ceiling-to-ceiling configuration for both standing and sitting positions. Li et al.[59](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR59 "Li, X. et al. Airborne transmission during short-term events: Direct route over indirect route. Building Simulation 15, 2097–2110 (2022).") investigated airborne transmission of respiratory droplets during short-term events by performing large-eddy simulations (LES) with two realistic face-to-face manikins, one of which was infected. Following Nazaroff[60](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR60 "Nazaroff, W. W. Indoor bio-aerosol dynamics. Indoor Air 26, 61–78 (2016).") and Ai et al.[61](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR61 "Ai, Z. et al. Tracer gas is a suitable surrogate of exhaled droplet nuclei for studying airborne transmission in the built environment. Building Simulation 13, 489–496 (2020)."), the study used a tracer-gas approach rather than the discrete phases to transport and disperse aerosols generated by two people in close proximity breathing in an office. Evaporation was also considered. The authors reported that direct airborne transmission was predominant, suggesting that dilution ventilation had limited effectiveness in mitigating the risk of infection, but determined when indirect transmission occurred. Furthermore, precautionary measures targeting the entire space in steady state could be less effective for the short-term events, highlighting for example the benefit of a physical barrier or a face mask to block the direct exhalation jet. Hu et al.[62](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR62 "Hu, Y., Shen, J., Zhang, J.J. & Gao, Z. A CFD approach to reduce the risk of Covid-19 airborne transmission in a typical office. in IAQVEC 2023, E3S Web of Conferences 396, 01063 (2023).") used ANSYS Fluent to model two realistic humanoid manikins sitting face to face in a typical ventilated office. They tested the advantages of four different partitions of the room for limiting the dissemination of virions during coughing (particles from 0.3 to 1000 µm) and talking (particles from 0.3 to 10 µm). They pointed out that the barriers captured particles larger than 50 µm and that smaller particles spread throughout the room. The cough jet slowed the upward buoyant plume generated by the human body, which was not observed with the low velocity through the mouth in the case of speaking. Finally, the authors observed that the partition reduced the risk from 100 to 20% with a single panel or semi-wrapped structure, or to less than 1% with a fully wrapped structure.

Among the particular cases of digital twins of premises, the literature includes numerous classroom case studies, likely because of the high risk of contamination with respiratory diseases between the students and their teacher. Most of the time, the flow calculations are carried out in the RANS formalism with a k-epsilon turbulence model and its variants such as, in particular, the realisable or renormalisation group (RNG) models, while the dispersion calculations are carried out with a Lagrangian approach to tracking particle trajectories. While the simulations generally take into account humanoid manikins, these are usually excessively simplified and do not wear masks. So, for example, Abuhegazy et al.[63](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR63 "Abuhegazy, M., Talaat, K., Anderoglu, O. & Poroseva, S. V. Numerical investigation of aerosol transport in a classroom with relevance to COVID-19. Phys. Fluids 32, 103311 (2020).") made use of ANSYS Fluent to simulate the distribution and deposition of particles from 1 to 50 µm in a classroom equipped with air conditioning. One student was assumed to emit particles, with no mention being made regarding the duration of the release. The particles with diameters of less than 15 µm mostly left the room through the extraction vents, while a large proportion of the particles with diameters greater than 20 µm settled on the accessible surfaces, including glass barriers. The results acutely depend on the shape of the obstacles, which include the individuals. Mirzaie et al.[64](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR64 "Mirzaie, M. et al. Covid-19 spread in a classroom equipped with partition—A CFD approach. J. Hazardous Mater. 420, 126587 (2021).") considered a teacher who coughed in front of a class. The numerical model was validated and used to study the effects of ventilation airflow velocities of 3, 5 and 7 m s−1 (entering through the inlet in a wall close to the ceiling and exiting through the open door of the classroom) on the dispersion of particles of diameter 0.15, 1, 10, 50, 100 and 150 µm. The effect of installing transparent solid barriers in front of seats on reducing the average particle concentration was examined. As expected, in the absence of partitions, the seats closest to the infected person had the highest average particle concentration. Seats in a classroom with partitions were exposed to a lower average particle concentration than a room without partitions. The average particle concentration in space decreased over time for all ventilation cases. The time required to reach a negligible particle concentration was longer without a partition than with a partition and decreased with increasing ventilation velocity. Interestingly, at a given time after their emission, the number of small suspended particles diminished as the ventilation velocity increased from 3 to 5 m s−1, but increased between 5 and 7 m s−1. The simulation therefore highlighted that an increase in air speed could have counter-intuitive effects on the spatio-temporal distribution of particles, depending on the geometry of the premises and the way in which they are ventilated. El-Shimi et al.[65](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR65 "El-Shimi, M., Morcos, S., Mostafa, G., Khalil, E.E., El-Hariry, G. & El-Degwy, A. A novel transparent cabin used in the classroom during the coronavirus pandemic: A CFD analysis. J. Eng. Appl. Sci. 71.10.1186, s44147-023-00316-3 (2024).") sought to optimise an air-conditioning configuration, with or without transparent cubicles around students, with open roofs or a HEPA filter to remove particles disseminated by an infected student breathing or sneezing in a classroom. They designed a large series of numerical experiments based on the ANSYS 2022 R2 software package, showing that optimal ventilation occurred with fresh air entering through side walls 1 m from the floor and stale air carrying particles loaded with virions exiting through the ceiling. They also demonstrated that the number of particles present in the airflow was inversely correlated with the hourly air exchange volume in the classroom. The presence of cabins had a strong effect on controlling the spread of the particles in the classroom: the faster the fan sucked in the air carrying the particles, the fewer particles there were inside the classroom. The particles were directed up and out of exit holes in the ceiling, with the air coming out pure to the rest of the class when filters were installed atop the cubicles. Jain et al.[66](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR66 "Jain, A., Duill, F. F., Schulz, F., Beyrau, F. & vanWachem, B. Numerical study on the impact of large air purifiers, physical distancing, and mask wearing in classrooms. Atmosphere 14, 716 (2023).") rightly pointed out that classrooms often do not have pre-installed ventilation and can be occupied by large numbers of students breathing while their teacher speaks loudly at the same time. Since it would be extremely complicated to experimentally carry out all or even just one of these configurations, they simulated the transport of dispersion of virulent particles by varying room occupancy as well as location, volumetric flow rate, and efficiency of an air purifier installed in the classroom. The location of the air purifier had a great influence on the dissemination of particles, except for the person sitting next to the student assumed to be infected, while the use of the physical distancing method by limiting the capacity of the half class would also be effective. Jain et al. did not develop an explicit model of respiratory mask, but tried to take into account its effectiveness during inhalation. They showed that when students wore masks, the risk of infection was comparable in a classroom operating at half or full capacity.

Other numerical studies, although much rarer, focus on more complex spaces than a simple room. For example, Ho[67](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR67 "Ho, C. K. Modelling airborne transmission and ventilation impacts of a Covid-19 outbreak in a restaurant in Guangzhou, China. Int. J. Comput. Fluid Dynamics 35(9), 708–726 (2021).") performed CFD modelling to simulate the spatial and temporal distribution of airborne pathogens during an observed COVID-19 outbreak in a restaurant in Guangzhou (China). While LES provides high-fidelity modelling to simulate time-varying turbulent processes initiated by ventilation and/or expiratory events (see Vuorinen et al.[68](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR68 "Vuorinen, V. et al. Modelling aerosol transport and virus exposure with numerical simulations in relation to SARS-CoV-2 transmission by inhalation indoors. Safety Sci. 130, 104866 (2020).")), Ho highlights the enormous cost of calculation of such a method, which is unsuitable for evaluating a large number of scenarios. The author rightly points out that trends in concentration and exposure as a function of time and location are adequately captured by the time-averaged turbulence models of the RANS formalism. This is particularly true when the objective is to compare the risks of different configurations or scenarios rather than to make absolute predictions. In the study in question, Solidworks software was implemented with a k-epsilon airflow turbulence model and a Lagrangian dispersion model. Ho simulated the dissemination of aerosols caused by the breathing of an infected customer by taking into account various configurations of tables and seats as well as the ventilation conditions. As expected, poor ventilation and recirculation increased pathogen concentration and thus the risk of infection. On the contrary, increasing the proportion of fresh air to 10, 50 or 100% of the total amount of air blown reduced the pathogen mass accumulated in the room over 73 min on average by about 30, 70 or 80%, respectively. Interestingly, the simulations were inspired by a real situation that occurred in the restaurant. The numerical results showed trends in simulated infection risk that were consistent with the observed infection rates reported by Lu et al.[69](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR69 "Lu, J. Y. et al. Covid-19 outbreak associated with air conditioning in restaurant, Guangzhou, China. Emerg. Infectious Diseases 26(7), 1628–1631 (2020).") at each of the tables surrounding the infected customer. Finally, Ho’s study is particularly effective at showing the possibilities offered by CFD when it comes to varying the details of the interior design (notably the furniture) of a complex ventilated space and making recommendations specific to this space, such as moving an internal element or modifying a ventilation parameter. Khodadadi et al.[70](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR70 "Khodadadi, A.R., Pournasr, A., Hataysal, E. & Tabarra, M. Investigation of airborne particle spread in an underground station. ISAVFT 19 (2022).") were interested in another type of ventilated semi-confined space. Specifically, they investigated the spread of particles coming from an infected passenger in a metro station with the aim of better understanding the epidemic risk for users of public transport. They performed simulations with ANSYS Fluent in the RANS formalism using k-omega turbulence modelling of the airflow and an Eulerian dispersion approach for the particle phase. They considered a simplified breathing humanoid manikin located at alternative positions on the platform or in the escalator of a metro station equipped with full-height screen doors (removing any piston effect from the trains). Two different supply ventilation conditions characterised by the air exchange rate were studied. At all station locations, the maximum volume concentration of aerosol particles as well as their travel time through the station were reduced with a higher air exchange rate. This CFD analysis proved invaluable, primarily in identifying the areas of the station (e.g., cross-platform passage corridors) with the highest particle concentration levels due to recirculating eddies and air stagnation.

The ventilated interior space of vehicles has also been the subject of a limited number of studies of the transport and dispersion of particles carrying infectious agents, in particular SARS-CoV-2. We pay special attention to these few works in our literature review. For instance, Duchaine et al.[71](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR71 "Duchaîne, F. et al. High-performance CFD for respiratory droplet turbulent dispersion in a ventilated city bus. Int. J. Comput. Fluid Dynamics 35(9), 758–777 (2021).") implemented the LES AVBP solver to predict the turbulent flow in a city bus for different operating conditions of the air-conditioning system. They performed Lagrangian tracking of particles on statically converged Eulerian flow fields to investigate their turbulent dispersion as a function of the position of the emitter in the bus. Four passengers and the driver, none of them wearing masks, were accounted for, with the passengers in the middle or rear of the bus or the driver releasing particles of 0.1–1000 µm when breathing or talking. The bus had a canonical geometry to represent the typical scale, aspect ratio and shape of such a vehicle seen in a city. It had two HVAC (heating, ventilation and air-conditioning) systems dedicated either to maintaining visibility via a defrost ducting and heating system at the driver’s location, or to recirculating the airflow and injecting fresh air to heat or cool the passenger cabin. Duchaine et al. highlighted the drastic impact of ventilation conditions on air circulation in the bus as well as on turbulence levels. They clearly illustrated the decisive influence of the flow patterns generated by ventilation systems on the trajectories and concentrations of particles in the bus. Finally, they noted the considerable merit that CFD simulations could have for evaluating the effectiveness of HEPA filtration and UV disinfection when particles are recirculated in the bus. While this study is undoubtedly valuable, we nevertheless question the usefulness of the high-fidelity LES model and the extremely expensive calculations when the authors only consider average speed and turbulence fields that could be obtained in the much less time-consuming RANS formalism. Furthermore, Duchaine et al. neglected the evaporation and buoyant plume phenomena. As we mentioned and justified previously in this article, it is legitimate not to take evaporation into account. On the other hand, as recognized by Duchaine et al. with ventilation systems producing low air velocities, it would be necessary to study the effect of buoyancy due to heat released by people as well as the wall and window temperatures.

Wu et al.[72](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR72 "Wu, F., Yu, C., Xu, R. & Li, H. Numerical simulation of droplet dispersion and passenger infection risk assessment in a subway carriage. Appl. Sci. 14, 590 (2024).") simulated turbulent airflow with a k-epsilon RNG model as well as the trajectories of Lagrangian particles emitted during breathing and coughing in the digital twin of a life-size metro coach. The objective was to understand the mechanisms of dispersion of pathogen-laden expiratory particles and to develop ventilation strategies in order to reduce the risk of respiratory disease transmission. Appropriately, Wu et al. pointed out that the interior space of a metro coach is very complex, notably due to the high density of passengers and the diverse operating conditions, compared to the environments considered in other research works. The simulations investigated the impact of several parameters on the transport and diffusion of particles. These parameters included particle size of 10, 50 or 100 µm; release velocity of 1 or 10 m s−1; and passenger density of 2 or 4 per m2 with two positions of the infected individual. While 28 seated passengers and 68 or 124 standing passengers were accounted for, the humanoid manikins were extremely simplified and did not wear masks. As main results, the study showed that 100-μm drops settled on the coach floor before they evaporated, while 10-μm droplets evaporated quickly, had a long suspension time and spread widely in the metro coach. The ventilation system was more or less effective in eliminating droplets emitted by the infected manikin depending on its position. As expected, high passenger density resulted in more droplets being trapped by passengers’ bodies, suggesting that the number of individuals in the metro coach should be limited. For their part, Xu et al.[73](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR73 "Xu, J. et al. Influences of the fresh air volume on the removal of cough-released droplets in a passenger car of a high-speed train using CFD. Comput. Model. Eng. Sci. 138(3), 2727–2748 (2024).") used ANSYS Fluent software to calculate turbulent airflow with the realisable k-epsilon model, and the Lagrangian trajectories of particles expectorated by an individual travelling on a high-speed train. They noted the lack of research articles investigating the impact of ventilation on flow field structure and particle removal efficiency in trains. This is what motivated the development of the digital twin of a train coach and passengers represented by simplified humanoid manikins not wearing masks. The infected individual was seated in the front, middle or rear of the coach and emitted cough particles of 10, 50 and 100 µm. In the study, Xu et al. discussed the influence of ventilation flow rate on the dissemination of the particles in the coach. First, they observed that the particle removal trends were similar when the air supply was doubled. However, the large vortices were more stable, and the faster airflow captured and retained particles more strongly as the ventilation rate increased. They determined that, given the ventilation configuration, the risk of infection was lower in the front than in the rear area of the coach. These results provide information on the transmission of infectious respiratory diseases and guidance for air supply methods in a railway coach. Like the previous authors, Yun and Kim[74](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR74 "Yun, S. & Kim, J. C. Numerical evaluation of a novel vertical drop airflow system to mitigate droplet transmission in trains. Atmosphere 13, 829 (2022).") share the opinion that studies on preventing infections caused by particle transmission in public transportation remain insufficient. Therefore, they performed ANSYS CFX-based simulations of turbulent airflow with a shear stress transport (SST) model (combining k-epsilon and k-omega models) as well as Lagrangian trajectories of particles from 3 to 750 µm in the Korean high-speed train (KTX). They considered a detailed humanoid manikin either without a mask or with a well-fitted mask assimilated to a medium with porosity equal to 0.88. In this study, Yun and Kim[74](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR74 "Yun, S. & Kim, J. C. Numerical evaluation of a novel vertical drop airflow system to mitigate droplet transmission in trains. Atmosphere 13, 829 (2022).") proposed a vertical drop airflow (VDA) designed with twenty inlets and outlets for each seat on the ceiling and floor of the coach to restrict the flow circulation and mitigate the effect of the dissemination of particles in the coach. According to the numerical results, the system reduced both the number of particles and the transmission of particles in the train coach, which would be effective in mitigating the risk of infection. As expected, wearing a well-fitted mask significantly decreased the number of particles in the coach, although the authors did not provide any information on mask effectiveness based on particle size.

In summary, the studies described above represent a valuable illustration of the potential of CFD and show the importance of ventilation in relation to the geometry of semi-confined rooms in the dissemination of virion-laden particles. These studies, however, often suffer from serious limitations regarding the realism of the semi-confined space modelled and, mainly, of the humanoid manikins populating this space, which are usually simplistic and do not wear masks (except in the last study mentioned). This proves that there is still a lot of room to carry out simulations using CFD software. Unlike most of the studies presented in this review, we have chosen to pay particular attention to the digital twin used in our simulations in terms of both the ventilated geometric entity and the humanoid manikins, which either wear a mask or not, and which wear it more or less tightly (with leakage). The case study that we have developed herein corresponds to a public railway transport coach, in which a passenger infected with a pathogenic biological agent is seated and possibly contaminates other travellers, as presented in the rest of the article.

## Results

As mentioned in the introduction, our modelling and simulation work aims at a better understanding of the airborne transmission of viruses such as SARS-CoV-2 carried by drops and droplets produced by human respiratory events. While our previous results[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023).") were obtained by assuming isothermal flow, other numerical works available in the literature as experiments that have tracked the particles released during respiratory events, have highlighted the generation of buoyant plumes influencing the trajectories and distributions of the emitted droplets. Therefore, the present article focuses on the inclusion of thermal effects in the 3D turbulent flow and dispersion of droplets produced by the breathing of a contaminated manikin, considered first on its own in a simulation domain to illustrate and evaluate the thermal phenomena, and then placed in a railway coach among other passengers to resume our initial practical application presented in this trilogy of articles. Later in the article, the “ [Methods](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Sec13)” section describes in detail the anisothermal flow and dispersion model and the associated assumptions. It also provides some reminders on the modelling of masks that are more or less well worn. All simulation results are presented hereafter.

### Distribution of droplets around the head and bust of a warm manikin

In our first series of computations, the head and bust of the manikin were placed in a parallelepipedic simulation domain representing a still atmosphere. The size of the domain was chosen to be large enough to respect good CFD practices: 1.7 m long, 1.4 m wide and 0.7 m high. Figure [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Fig1) shows the mesh of the manikin associated with three ways of wearing the mask or not. The meshes consist of tetrahedral cells (around 500,000 cells in case #1, 246,000 cells in case #2, and 672,000 cells in case #3), with the size of the cells on the surface of the manikin varying between 3 mm and 1 cm. The maximum cell size is 5 cm on the surfaces of the simulation domain. The quality of the three meshes was assessed, with at least 99% of the cells having a skewness of less than 0.001.

### Modelling of a railway coach populated with humanoid manikins

The 3D numerical study of an isolated humanoid manikin demonstrates that our CFD model is able to account for the effects on airflow and droplet dispersion near and above an individual that is warmer than its environment. In this section, the series of calculations involving the humanoid manikin is supplemented by a practical application that serves as a common thread running also through our previous articles[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023)."). Regional rail transport networks, as well as metro networks in urban areas, put millions of commuters into contact with each other every day. Even if the strongest successive waves of the COVID-19 epidemic now seem to have passed, it remains a major global public health issue to know how an airborne pathogen present in the particles emitted by infected passengers (whether or not they wear a facial mask according to the rules in force locally) can spread in the semi-confined ventilated space of a railway coach.

While there exists a wide variety of manufacturers and models of rail coaches, we have considered the same generic carriage as in our prior studies[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Once more, our objective is to demonstrate the feasibility of the 3D numerical study, and not to designate a particular rail coach whose configuration would be specifically conducive to disseminating viruses or to preventing their dissemination. As our approach can be implemented for any type of rail transport, new calculations could be performed after consulting local or national railway companies to obtain information about the specific rail coaches operated in their cities or countries. The 3D digital twin of a typical commuter train was created using high-precision data from the [www.turboquid.com](http://www.turboquid.com/) website. ### Initialisation of the airflow in the rail coach and around the passengers

The turbulent airflow in the coach is generated by a ventilation system, which works in the same way as in a real railway coach. The ventilation takes place from bottom to top and from the ends of the coach to slots located in the ceiling of the coach. We assume that only fresh air is supplied from the outside of each end of the carriage. The ventilation of the rail coach involves the entire space of the carriage. The renewal rate of the air is 8.7 or, in other words, the air is refreshed every 7 min. The interested reader can find more details in the “ [Methods](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Sec13)” section. Although such a ventilation system is quite common, it can be different depending on the rail coach. Other simulations could therefore be considered accounting for alternative blowing or extracting air vents.

The ventilation features were translated into inlet and outlet boundary conditions to allow airflow simulations to be carried out. First, we computed the stationary solution of the 3D turbulent flow due to the ventilation before the breathing of the passengers was considered. This initial aerodynamic simulation was, of course, not influenced by whether or not the passengers wear a mask. Unlike our prior numerical studies[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023).") carried out assuming isothermal conditions, we improved the realism of the airflow computations by considering humanoid manikins warmer than the environment of the coach, which generates free convection and radiation effects. The airflow was therefore now a combination of ventilation in the coach and thermal phenomena induced by the passengers represented by the manikins. Details about flow modelling are given in the “ [Methods](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Sec13)” section. As shown in Fig. [17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Fig17), all surfaces in the simulation domain are at a temperature of 20 °C, except the passengers’ heads (and masks if any), which are at 32 °C. Subsequently, we present some original results of the anisothermal flow calculation.

### Simulation of the unsteady airflow and dispersion of droplets

In the next step of the numerical study, we performed transient computations of the airflow and dispersion of the particles emitted by passengers in the course of dissemination events. Hereafter, we focus on passengers wearing perfectly fitted masks among whom an infected passenger is breathing, thereby producing droplets of around 1 µm in aerodynamic diameter. More details on unsteady flow and dispersion modelling are given in the “ [Methods](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Sec13)” section of the article. The position of the spreader in the coach is identical to that defined in the previous numerical studies[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023).") **,** with thermal phenomena now being taken into account. The passenger’s breathing follows a five-second cycle with two phases of exhalation and inhalation of approximately 2 s each, interrupted by a pause of around 1 s. See more explanations in the “ [Methods](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Sec13)” section. In this study, as in the previous one, the simulated sequence lasts 600 s, which is a sufficient duration for droplets to spread throughout compartment #1, where the manikin is seated in the railway coach, and to approach the stationary regime between production of droplets by the breathing of the masked infected manikin, distribution of the droplets in the compartment and evacuation of droplets by the ventilation of this same compartment.

Figure [21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Fig21) shows 2D vertical sections of the velocity modulus and temperature fields across the supposedly infected humanoid manikin at different times between t = 0 s and t = 8 s. The initial instant corresponds to the beginning of the respiratory cycle and thus the dissemination event, which is in this case the breathing of the manikin. These visualisations highlight the buoyant plumes generated above the passengers. From t = 1 s, the warmer air (34 °C) exhaled by the spreader is quickly caught in the buoyant plume of this passenger and immediately carried upwards. It is a key point to note that the jet of exhaled air has no impact on the passenger opposite the spreader.

It should be recalled that the previous results were obtained for perfect mask use on the face of the infected passenger who disseminates the pathogen. For the sake of brevity, the case of the leaky mask is not presented in the article. Indeed, the simulations show that the flow and dispersion are close to those observed for a well-fitted mask. One can observe a significant reduction in the velocity of the air and droplets emitted through the mask. Velocities through leaks at the nose, cheeks and chin are also low. Thus, the exhaled air and the droplets, which are warmer than the ambient atmosphere, are drawn into the buoyant plume that is created above the passenger. Whether the mask is worn perfectly or loosely, the droplets rise to the upper part of the railway coach, where they are then carried away by the ventilation. In addition, the airflow towards the passenger seated opposite the infected individual is greatly reduced compared to when a mask is not worn. The consideration of thermal effects accentuates the trend already observed when thermal effects are not taken into account: the droplets occupy a significantly lower proportion of the railway coach volume when the mask is worn (correctly or with leaks). It therefore appears that wearing a mask is desirable in all cases, including when the mask does not adhere perfectly to the face.

## Discussion

The research work presented in this article is an original extension of previous publications[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023)."), with this series aiming generally to highlight the relevance and value of CFD modelling and simulation to better understand and better control the airborne transmission of infectious agents such as SARS-CoV-2. On one hand, our studies demonstrate that the phenomenology of turbulent flow and dispersion of drops and droplets emitted by a humanoid manikin during respiratory events is correctly represented by the physical model. On the other hand, they focus on achieving a practical application of the capabilities of the CFD model known as Code\_SATURNE that we used for the computations. The literature review shows that only very limited research has been carried out on the distribution of particles in the coaches of metro and commuter rail networks. The long and narrow structure of railway coaches sets them apart from conventional buildings. Given their role as a pivotal mode of transportation, we believed that it would be a valuable challenge to perform original CFD simulations in this type of vehicle, which brings together millions of passengers daily, all over the world.

We implemented the 3D digital twin of a coach with common features regarding its geometry and ventilation. Next, we simulated the turbulent flow of air inside the ventilated semi-confined space of the rail coach populated with humanoid manikins, as well as the dispersion of particles produced by an individual’s coughing and breathing. One of the passengers seated in the coach, who is presumed to be infected, produces liquid particles with a diameter ranging from 1 to 1000 µm depending on the respiratory event. These particles carry the pathological biological agent, which is transported and dispersed throughout the rail coach, putting other passengers who inhale airborne particles at risk of contamination.

The modelling was completed by the development of a flexible method enabling the CFD simulations to account for the wearing of a surgical mask by humanoid manikins, in particular the spreader passenger. This model relates to the dynamics of breathing and coughing and the filtration efficiency of the mask upon exhalation and inhalation, by considering the diameter of the expectorated particles and by distinguishing between the case in which the mask is perfectly fitted and that in which the mask is worn loosely with leaks between the face of the wearer and the mask itself. The assumptions and parameters of the masked manikin model were determined through an extensive bibliography dealing with particles produced by humans and also with the use of surgical masks.

This article describes the final step of our modelling approach, which consisted in integrating the heat exchanges by convection and radiation between the humanoid manikins and the coach. Importantly, the passengers are assumed to be warmer than their environment. This results in buoyant plumes forming near and above the individuals. This new 3D numerical study was carried out in two stages. First, dispersion simulations were performed by considering the head and bust of a breathing manikin immersed in an atmosphere at rest in order to test and verify the consistency of the flow and dispersion results obtained with the model accounting for thermal effects with regard to experiments described in the literature. Three situations were considered: the manikin did not wear a mask, it wore a tight-fitting mask, or it wore a leaky mask. Next, breathing manikins with a perfectly-fitted mask, including the spreader passenger, were introduced into the digital twin of the rail coach, and flow and dispersion computations were carried out. The space and time distribution of droplets produced by the infected manikin, as well as the droplet number history, were assessed in the interior volume of the coach. Finally, the great influence of taking into account thermal effects on the dispersion of particles was discussed in combination with the very significant link between mask use and the number of particles occupying the railway coach.

The main achievements of our numerical study are summarised hereafter:

- In this article, we have provided an update of the bibliographic review and scientific data on the production of particles by humans during respiratory events. We were mainly interested in breathing (but also, although this was not presented in the article, in speaking, coughing and sneezing, which could be further studied in future work). We focused on experimental tests showing the significant influence of thermal effects on the distribution of particles around and above the individual emitting them. We have also very comprehensively updated the review of modelling and simulation work on the dispersion of particles caused by breathing and other dissemination events in ventilated semi-confined spaces. It appears that 3D numerical studies taking into account the wearing of an individual mask are rare, with even fewer studies involving applications to means of transport, which justifies the contribution that we wished to make to this field. In the work presented, we combined realistic data on the geometry and ventilation of a commuter train, the humanoid manikins that populate it and a mask model based on filtration efficiency and characterisation of the flow around it, including in the presence of leaks, to account for the phenomena of free convection and radiation due to the manikins. Of course, our numerical study is in essence multi-parametric, and these parameters could be modified in subsequent studies by considering another train model, other respiratory events or even other experimental data on particle generation or mask effectiveness.

- Steady and unsteady 3D numerical simulations were carried out on finite volumes using the CFD software known as Code\_SATURNE, which we chose because of its capacity for in-depth validation. As for the turbulent flow inside the railway coach, we simultaneously took into account the forced mechanical ventilation, the breathing (or any other dissemination event) of the passengers represented by manikins and the buoyant plumes generated by these passengers. Regarding the dispersion of drops and droplets associated with respiratory events, we used both a Lagrangian approach and an Eulerian approach (calculations not presented in the present article). In a previous study, we showed the remarkable agreement of the results produced by either one of these approaches. We therefore verified that CFD and, in particular, Code\_SATURNE and the models that we developed, were perfectly suitable for simulating the dispersion of infectious agents in confined and ventilated spaces. It should be recalled that, in our previous articles, we notably highlighted the fundamentally different aerodynamic behaviour of droplets, which constitute an aerosol that follows the flow almost perfectly, and that of drops, which move away from the flow due to their inertia and their deposition by sedimentation.

- The 3D numerical results were post-processed to produce educational graphical representations in the form of images and videos, including in particular the history of the spatial distribution of particles emitted by the infected passenger. Although ancillary from a research point of view, this aspect is important when it comes to exporting scientific results, for example to health authorities who are not familiar with modelling and simulation, and indeed this is one of the objectives of our study.

- In this article, we focused on taking into account heat exchanges by radiation and free convection, this latter mode being predominant in the applications discussed. The results of the simulations highlight the positively buoyant plume, which is well described in the scientific literature on the subject and which forms near and above manikins that are warmer than their environment. When droplets are emitted from a manikin, the aerosol is carried upwards by thermal effects. Furthermore, simulations show that wearing a mask amplifies this phenomenon because it greatly reduces the velocity of the air jet exhaled by individuals during the respiratory cycle. This jet velocity then being low compared to the vertical component of the ascent velocity, the exhaled warm air reinforces the buoyancy phenomenon by carrying the aerosol emitted by the contagious individual. Our simulations very clearly trace the role of the mask, which was mentioned in our review and highlighted by various experimental studies. The mask makes it possible to limit contamination from one individual to another when they are placed face to face and to reduce the number of airborne droplets and the extent of their dissemination from a contagious individual, provided, however, that the air extractions are placed in the upper part of the ventilated environment studied. This is especially true if the mask is worn correctly, but it remains applicable even if the mask is worn in more realistic conditions, i.e., with leaks around the face. This reduction comes from the filtration of the air exhaled by the mask and the decrease in the velocity of the air passing through the mask. In this regard, it should be remembered that in the event of a cough, the mask, whether worn more or less well, blocks the drops and slows down the droplets, reducing the spectrum of particles associated with this dissemination event and bringing it closer to the breathing situation in which only droplets are emitted at low velocity[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023)."). When integrating the effects of free convection, CFD modelling confirms the benefit of wearing a mask even loosely. Furthermore, as aerosols are carried upwards by the buoyant plume, it appears particularly relevant to provide effective air extractions in the upper part of confined environments, whether in public transport, professional premises, schools or other places. It must be repeated that the risk reduction observed in our study is effective only because the ventilation strategy led to placing the air extractions on the ceiling of the coach. A different arrangement of the air injection and extraction devices would result in a different distribution of droplets in the coach. To assess the health risk of a given confined environment, it therefore appears essential to carefully study airflows using CFD modelling, while integrating the effects of free convection and buoyant plume phenomena.

- In our simulations, the benefit of the mask is not only proved but quantified under the conditions and assumptions of the study performed. To our knowledge, this has never been done before. Even if this quantification depends on the ventilated space considered, the location of the disseminating individual and the filtration efficiency of the mask, we are now able to propose a CFD-based method allowing this quantification. In this regard, the results presented in this article, compared to the results in the absence of thermal modelling, show a significant reduction in the number of droplets suspended in the coach. This reduction of about a factor of five is due to the very effective entrainment of droplets towards the upper part of the railway coach and their rapid extraction by the ventilation system located on the ceiling. Comparison with the results obtained when passengers are not masked demonstrates a very significant reduction in the risk of contamination when a mask is worn. However, we must remember that masks, even when properly worn, allow droplets to pass through and remain in suspension in the confined atmosphere of the coach. We should therefore emphasise that wearing a mask is recommended because it strongly limits contamination by respiratory disease agents, but it does not completely prevent the risk of infection.

- Our numerical work appears to represent a useful, relevant application of CFD to the propagation of an infectious agent in the ventilated space of a railway coach. More generally, it demonstrates the potential of 3D simulation to explain complex phenomenology and to identify strategies to respond, in the situation addressed, to a public health problem. Because they are less expensive and easier to carry out than experiments, modelling and simulation using validated, proven software like Code\_SATURNE offer unlimited opportunities to study complex threat scenarios with high societal stakes, whether or not these scenarios are biological in origin.


Our research work offers several scientific perspectives that are described hereafter.

- This study implements the Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes equations (RANS) and a k-epsilon type turbulence model, which constitutes a robust approach that is relatively economical in computational cost and is widely validated, notably through its use of Code\_SATURNE. Alternatively, large-eddy simulations (LES) could be carried out, provided that much more computer resources are used. While the averaged results would undoubtedly be similar, the LES would provide more detailed information on the space and time fluctuations of the turbulent flow and on phenomena affecting particles such as propagation, mixing and dilution. The respective advantages of the RANS and LES methods could be established by comparing the results obtained with each approach.

- Though our study now takes into account thermal phenomena influencing airflow and particle dispersion, we have not considered mass transfers during the evaporation of liquid particles or condensation on these particles. If we were to account for this, we would obtain the evolution of the sizes of the drops or droplets when they are transported and dispersed in a ventilated space. This would make it possible to study the influence of ambient temperature and humidity on the transmission of airborne pathogens in a more comprehensive manner than previously carried out. We should nevertheless note that the droplets, if they evaporate, lead to a dry residue corresponding to one or a few virions with a size of around 100 nm. As the dynamics of particles between 0.1 and a few micrometres is similar, considering the evaporation of droplets should not significantly modify their spatial and temporal distribution. Moreover, it would be worthwhile to confirm that processes specific to aerosol physics such as agglomeration, thermophoresis or diffusiophoresis have a negligible role in the practical applications that we consider.

- Our numerical work examined the modelling of dynamic and thermal phenomena influencing airflow and the dispersion of suspended particles. Subsequently, it would be very interesting to integrate into our model certain aspects of the biology of airborne pathogens such as the COVID-19 virus. Thus, we could try to estimate the virion load in the droplets and drops produced by the infected passenger depending on the stage of the illness, determine the number of droplets and therefore virions inhaled by people in the vicinity of the spreader or take into account the evolution of the pathogenicity of virions during their aerial transport in liquid or dried particles and once the particles are deposited. This update of our model would require cooperation with biologists specialised in infectious diseases.

- Our study was limited to a distribution of identical humanoid manikins in the railway coach. Indeed, we focused on the feasibility of simulating the dissemination of particles in a ventilated space while considering a reasonable yet perfectible degree of realism of the railway coach and the manikins populating it. It would be possible to introduce more diverse manikins into the digital twin through the presence of men, women and children. Likewise, we could without significant difficulty consider seated and standing passengers, and the passenger spreader could be positioned in another location in the coach. The spreader could speak or sneeze, processes which have not been studied numerically due to lack of time. Furthermore, we preferred to consider a plausible number of particles produced by different dissemination events and treat the different particle sizes separately. In fact, the literature reveals a wide variety of observations and a current lack of consensus on the spectrum of particles generated, for example, by coughing. That said, we could just as easily take into account particle size spectra associated with the different dissemination events. It should be noted that, in this article, we have favoured the case of breathing, in particular through a mask, which selects the droplets and limits the usefulness of considering a size spectrum of the particles emitted by the infected manikin.

- Finally, in a very prospective way, we would like to mention the value of carrying out 3D calculations by varying the position of the spreader manikin sitting or standing in the railway coach, the more or less well-adjusted wearing of its mask and the droplets emitted depending on the respiratory events. The database of results of the space and time distribution of particles would be used to train an artificial intelligence algorithm, typically a deep neural network. The exploitation of this algorithm would then provide almost instantaneous information on the distribution of particles in a given situation (such as placing the spreader in a particular position), enable multi-parametric sensitivity studies and allow an estimation of the uncertainties in the distribution of particles propagating in the railway coach.


Also, on a practical level, this work could initiate several very useful and instructive developments.

- Even though the geometry and ventilation of the railway coach considered in our simulations are representative, there are nevertheless numerous models of trains and rail coaches. It would therefore be particularly interesting for railway companies to carry out simulations comparable to those presented in the article using geometric and HVAC data from trains and coaches circulating on their networks. We must also emphasise that although the ventilation conditions in the calculations carried out are realistic, they probably do not strictly correspond to the conditions actually encountered in public transport. In particular, the operation of an air-conditioning system usually involves partial recycling of the air passing through the coach. If it does not include a sterilising treatment or an effective filtration system, for example a high-efficiency particulate absorbing (HEPA) filter, the air recycling has the effect of accelerating the dispersion of aerosols in the coach. In future, beyond the framework of this generic study, this could be considered on a case-by-case basis using data pertaining to the fractions of fresh and recycled air and to the filters and air purification systems in place. More generally, it would certainly be very profitable to refine the results and orientations arising from this study and adapt them to real train models and coaches of global rail networks.

- It could also be interesting to study the effect of modifications to the internal configuration of the railway coach studied (or any other coach) on airflow and the space and time distribution of particles. Modifications could include, for example, turning seats around to face the windows or installing partition walls between rows of seats. It would thus be possible to determine whether a particular configuration would limit the dissemination of particles (and to what extent) and whether it would be worth implementing. The interest in simulation noted in the first epidemic periods of COVID-19 could be revived in the event of new pandemics.

- The method developed in our work could easily be transposed to other types of masks (notably the FFP2 mask made compulsory in certain countries at the height of the COVID-19 pandemic) by adapting the geometry of the mask, its filtration efficiency and its leakage parameters. Furthermore, the approach that we have developed and the software implemented could be used for other types of more or less confined and ventilated spaces, whether large or small, that are intended for private or collective use. Thus, we could simulate the dissemination of biological agents or any other chemical or radiological agents in other means of transport (planes or boats), performance halls, restaurants, nurseries, school rooms or open-space offices, to name just a few. Numerical simulation would be very valuable for deriving informative results on the optimisation of ventilation and the internal configuration of premises in order to reduce the transmission of harmful agents regardless of their nature. It should also make it possible to assess the criticality of the gathering of people, some of whom carry infectious agents, in semi-confined spaces such as those given as examples, and to provide general guidelines and recommendations in view of stemming the dissemination of infectious agents such as SARS-CoV-2 .


## Methods

The principle of our study is to use a reliable CFD tool that has proved its ability to model mixed convection flows generated by ventilation and temperature differences between humanoid manikins and the semi-confined environment of a railway coach as well as the dispersion of droplets and drops produced by the manikins. Our approach is legitimate because the exploited software has been carefully validated in comparable situations of aerosol dispersion in laminar and turbulent flows. It is important to note that our digital experiments are likely to at least partially replace experimental tests that would be very complicated to carry out in a real train car with manikins or real passengers wearing a mask or not. The numerical study involved a series of steps including choices related to modelling, the search for available relevant data, the development of a digital twin of a railway coach occupied by humanoid manikins wearing masks or not, flow and dispersion simulations and, finally, the post-processing of the results to make them easily usable. Some of the modelling and data aspects are mentioned in this section, which first provides essential information on the simulations carried out in this study, then deals successively with the ventilation in the railway coach, the particle source terms associated with the breathing of human beings wearing or not a fitted or leaky mask, and the thermal conditions adopted for the simulations.

### Presentation and use of Code\_SATURNE

The simulations presented in this article were performed using Code\_SATURNE[75](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR75 "                   https://www.code-saturne.org/cms/web/                                    ") (version 7.0), a general-purpose, open-source CFD computational tool developed by the R&D division of the French electricity supplier EDF and by the Atmospheric Environment Teaching and Research Centre (CEREA) in France. Code\_SATURNE is a finite volume code using a fully co-located arrangement for all variables on structured or unstructured 2D or 3D meshes with, for example, tetrahedral or hexahedral cells. It has several numerical solvers, based on prediction and correction steps, for laminar or turbulent, steady or unsteady, uncompressible or compressible, isothermal or non-isothermal, non-reactive or reactive, monophasic or multiphasic flows. Code\_SATURNE has benefited from verification and validation procedures as part of quality assurance evaluations. These aspects were discussed in our prior publications[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023).") and are therefore not recalled here.

The approach followed in this study differs from that detailed in articles[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023).") by considering non-isothermal flows while taking into account thermal effects due to the presence of warm humanoid manikins breathing either in a still atmosphere or in the environment of a railway coach. In this context, heat transfers (predominantly through convection and to a lesser extent through radiation) are accounted for by solving an additional convection–diffusion equation for temperature. Radiative transfers are modelled by the discrete ordinates method with 24 discretised directions. Code\_SATURNE does not have a radiation model between surfaces such as “surface-to-surface” (S2S), and the other available radiation model, the P1 model, assumes a strictly positive absorption coefficient. In our computations, air is assumed to be transparent and therefore has an absorption coefficient equal to 0 m−1. The airflow is assumed to be incompressible, but air is considered to be an expandable fluid. Its density \\(\\rho\\) is assumed to vary linearly with the temperature \\(T\\) according to the relation: \\(\\rho =1.293 (273.15/ T)\\). The effects of air buoyancy are at the origin of the buoyant plume of droplets. They are taken into account in Code\_SATURNE when solving the Navier–Stokes equations by integrating the variation of the density with the temperature by the default “dilatable steady algorithm” method. With this method, the unsteady density variation term is not accounted for in the continuity equation. The use of the Boussinesq approximation would have been possible, but the maximum difference in air temperature in our case is at the limit of the approximation (14 °C in the simulations presented for a maximum of 15 °C for air[76](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR76 "Ferziger, J.H., & Peric, M. in Computational Methods for Fluid Dynamics, 14–15. (Springer, 2001).")). The other properties of air such as viscosity, specific heat and thermal conductivity are assumed to be constant over the temperature range considered.

Code\_SATURNE provides several approaches to the flow dynamics, principally the Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes (RANS) and large-eddy simulations (LES) formalisms. In this study, we have chosen to use the former approach with the k-epsilon turbulence model[77](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR77 "Launder, B. E. & Spalding, D. B. Lectures in Mathematical Models of Turbulence (Academic Press, 1972)."). This closure model solves the equations of the turbulent kinetic energy (k) and of its dissipation rate (epsilon), with specific additional terms due to buoyancy being included for the present research. The k-epsilon model has known limitations related in particular to curvature, density stratification or swirl if these physical phenomena are present[78](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR78 "Duraisamy, K., Iaccarino, G. & Xiao, H. Turbulence modeling in the age of data. Annu. Rev. Fluid Mech. 51, 357–377 (2019)."), which is not the case of the numerical study in the railway coach. However, it is reliable and robust for simulating mixed convection at low to moderate turbulence levels for indoor environments. Moreover, it gives results in quite short spans of time. It is thus widely used for scientific and industrial applications[78](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR78 "Duraisamy, K., Iaccarino, G. & Xiao, H. Turbulence modeling in the age of data. Annu. Rev. Fluid Mech. 51, 357–377 (2019)."), [79](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR79 "Bardina, J. E., Huang, P. G. & Coakley, T. J. Turbulence modeling validation, testing, and development. NASA Rep. 1, 110446 (1997)."). More specifically, we implemented an update of the standard k-epsilon model, namely the k-epsilon linear production model, which assumes that the turbulent production source term is linear with respect to the flow strain. This modification of the k-epsilon model has been demonstrated to improve predictions of turbulent flows in numerous cases (for instance impacting jets) with results that are close to a Reynolds stress equation model[80](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR80 "Guimet, V. & Laurence, D. A linearised turbulent production in the k-ε model for engineering applications. in Proceedings of the 5th International Symposium on Engineering Turbulence Modelling and Measurements. Mallorca, Spain. Sept. 16–18, 2002. Engineering Turbulence Modelling and Experiments 5, 157–166 (2002)."). Near the walls, a special interpolation method is needed, since k and epsilon take large values compared to the core of the flow, which means that the linear interpolation method used by a finite volume solver like Code\_SATURNE is no longer appropriate[77](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR77 "Launder, B. E. & Spalding, D. B. Lectures in Mathematical Models of Turbulence (Academic Press, 1972)."). In this study, an enhanced near walls treatment was implemented through a scalable wall function (also known as hybrid wall function) for modelling of the laminar sub-layer and buffer region throughout the turbulent flow simulation. This approach is a blended wall treatment for moderate Reynolds number flows and \\(\\text{k}\\)-epsilon based CFD simulations depending on the local value of the dimensionless wall distance _y_ +. It is known to avoid issues in CFD problems with different velocity scales on a rather uniform near-wall mesh. In this study the _y_ + map around the manikins and inside the coach highlights a very good resolution, with _y_ + values between 1 and 80 near the mouth of the manikins.

Code\_SATURNE comprises both Eulerian and Lagrangian transport and dispersion models. Both approaches are coupled to the turbulent flow simulations through the forces acting on the particles and by the average and fluctuating components of the velocity. In a previous study[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), we demonstrated that the results provided by Code\_SATURNE using either approach were similar. Thus, in this study, we carried out simulations only with the Lagrangian model. Particles were considered individually accounting for their own diameters, and their trajectories were determined by solving as many equations of motion as there were particles. It was assumed that the particles were at the same temperature as the surrounding air and that there was no thermal exchange between the particles and the carrier phase (air). Because we considered micrometric particles, their deposition on surfaces was considered to be nil or negligible and was therefore not taken into account. The equations solved by Code\_SATURNE are not shown in this article as they were presented exhaustively in our earlier study[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022).") **,** to which the interested reader is referred for more information.

Regarding the set-up of Code\_SATURNE, the flow equations were solved using the SIMPLEC velocity–pressure coupling algorithm. The stationary flow generated by the mixed convection in the railway coach was computed using a pseudo-steady CFL-limited solver with a space- and time-varying time step. The unsteady flow locally perturbed by the breathing of the manikins was computed along with the dispersion of particles. The time step was chosen depending on the mesh spatial discretisation and flow velocity to ensure the robust convergence and the accuracy of the simulations. It was constant and longer for slower events. In the simulations around the head of the manikin, the time step was 0.01 s in case #1 without a mask, 0.1 s in case #2 with the perfectly fitting mask, and 0.005 s in case #3 with the leaky mask. In each case, the simulated sequence lasted 120 s. In the unsteady simulations in the rail coach, the time step was 0.02 s, and the simulated sequence of breathing lasted 600 s. Flow simulations in the coach were carried out using unstructured meshes of tetrahedral cells. Meshes with different geometrical characteristics and levels of refinement were benchmarked. The flow results were compared in order to evaluate the sensitivity of the numerical solution as a function of the mesh refinement. This analysis showed that the flow fields were almost identical from the finest mesh to the coarsest mesh, which was used to run the flow simulations and infer the concentration results based on Lagrangian dispersion modelling. Similar results regarding the flow in the railway coach were presented and commented on in the previous study[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."). Therefore, they are not reported here.

The computer used for the simulations accounting for thermal effects was equipped with a Bi-Xeon® Intel CPU processor equipped with 2 × 40 hyper-threaded cores. Tables [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Tab1) and [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Tab2) indicate the principal features and durations of the flow and dispersion simulations, respectively around the head and bust of the manikin and in the railway coach occupied by manikins.

### Modelling of the ventilation in the railway coach

Ventilation characteristics are crucial because they condition the 3D forced airflow inside the railway coach. This flow interacts with the free convection due to heat sources constituted by passengers and the warm air they exhale. There are many different train models around the world that can have significantly different heating, ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC) systems. It is not easy to access the specific ventilation conditions of a particular train model because this information is generally only available from train manufacturers and railway operators. However, the scientific and technical literature provides insights into the layout of ventilation in trains, especially since the COVID-19 pandemic, during and after which the best ventilation strategy in transport has become a more widely considered research topic.

Several national agencies such as the Japan Railway Technical Research Institute[81](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR81 "Numerical simulation of train cabin ventilation by open windows. News release, Japan Railway Technical Research Institute, October 28, 2020.") or the Federal Railroad Administration of the US Department of Transportation[82](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR82 "Ventilation design and procedures for passenger rail. Federal Railroad Administration, U.S. Department of Transportation. RR 23-17, September 2023.") issue recommendations on ventilation design and procedures to ensure the health and safety of railway users and company staff. The European Union Agency for Railways[83](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR83 "Covid-19 information bulletin. Ventilation in railway vehicles. European Union Agency for Railways. Sept. 2020") highlights the diversity of ventilation methods for railway coaches, from simple opening of windows to forced mechanical ventilation and sophisticated air conditioning systems. The agency stresses the need to ventilate trains as well as possible, including naturally when possible, by favouring a distribution of air injection along the coach rather than the use of a single injection nozzle. Finally, the agency describes as typical the situation where fresh air is supplied at the level of the coach floor and extracted at the level of the coach ceiling, i.e. an air circulation from bottom to top across the passenger area.

Wang et al.[84](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR84 "Wang, T. et al. The airflow characteristics and thermal comfort evaluation in high-speed train cabin with mixing ventilation. An experimental and numerical study. Building Environ. 250, 111187 (2024).") experimentally and numerically investigate the flow characteristics and thermal comfort in a high-speed train coach where air is distributed from the ceiling above the central aisle to the lower part at the ends of the coach. Xu et al.[74](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR74 "Yun, S. & Kim, J. C. Numerical evaluation of a novel vertical drop airflow system to mitigate droplet transmission in trains. Atmosphere 13, 829 (2022).") study the influence of ventilation flow rate on the extraction of particles emitted by passengers in a high-speed train coach in which air is distributed along the walls at the lower and upper parts and extracted at the ends of the coach. Shinohara et al.[85](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR85 "Shinohara, N. et al. Survey of air exchange rates and evaluation of airborne infection risk of Covid-19 on commuter trains. Environ. Int. 157, 106774 (2021).") investigate the probability of contamination as a function of the air exchange rate in a Tokyo subway coach where fresh air is injected from the ceiling to the floor before being sucked into the upper part of the coach. The coach operates with the windows closed or open and stops by opening its doors. For their part, Wu et al.[72](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR72 "Wu, F., Yu, C., Xu, R. & Li, H. Numerical simulation of droplet dispersion and passenger infection risk assessment in a subway carriage. Appl. Sci. 14, 590 (2024).") study a realistic model of a subway coach where the air, initially injected at the top near the walls, descends to the floor before adopting an upward movement towards the central part of the coach. De Kreij et al.[86](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR86 "De Kreij, R. J. B., Wykes, M. S. D., Woodward, H. & Linden, P. F. Modeling disease transmission in a train carriage using a simple 1D-model. Indoor Air 32, 13066 (2022).") document a real-world carriage model in which air is injected from the ends of the coach and extracted through slots located along the ceiling in the centre of the coach. They develop a 1D model of longitudinal dispersion of droplets emitted by passengers in order to study the risk of transmission within the carriage and strategies to limit it such as judicious seat occupancy, physical distancing or ventilation flow rate.

There are also several experimental or numerical studies that focus on the performance of different ventilation modes. For example, Schmeling and Volkmann[87](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR87 "Schmeling, D. & Volkmann, A. On the experimental investigation of novel low-momentum ventilation concepts for cooling operation in a train compartment. Building Environ. 182, 107116 (2020).") experimentally investigate several ventilation concepts with respect to thermal comfort, spatial homogeneity, heat removal efficiency, and their integration capability. The study is conducted at the lower level of a double-decker high-speed train. Air is injected alternately as micro-jets or uniformly and at low velocity from the upper part of the cabin (ceiling or luggage rack) or from the floor. Air is extracted laterally at the top of the cabin. The results show that all concepts have advantages and disadvantages, with the hybrid approach that combines multiple ventilation concepts appearing to be a good compromise of the studied criteria. Wu et al.[88](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR88 "Wu, S., Li, T. & Zhang, J. Ventilation performance study of a high-speed train cabin based on displacement ventilation and mixed ventilation. Phys. Fluids 36, 077108 (2024).") investigate whether the combination of displacement ventilation and mixed ventilation can overcome the respective shortcomings of these modes to improve the ventilation performance of high-speed train coaches. The results show that when more fresh air enters the carriage through the upper vents, the flow field is mainly driven by mechanical forces. When more fresh air enters the carriage through the lower vents, the flow field is mainly driven by buoyancy. Meanwhile, the airflow mainly propagates upwards with lower longitudinal diffusion of particles. In the event of an epidemic, Wu et al.[88](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR88 "Wu, S., Li, T. & Zhang, J. Ventilation performance study of a high-speed train cabin based on displacement ventilation and mixed ventilation. Phys. Fluids 36, 077108 (2024).") recommend increasing the flow rate of the lower air supply. To improve ventilation performance, it is necessary to promote the synergy between buoyancy and mechanical forces. Ahmadzadeh and Shams[89](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR89 "Ahmadzadeh, M. & Shams, M. Multi-objective performance assessment of HVAC systems and physical barriers on Covid-19 infection transmission in a high-speed train. J. Building Eng. 53, 104544 (2022).") seek to optimize ventilation parameters to limit the transmission of SARS-CoV-2. They numerically study a rail coach model with air inlets above the windows and outlets through vents of variable sizes and positions, located either on the ceiling, in the centre and along the coach, or on the floor of the coach. The preferred solution is a high extraction flow rate in the upper part of the coach. Finally, Yun and Kim[74](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR74 "Yun, S. & Kim, J. C. Numerical evaluation of a novel vertical drop airflow system to mitigate droplet transmission in trains. Atmosphere 13, 829 (2022).") are interested in two ventilation systems, one usual of Korean express trains where air is injected along the windows and extracted in the lower part of the carriage, the other alternative where air circulates from bottom to top, the latter seeming more effective in evacuating particles emitted by passengers.

Finally, this review shows that several ventilation strategies are present in railway transport: from the floor, from the ceiling or a mix of both. The ventilation system is designed to reconcile the thermal comfort of passengers, and the evacuation of heat with the constraint of evacuating smoke in the event of a fire. The subject is therefore not simple and, to our knowledge, there is no ideal system or consensus on the subject. In our 3D numerical study, the choice concerning ventilation resulted from our review of the scientific literature, summarized above, contacts with suburban train and mainline train operators in France and the geometry files of the selected railway coach. Aware that the strategy adopted for ventilation has a major effect on the dispersion of particles, we decided to adopt a bottom-up air ventilation mode which, according to the literature and our own work, is favourable to the evacuation of particles. We have kept in this article the same ventilation conditions compared to those used in our previous publications[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023)."). It should be noted that ventilation is a parameter to be adjusted depending on the case to be treated.

Ventilation characteristics concern the air supply and extraction conditions, that is to say the location, shape and dimensions of the supply and extraction vents as well as the flow rates passing through these vents. In our simulation, the ventilation system is organised by zones corresponding to the compartments represented in Fig. [25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Fig25), which sketches the layout of the ventilation in the rail coach. By hypothesis, only fresh air is supplied from the outside to each end of the coach at imposed velocities and flow rates. Air extraction is performed through slits in the roof of each compartment. In compartments #1 and #3, which are occupied by passengers, the exit velocities and flow rates are imposed, while in compartment #2, air is extracted at atmospheric pressure, resulting in a balance of overall airflows between the compartments. We adjusted the air extraction rates of compartments #1 and #3 to have air moving from the ends to the middle of the coach, with 50% of the air being extracted through compartment #2. The velocities of the supplied and extracted air are less than 0.2 m s−1 as prescribed by railway operators to ensure comfortable conditions for passengers. The renewal rate of the air in each compartment, computed using the flow rate supplied to the compartment, is equal to 8.7. The residence time is obtained by dividing the volume of each compartment by the airflow rate supplied to the compartment. Ventilation data used as inlet or outlet boundary conditions of the simulations are gathered in Table [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Tab3).

### Modelling of a manikin wearing a mask

In order to bring as much realism and generality as possible to the 3D numerical study, we considered three cases of mask use, involving the widespread surgical-type mask, by humanoid manikins. In case #1 or the base case, the manikin wears no mask. In case #2, the manikin wears a perfectly fitted mask without any leaks between its face and the mask. Whether or not a mask is worn has a considerable influence on the respiratory events experienced by the manikin, especially its breathing, which is the subject of this article. In case #1 where no mask is worn, the air inhaled and exhaled by the manikin passes through its mouth, which is a limit of the 3D simulation domain where boundary conditions are imposed. In case #2 and case #3 where the mask is worn, the air emitted by the manikin is slowed down and diffused through the filtering part (made of a porous material) of the mask. In our approach, we do not explicitly take the mask into account, nor do we mesh the space between the part of the manikin’s face covered by the mask and the mask itself. The external surface of the filtering part of the mask, which is much larger (by a factor of about 30) than the surface of the mouth, is a limit of the 3D simulation domain where boundary conditions are imposed. In case #2, in which the mask fits perfectly, any air and particles not retained by the mask pass through the filtering part of the mask. In case #3, in which the mask is worn loosely, the air and particles exit both through the filtering part of the mask and through leakage surfaces on the edges of the mask. ### Modelling of the breathing dissemination event

In this study, we consider the normal breathing of a humanoid manikin presumed to be infected by a pathogen such as the SARS-CoV-2 virus. The release of droplets due to exhalation is repeated at each breathing cycle. When no mask is worn, the velocity of the air carrying the droplets is slightly higher than the air velocity around the manikin. Wearing a mask reduces the velocity of the air carrying droplets passing through the mask, and the air velocity is also low through any leaks around the manikin’s face. We considered droplets with a diameter of 1 µm because it is accepted that droplets of less than 10 µm in diameter exhibit the same overall behaviour in the air. Moreover, exhalation is generally not accompanied by the production of drops, and at any rate, drops of more than 10 µm in diameter are properly filtered by surgical masks. Furthermore, a realistic number of droplets or drops (in order of magnitude) was released from the mouth of the infected passengers, namely 1000 particles for each exhalation. In the digital twin of the railway coach, the spreader manikin wearing a mask is assumed to be seated in compartment #1 of the coach and to occupy the position indicated in Fig. [28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#Fig28), just as in the previous study[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR1 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the dispersion of droplets and drops carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 12, 4025 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR2 "Armand, P. & Tâche, J. 3D modelling and simulation of the impact of wearing a mask on the dispersion of particles carrying the SARS-CoV-2 virus in a railway transport coach. Nat. Sci. Rep. 13, 8929 (2023).") with masked or non-masked manikins. This location is arbitrary and could easily be modified, but it leads to interesting observations.

### Thermal boundary conditions

The simulations considering the head and bust of a humanoid manikin were carried out in a simulation domain with boundaries kept at a stable temperature of 20 °C. The air injected at the ends of the rail coach by the ventilation system was assumed to have a constant temperature equal to 20 °C. The surfaces of the coach (walls, seats, floor, roof and windows) were assumed to be at the same steady temperature of 20 °C. The thermal condition on the air outlets was a zero flux condition, i.e., the temperature of the extracted air was free on these surfaces. The air exhaled by the humanoid manikins, whether through the mouth or through the mask, was assumed to be at a temperature of 34 °C. Note that in reality, the temperature of the air exhaled by a human being varies between approximately 32 and 35 °C[90](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR90 "McFadden, E. R. Jr. et al. Thermal mapping of the airways in humans. J. Appl. Physiol. 58(2), 564–570 (1985)."). The temperature of the exposed surface of the manikin’s head and of the mask was set at 32 °C, which is consistent with the temperature range found in the literature[91](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR91 "Liu, Y., Liu, Z. & Luo, J. Numerical investigation of the unsteady thermal plume around human body in closed space. Procedia Eng. 121, 1919–1926 (2015)."), [92](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR92 "Tacutu, L., Bode, F., Năstase, I., Croitoru, A., & Dogeanu, A. Experimental and numerical study on the thermal plumes of a standing and lying human in an operating room. Sci. Technol. Built Environ. (2021)."). The bodies of the manikins were assumed to be covered by clothing, and in thermal equilibrium with their environment, therefore at a temperature of 20 °C. This is a simplifying assumption given that publications indicate temperatures of the covered parts of manikins generally between 1 and 4 °C above the ambient temperature[93](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR93 "Feng, G., Bi, Y., Zhang, Y., Cai, Y. & Huang, K. Study on the motion law of aerosols produced by human respiration under the action of thermal plume of different intensities. Sustain. Cities Society 54, 101935 (2020)."). Manikins can even have much higher body temperatures. For instance, in study[94](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84411-2#ref-CR94 "Liu, Y., Liu, Z. & Luo, J. Numerical investigation of the unsteady thermal plume around human body in closed space. in Proceedings of the 9th Int. Symposium on Heating, Ventilation and Air Conditioning (ISHVAC) and the 3rd Int. Conference on Building Energy and Environment (COBEE). Procedia Engineering 121, 1919–1926 (2015)."), the authors adopt a head temperature of 32 °C and that of the rest of the body of 30 °C. Considering that the body temperature of the manikins is not in thermal equilibrium with their environment, this would reinforce the buoyancy effects highlighted in our study. In order to simplify the thermal problem and in the absence of precise data, the emissivities of all surfaces were assumed to be equal to 0.8. Remember that we did not take into account the heat exchanges between the air and the particles in the simulations. It was therefore unnecessary to specify the temperature of the particles. In practice, we considered that the particles were injected at air temperature, therefore also at 34 °C, and likewise through the leakage surfaces.
