Distribution and impact of bacterial and fungal aerosols in public gathering locations of Wuhan in winter in 2024


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1.pdf)

## Abstract

Bacterial and fungal aerosols are widely distributed in the air, and pose a significant threat to human health. In many densely populated and active locations such as subways, supermarkets, fast food restaurants, hospital registration halls, and the other locations, the bacterial and fungal contamination in indoor air cannot be ignored. To understand the concentration distribution of bacterial and fungal aerosols with different particle sizes in these crowded locations, the on-site sampling and experimental analysis were first conducted on bacterial and fungal aerosols in four types of locations, and other related environmental parameters were tested in Wuhan during the winter of 2024. Then, the concentration levels and distribution characteristics of bacterial and fungal aerosols in different locations were analyzed. Finally, the concentration levels of microbial aerosols in different locations and their potential health risks were evaluated. The results indicated: ① The average concentration ordering of bacterial aerosols was fast food restaurant > supermarket > hospital > subway, and the average concentration of fungal aerosols was supermarket > fast food restaurant > hospital > subway. ② The median diameter ordering of bacterial aerosols was hospital > subway > fast food restaurant > supermarket, and that of fungal aerosols was fast food restaurant > hospital > subway > supermarket. ③ In the air of the four types of locations, the average level of bacterial aerosols was low, while the level of fungal aerosols was lower in supermarkets than that in subways, hospital halls, and fast food restaurants. ④ The health impact coefficient of microbial aerosols in all locations was significantly less than 1, indicating a low health risk. This study provides valuable insights for the evaluating of environmental air quality in personnel gathering locations, and provide data support for the formulation of public health policies.

## Sampling preparation and analysis methods

### Sampling preparation

Before microbial sampling, the preparation of the culture medium was carried out, mainly using instruments such as microbalance, pressure sterilization pot, clean workbench, and related drugs used to ensure the activity of bacterial and fungal in culture media. Due to the different conditions required for the growth of bacteria and fungi, different culture media need to be prepared. The commonly used bacterial culture medium is nutrient agar, while the fungal culture medium is Sabouraud agar. One can place the prepared culture dish into the sampler and then start the sampler for testing. In this research, we used the JMT-6 impact microbial sampler (Shandong Anhai Environmental Technology Ltd., China. Capture rate ≥ 98%; Flow control accuracy ≤ 5%) to simultaneously sample bacterial and fungal aerosols at a selected location, with a sampling time of 15 min. The standard flow rate of the sampler should be calibrated to 28.3L/min before sampling. It is necessary to ensure that the holes on the disc are unobstructed, and then assemble the impactor in order. The sampler is divided into 6 grades based on the size of the sieve aperture (The grade I corresponds to particle diameter > 7 μm. The grade II corresponds to particle diameter 4.7 ~ 7 μm. The grade III corresponds to particle diameter 3.3 ~ 4.7 μm. The grade IV corresponds to particle diameter 2.1 ~ 3.3 μm. The grade V corresponds to particle diameter 1.1 ~ 2.1 μm. And the grade VI corresponds to particle diameter 0.65 ~ 1.1 μm), and each grade has 400 micro sieves. Environmental parameters were tested using various testing instruments such as temperature tester (testing range 0 ~ 50 °C) and humidity tester (testing range 5–95%). After completing the samplings, the collected samples were placed into an electric constant temperature incubator for cultivation, cultivating bacteria for 48 h at 36 °C and fungi for 72 h at 28 °C.

### Sampling procedure

The collection of samples began on December 16, 2024, and lasted for 2 days, with collection taking in the morning and afternoon. A temperature and humidity meter (MBS-330, Weichuang Electronics Co., Ltd., China) was used to measure the air temperature and relative humidity of sampling locations including indoor and outdoor data. The collection of samples and the detection of environmental temperature and humidity were carried out simultaneously.

The sampling height is 1.5 m, and the duration of sampling is 15 min, with one time sampling. Before and after sampling, each level of the sampler was wiped with 75% alcohol. The specific process is as follows: ① Hold the impactor from the top with one hand and hang it on three spring hooks with the other hand. ② Connect the impactor outlet and the host air inlet with a rubber tube, and remove the upper cover of the impactor air inlet. ③ Plug in the host to the power supply (AC220V), press the “power switch” on the host, adjust the “flow adjustment” knob to stabilize the flowmeter rotor at 28.3L/min. This method is used for both indoor and outdoor sampling.

### Analysis methods

#### Counting and calibration of microorganisms

After cultivation, the bacteria and fungi in the culture dish need to be counted. Microbial aerosols pass through the sieve holes with different sizes in the sampler and collide with the culture dish. The collision positions may overlap, causing certain errors in the counting results of microbial colonies after cultivation. Therefore, it is necessary to correct the counting results. This article uses the positive-hole method[19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#ref-CR19 "Nasir, Z. A., Mula, V. & Stokoe, J. Evaluation of total concentration and size distribution of bacterial and fungal aerosolin healthcare built environment. Indoor Built. Environ. 24(2), 269–279.                    https://doi.org/10.1177/1420326x13510925                                     (2015).") to correct the results.

The calculation of the total concentration of bacterial or fungal aerosols is shown in Eq. ( [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#Equ1)):

$$C = \\frac{{P\_{1} + P\_{2} + P\_{3} + P\_{4} + P\_{5} + P\_{6} }}{T \\times L}$$

(1)


where _C_ (cfu /m3) is the concentration of bacterial or fungal aerosols in the environment, _P_ _i_ (cuf) is the colony count after being grade _i_ corrected in culture dish, _T_ (min) denotes the sampling duration, and _L_ (m3/ min) is the sampling flow rate.

#### Median particle size of bacteria and fungal aerosols

The data of median particle size is often used to reflect the overall distribution of bacteria and fungal aerosols in the air[20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#ref-CR20 "Nasrabadi, A. M., An, S. & Kwon, S. B. Investigation of live and dead status of airborne bacteria using UVAPS with LIVE/DEAD BacLight Kit. J. Aerosol. Sci. 115, 181–189.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jaerosci.2017.10.012                                     (2018)."), [21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#ref-CR21 "Polednik, B. Exposure of staff to aerosols and bioaerosols in a dental office. Build. Environ 187, 107388.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.buildenv.2020.107388                                     (2021)."), [22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#ref-CR22 "Rathnayake, C. M., Metwali, N. & Jayarathne, T. Influence of rain on the abundance and size distribution of bioaerosols. Atmos. Chem. Phys. 17(2459), 1–29.                    https://doi.org/10.5194/acp-2016-622                                     (2016)."). By logarithmically fitting the effective interception particle diameter of each stage of the sampler with the cumulative percentage of microbial aerosols from grade I to grade VI, the diameter corresponding to a cumulative percentage of 50% is calculated as the median particle size. The parameters of the regression model are determined using the least squares method.

#### Lifetime health risk assessment

Some microorganisms in the air are pathogenic, so it is of great significance to evaluate the lifetime health risks of microorganisms exposed to different functional areas[24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#ref-CR24 "Tian, G., Bai, X. M. & Liu, X. Q. Application of pesticide residue risk assessment in monitoring vegetables, fruits, and edible fungi. J. Nucl. Agric 36(2), 12.                    https://doi.org/10.11869/j.issn.100-8551.2022.02.0402                                     (2022) (in Chinese)."), [25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#ref-CR25 "Wang, Y., Fu, Y. & Wang, C. Dissimilar emission characteristics between bioaerosol and suspended particles from gaseous biofilters and bioaerosol health risk evaluation. Aerosol Air Qual. Res. 18(7), 1874–1885 (2018)."), [26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#ref-CR26 "Wang, Q. N., Bai, Y. Y. & Xie, J. F. Multi-Scale polyimide/carbon fibers hybrid filters for hot gas filtration. Mater. Sci. Forum 814, 464–469.                    https://doi.org/10.4028/www.scientific.net/MSF.814.464                                     (2015)."). The health impact coefficient was used as the evaluation indicator in this paper, which is the ratio of the daily exposure dose of microbial aerosols in the testing place to the daily exposure standard value of health risk. When the health impact coefficient is greater than 1, it is considered that the concentration of microbial aerosols in the air of testing place poses a high risk to human health, while when it is less than 1, the risk is considered low. The calculation of the health impact coefficient of human exposure is shown in Eq. ( [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#Equ2)):

$$HQ = \\frac{ADD}{{R\_{f} D}}$$

(2)


Here, _HQ_ is the impact coefficient of health on human exposure; _ADD_ (cfu/kg·d) is the daily average exposure dose of microbial aerosols, _R_ _f_ _D_ is the standard value of daily health risk exposure. In this study, _R_ _f_ _D_ was set as 500 cfu/kg·d according to the reference (Dahlstrom et al., American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists, Encyclopedia of Toxicology, Third Edition, 2014).

The calculation of daily average exposure dose of microbial aerosols is shown in Eq. ( [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#Equ3)):

$$ADD = \\frac{C \\times IR \\times EF \\times ED}{{BW \\times AT}}$$

(3)


where _C_ (cfu/m3) is the concentration of microbial aerosols in the air of the testing site, _IR_ (m3/d) is the respiratory rate, _ED_ (a) is the exposure time, _EF_ (d/a) is the exposure frequency, _BW_ (kg) is the body weight, and _AT_ (d) is the average exposure time.

Due to the fact that the sample collection sites in this study were Hankou and Wuchang districts of Wuhan City (subway, supermarket, fast food restaurant, and hospital), according to the “Chinese Population Exposure Parameter Manual (Adult Volume)” and combining specific venue functions, the average long-term respiratory volume of the population in Wuhan was taken as the _IR_ in this study, that was 16.2 m3/d, _ED_ exposure time was 30 a, _EF_ was 250 d/a, and _BW_ was the average weight of adults in Chinese cities as 61.2 kg. According to the average life expectancy of the population in Wuhan, it is 78.50 years old, _AT_ was set to be 365 \* 78.50 d.

In this research, the lifetime health risk calculation was performed mainly based on the following assumptions: ①Microbial concentration is stable or predictable.②There is a linear relationship between inhalation dose and alveolar deposition dose, which means the proportion of inhaled microorganisms reaching the alveoli is fixed. ③The filtration efficiency of the upper respiratory tract (nasal cavity, mucus ciliary system) is considered a static barrier. ④The cumulative effects of lifetime exposure can be additive, treating staged exposure as a simple sum and ignoring immune memory or threshold effects. The limitations of lifetime microbial risk assessment mainly stem from the oversimplification of complex biological environmental interactions.

#### Selection of sampling sites

In the winter of 2024, we selected different functional areas of four subway lines with high passenger flow, two supermarkets with high shopping volume, two fast food restaurants, and five hospitals in Wuhan as the indoor bacterial and fungal aerosol testing sites. Regarding the sampling points in the functional area, we have considered the planar and three-dimensional layout of each location, and the number of sampling points was determined by the area of the location. The sampling height was 1.5 m, avoiding opening air vents. The subway lines selected included Line 2, Line 4, Line 7, and Line 8, and four platforms along different lines were chosen as the sampling points. Five hospitals were selected in Hankou and Wuchang districts of Wuhan, including three Tertiary Grade A comprehensive hospitals and two Secondary Grade C comprehensive hospitals located. Visually, four hospitals had good cleanliness, while one hospital had average cleanliness. The subway sampling points included the subway platform, the middle and rear of the subway carriages, the supermarket sampling points included the fruit and vegetable areas, the fast food restaurant sampling points included the dining areas and food preparation areas, and the hospital sampling points included the registration halls, emergency halls, internal medicine waiting halls, surgical waiting halls, and corridors. When sampling microorganisms in the air of different functional areas in four types of locations, indoor air environmental parameters were also tested. After the sampling was completed, the collected samples were placed into an electric constant temperature incubator for cultivation, cultivating bacteria for 48 h at 36 °C and fungi for 72 h at 28 °C. The basic information of different functional areas in each sampling point is shown in Table [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#Tab2) which includes the measurement data of environmental temperature and humidity during sampling. The measurement duration of temperature and humidity is 1 h, with values taken every minute, and then calculated the average of detecting data.

After comprehensive analysis of the average bacterial concentration in various locations, the subway had the lowest bacterial concentration, which was (449 ± 186) cfu/m3; Compared with the subways, the bacterial concentration in fast food restaurants, supermarkets, and hospitals was higher, with values of (838 ± 282) cfu/m3, (750 ± 558) cfu/m3, and (483 ± 197) cfu/m3, respectively, and the concentration of fast food restaurants was highest among these four types of locations. Analyzing the average fungal concentration in the four types of locations, one could see the fungal concentration in the subway was lowest, with values of (186 ± 96) cfu/m3. Compared with outdoor air, the concentration of bacterial and fungal aerosols in subways and hospitals was lower than that in outdoor aerosols, while the concentration of bacterial and fungal aerosols in supermarkets and fast food restaurants was higher than that in outdoor aerosols. B/F was the ratio of bacterial aerosol to fungal aerosol concentration in the testing site. Figure [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#Fig1) shows that the B/F values in four types of locations were all greater than 1, indicating that the environment in the testing site was conducive to the growth and reproduction of bacteria. The main reason for the difference in concentration of bacterial and fungal aerosols in different locations was that each place had different functions, resulting different sources of bacterial and fungal pollution. The reproduction of bacteria and fungi was closely related to the ventilation, indoor temperature and humidity in the testing areas[23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#ref-CR23 "Hassan, A. & Zeeshan, M. Microbiological indoor air quality of hospital buildings with different ventilation systems, cleaning frequencies and occupancy levels. Atmos. Pollut. Res. 13(4), 2–21.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apr.2022.101382                                     (2022)."). Ventilation could directly alter the physical and chemical environment that microorganisms relied on for survival and reproduction. When humid air was taken away, the moisture content on the surface of objects and in the air decreases, which directly deprived microorganisms of the water they needed for reproduction. The flow of air could blow away suspended microbial spores, preventing them from accumulating to pathogenic concentrations in a certain area. In still air, the spores would continuously settle onto the surface of objects and began to reproduce once conditions were suitable (such as humidity). Ventilation broke the cycle of accumulation and settling. Compared to fast food restaurants and supermarkets, the air conditioning systems of subways and hospitals had strong ventilation and purification functions, thereby overall reducing the concentration of microorganisms in the air. In addition, through on-site testing, it was found that the air humidity in the supermarkets was significantly higher than that in other locations, because fruit and vegetable preservation required spraying water mist, which increased the air humidity and provided a favorable environment for fungal growth. Therefore, the fungal concentration in the supermarkets was higher than in the other three types of locations.

### Concentration distribution of bacterial and fungal aerosols with different particle diameters

Particle diameter is one of the important factors affecting the infectivity of microbial aerosols. The smaller the particle diameter, the stronger the infectivity. The bacterial concentration in the air inside the subway carriage was 251 ~ 1200 cfu/m3, with a bacterial pollution level of “clean”. The bacterial concentration in the air of the platform areas was less than 250 cfu/m3, indicating the lowest level of bacterial pollution. The bacterial concentration in the air of supermarkets, fast food restaurants, and hospitals was within the range of 251 –200 cfu/m3. The fungal concentration in the air of various areas of supermarkets was within the range of 401– 700 cfu/m3, and that of fast food restaurants and hospitals was less than 400 cfu/m3. In all testing areas,the concentration of bacterial aerosols was highest in the food preparation areas of fast food restaurants, and lowest in the subway platform, respectively at 1068 cfu/m3 and 192 cfu/m3. The highest and lowest concentration of fungal aerosols were found in supermarket vegetable areas and subway platforms, with values of 616 cfu/m3 and 121 cfu/m3, respectively. The B/F values in different areas of four types of locations were all greater than 1, indicating that the concentration of bacterial aerosols in various locations was higher than that of fungi aerosols, and the intensity of bacterial pollution was higher than that of fungi.

Comparing between the subway carriages and platforms in subway station, it could be found that the concentration levels of bacteria and fungi in the air of subway platforms were significantly lower than those inside the carriages, but those in the air of the middle of the carriage were slightly higher than those at the rear of the carriage. Compared to the platforms, the carriages were densely populated and mostly concentrated in the middle, and the air conditioning and ventilation facilities might not be able to achieve complete purification. During the operation of the subway, microorganisms on the seats and armrests would accumulate continuously and spread into the air as passengers’ moving in the carriage.

Comparing between the vegetable areas and fruit areas in the supermarkets, it could be found that the bacterial concentration in the air of the fruit areas was higher than that of the vegetable areas, while the fungal concentration in the vegetable areas was higher than that in the fruit areas. Through on-site investigation, it was found that the staff in the fruit areas often applied anti-corrosion drugs to ensure that the fruits were not easily rotten. In the vegetable areas, in order to ensure the freshness of vegetables, the vegetable areas were equipped with spray devices, which resulted in higher environmental humidity in the vegetable areas. This humid environment was conducive to the breeding and reproduction of fungi.

Comparing between the dining areas and preparation areas in fast food restaurants, it could be found that the bacterial concentration level in the air of the preparation areas was significantly higher than that of the dining areas, and the fungal concentration level was slightly higher than that of the dining areas. The possible reason was that the preparation areas were mostly located in the interior of fast food restaurants, with relatively poor air circulation compared to the dining areas, and there were many types of meat and vegetables stored in the preparation areas, which are prone to bacterial growth. The ventilation in the dining areas was good, and the environment was relatively dry, which was not conducive to the growth of bacteria and fungi.

Comparing among the five areas in the hospitals: registration hall, emergency hall, internal medicine waiting room, surgical waiting room, and corridor, it could be found that the concentration of bacterial and fungal aerosols in the registration halls and corridors was higher than that in other three areas. It was mainly due to the fact that corridors and registration halls were areas with high pedestrian traffic. The frequent human activities could increase the concentration of bacteria and fungi in the air. Hospital is a type of place where the microorganisms were prone to accumulate, which lead to a bad impact on the health of patients and doctors. Therefore, it is necessary to strictly control the concentration of bacterial and fungal aerosols in hospitals and improve the indoor air cleanliness level.

When the health impact coefficient is greater than 1, it is considered that the concentration of microbial aerosols in the air poses a high risk to human health, and when it is less than 1, the risk is considered low. Figure [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23482-1#Fig5) also shows that the bacterial exposure risk coefficient of subway carriages was higher than that of subway platforms, and in the middle area of the carriages, the coefficient was higher than that in the rear. The fungal exposure risk coefficient showed the same characteristics. The risk coefficient of bacterial exposure in the fruit areas of supermarkets was higher than that in the vegetable areas, while the risk coefficient of fungal exposure was higher in the vegetable areas than that in the fruit areas, with the value of 0.11. The risk coefficient of bacterial exposure in the preparation areas of fast food restaurants was significantly higher than that in the dining areas, with the value of 0.17, while the risk coefficient of fungal exposure in dining areas was slightly lower than that in the preparation areas. The risk coefficient of bacterial exposure in the registration hall and corridor of the hospitals was higher than that in other areas of the hospitals, and the risk coefficient of fungal exposure in all areas of the hospitals was 0.03 –0.05. The high flow of people in the registration halls and corridors had caused an increase in the concentration of bacterial aerosols in the air. Therefore, more attention should be paid to the impact of bacterial aerosols on human health in the hospital registration halls and corridors. In summary, the health coefficient HQ of different areas in four types of locations, namely subway, supermarket, fast food restaurant, and hospital, is significantly less than 1, indicating that the microbial aerosols in the air of the four types of locations in Wuhan measured in the winter 2024 had a lower risk to human health, and have a relatively small impact on the health of personnel exposed to these locations for a long time. The concentration of bioaerosols is significantly affected by factors such as season, temperature and humidity, and human activities. For example, the bacterial concentration may be higher in winter than in summer, while the fungal concentration is the opposite. The activity at the time of sampling may also not represent the true exposure state. The exposure time, respiratory rate, and immune status of different populations (such as occupational exposure individuals and ordinary residents) are different, making it difficult to evaluate them uniformly. Therefore, the risk coefficient HQ obtained in this study can only be used as a reference for exposure risk in certain specific areas of Wuhan.

## Conclusions

Through testing and analyzing the concentration of bacterial and fungal aerosols in the air of four types of densely populated locations in Wuhan, including subways, supermarkets, fast food restaurants, and hospitals, as well as their different areas in the winter of 2024, the following conclusions were drawn:

1. (1)
The comparing result of the bacterial concentration levels in four types of locations was that fast food restaurants > supermarkets > hospitals > subways, and the overall pollution level was “clean”. The comparison of fungal concentration levels is that supermarkets > fast food restaurants > hospitals > subways, and the fungal pollution level in subways, hospitals, and fast food restaurants is “clean”, while the fungal pollution level in supermarkets is “cleaner”.

2. (2)
The bacterial particle diameters in the air of three types of locations: subways, supermarkets, and fast food restaurants were mainly concentrated in the grade I, grade IV, and grade V, with a total proportion of over 60%. The bacterial particle diameters in hospitals were mainly concentrated in grade I, grade II, and grade IV, accounting for a total proportion of 61%. The median diameter of bacterial aerosols were different in the four types of locations. The median diameter of bacteria in the air of supermarkets was smaller, while that in the air of hospitals was larger. The fungal particle diameters in the air of various locations were mainly distributed in the grade III, grade IV, and grade V, with a total proportion of over 60%. The median diameter of fungal aerosols varies in different locations. The median diameter of fungi in the air of supermarkets was smaller, while the median diameter of fungi in the air of fast food restaurants was the largest.

3. (3)
There were differences in the concentration levels of microorganisms in the air of different areas in the same place. Comparison result of the concentration levels of bacteria and fungi in the subways air was middle of the carriages > rear of the carriages > platforms. In the air of supermarkets, the bacterial concentration level in the fruit areas was higher than that in the vegetable areas, and the fungal concentration level in the vegetable areas was higher than that in the fruit areas. In the air of fast food restaurants, the concentration level of bacteria and fungi was higher in the preparation areas than that in the dining areas. The comparison result of bacterial concentration levels in various areas of the hospitals was corridors > registration halls > surgical waiting halls > internal medicine waiting halls > emergency halls, while that of the fungal concentration levels was registration halls > corridors > surgical waiting halls > internal medicine waiting halls > emergency halls.

4. (4)
The health risk coefficient of bacterial aerosols in the preparation areas of fast food restaurants was the highest, with the value of 0.17. The health risk coefficient of fungal aerosols in the vegetable areas of supermarkets was the highest, at 0.11. Personnel exposed in the four types of locations for a long time had relatively low health risks.

5. (5)
In public areas with high health risk factors, effective public management measures need to be taken. In addition to strengthening ventilation in the area, efficient air filters should be used to reduce bacterial and fungal aerosol concentrations. In addition, regularly use disinfectants such as 75% alcohol or chlorine containing disinfectants for disinfection. Extend the ultraviolet irradiation time to more than 2 h to destroy the structure of the virus through ultraviolet irradiation, thereby achieving the goal of effectively removing pollution.


### Limitations

- Short-term sampling may not fully represent the long-term and overall airborne microbial exposure levels in this environment. The concentration of bacterial and fungal aerosols is greatly affected by environmental temperature and humidity. The species and quantification in the sample were also significantly affected by seasonal changes. In this study, the sampling time at different locations was randomly selected, so the detection data may only represent the results of a certain period of time at a sampling site. However, these detection results can still objectively reflect the overall situation of different sample detection sites, which is very consistent with our specific environmental analysis of the respective sampling sites.

- The primary basis for the risk assessment is the total colony count. However, as species-level identification (particularly for potential pathogens) was not performed for all microorganisms, it is not possible to precisely evaluate the associated health risks. The dose–response relationship between total colony count and health effects remains unclear, and there is a lack of globally unified authoritative evaluation standards.

- Therefore, the findings of this study should be regarded as a preliminary screening and relative assessment of indoor airborne microbial contamination. A more accurate risk assessment would require integrating long-term monitoring, molecular biological identification (e.g., qPCR, metagenomic sequencing), and targeted analysis of specific pathogens.
