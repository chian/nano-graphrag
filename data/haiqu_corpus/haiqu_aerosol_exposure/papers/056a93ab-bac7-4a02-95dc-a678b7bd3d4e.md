Distribution and antibiotic resistance patterns of airborne staphylococci in urban environments of Delhi, India


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4.pdf)

## Abstract

Airborne microbial contamination, especially involving antibiotic-resistant bacteria (ARB) and antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs), poses a growing public health concern in urban environments. This study explores the prevalence and diversity of staphylococci, including methicillin-resistant staphylococci (MRS), in bioaerosols from various urban settings in Delhi, India. Indoor and outdoor air samples showed significantly high staphylococcal loads far above the WHO’s recommended limit of 1000 CFU/m³ for microbial exposure. Seasonal variations revealed a peak in airborne MRS during winter, while monsoon rains reduced outdoor bioaerosol contamination. Eight staphylococcal species were identified, with _Staphylococcus epidermidis_ and _Staphylococcus arlettae_ being the most prevalent human- and animal-associated species, respectively. Notably, 73% of MRS isolates exhibited multidrug resistance (MDR), showing resistance to macrolides, β-lactams, and other commonly used antibiotics. Genotypic analysis confirmed the presence of ARGs among airborne MRS encoding resistance for beta-lactam, trimethoprim, gentamicin, macrolides, chloramphenicol and lincosamides. Notably, with 14 out of 36 MDR isolates carrying the _mecA_ gene encoding for methicillin resistance. This study emphasizes the potential health risks posed by airborne reservoirs of antibiotic resistance in urban environments and underscores the urgent need for comprehensive environmental AMR surveillance to develop effective mitigation strategies.

## Introduction

Microbes are ubiquitous across environments and play a crucial role in different aspects of the ecosystems. Airborne dissemination is a natural and significant process in the biosphere that transports a variety of microorganisms along with related biological particles. These aerosolized particles of biological origin are referred to as bioaerosols[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR1 "Xie, W. et al. This study, therefore, aims to assess the prevalence and diversity of staphylococci in bioaerosols from various urban environments in Delhi, including Vasant Vihar Urban Slum (VVUS), Munirka Market Complex (MMC), Munirka Apartment (MA), and the Sewage Treatment Plant at Jawaharlal Nehru University (STP, JNU). Additionally, the multidrug resistance patterns of the molecularly confirmed airborne staphylococcal isolates were analysed, along with their ARG profiles.

## Results

### Seasonal and spatial distribution of total presumptive staphylococci load in Delhi air

The bacterial isolates grown on mannitol salt agar (MSA), a selective media for staphylococci, were considered as presumptive culturable airborne staphylococcal load (PCASL). PCASL exceeded 1000 CFU/m3 in both indoor and outdoor air across all the locations, with the highest recorded load during the winter (16000 CFU/m3) at Munirka market complex (MMC), followed by Vasant Vihar Urban Slum (VVUS) (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig1) a and b). Both sites were also notably crowded (Table [S1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#MOESM1)). The sites VVUS, MMC and STP showed significant difference ( _p_-value < 0.05) in the variance of staphylococcal load between seasons. For example, VVUS exhibited significant differences between winter vs. summer and monsoon vs. summer but no significant difference between winter vs. monsoon. Similarly, MMC showed significant variance between monsoon vs. winter ( _p_-value < 0.0001) and winter vs. summer ( _p_-value < 0.001), but no significant difference between monsoon vs. summer. Likewise, the variance in staphylococcal counts at the STP showed significant differences for monsoon vs. winter ( _p_-value < 0.006), monsoon vs. summer ( _p_-value < 0.0001), and winter vs. summer ( _p_-value = 0.006). In contrast, MA did not exhibit any significant differences in the variations across the three seasons.

Similar to the PCASL results, winter exhibited the highest PBPS load, particularly in outdoor air (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig1) c).

### Species diversity among airborne methicillin-resistant staphylococci isolates

Due to shared biochemical characteristics between staphylococci and related genera (e.g. micrococci), confirmation of staphylococci species and species diversity was performed using 16S rRNA gene sequencing followed by NCBI BLAST for bacterial homology. The 16S rRNA partial gene sequences of all the 36 MDRS isolates have been deposited in GenBank as per the accession numbers summarized in the Table [S6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#MOESM1) (Supplementary material). A total of 65 airborne methicillin-resistant PBPS isolates were detected across all seasons. of these, 49 were confirmed as staphylococci species, while the remaining isolates were identified as belonging to _Micrococcus_, _Kocuria_, _Corynebacterium_, and _Aerococcus_ genera (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig2) a and b), likely due to their shared habitat and morphology with _Staphylococcus_ [9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR9 "Parte, A. C., Sardà Carbasse, J., Meier-Kolthoff, J. P., Reimer, L. C. & Göker, M. List of prokaryotic names with standing in nomenclature (LPSN) moves to the DSMZ. Int. J. Syst. Evol. MicroBiol. 70(11), 5607–5612.                    https://doi.org/10.1099/ijsem.0.004332                                     (2020)."). Previously, these genera have been commonly identified in bioaerosols[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR27 "Kandi, V. et al. Emerging bacterial infection: identification and clinical significance of Kocuria species. Cureus 8(8).                    https://doi.org/10.7759/cureus.731                                     (2016)."), [28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR28 "Rasmussen, M. Aerococcus: An increasingly acknowledged human pathogen. Clin. Microbiol. Infect. 22(1), 22–27.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmi.2015.09.026                                     (2016)."). Moreover, _Corynebacterium_ and micrococci belong to the phylum Actinobacteria, whereas staphylococci belong to the phylum Firmicutes, indicating a distant evolutionary relationship. However, these genera may still be detected alongside staphylococci in the air samples due to shared habitats and characteristics[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR9 "Parte, A. C., Sardà Carbasse, J., Meier-Kolthoff, J. P., Reimer, L. C. & Göker, M. List of prokaryotic names with standing in nomenclature (LPSN) moves to the DSMZ. Int. J. Syst. Evol. MicroBiol. 70(11), 5607–5612.                    https://doi.org/10.1099/ijsem.0.004332                                     (2020)."). For instance, _Corynebacterium_ and staphylococci coexist on human skin and have adapted to similar environmental conditions[29](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR29 "Grice, E. A. & Segre, J. A. The skin microbiome. Nat. Rev. Microbiol. 9(4), 244–253.                    https://doi.org/10.1038/nrmicro2537                                     (2011)."). Likewise, micrococci and staphylococci share morphological similarities, such as their cocci shape. Additionally, micrococci can persist in bioaerosols due to their resilience in harsh environmental conditions.

The 49 confirmed staphylococcal isolates belonged to eight species: _S. cohnii_, _S. sciuri_, _S. arlettae_, _S. epidermidis_, _S. aureus_, _S. saprophyticus_, _S. nepalensis and S. kloosii_ (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig2) a). Notably, 95% (47/49) were CoNS, while only two were identified as CoPS. These results align with previous findings of CoNS predominance in bioaerosols[30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR30 "Bonvegna, M. et al. Of the identified species, 55% (27/49) were human-associated, with _S. epidermidis_ (8 isolates, 16%) being the most prevalent, followed by _S. haemolyticus_ (7 isolates, 14%), _S. saprophyticus_ (7 isolates, 14%), and _S. cohnii_ (3 isolates, 6%). Animal-associated staphylococci accounted for 45% (22/49), with _S. arlettae_ (16 isolates, 34%) being the dominant species, followed by _S. nepalensis_ (4 isolates, 8%) and _S. kloosii_ (2 isolates, 4%).

### Methicillin resistance among the airborne presumptive staphylococci isolates

Methicillin-resistance was assessed using the oxacillin screening method. Out of the 323 PBPS, 65 isolates (20%) were methicillin-resistant. The highest proportion of presumptive MRS was observed in VVUS and MMC, regardless of the season (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig3) a–c). Although the total staphylococcal load was lower during the summer, the percentage of methicillin resistance was higher across all locations. For instance, no methicillin-resistant isolates were found at STP, JNU during monsoon (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig3) a). But, 100% of summer isolates were methicillin-resistant (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig3) c), with only one resistant isolate detected during winter (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig3) b). At MMC, 67% of indoor isolates and 32% of outdoor isolates were methicillin-resistance in summer (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig3) c).

### Multidrug resistance profiling of methicillin-resistant staphylococci species

Resistance profiles of the 49 MRS isolates were determined against 15 antibiotics from 10 classes. Notably, 36 isolates (73%) were classified as MDRS (Fig. [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig5) a, Table [S5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#MOESM1)).

Among the total 49 isolates (Fig. [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig4) and Table [S5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#MOESM1)), 75% were resistant to macrolides (azithromycin and erythromycin), likely reflecting increased antibiotic usage during the COVID-19 pandemic[31](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR31 "Bogdanić, N., Močibob, L., Vidović, T., Soldo, A. & Begovać, J. Azithromycin consumption during the COVID- 19 pandemic in Croatia, 2020. PLoS ONE  17(2).                    https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0263437                                     (2022)."), [32](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR32 "Atal, S., Misra, S. & Balakrishnan, S. Care. 10(12), 4341.                    https://doi.org/10.4103/jfmpc.jfmpc_812_21                                     (2021)."). Resistance to β-lactams was observed in 49–73% of isolates (ampicillin 73%, cefoxitin 63% and cefuroxime 49%), and, 59% showed resistant to trimethoprim. Resistance to clindamycin (37%), tetracycline (32%) and chloramphenicol (32%) was also noted. Fluoroquinolone resistance was lower, with norfloxacin (20%) and levofloxacin (18%) being less effective than ciprofloxacin (8%). None of the isolates exhibited resistance to rifampicin or linezolid.

Species-wise, _S. arlettae_ among animal-associated species exhibited resistance to the majority of the tested antibiotics, while _S. epidermidis_ isolates showed the highest resistance among human-associated species followed by _S. haemolyticus_, _S. aureus_ and _S. saprophyticus_ and _S. cohnii_ (Fig. [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig5) b).

Despite the growing awareness of AMR, there is a paucity of studies focusing on airborne ARBs and their corresponding ARGs, which is an emerging public health and environmental concern.

This study aimed to assess the prevalence of staphylococci, including both CoPS and CoNS, in bioaerosols collected from various urban settings in Delhi, India. The findings revealed a significantly high staphylococcal load in both indoor and outdoor air, with concentrations exceeding 16,000 CFU/m3, far surpassing the WHO-recommended limit of 1000 CFU/m3 for microbial exposure in air[38](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR38 "W.H.O., WHO guidelines for indoor air quality: Dampness and mould. Regional Office for Europe & Parker, M.T. Hospital acquired infections: Guidelines to laboratory methods. (WHO, Copenhagen, 2009)."). The highest staphylococcal load was observed during the winter (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig1) a and b), especially in crowded urban areas like MMC and VVUS, indicating that human activities and population density significantly contribute to microbial aerosolization[39](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR39 "Heo, K. Sci. 104, 58–65.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jaerosci.2016.11.008                                     (2017)."). The higher concentration of airborne staphylococci during the winter season in Delhi may be attributed to cooler temperatures, which may preserve and stabilize bacteria, allowing them to survive longer in the air. Additionally, the relatively lower humidity levels in winter can reduce the natural die-off of bacteria, resulting in higher concentrations of staphylococci in the air. Furthermore, poor air quality and elevated levels of particulate matter during winter may provide surfaces for bacteria to attach, facilitating their transport through the air[15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR15 "Kumar, P., Singh, A. Interestingly, the staphylococcal load indoors remained relatively high throughout the monsoon and summer seasons, likely due to favourable conditions for microbial growth, such as optimal humidity and temperature indoors. In contrast, the lower outdoor loads during the monsoon can be attributed to rainfall, which reduces airborne particulate matter and microbial contaminants[20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR20 "Moon, K. W., Huh, E. H. & Jeong, H. C. Seasonal evaluation of bioaerosols from indoor air of residential apartments within the metropolitan area in South Korea. Environ. Monit. Assess. 186, 2111–2120.                    https://doi.org/10.1007/s10661-013-3521-8                                     (2014)."). The lowest staphylococcal load occurred in summer, when adverse meteorological conditions in Delhi—such as high temperature, low relative humidity, and intense sunlight—may have contributed to the lethal effects on airborne microbes. In contrast, studies from other regions have reported higher staphylococcal load during the summer[20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR20 "Moon, K. W., Huh, E. H. & Jeong, H. Another intriguing result was the discrepancy between genotype and phenotype in several isolates. ## Conclusion

This study is the first of its kind in Delhi, revealing a significantly high staphylococcal load in both indoor and outdoor air of urban environments, exceeding 16,000 CFU/m3—well above the WHO-recommended limit of 1000 CFU/m3 for microbial exposure. Approximately 75% of the MRS isolates demonstrated multidrug resistance. The antibiotic resistance phenotypes correlated with genotypes associated with commonly used antibiotics, such as trimethoprim, gentamicin, β-lactams, and macrolides. These findings suggest that environmental reservoirs of AMR pose potential public health risks in urban areas, particularly due to high prevalence of MDR staphylococci harbouring ARGs.

The study highlights the critical need for comprehensive monitoring and reporting on environmental AMR, including both antibiotic-resistant bacteria and their associated genetic markers. Such efforts are essential to accurately assess the scope of the AMR threat and to inform the development of integrated action plans aimed at mitigating the growing challenges posed by AMR in urban settings.

## Materials and methods

### Study site and meteorological conditions

The land use pattern of an area can significantly influences the prevalence, composition, and seasonal dynamics of the bioaerosols at specific sites. In this study, four sites in the southern part of the Delhi region were selected for air sampling. An image map of all four sampling locations was created using ArcMap software ver. 10.7.1 and is presented in Fig. [S1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#MOESM1) (supplementary material).

Air sampling was conducted in the southwest Delhi region after the second wave of COVID-19, from August 28, 2021 to April 30, 2022. The sampling period spanned three distinct seasons— summer, monsoon, and winter — to capture seasonal variability. Brief descriptions of the sampling sites are provided in Table [S1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#MOESM1) (Supplementary Material). Environmental and meteorological parameters, including temperature, relative humidity, and light intensity, were recorded at the sampling sites during all three sampling seasons and are summarized in Table [S2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#MOESM1) (Supplementary Material).

### Sampling procedures

Air samples were collected using an SKC biosampler (UK) operating at a flow rate of 12.5 L/min for 30 min in 20 mL phosphate-buffered saline (PBS, pH 7.4, 10 mM) as the collection medium. Environmental parameters such as temperature, relative humidity (RH), wind speed, and light intensity were recorded using a handheld Fisherbrand Enviro-Meter during air sampling. The type of ventilation system and the number of individuals present at each site were also documented. All equipment and materials were sterilized before sampling to avoid contamination. Bioaerosol sampling was performed at a height of 1.5 m above the ground, simulating the human breathing zone. The sampler targeted breathable bioaerosols, primarily capturing particles ranging from PM2.5 to PM10[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR14 "Kumari, H., Chakraborti, T., Singh, M., Chakrawarti, M. K. & Mukhopadhyay, K. Prevalence and antibiogram of coagulase negative staphylococci in bioaerosols from different indoors of a university in India. BMC Microbiol. 20, 1–14.                    https://doi.org/10.1186/s12866-020-01875-8                                     (2020)."). After collection, the bacterial samples were kept on ice until transported to the laboratory.

### Enumeration of bacterial colonies

After air sampling, 100 µL of collected liquid bioaerosol was spread onto 90 mm Petri plates in triplicates using an L-shaped spreader. The plates contained mannitol salt agar (MSA) with cycloheximide (100 µg/mL) to inhibit fungal growth. Colony counts were conducted after 72 h of incubation, and the presumptive culturable airborne staphylococcal load (PCASL) was expressed as colony forming units (CFU) per cubic meter of air (CFU/m3) according to established protocol[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR14 "Kumari, H., Chakraborti, T., Singh, M., Chakrawarti, M. K. & Mukhopadhyay, K. Prevalence and antibiogram of coagulase negative staphylococci in bioaerosols from different indoors of a university in India. BMC Microbiol. 20, 1–14.                    https://doi.org/10.1186/s12866-020-01875-8                                     (2020).").

### Isolation of airborne staphylococcal isolates and biochemical characterization

Each colony that grew on MSA plates was subjected to secondary purification on MSA. Subsequently, tertiary purification was performed on Baird Parker Agar (BPA) plates. Purified colonies were then streaked onto Brain Heart Infusion (BHI) agar plates for the preparation of bacterial stocks in 30% glycerol (v/v) for subsequent biochemical analyses, included Gram staining, catalase test, oxidase test, coagulase test, and staphylo-monotec test as previously described[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR14 "Kumari, H., Chakraborti, T., Singh, M., Chakrawarti, M. K. & Mukhopadhyay, K. Prevalence and antibiogram of coagulase negative staphylococci in bioaerosols from different indoors of a university in India. BMC Microbiol. 20, 1–14.                    https://doi.org/10.1186/s12866-020-01875-8                                     (2020)."). Detailed results are provided in Table [S3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#MOESM1) (Supplementary Material).

### Agar screening for preliminary oxacillin resistance

Preliminary detection of methicillin-resistant coagulase-positive staphylococci (MRCoPS) and coagulase-negative staphylococci (MRCoNS) was performed using Mueller- Hinton agar supplemented with 4% NaCl and containing 6 µg/mL and 1 µg/mL of oxacillin, respectively, following Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI) guidelines[54](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR54 "CLSI. Performance standards for antimicrobial susceptibility testing, 28th edition. CLSI guidelines M100. (Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute, 2018)."). Colonies growing on these plates were considered presumptive oxacillin-resistant. _S. aureus_ ATCC 33591 and _S. aureus_ ATCC 29213 were served as resistant and sensitive control strains, respectively, for CoPS while for CoNS, _S. epidermidis_ ATCC 35984 and _S. epidermidis_ ATCC 14990 were used as resistant and sensitive reference strains, respectively.

### Disc diffusion assay for antibiotic susceptibility testing

Antimicrobial susceptibility testing of the staphylococcal isolates was conducted using the disk-diffusion method, following CLSI guidelines[54](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR54 "CLSI. Performance standards for antimicrobial susceptibility testing, 28th edition. CLSI guidelines M100. (Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute, 2018)."). A total of 15 antimicrobial agents from 10 functional classes, encompassing four mechanisms of action, were tested. These included cell wall synthesis inhibitors (ampicillin 10 µg, cefoxitin 30 µg, trimethoprim 5 µg, cefuroxime 30 µg), protein synthesis inhibitors (azithromycin 15 µg, clindamycin 2 µg, erythromycin 15 µg, gentamycin 10 µg, linezolid 30 µg, tetracycline 30 µg, chloramphenicol C 30 µg), DNA synthesis inhibitors (ciprofloxacin 5 µg, levofloxacin 5 µg, norfloxacin 10 µg), and RNA synthesis inhibitors (rifampicin 5 µg).

### Broth microdilution assay for MIC determination

The minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) of oxacillin of all the presumptive oxacillin-resistant staphylococcal isolates was determined using the broth micro-dilution method in cation-adjusted Mueller Hinton Broth (MHB), according to CLSI guidelines[54](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR54 "CLSI. Performance standards for antimicrobial susceptibility testing, 28th edition. CLSI guidelines M100. (Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute, 2018)."). Antibiotics were serially double-diluted in the medium, and an inoculum density of 5 × 105 CFU/mL, prepared from overnight bacterial culture, was used. Uninoculated media served as the negative control, and untreated inoculated wells were the positive control. The MIC was defined as the lowest antibiotic concentration that prevented visible turbidity after 24 h of incubation. _S. aureus_ ATCC 25923 was used as the reference strain.

### 16S rRNA gene amplification for Sanger sequencing and staphylococci species identification

Universal primer set 27F and 1492R was used to amplify the 16S rRNA gene from DNA templates extracted following a previously described protocol[55](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#ref-CR55 "Kameoka, S. et al. Benchmark of 16S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing using Japanese gut microbiome data from the V1–V2 and V3–V4 primer sets. BMC Genom. 22(1), 1–10.                    https://doi.org/10.1186/s12864-021-07746-4                                     (2021)."). The resulting PCR products were sequenced using Sanger sequencing with both primers. Bacterial species were identified by comparing the sequences against the NCBI BLAST database, with species confirmed when a match showed ≥ 99% similarity.

### Detection of mecA encoding for methicillin resistance

The presence of the _mecA_ gene, which encodes methicillin resistance in staphylococci, was detected using PCR. The PCR reaction mixture (25 µL) contained template DNA (≥ 10 ng/µL), forward and reverse (0.4 µM) (Table [S4a](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#MOESM1)), 2.5 mM dNTP, 1X Ex Taq buffer, and Ex Taq polymerase (0.10 U/µL) (Takara Co., Ltd., Kyoto, Japan). PCR amplification involved an initial denaturation at 95 °C, 3 min, followed by 30 cycles of denaturation (95 °C for, 30 s), annealing (47 °C for 45 s), and extension (72 °C for 30 s); with a final extension at 72 °C for 5 min. PCR products of 310 bp for _mecA_ and 651 bp for _femB_ were visualised using ethidium bromide staining. _S. aureus_ ATCC 33591 and _S. aureus_ ATCC 29213 served as positive and negative controls, respectively.

### Detection of other antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs)

In addition to detecting _mecA_, we performed multiplex PCR assays targeting 14 different resistance genes (Table [S4a](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#MOESM1)). these included _blaZ_ for ampicillin resistance, _ermC_ and _ermA_ for erythromycin and _tetK_ and _tetM_ for tetracycline resistance. Each gene was amplified using specific multiplex PCR protocols detailed in Table [S4b](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#MOESM1) (Supplementary Material).

### Statistical analysis

GraphPad Prism version 8.0 software was used to perform statistical significance, and the results are presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD).

The normality test was performed using Shapiro-Wilk method, as the sampling size was less than 50, employing GraphPad Prism version 8.0. Our staphylococcal load data across seasons, for both indoors and outdoors, passed the normality test. Subsequently, we have applied two-way-ANOVA with Tukey’s multiple comparison test (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95462-4#Fig1) a and b) to determine if there were any significant difference among staphylococcal loads from indoor and outdoor environments of each site vs. seasons. Additionally, to assess whether the variance between indoor and outdoor data was significant, we conducted two-way-ANOVA for staphylococcal load across different sites, comparing indoor and outdoor environments for each season separately.
