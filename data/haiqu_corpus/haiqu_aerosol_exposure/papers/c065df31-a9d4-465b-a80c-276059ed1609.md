Short-term effects of meteorological factors on hand, foot, and mouth disease in Zhengzhou, China


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w.pdf)

## Abstract

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) demonstrates distinct seasonal patterns in high-incidence regions, highlighting the growing interest in the relationship between meteorological factors and disease transmission. Nevertheless, findings across different geographic areas remain highly heterogeneous. Data on HFMD cases in Zhengzhou City from 2009 to 2023 were obtained from the Zhengzhou Center for Disease Control and Prevention, while meteorological data were sourced from the National Meteorological Data Sharing Service System. Distributed lag nonlinear models (DLNM) was employed to quantify the effects of meteorological variables and extreme weather events on HFMD incidence, as well as to evaluate the impact of various lag periods. DLNM analysis revealed nonlinear exposure–response relationships between HFMD risk with daily mean temperature, relative humidity, and atmospheric pressure. Extreme weather events further elevated the risk. Daily mean temperature demonstrated the peaking relative risk ( _RR_) at 27 °C ( _RR_ = 1.17, 95% _CI_: 1.007–2.349), while relative humidity at 83% corresponded to the highest _RR_ ( _RR_ = 1.07, 95% _CI_: 1.021–1.121). Atmospheric pressure exhibited maximal risk at 1044 hPa ( _RR_ = 1.53, 95% _CI_: 1.014–2.293). These conditions correspond to the higher end of the local climatic distribution, suggesting that HFMD risk increases under relatively warm, humid, and high-pressure conditions. Meteorological factors, including daily average temperature, relative humidity, atmospheric pressure, and extreme weather events, are associated with altered HFMD incidence in Zhengzhou through nonlinear and lagged relationships. These findings provide scientific evidence for early warning systems and targeted public health interventions based on weather forecasts.

## Materials and methods

### Data collection

HFMD cases from January 1, 2009, to December 31, 2023, in Zhengzhou City were obtained from the Disease Surveillance Information Reporting System of the Zhengzhou Center for Disease Control and Prevention. Demographic data were extracted from the Zhengzhou Statistical Yearbook. Daily meteorological variables, including average temperature (°C), relative humidity (%), and atmospheric pressure (hPa), were retrieved from Zhengzhou Meteorological Data Service.

### Diagnostic criteria and case information

HFMD cases were diagnosed in accordance with the Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease Clinical Guidelines (2008 and 2018 edition)[15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#ref-CR15 "Li, X. W. et al. Chinese guidelines for the diagnosis and treatment of hand, foot and mouth disease (2018 edition). World J. Pediatr. 14 (5), 437–447.                    https://doi.org/10.1007/s12519-018-0189-8                                     (2018)."). Mild cases were characterized by the presence of rashes on the hands, feet, mouth, or buttocks, with or without fever. Severe cases were identified by neurological complications, such as meningitis, encephalitis, encephalomyelitis, or cardiorespiratory failure.

Demographic and clinical information collected included patient sex, age, occupation, date of diagnosis, date of symptom onset, disease severity, and residential address. All personally identifiable information was anonymized to maintain patient confidentiality. Case report forms were completed by trained healthcare professionals and submitted to the surveillance system within 24 h. Records with incomplete address information or missing data were excluded from the final analysis.

### Statistical analyses

All statistical analyses were conducted in _R_ version 4.3.3 using the dlnm and splines packages. Two-sided _P_ values less than 0.05 were considered as statistically significant.

### Model analysis

$$Log\\left\[ {E\\left( {Y\_{{it}} } \\right)} \\right\] = \\alpha + cb\\left( {Meteorological{\\text{ }}factors\_{{it}} ,lag = {\\text{1}}0} \\right) + ns\\left( {time,df} \\right){\\text{ }} + holiday{\\text{ }} + {\\text{ }}dow$$

Where _Y_ _it_ represents the daily number of HFMD cases in region _i_ on day _t_, and α representing the intercept. Meteorological factors for region _i_ on day _t_ include daily mean temperature, mean relative humidity, and mean atmospheric pressure. All three meteorological variables were simultaneously included in the same DLNM framework to estimate their independent effects while mutually adjusting for potential confounding among environmental factors. The maximum lag was set to 10 days. The cross-basis ( _cb_), implemented using a B-spline ( _bs_) with 3 degrees of freedom ( _df_), models the nonlinear and lagged effects of meteorological variables. Long-term temporal trends were controlled using a natural cubic spline for time with 12 df. Additionally, the variables _dow_ _t_ and _holiday_ represent the day of the week and public holidays respectively.

## Results

### Epidemiological characteristics of HFMD and meteorological factors in Zhengzhou from 2009 to 2023

From 2009 to 2023, Zhengzhou exhibited marked temporal fluctuations in HFMD incidence. The average annual incidence rate was 14.48 per 10,000 population, peaking at 26.75 per 10,000 in 2016, followed by a gradual decline. Notably, HFMD incidence dropped sharply between 2020 and 2022 under stringent non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPIs), with a subsequent rebound post-2022 as restrictions were lifted (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#Fig1); Supplementary Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#MOESM1)). Disease severity also displayed temporal variation. The highest proportion of severe cases was recorded in 2011, with a severe case rate of 7.46% and a case fatality rate of 18.80 per 10,000. Since 2016, both metrics declined significantly, with no HFMD-related deaths reported after 2019. These trends were statistically significant ( _P_ < 0.001).

During the study period, the average daily meteorological conditions were as follows: temperature 15.48 ± 9.93 °C, relative humidity 60.38 ± 18.12%, and atmospheric pressure 1016.71 ± 10.23 hPa (Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#Tab1)). Overall, HFMD incidence exhibited pronounced seasonal variation. Prior to 2020, a clear bimodal pattern was observed, with a major peak occurring between April and June and a secondary peak between October and November. Among these, the highest number of cases was consistently recorded in May. Following the implementation of non-pharmaceutical interventions during the COVID-19 pandemic, the seasonal pattern of HFMD changed markedly. From 2020 to 2022, the traditional bimodal pattern was replaced by a single annual peak, occurring in September 2020, June 2021, and between July and September 2022, respectively. In 2023, after the relaxation of most intervention measures, HFMD incidence again exhibited a single peak occurring in July. These temporal patterns corresponded closely with seasonal fluctuations in temperature and humidity, as depicted in Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#Fig2), suggesting a strong link between meteorological conditions and HFMD transmission dynamics.

Given the significant correlations observed among meteorological variables, particularly between atmospheric pressure and both temperature and relative humidity, simple correlation analyses may not adequately reflect the independent association between atmospheric pressure and HFMD incidence. Therefore, partial correlation analyses were conducted to further disentangle the potential confounding effects of temperature and relative humidity. As shown in Supplementary Table [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#MOESM1), atmospheric pressure remained significantly associated with HFMD incidence after controlling for temperature ( _r_ = − 0.042, _P_ = 0.002) and relative humidity ( _r_ = − 0.38, _P_ < 0.001). When both temperature and relative humidity were simultaneously adjusted for, the association between atmospheric pressure and HFMD incidence remained statistically significant ( _r_ = − 0.046, _P_ = 0.001). These findings suggest that atmospheric pressure exerts an independent inverse effect on HFMD incidence beyond the influence of other major meteorological factors. To further validate the independence of these variables and rule out potential multicollinearity issues, we calculated the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF). As shown in Supplementary Table [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#MOESM1), the VIF values for daily mean temperature, relative humidity, and atmospheric pressure were 5.46, 1.05, and 5.54, respectively. All values were below the threshold of 10, confirming that multicollinearity was not a confounding issue in our model.

## Cumulative lagged effects of meteorological factors on HFMD

Figure [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#Fig3) illustrates the exposure–lag–response relationships between meteorological factors and HFMD incidence, highlighting the nonlinear and delayed effects of temperature, relative humidity, and atmospheric pressure. Temperature exhibited a characteristic U-shaped association (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#Fig3) A/D), with both extreme heat and cold increasing HFMD risk via distinct lag structures. Relative humidity showed a similar nonlinear pattern, with prolonged exposure to high humidity leading to elevated cumulative risk, particularly at extended lag periods (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#Fig3) B/E). A rising trend in HFMD risk was also observed with high atmospheric pressure as lag time increased (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#Fig3) C/F).

Using the median daily temperature (16.5 °C) as a reference, the exposure–response curve (Fig. [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#Fig4) A) revealed a protective effect in the range of − 5 °C to 16.5 °C, while temperatures between 17 °C and 34 °C were associated with a gradual increase in HFMD risk. ### Effects of meteorological factors on HFMD at different cumulative lag days

Figure [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#Fig5) illustrates temporal variations in meteorological-HFMD associations through DLNM. For temperature, no significant risk was observed at low temperatures on lag days 0 and 3. However, at longer lags (7 and 10 days), temperatures below the median demonstrated protective effects, while rising temperatures significantly increased HFMD risk. In contrast, higher temperatures at lag 3 and lag 7 days were significantly associated with elevated risk ( _P_ < 0.05). Relative humidity demonstrated divergent temporal patterns. On lag day 0, high humidity (≥ 62%) was associated with a protective effect, with relative risk ( _RR_) decreasing as humidity increased. At lags 3 and 7, the pattern reversed: lower humidity (0–62%) remained protective, whereas higher humidity levels were associated with increased risk, followed by a decline in _RR_. The elevated risk associated with high humidity at these lags was statistically significant ( _P_ < 0.05). Atmospheric pressure exhibited threshold-dependent effects. Sub-median atmospheric pressure (< 1017 hPa) at lag 0 and 10 days was associated with protective effect. Conversely, supra-median pressure (≥ 1017 hPa) at lags 3 and 7 days was linked to a significant monotonic increase in _RR_.

### Effects of extreme meteorological factor on the incidence of HFMD

Time-lagged associations between extreme meteorological exposures and HFMD incidence were quantified through distributed using DLNM, analyzing both single-day and cumulative effects.

### Single-day lag associations

Figure [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#Fig6) and Supplementary Tables [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#MOESM1)– [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#MOESM1) present the single-day effects of extreme meteorological conditions across different lag days. Exposure to extreme cold (P5: 0 °C) demonstrated a consistent protective effect beginning from lag day 3, reaching statistical significance from lag days 6 to 10, with the strongest effect at lag 10 ( _RR_ = 0.91; 95% _CI_: 0.855–0.970). In contrast, extreme heat (P95: 29 °C) exhibited a U-shaped pattern with elevated, but not statistically significant, _RR_ across all lag days.

Extremely low humidity (P5: 29%) displayed a U-shaped lag-response, with protective effects emerging from lag day 2 and reaching significance between days 3–9, peaking at lag 6 ( _RR_ = 0.98, 95% _CI_: 0.969–0.988). Conversely, extremely high humidity (P95: 88%) showed an inverted U-shaped pattern, being protective at lag days 0–2 but increasing HFMD risk from lag days 3–10, peaking at lag 7 ( _RR_ = 1.02; 95% _CI_: 1.013–1.030).

Extremely low atmospheric pressure (P5: 1001 hPa) presented a protective inverted U-shaped lag pattern. Statistically significant protective effects were observed on lag 0 and lag 9, persisting for approximately two days. The most pronounced protective effect occurred at lag 0 ( _RR_ = 0.97; 95% _CI_: 0.949–0.994). Extremely high atmospheric pressure (P95: 1033 hPa) was associated with increased risk beginning at lag day 2, peaking at lag 4 ( _RR_ = 1.03, 95% _CI_: 1.007–1.044), followed by gradual decline.

### Cumulative effects of extreme meteorological factors on HFMD at different lag times

Supplementary Tables [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#MOESM1)– [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#MOESM1) and Fig. [7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#Fig7) illustrate the cumulative lag effects of extreme meteorological exposures on HFMD. Extreme cold (P5: 0 °C) was associated with sustained protective effects across cumulative lags, peaking at lag days 0–10 ( _RR_ = 0.71, 95% _CI_: 0.567–0.900). Conversely, extreme heat (P95: 29 °C) was linked to progressively increasing risk, becoming significant from lag 0–8 and peaking at lag 0–10 ( _RR_ = 1.17; 95% _CI_: 1.026–1.327).

Cumulative exposure to extremely low humidity (P5: 29%) showed significant protection beginning at lag 0–6, with maximum benefit at lag 0–10 ( _RR_ = 0.88, 95% _CI_: 0.814–0.951). Conversely, extremely high humidity (P95: 88%) demonstrated transient protective effects during lag 0–3 days, with peak protection at lag days 0–2 ( _RR_ = 0.95, 95% _CI_: 0.911–0.977). However, this pattern reversed after lag day 7, with a non-significant increase in risk observed by lag days 0–7.

Exposure to low atmospheric pressure (P5: 1001 hPa) was associated with cumulative protective effects, reaching significance at lag days 0–1 ( _RR_ = 0.96, 95% CI: 0.922–0.995). High atmospheric pressure (P95: 1033 hPa), however, was associated with increased cumulative risk from lag days 0–3, peaking over lag days 0–10 ( _RR_ = 1.28; 95% _CI_: 1.100–1.480).

In summary, extreme meteorological conditions exhibit complex lagged and cumulative impacts on HFMD incidence. Extreme low temperatures and humidity show protective effects. In contrast, extremely high temperatures and humidity serve as risk factors during the lag period, with meteorological effects exhibiting different trends over time. Atmospheric pressure changes demonstrate similar lag-dependent trends, highlighting the importance of considering both intensity and duration of exposure in disease modeling.

## Discussion

This longitudinal study analyzed surveillance data from 2009 to 2023 to quantify meteorological influences on HFMD transmission dynamics in Zhengzhou, China, providing an evidence base for targeted interventions. Nonlinear associations were identified between HFMD incidence and environmental variables, including temperature, humidity, and atmospheric pressure.

Between 2009 and 2023, Zhengzhou exhibited HFMD outbreaks with an average annual incidence of 14.48 per 10,000 population. The epidemic peaked in 2016 at 26.75 per 10,000, followed by a steady decline, particularly after 2018. However, a notable resurgence was observed in 2023, potentially linked to the relaxation of nonpharmaceutical interventions[21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#ref-CR21 "Zhao, Z. et al. Impact of the coronavirus disease 2019 interventions on the incidence of hand, foot, and mouth disease in Mainland China. Lancet Reg. Health West. Pac. 20, 100362.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lanwpc.2021.100362                                     (2022). From NLM PubMed-not-MEDLINE."). Severe cases averaged 1.54 per 10,000 annually, with the highest severity (7.46%) and case-fatality rate (18.80 per 10,000) recorded in 2011. Following 2016, the number of severe cases declined markedly, with no severe cases reported since 2019 and no fatalities recorded after 2021, supporting the effectiveness of current vaccination and preventive strategies[22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#ref-CR22 "Lu, J. et al. Hand, foot and mouth disease in Guangdong, China, in 2013: new trends in the continuing epidemic. Clin. Microbiol. Infect. 20 (7), O442–445.                    https://doi.org/10.1111/1469-0691.12468                                     (2014). From NLM Medline."), [23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#ref-CR23 "Xing, W. et al. Hand, foot, and mouth disease in China, 2008-12: an epidemiological study. Lancet Infect. Dis. 14 (4), 308–318.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/S1473-3099(13)70342-6                                     (2014). From NLM Medline.").

Our findings highlight a temperature-dependent modulation of HFMD transmission risk. Elevated temperatures were associated with increased outbreak potential, while cooler conditions exhibited protective effects. Maximum risk probability occurred at 27 °C, aligning with reports from Beijing where optimal risk occurs between 25.0 °C and 27.5 °C [9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#ref-CR9 "Xu, M. et al. The paradoxically lower risk at temperatures exceeding P95 percentile (29 °C) may attributed to behavioral adaptations during extreme heat, such as reduced outdoor exposure and increased time spent in air-conditioned environments[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-39545-w#ref-CR25 "Wang, P., Zhao, H., You, F., Zhou, H. & Goggins, W. A positive association was also observed between atmospheric pressure and HFMD incidence. Cumulative risk peaked at 1044 hPa ( _RR_ = 1.53; 95% _CI_: 1.014–2.293). Extremely low pressure (P5: 1001 hPa) demonstrated protective effects with minimal risk observed at lag 0 ( _RR_ = 0.97, 95% _CI_ 0.949–0.994). In contrast, persistent exposure to extremely high pressure (P95) significantly elevated cumulative risk across lag 0–10 days ( _RR_ = 1.28, 95% _CI_ 1.100–1.480). These findings are generally consistent with previous epidemiological studies reporting a positive association between elevated atmospheric pressure and increased HFMD incidence. Taken together, the spatiotemporal heterogeneity in meteorological impacts on HFMD underscores the necessity for adaptive prevention strategies that account for dynamic weather patterns and associated human behavioral responses. Compared with earlier studies, our findings further extend existing evidence by characterizing both immediate and cumulative lag effects of atmospheric pressure on HFMD transmission using a DLNM framework.

It should be noted that meteorological variables such as temperature, relative humidity, and atmospheric pressure are inherently interrelated, reflecting shared physical and climatic processes. In the present study, strong correlations were observed, particularly between temperature and atmospheric pressure. Although VIF diagnostics indicated that the degree of multicollinearity remained within acceptable limits, suggesting that model estimates were not severely distorted, some degree of interdependence among these variables is unavoidable. Therefore, the estimated exposure–response relationships should be interpreted as conditional associations, reflecting the effect of each meteorological factor while accounting for the presence of the others. This interdependence underscores the complexity of climatic systems, where meteorological factors often function as a composite environmental exposure rather than isolated drivers. Validation in regions with different climatic patterns would be valuable to further confirm the independent roles of these meteorological factors.

The combined descriptive and modeling results have important practical implications for public health preparedness. The DLNM analyses indicate that warm and humid climatic conditions, particularly those occurring in late spring and summer, are associated with higher risk periods. In practical terms, authorities should heighten vigilance not only during the typical season but also during prolonged warm spells or unusually warm autumns that extend into September or October. Incorporating meteorological monitoring into routine surveillance could trigger timely alerts when these high-risk windows appear. Conversely, the model indicates a clear protective effect associated with cold winters and dry cold spells. These environmental barriers effectively suppress viral transmission, likely through reduced viral stability and decreased contact opportunities. Recognizing these low-risk periods allows public health resources to be conserved or shifted to other seasonal respiratory pathogens, optimizing the allocation of medical efforts throughout the year.

This study has several limitations. First, although various meteorological variables were analyzed, potential interactive effects with air pollutants were not assessed. Second, as the data were geographically limited to Zhengzhou, generalizability to other regions may be constrained. Considering the regional differences in HFMD epidemiology driven by climatic and sociodemographic factors, further validation studies in diverse settings are warranted. Third, although multicollinearity among meteorological variables was assessed using VIFs and found to be within acceptable limits, some degree of interdependence between temperature, humidity, and atmospheric pressure is unavoidable. Therefore, the estimated effects should be interpreted as conditional associations rather than entirely independent effects.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, meteorological factors, including high temperature, humidity, atmospheric pressure, and extreme weather events, play a significant role in influencing the risk of HFMD. This study provides robust evidence to inform the refinement of HFMD prevention and control strategies. We advocate for the integration of multi-pathogen surveillance systems, meteorological early warning mechanisms, and regionally coordinated prevention frameworks to effectively reduce the disease burden. Furthermore, targeted interventions that consider climatic variability and human-environment interactions will be crucial for mitigating HFMD transmission.
