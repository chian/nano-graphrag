Predictive algorithm for COVID-19 infection risk in indoor environments


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-12626-y.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-12626-y.pdf)

## Abstract

After the onset of the global COVID-19 pandemic, the deep connections between environmental factors and the transmission of airborne infectious diseases (including COVID-19) has become an area of relevant scientific and social interest. Indoor environments, where we spend a significant part of our daily lives, play a crucial role in shaping the dynamics of disease spread. The mitigation of infection risk related to poor indoor air quality and its link with the transmission of airborne diseases has emerged as a focal point for research and intervention strategies. This paper presents the results of a specific collaborative project in this field, focused on the utilization of Internet of Things (IoT) devices for comprehensive indoor environmental monitoring and infectious risk forecasting. In the frame of developing effective countermeasures for COVID-19 and future pandemic preparedness, our primary goal was to develop a predictive model for infection risk in indoor environments. Parameters such as humidity, temperature, CO2, and particulate matter concentrations (namely PM10 and PM2.5), have been assessed and modelled as indicators of indoor air quality, with these measures having been combined to generate a predictive algorithm specifically able to provide information about the transmission dynamics of COVID-19 and airborne infectious diseases within indoor spaces. This newly-developed Algorithm for the Prediction of Risk of Infections (APRI) relies on rigorous analyses and established different risk thresholds based on temperature, humidity, and CO2 levels. The model showed significant associations between environmental factors, such as temperature, CO2 levels, humidity, and particulate matter concentrations. A pivotal role of PM10 and PM2.5 in shaping air quality in indoor environments has been highlighted, as low PM concentrations corresponded in our predictive model to a minimal risk of airborne infectious diseases, while medium or high PM levels were associated with variations in temperature, humidity, and CO2 levels, thus corresponding to an elevated risk of infection, particularly in the frame of highly diffusive diseases like COVID-19.

### Labeling the dataset

To construct a usable dataset for supervised learning, it was necessary to first assign labels to the collected observations, as the raw data consisted solely of environmental measurements (e.g., temperature, humidity) without any predefined indicators of COVID-19 transmission risk. To address this, we employed an unsupervised learning approach, K-means clustering, to infer risk levels based on environmental patterns. The k-means method aims to partition the points into k groups such that the sum of squares from points to the assigned cluster centers is minimized. At the minimum, all cluster centers are at the mean of the set of data points which are nearest to the cluster center. Typically, to process the learning data, the K-means algorithm in data mining starts with a first group of randomly selected centroids, which are used as the beginning points for every cluster, and then performs iterative (repetitive) calculations to optimize the positions of the centroids. It halts creating and optimizing clusters when either: The centroids have stabilized — there is no change in their values because the clustering has been successful. The defined number of iterations has been achieved. For our algorithm, centroids were chosen based on certain thresholds as referred in the study carried out during the COVID-19 pandemic by Di Gilio et al.[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-12626-y#ref-CR2 "Di Gilio, A. et al. CO₂ concentration monitoring inside educational buildings as a strategic tool to reduce the risk of SARSCoV-2 airborne transmission. Environ. Res. 202, 111560.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.envres.2021.111560                                     (2021).") to identify specific CO2 thresholds associated to low, medium and high-risk of COVID-19 spread in indoor environments. The thresholds are based on temperature, humidity and CO2 levels and were identified as described in Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-12626-y#Fig3).

## Apply ML techniques: inference with regression

After labeling the dataset through clustering, the next step is to enable prediction of risk levels for new, unseen observations. While clustering groups existing data into discrete categories, it does not provide a way to estimate risk continuously or to infer risk on future measurements. To address this, supervised machine learning regression techniques are applied to learn the relationship between environmental parameters and the assigned risk labels. The goal is to develop a predictive model capable of estimating a risk score for any new observation based on its sensor data, allowing real-time assessment and more nuanced risk evaluation beyond fixed clusters.

Regression is considered one of the most common Machine Learning (ML) tasks. There are various approaches, for example, using a standalone model of the Linear Regression or the Decision Tree. But in general, a single decision tree could not be enough, and results could not be very stable and reliable. The idea is to make use of ensemble learning: it is a training on multiple ML algorithms and a combination of their predictions in some way. Such an approach tends to make more accurate predictions than any individual model. An Ensemble model is a model that consists of many base models. As commonly said, ‘Forest is better than a single tree’: therefore, we choose to implement a Random Forest algorithm in our analysis. Random forest consists of many individual decision trees that operate as an ensemble. Each individual tree in the random forest spits out a class prediction and the class with the most votes becomes our model’s prediction. While some trees may be wrong, many other trees will be right, so as a group the trees are able to move in the correct direction. Random Forest limits the greatest disadvantage of Decision Trees. It almost does not overfit due to subset and feature randomization. Firstly, it uses a unique subset of the initial data for every base model which helps to make Decision Trees less correlated. Secondly, it splits each node in every Decision Tree using a random set of features. Such an approach means that no single tree sees all the data, which helps to focus on the general patterns within the training data and reduces sensitivity to noise. The choice of Random Forest was motivated not only by its performance but also by its robustness to noise, ability to handle non-linear relationships, and capability to assess feature importance. Moreover, it supports rapid training and inference even on moderately large datasets. To ensure transparency and reproducibility, we carefully selected the hyperparameters based on a grid search with cross-validation. The final model was configured with:

- Number of trees (n\_estimators): 100, to ensure stability without incurring excessive computational cost;

- Maximum depth (max\_depth): set to 10 to control overfitting and balance bias-variance;

- Minimum samples per split (min\_samples\_split): 2;

- Number of features considered at each split (max\_features): sqrt, the default setting for regression tasks, to maintain model diversity.


The model was trained on a labeled dataset using an 80/20 split between the training and testing sets. The results achieved are quite stable as can be seen from the following metrics assessed:

MAE (Mean Absolute Error): 0.02920878338135861 MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error): 0.050928344179091666 R2 (R-squared): 0.9740023405842765 RMSE(Root Mean Square Error): 0.11198848944765573.

R-squared, which is the proportion of variation in the outcome that is explained by the predictor variables, has a high value that shows great performances.

This result is particularly notable because these PM variables were not included in the initial clustering phase, which focused on other environmental parameters (e.g., temperature, humidity, CO2 levels). The fact that PM values emerged as dominant predictors in the regression phase, despite not being involved in clustering, highlights their strong relationship with the target variable in the inference process.

### Comparison with other algorithms

Before choosing the Random Forest as the algorithm for making inferences on the new data, we conducted a comprehensive comparison of multiple machine learning models to evaluate their performance on the available dataset. The selected models included widely adopted algorithms in the literature for regression tasks: Decision Tree Regressor, XGBoost Regressor (XGB), and CatBoost Regressor, in addition to Random Forest. Each model’s predictive performance was assessed based on key statistical indicators: median, mean, and standard deviation of RMSE (Root Mean Square Error). To better visualize error distributions and variability, we also employed box plots (see Fig. [8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-12626-y#Fig8)). Although all models demonstrated reasonably good average performance, Random Forest consistently yielded the most stable and accurate predictions, with lower error variability and fewer extreme values. The XGBoost Regressor, despite being widely recognized for its strong performance on structured/tabular data, underperformed in our context. This can be attributed to several factors. First, XGBoost is highly sensitive to hyperparameter tuning, and despite applying a basic optimization routine, it may not have fully converged to its optimal configuration due to the limited size and noise characteristics of our dataset. Second, the model may have struggled with overfitting, especially considering the relatively small number of samples (approx. 35,000) and potential collinearity between some features. Third, the underlying data distribution and noise levels may have favored models like Random Forest that leverage bagging and random feature selection, rather than boosting techniques which accumulate errors iteratively. As illustrated in Fig. [8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-12626-y#Fig8), the box plot shows that while most models produced fairly symmetrical error distributions without significant outliers, XGBoost displayed greater variability and a skewed error pattern, indicating less reliable generalization. For these reasons, Random Forest was ultimately selected as the most robust and interpretable model for this task, balancing accuracy, generalization, and practical applicability without requiring extensive tuning.

## Discussion and conclusions

This study investigated the potential of machine learning models to predict COVID-19 transmission risk based on indoor air quality parameters collected by Internet of Things (IoT) sensors. By comparing various algorithms, the study identified a Random Forest model that achieved the best performance in predicting risk based on temperature, humidity, CO2, and PM concentrations. This finding highlights the strong correlation between air quality and potential COVID-19 transmission, supporting previous research suggesting that specific pollutants and environmental factors can influence viral survival and spread[49](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-12626-y#ref-CR49 "Zhu, Y. et al. Associations between PM₂.₅ exposure and COVID-19 cases in 186 US counties. Sci. Total Environ. 790, 148105 (2021).                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.scitotenv.2021.148105                                    ").

It is important to clarify that while our results demonstrate a significant correlation between particulate matter (PM) concentrations and COVID-19 infection risk, they do not inherently confirm a direct causal relationship. Elevated PM levels may serve as proxies or indirect indicators of underlying environmental and behavioral conditions that facilitate viral transmission. For example, increased PM levels might reflect poor ventilation, higher occupancy densities, or specific indoor activities generating aerosols.

The ability to infer transmission risk based on readily available environmental sensor data offers valuable insights for preventive public health strategies, particularly in public spaces and high-risk settings like schools, offices and healthcare facilities. Several key strengths contribute to the study’s significance:

1. 1.
Real-world data: Utilizing data from actual sensor deployments ensures the model’s applicability to real-world scenarios.

2. 2.
Comprehensive parameter analysis: The model considers multiple air quality parameters, providing a more holistic understanding of the relationship with COVID-19 risk.

3. 3.
Robust model selection: Comparing different algorithms ensures the chosen model offers the best performance and generalizability.

4. 4.
Scalability and flexibility: The model’s rapid inference and compatibility with mobile devices facilitate real-time monitoring and intervention strategies.


However, some limitations deserve consideration:

- Model validation: While the chosen model performed well, further validation with expert input and comparison with established metrics could strengthen its reliability.

- Limited scope: The study focused on specific parameters and may not capture all potential risk factors. Investigation into additional environmental and behavioral factors could further enhance the model’s accuracy.


Our analysis primarily emphasizes environmental parameters; however, it is important to recognize potential confounding factors such as human behavior, occupancy density, and ventilation rates. Human activities, such as speaking loudly or coughing, occupancy patterns, and the effectiveness of ventilation systems significantly influence indoor air quality and consequently the risk of airborne infection transmission. Although our current model does not explicitly incorporate these behavioral and occupancy variables due to ethical and logistical constraints, their inclusion in future iterations could substantially refine predictions, helping to distinguish clearly between environmental conditions and behavioral determinants of infection risk.

## Future work

While the current model provides a robust framework for assessing COVID-19 infection risk based on indoor environmental parameters, several avenues for future development remain open. First, additional expert input from epidemiologists, building engineers, and public health officials will be sought to refine the environmental thresholds and validate model generalizability across different regions and seasons. Second, the model could be expanded to integrate behavioral data (e.g., room occupancy rates, activity levels) where ethically and legally feasible. Third, future versions will aim to include adaptive learning mechanisms, allowing the algorithm to recalibrate automatically as new data become available. Furthermore, broader deployment across varied indoor settings—such as hospitals, public transportation, and commercial venues—will help stress-test the model’s reliability and adaptability. Finally, integration with smart HVAC and ventilation systems may allow the algorithm to be used not only for monitoring but also for automated risk mitigation actions in real-time. Future research should include controlled studies designed explicitly to differentiate between direct effects of PM on virus viability and indirect associations resulting from other co-varying environmental or behavioral factors.

## Data privacy/ethics

Throughout the project, strict attention was paid to data privacy and ethical compliance, particularly given the involvement of educational institutions and the potential sensitivity of indoor monitoring data. All data were anonymized prior to analysis to ensure that no personally identifiable information could be linked to individuals, classrooms, or specific schools. The collection and processing of environmental data adhered to relevant GDPR guidelines and institutional ethical standards. Furthermore, sensor deployment was carried out with the full knowledge and consent of participating schools, and no behavioral or health data from students or staff were recorded. The research design prioritized transparency, minimal intrusion, and respect for the privacy of all participants, in line with the ethical imperatives of public health research in sensitive environments.

### Contributions

Antonella Longo and Prisco Piscitelli conceived the research. Antonella Longo designed and managed the research and provided supervision and guidance throughout the research project. Chiara Rucco was involved in all stages of the research process, from data collection and preparation to training datasets and developing the machine learning algorithm. Prisco Priscitelli and Alessandro Miani provided contributions from a medical and epidemiological standpoint, aiding in formulating thresholds for assessing air quality risks based on pertinent medical literature.
