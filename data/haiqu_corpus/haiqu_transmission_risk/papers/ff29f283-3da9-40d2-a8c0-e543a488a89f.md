## Abstract

The large-scale use of antivirals during influenza pandemics poses a significant selection pressure for drug-resistant pathogens to emerge and spread in a population. This requires treatment strategies to minimize total infections as well as the emergence of resistance. Here we propose a mathematical model in which individuals infected with wild-type influenza, if treated, can develop _de novo_ resistance and further spread the resistant pathogen. Our main purpose is to explore the impact of two important factors influencing treatment effectiveness: _i_) the relative transmissibility of the drug-resistant strain to wild-type, and _ii_) the frequency of _de novo_ resistance. For the endemic scenario, we find a condition between these two parameters that indicates whether treatment regimes will be most beneficial at intermediate or more extreme values ( _e.g._, the fraction of infected that are treated). Moreover, we present analytical expressions for effective treatment regimes and provide evidence of its applicability across a range of modeling scenarios: endemic behavior with deterministic homogeneous mixing, and single-epidemic behavior with deterministic homogeneous mixing and stochastic heterogeneous mixing. Therefore, our results provide insights for the control of drug-resistance in influenza across time scales.

## Introduction

Rapid antigenic evolution in the influenza virus increases the likelihood of emergence of novel strains, against which little to no immunity may exist in the host population [\[1\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Ferguson1)– [\[4\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Nelson1). In this scenario, if vaccines are not yet available or non-pharmaceutical interventions have limited impact on disease containment, antiviral treatment plays a crucial role in the control of the disease [\[3\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Ferguson2), [\[5\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-JaberiDouraki1)– [\[7\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Alexander1). A critical constraint in the deployment of antivirals agents ( _e.g._, M2 inhibitors and neuraminidase inhibitors [\[8\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Regoes1)) is the evolution of highly transmissible drug-resistant mutants [\[9\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Weinstock1). Resistance decreases the effectiveness of chemotherapy in infected patients, prolonging recovery or leading to outright treatment failure [\[10\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Levin1). Epidemics of untreatable strains have the potential to cause major morbidity and mortality [\[11\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Lipsitch1)– [\[14\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Imai1), with significant economic costs for both the individual and for society writ large [\[15\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Althouse1). Consequently, public health policy has a growing need to understand the key factors that lead to the rise and spread of resistance, and to devise strategies that amplify the effectiveness of existing drugs, while halting the spread of resistance [\[16\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Ferguson3)– [\[19\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-zurWiesch1).

In addition to important precautionary measures, such as improvement of hospital counter-infection methods and regulation of antiviral use, mathematical models can be used to explore plausible competition scenarios between sensitive and resistant strains and the impact of treatment strategies on these dynamics [\[17\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Lipsitch2), [\[19\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-zurWiesch1)– [\[22\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Althouse2). Previous models of the development of resistance of influenza to antiviral agents have focused on efforts to minimize the fraction of drug-resistant infections during an epidemic outbreak [\[5\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-JaberiDouraki1)– [\[7\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Alexander1), [\[17\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Lipsitch2) and to give recommendations that inform policy [\[8\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Regoes1), [\[15\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Althouse1), [\[18\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Wu1), [\[23\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Schwartz1)– [\[25\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Fiore1). However, the study of the long-term (endemic) dynamics of drug-resistance has received less attention [\[26\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Qiu1).

The present work assesses the effectiveness of treatment at minimizing the total number of infections while halting the spread of drug-resistance, both from an endemic and a single-epidemic perspective. We focus our attention on two points: _i_) the relative transmissibility of the drug-resistant strain with respect to the wild-type (drug-sensitive) strain, and _ii_) the frequency of _de novo_ resistance. Point _i_) is related to the fitness cost associated with the evolution of drug resistance, reflected in a reduced transmissibility of the drug-resistant pathogen relative to its wild-type counterpart [\[8\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Regoes1), [\[27\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Lipsitch4). Recent evidence has demonstrated, however, that this reduction in fitness may be limited due to compensatory mutations which can restore fitness without loss of resistance-conferring genes [\[9\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Weinstock1), [\[28\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Handel1). Point _ii_) represents the probability that treatment leads to resistance within the treated host ( _de novo_). Both quantities are crucial in the population dynamics of drug-resistance, specially due to their variability within different epidemiological settings [\[8\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Regoes1). Nonetheless, their combined effect on the effectiveness of treatment regimes during influenza pandemics is not fully understood [\[5\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-JaberiDouraki1).

We build on a previous model [\[17\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Lipsitch2) to examine these issues in the long-term (endemic disease prevalence) as well as in the short-term (single-epidemic). Lipsitch et al. [\[17\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Lipsitch2) observed that intermediate levels of antiviral use are indicated to reduce the attack rate during an influenza pandemic. Complementing these results, we find that to effectively reduce the endemic levels of the wild-type and resistant strains, treatment regimes ( _i.e._, treated fraction) should be at intermediate levels if the resistant strain is highly transmissible and _de novo_ resistance is rare. However, if resistance comes with a high fitness cost and _de novo_ resistance is frequent, then higher levels of antiviral use may be preferable. In the single epidemic case we compare our optimal treatment regime with that of [\[17\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Lipsitch2), showing that their relative effectiveness also depends on the strains’ relative transmissibility and the frequency of _de novo_ resistance. Moreover, we demonstrate the applicability of our optimal treatment regimes by evidencing its effectiveness at quelling the spread of resistance when considering the effects of the stochasticity inherent to the transmission dynamics and the complex contact structure in the population.

## Methods

### Model Formulation

We extend a version of the model in [\[17\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Lipsitch2) to include demography (see [Figure 1](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone-0059529-g001)). Susceptible hosts, ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e001), enter the population at a per-capita rate ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e002) and die at rate equal to ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e003), keeping the total population size, ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e004), constant. Susceptible individuals can be infected by pathogens either sensitive or resistant to the available antiviral (this model does not include superinfection with both strains). A fraction ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e005) of patients infected with the wild-type strain are treated, and a fraction ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e006) of those treated develop resistance _de novo_. Therefore, individuals infected with the wild-type strain are either untreated (![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e007)), effectively treated (![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e008)), or resistant to treatment (![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e009)). Infection with a resistant strain is either developed _de novo_ or acquired from another resistant-infected individual. Susceptible individuals become infected at a rate proportional to the densities of susceptible and infected individuals, and to the transmission rates of each class, ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e010), and ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e011), respectively. Untreated, treated, and resistant infected individuals recover at per-capita rates ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e012), and ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e013), respectively. We assume no disease-induced mortality, and that the pathogen induces sterilizing immunity [\[21\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Keeling1), [\[29\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Anderson1).

#### Stability of fixed points.

For the stability analysis of the FPs we study the eigenvalues of the matrix in the linearized system around the FPs: equilibria that have eigenvalues with negative real part are stable, whereas equilibria that have eigenvalues with positive real part are unstable [\[34\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Strogatz1). We present here the results of the analysis; detailed analytic derivations can be found in the [Text S1](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529.s011).

The main goal of this work is to derive treatment regimes ( _i.e._, treated fractions) that minimize the wild-type infections while restraining the spread of resistance. From the CFP in (10), it is clear that for very low treatment levels, the wild-type strain is prevalent in the population, and the resistant strain prevalence stays at minimal levels [\[10\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Levin1), _i.e._, ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e078). #### Frequency of _de novo_ resistance and endemic levels of resistance.

We have shown how the frequency of _de novo_ resistance, ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e233), plays a crucial role in devising effective treatment strategies. In addition, we find that smaller values of ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e234) lead to more abrupt transitions from wild-type to resistant strains. In other words, the smaller the probability of developing _de novo_ resistance, the faster the RFP gains stability when the system is close to the threshold ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e235) ( [Figure 5](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone-0059529-g005)). Thus, for small ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e236), the system becomes more sensitive to variations in ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e237), ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e238), ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e239), and ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e240) near this threshold. This represents a potentially dangerous scenario: if the likelihood of _de novo_ resistance is small, a policy-maker might underestimate the prospects of resistance emergence and, consequently, increase treatment levels to eradicate the wild-type strain. However, if treatment is increased above ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e241), an abrupt transition may occur to a state where only resistant strains persist.

### The Single Epidemic Case

Frequently, public health programs and interventions are designed to prevent the emergence of drug resistance within a single epidemic. To address this issue, we model a closed population ( _i.e._, ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e256) in model (1)–(5)), and examine again the role of _i_) the relative transmissibility (![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e257)) and _ii_) the frequency of _de novo_ resistance (![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e258)) on the effectiveness of treatment regimes. In this assessment we focus on the final epidemic size (FS), defined as the proportion of the population infected during the epidemic. As in [\[17\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Lipsitch2), we introduce the following correction to our numerical integrations: if ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e259) then ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e260). This prevents spurious results induced by the transmission of “non-cases” (since ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e261), initially ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e262) can only increase due to _de novo_ resistant cases; given that ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e263) is continuous in the ODE framework, the condition above avoids that a fraction of a _de novo_ resistant case can cause a direct resistant infection). We now wish to contrast the effectiveness of ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e297) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e298) as a function of the relative transmissibility ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e299), assuming relatively low frequency of _de novo_ resistance ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e300). [Figure 7](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone-0059529-g007) shows the FSs (due to resistant strain (black) and total (blue)) vs. ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e301) for ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e302) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e303). A treatment regime ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e304) would “prioritize” the avoidance of resistance, while compromising the reduction of the overall epidemic; conversely, ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e305) will, by definition, “prioritize” the minimization of the total epidemic size, while disregarding the spread of resistance. As a result, ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e306) is more effective than ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e307) at halting the spread of resistance in the population, whereas ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e308) is a better option to reduce the overall epidemic. Moreover, since ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e309) (see (14)), as ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e310) increases, a treatment regime ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e311) will systematically diminish the spread of resistance by reducing the treated fraction. Consequently, for higher ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e312), ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e313) will have minimal effects on reducing the total epidemic size (compare the diamond with the horizontal blue line, where no treatment is applied). Therefore, the decision to use ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e314) or ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e315) as a treatment regime will mainly depend on how policy makers balance a larger epidemic produced largely by the wild-type strain, with minimal resistant cases (using ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e316), for which we have a better biological and mathematical understanding), versus a smaller overall epidemic with higher resistance incidence (using ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e317)).

#### Relative transmissibility and non-pharmaceutical interventions.

It is likely that treatment alone cannot completely quell an emerging epidemic [\[35\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Longini1). In such cases, non-pharmaceutical interventions ( _e.g._, social distancing, case isolation, travel restrictions) could help to significantly mitigate the extent of the epidemic [\[3\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Ferguson2), [\[8\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Regoes1), [\[16\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Ferguson3). These can affect the transmissibility of the wild-type and the resistant strain while maintaining the relative transmissibility of the latter (![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e329)). Here we investigate the competition dynamics between the wild-type and the resistant strain as a function of ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e330), and the transmissibility of the wild-type strain ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e331) (which varies due to non-pharmaceutical interventions) under different treatment regimes.

These observations suggest that if the wild-type strain features relatively low transmission, the best strategy to contain both strains is to treat “hard and early”. However, if the transmissibility is higher and the fitness cost of resistance is low, then this strategy can have devastating consequences as the resistant strain can infect a large fraction of the population. This demonstrates the importance of effective non-pharmaceutical interventions that could reduce ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e357).

For larger values of ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e358) an interesting process occurs. Starting from low ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e359) the wild-type dominates. As ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e360) increases – crossing the “vertical” null isocline where both FSs are equal – the resistant strain begins to prevail, until crossing the “slant” null isocline where the wild-type strain starts to regain its dominance. A possible explanation for this dominance shift is that as the wild-type strain becomes more transmissible, it depletes the pool of susceptibles too quickly, leaving the resistant strain with few individuals to infect once it emerges. However, for even larger ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e361) and high ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e362), increasing ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e363) also increases the FS of the resistant strain. In this scenario, the relative transmissibility is so high that even if the wild-type strain can spread rapidly, the resistant strain will eventually “catch up” and outcompete it.

Thus, when trying to predict the outcome of the competition dynamics between wild-type and resistant strains, knowing the relative transmissibility of the latter is not sufficient. One must also know the actual value of its transmissibility. In the endemic case, however, when treatment is fixed, the relative transmissibility completely determined which strain ultimately dominated ( [Figure 2](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone-0059529-g002)). These considerations complement observations made in [\[17\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Lipsitch2).

### The Impact of Contact Structure

Two strong simplifications made in our model were to ignore the complex contact structure of human populations and the stochastic nature of the transmission and _de novo_ resistance dynamics. While these assumptions allowed us to obtain closed-form solutions for effective treatment regimes, the social network underlying the epidemic process is known to have non-trivial effects on transmission dynamics [\[21\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Keeling1), [\[22\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Althouse2), [\[36\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-PastorSatorras1), [\[37\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Read1). In this section we use a model equivalent to (1)–(5) that features contact structure [\[38\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Barabasi1) and stochasticity [\[39\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Newman1). We again assume ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e364), and utilize Monte-Carlo (MC) simulations to assess the effectiveness of ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e365) (Eq. (14)) in single epidemic situations.

## Discussion

The rapid development of an effective vaccine against an emerging novel influenza virus presents considerable challenges. Thus, antiviral agents could play a central role as a first-line defense against emerging epidemics of influenza. The large-scale use of these drugs could, in turn, select for the evolution of drug-resistant strains [\[8\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Regoes1), making the strategic distribution of antivirals essential in quelling the spread of drug-resistance while limiting the overall epidemic size. In this work we have discussed the influence of two key parameters on the effectiveness of treatment: the relative transmissibility of the drug-resistant strain (![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e388)), and the frequency of _de novo_ resistance (![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e389)). We extended a previous model [\[17\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Lipsitch2) to include demography and performed analytical calculations of the reproductive numbers, stability of the fixed points, and conditions for the exclusion or coexistence of resistant and wild-type pathogen strains.

In the endemic case we found that, depending on the values of ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e390) (or equivalently ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e391)) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e392), the optimal treatment regime will be at intermediate (case **(A)** for high ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e393) and low ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e394) ) or more extreme values (case **(B)** for low ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e395) and high ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e396)). Intuitively it is clear that if the resistant strain is highly transmissible (high ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e397)), then treatment should be moderate in order to limit the selective advantage of drug-resistant phenotypes. Conversely, if the resistant strain is weakly transmitted (low ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e398)), then more intense treatment regimes are preferred since resistance-only endemic levels will be relatively low. These recommendations are valid as long as infections with a wild-type or a resistant strain represent the same harm to the host ( _e.g._, strains with similar infectious periods and virulence). In addition, we also remarked that when ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e399) is low, the optimal treatment regime can be approximated by ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e400). In the single epidemic case, numerical simulations also suggest that if ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e401) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e402) are low, ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e403) is still a useful quantity when designing treatment strategies. However, in contrast to the endemic case, knowing the relative transmissibility of the resistant strain is not enough to predict the final outcome of the competition between the two strains. In this case, the strain that successfully spreads first has a significant impact on which strain infects more individuals during the epidemic. Our results also indicate that early and high treatment regimes are most effective at reducing the number of infections while hindering the rise of resistance, when the transmissibility of the wild-type strain is relatively low. This emphasizes the importance of non-pharmaceutical interventions aimed at reducing the transmission rate of the disease.

Further, we showed that for small ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e404), the parameter ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e405) is robust to the presence of contact heterogeneity and stochasticity, as it still minimizes both the epidemic size and the risks of resistance emergence. This reinforces the public health implications of the effective treatment expressions derived herein.

An interesting similarity across time scales is the impact of the frequency of _de novo_ resistance on ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e406): as ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e407) increases, the effectiveness of ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e408) becomes compromised. While we give mathematical and biological arguments for this property, the inherent uncertainty in the empirical values of ![](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529.e409) make this observation potentially relevant to the designing of treatment strategies [\[8\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Regoes1).

Our model, like any other, is not exempt of simplifying assumptions, or uncertainties about the model parameter values and transmission dynamics of wild-type and resistant strains. Thus, rather than providing specific quantitative recommendations for treatment policies, we emphasize the qualitative character of our observations. Moreover, we recognize that even if these uncertainties were resolved, we still face ethical issues when deciding to implement treatment policies based on our recommendations; _e.g._, treat _only_ a certain fraction of those infected if relative transmissibility is high and _de novo_ resistance is unlikely. This is a difficult case for the public health planner, and the choice is left to them. If relative transmissibility is low and _de novo_ resistance is more likely, then our recommendations are less controversial: treat people as they come in based on their clinical profile. In terms of the assumptions made in our analysis, we considered that treatment and _de novo_ resistance happen immediately after infection. In the [Text S1](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529.s011) we present a model that features stage progressions (treatment and _de novo_ resistance occur at certain rates rather than instantaneously) and show that its dynamics are analogous to those presented here (see [Figure S1](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529.s001), [S2](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529.s002), [S3](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529.s003)). We also assumed that the fraction of treated individuals can, with no regard to economic and social costs, attain any value between 0 and 1, and remain constant throughout time. This is generally not true as treatment availability and costs vary with time and socioeconomic context (models in [\[5\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-JaberiDouraki1), [\[6\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Moghadas1), [\[41\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Hansen1), [\[42\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Moghadas2) explore different time-dependent treatment regimes). We have considered a model with equal birth and death rates, thus, it may also be important to study the impact of demographics on the effectiveness of treatment regimes, though less so in the single epidemic case. We have also excluded coinfection with both strains, which is known to affect the evolution of the influenza virus ( _e.g._, viral reassortment [\[4\]](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0059529#pone.0059529-Nelson1)), and could in turn influence the development of drug-resistant phenotypes. We suspect that accounting for coinfection might lead to new and interesting dynamics.

Our results shed light on the epidemiological impact of the interplay between treatment regimes and relative transmissibility of a strain of influenza resistant to antiviral treatment and the frequency of _de novo_ resistance, both aspects which are difficult to assess empirically. These findings could have important implications for the strategic distribution of antivirals in a population in response to the emergence of a novel influenza strain.
