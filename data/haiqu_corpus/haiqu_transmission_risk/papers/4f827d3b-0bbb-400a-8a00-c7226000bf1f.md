Transmission potential of smallpox in contemporary populations

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a.pdf)

## Abstract

Despite eradication[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988)."), smallpox still presents a risk to public health whilst laboratory stocks of virus remain[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR2 "World Health Organization. Future Research on Smallpox Virus Recommended Press Release WHO/77 (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1999)."), [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR3 "US sounds alarm over smallpox weapon threat. Nature 399, 628 (1999)."). One factor crucial to any assessment of this risk is _R_ 0, the average number of secondary cases infected by each primary case. However, recently applied estimates have varied too widely ( _R_ 0 from 1.5 to >20) to be of practical use, and often appear to disregard contingent factors such as socio-economic conditions and herd immunity[4](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR4 "Henderson, D. A. et al. Smallpox as a biological weapon: medical and public health management. J. Am. Med. Assoc. 281, 2127–2137 (1999)."), [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR5 "Henderson, D. A. Bioterrorism as a public health threat. Emerg. Infect. Dis. 4, 488–492 (1999)."), [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR6 "Henderson, D. A. The looming threat of bioterrorism. Science 283, 1279–1282 (1999)."), [7](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR7 "O'Toole, T. Smallpox: an attack scenario. Emerg. Infect. Dis. 4, 488–492 (1999)."), [8](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR8 "Meltzer, M. I., Damon, I., LeDuc, J. W. & Millar, J. D. Modeling the potential responses to smallpox as a bioterrorist weapon. Emerg. Infect. Dis. 7 (in the press; also available on                    http://www.cdc.gov/ncidod/eid/vol7no6/meltzer.htm                                    )."). Here we use epidemic modelling[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992).") to show a more consistent derivation of _R_ 0. In isolated pre-twentieth century populations[10](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR10 "Creighton, C. A History of Epidemics in Britain Vol. 2 (Cambridge Univ. Press, Cambridge, 1891)."), [11](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR11 "Moody, J. The Burford small-pox outbreak of 1758. Tolsey Pap. No. 1 (The Tolsey Museum, Burford and Burford School, 1980)."), [12](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR12 "Registrar General. Annual Report of the Registrar General 1869–74 (Her Majesty's Stationery Office (HMSO), London, 1870–75).") with negligible herd immunity, the numbers of cases initially rose exponentially, with an _R_ 0 between 3.5 and 6. Before outbreak controls were applied, smallpox also demonstrated similar levels of transmission in 30 sporadic outbreaks in twentieth century Europe[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988)."), taking into account pre-existing vaccination levels[13](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR13 "Mack, T. M. Smallpox in Europe, 1950–1971. J. Infect. Dis. 125, 161–169 (1972)."), [14](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR14 "World Health Organization. Health Aspects of Chemical and Biological Weapons (WHO, Geneva, 1970).") (about 50%) and the role of hospitals in doubling early transmission. Should smallpox recur, such estimates of transmission potential ( _R_ 0 from 3.5 to 6) predict a reasonably rapid epidemic rise before the implementation of public health interventions, because little residual herd immunity exists now that vaccination has ceased.

## Main

Before eradication, smallpox caused significant epidemics and mortality[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988)."). Following the WHO (World Health Organization) campaign, smallpox was eradicated by 1979 and routine vaccination gradually cased worldwide[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988)."). Official stocks of virus now remain in only two laboratories, one at CDC in Atlanta, Georgia, and the other at VECTOR, Novosibirsk, Russia[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988)."). There is concern, however, that the virus might be held clandestinely and less securely elsewhere[3](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR3 "US sounds alarm over smallpox weapon threat. Nature 399, 628 (1999)."). Release of smallpox back into the population could constitute a significant public health problem on account of the transmissibility of the virus and waning immunity. The greater the transmissibility of the virus the greater its potential to spread, and the more intense are the intervention strategies needed to bring it under control. Significant discrepancies exist in the recent literature[4](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR4 "Henderson, D. A. et al. Smallpox as a biological weapon: medical and public health management. J. Am. Med. Assoc. 281, 2127–2137 (1999)."), [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR5 "Henderson, D. A. Bioterrorism as a public health threat. Emerg. Infect. Dis. 4, 488–492 (1999)."), [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR6 "Henderson, D. A. The looming threat of bioterrorism. Science 283, 1279–1282 (1999)."), [7](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR7 "O'Toole, T. Smallpox: an attack scenario. Emerg. Infect. Dis. 4, 488–492 (1999)."), [8](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR8 "Meltzer, M. I., Damon, I., LeDuc, J. W. & Millar, J. D. Modeling the potential responses to smallpox as a bioterrorist weapon. Emerg. Infect. Dis. 7 (in the press; also available on                    http://www.cdc.gov/ncidod/eid/vol7no6/meltzer.htm                                    )."), so it is essential to provide a rational assessment of the transmissibility of smallpox by reference to adequate and relevant data.

Reports of outbreaks of smallpox caused by variola major were sought that contained sufficient numerical detail to derive estimates of transmissibility, _R_ 0 (see Methods). Such reports are scarce, and those in [Table 1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Tab1) represent all that were found from the extensive published data. Data for four of the outbreaks were from the pre-vaccination era. Analysis of the cumulative smallpox deaths for Boston, USA[10](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR10 "Creighton, C. A History of Epidemics in Britain Vol. 2 (Cambridge Univ. Press, Cambridge, 1891).") in 1721 and Burford, England[11](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR11 "Moody, J. The Burford small-pox outbreak of 1758. Tolsey Pap. No. 1 (The Tolsey Museum, Burford and Burford School, 1980).") in 1758 ( [Fig. 1a](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Fig1), [b](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Fig1) and [Table 1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Tab1)) gave _R_ 0 values of 4.3 and 3.4, respectively, which produced extremely good fits to the data. As was often the case for small isolated communities, nearly the whole population of Burford was susceptible. Similarly, outbreaks in Chester[10](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR10 "Creighton, C. A History of Epidemics in Britain Vol. 2 (Cambridge Univ. Press, Cambridge, 1891).") (1774) and Warrington[10](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR10 "Creighton, C. A History of Epidemics in Britain Vol. 2 (Cambridge Univ. Press, Cambridge, 1891).") (1773) ( [Fig. 1c](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Fig1), [d](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Fig1), and [Table 1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Tab1)) gave estimates for _R_ 0 of 5.8 and 4.7 (range for Warrington 4–5.3), respectively. In both of these outbreaks, only children less than 10 years old were afflicted, so the numbers of susceptible individuals were estimated from the relevant birth and age-dependent mortality rates ( [Table 1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Tab1)). The predicted epidemic curve fitted the data for Chester very well. For Warrington, however, a range of _R_ 0 values was derived that depended on the estimated population size, which was calculated to be between 6,000 and 8,000, respectively. The fitted epidemic curve that is shown for Warrington, which is based on an _R_ 0 of 4.7 and a population estimate of 7,000, followed the initial course of the observed epidemic well but over-predicted the cumulative number of deaths by the end of the epidemic. It is possible that the case fatality rate of 25% given by Creighton[10](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR10 "Creighton, C. A History of Epidemics in Britain Vol. 2 (Cambridge Univ. Press, Cambridge, 1891).") was an overestimate as the other contemporaneous epidemics modelled here had case fatality rates of about 15%. Adjusting the case fatality rate to 15% greatly improved the fit to the data, closely fitting all 12 data points and producing an estimated _R_ 0 value of 4.8 (see [Fig. 1d](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Fig1) and [Table 1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Tab1)).

Smallpox was endemic in London from the seventeenth to the twentieth century. Weekly mortality statistics were recorded, first in the London Bills of Mortality and later in the Registrar General's reports[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR12 "Registrar General. Annual Report of the Registrar General 1869–74 (Her Majesty's Stationery Office (HMSO), London, 1870–75)."). Superimposed on an endemic level of disease, epidemics generated a cyclical pattern of smallpox deaths[15](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR15 "Duncan, C J., Duncan, S. R. & Scott, S. Oscillatory dynamics of smallpox and the impact of vaccination. J. Theor. Biol. 183, 447–454 (1996).") ( [Fig. 1f](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Fig1)). Such patterns are characteristic of the dynamics of endemic diseases and the models that are often used to describe them[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992)."). Analysis of these individual epidemics using the methods described above was not possible because independent estimates of the numbers susceptible in the population could not be made. The poorly documented levels of variolation[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988).") and vaccination that were being practised compounded the problem. If the problems due to vaccination are neglected, the inter-epidemic interval[15](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR15 "Duncan, C J., Duncan, S. R. & Scott, S. Oscillatory dynamics of smallpox and the impact of vaccination. J. Theor. Biol. 183, 447–454 (1996).") between 1836 and 1890 suggests that the transmission rate, _R_ (see Methods) was about 5. However, vaccination was being practised, so this provides a lower bound for the estimate of _R_ 0. Interepidemic intervals as low as 2–3 years[15](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR15 "Duncan, C J., Duncan, S. R. & Scott, S. Oscillatory dynamics of smallpox and the impact of vaccination. J. Theor. Biol. 183, 447–454 (1996).") and presumably higher values of _R_ 0 had also applied previously in London, and these have been variously correlated with greater susceptibility to disease, greater density of population or lack of vaccination. Dietz and Heesterbeek[16](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR16 "Dietz, K. & Heesterbeek, J. A. Bernoulli was ahead of modern epidemiology. Nature 408, 513–514 (2000).") also report on an analysis of smallpox in Paris by Bernoulli in 1766. His estimated force of infection, _λ_, of 0.125, and life expectancy, _L_, of 32 years, give an estimated _R_ 0 of between 4 and 5 for smallpox in this other major European city (given that[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992).") _R_ 0 = _λ_ _L_ or _R_ 0 = 1 + _λ_ _L_).

Although no longer endemic in Europe by the early 1950s, smallpox still caused isolated epidemics after the importation of the disease from endemic areas despite the considerable level of herd immunity in European populations[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988)."), [13](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR13 "Mack, T. M. Smallpox in Europe, 1950–1971. J. Infect. Dis. 125, 161–169 (1972)."), [17](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR17 "Dixon, C. W. Smallpox (Churchill, London, 1962)."). One of the best documented epidemics was in Yugoslavia in 1972 (ref. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988).")). Starting in Kosovo, the outbreak went unrecognized until the second generation of infection, when vaccination and quarantine of contacts was initiated, followed by vaccination of about 95% of the population. We fitted equation (1) to the data, parameterized to take into account these interventions ( [Table 2](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Tab2)), and hence estimated _R_ (see Methods) to be about 5.4; this value fell rapidly to below 1 with the introduction of quarantine and vaccination ( [Fig. 1e](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Fig1), [Table 1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Tab1)). However, about 50% of the population were probably protected by vaccinations that pre-dated the outbreak[13](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR13 "Mack, T. M. Smallpox in Europe, 1950–1971. J. Infect. Dis. 125, 161–169 (1972)."), [14](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR14 "World Health Organization. Health Aspects of Chemical and Biological Weapons (WHO, Geneva, 1970)."), such that the initial _R_ 0 would have been nearer 10.8. The epidemic curve from equation (1) provided a reasonable overall fit to the data from Kosovo but underestimated the number of cases in the earlier part of the outbreak, probably on account of the small size of the outbreak and the fact that there were only two generations of infection before interventions were applied.

Thirty European smallpox importations between 1958 and 1973 were also briefly summarized in ref. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988)."). Stratified by the time delay between importation and recognition of the outbreak, and its notification to the WHO (0–7, 8–14, 15–21 and 22–42 days), the outbreaks that took longer to be notified resulted in a greater average number of cases before the epidemics were halted (5.4, 8.8, 15.8, 67.8 cases, respectively). With the median delay adjusted by the addition of a latent period for the index case and expressed in units of intergeneration time (19 days), a clear relationship was derived between the delay and the total number of cases ( [Fig. 2a](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Fig1)). Each generation's delay increased the average number of cases by _e_ 1.71, giving a mean estimate for _R_ of 5.5 ( _μ_ ± s.e.m. = 4.9 to 6.3). As before, these estimates are affected by previous vaccination levels; 50% vaccination, as has been suggested[13](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR13 "Mack, T. M. Smallpox in Europe, 1950–1971. J. Infect. Dis. 125, 161–169 (1972)."), [14](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR14 "World Health Organization. Health Aspects of Chemical and Biological Weapons (WHO, Geneva, 1970)."), would give an estimate for _R_ 0 of about 11. Interestingly, about 50% of cases in most of these European outbreaks, including in Kosovo, were acquired in hospital by staff and patients[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988).") before the outbreak was recognized and hospital infection-control measures had been put in place ( [Fig. 2b](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Fig1)). Without hospital-associated cases, the _R_ 0 for smallpox would have been about 5.5 for community-acquired disease.

As expected from data on other infectious agents[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992)."), the estimates of _R_ 0 derived for smallpox varied with time and place. Over the 1700s to 1900s, they tended to be in the range 3.5–6, but under particular circumstances rose to nearer 10–12 (that is, with crowding and poorer socio-economic conditions in eighteenth century London[15](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR15 "Duncan, C J., Duncan, S. R. & Scott, S. Oscillatory dynamics of smallpox and the impact of vaccination. J. Theor. Biol. 183, 447–454 (1996).") or when exacerbated by hospital-associated infections in more recent European outbreaks[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988).")). This analysis is in accordance with inferences from the vaccination level required for smallpox eradication[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988)."), [9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992).") in industrialized countries. The value of 70–80% that was found to be effective would suggest an _R_ 0 between 3.3 and 5 (ref. [9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992).")). However, vaccination levels of 80% were not effective in eradicating smallpox in India because of much higher population densities and poorer socio-economic conditions[18](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR18 "Arita, I., Wickett, J. & Fenner, F. Impact of population density on immunization programmes. J. Hyg. Cambridge 96, 459–466 (1986)."), which would imply _R_ 0 values higher than 5. For contemporary outbreaks in industrialized countries, which now have low levels of herd immunity (18%, see Methods), an _R_ 0 value of 4–6 would probably apply for community-acquired infections, but might transiently be higher (for example, 10–12) before the disease was correctly recognized and appropriate hospital infection controls implemented. These values differ from recently applied values. The higher values[4](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR4 "Henderson, D. A. et al. Smallpox as a biological weapon: medical and public health management. J. Am. Med. Assoc. 281, 2127–2137 (1999)."), [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR5 "Henderson, D. A. Bioterrorism as a public health threat. Emerg. Infect. Dis. 4, 488–492 (1999)."), [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR6 "Henderson, D. A. The looming threat of bioterrorism. Science 283, 1279–1282 (1999).") from 10 to 20 have been estimated from atypical outbreaks with unusually high transmission rates[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988)."), [19](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR19 "Wehrle, P. F., Posch, J., Richter, K. H. & Henderson, D. A. An airborne outbreak of smallpox in a German hospital and its significance with respect to other outbreaks in Europe. Bull. World Health Organ. 4, 669–679 (1970)."), whereas the lower estimates of around 2 appear to have neglected concurrent herd immunity[8](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR8 "Meltzer, M. I., Damon, I., LeDuc, J. W. & Millar, J. D. Modeling the potential responses to smallpox as a bioterrorist weapon. Emerg. Infect. Dis. 7 (in the press; also available on                    http://www.cdc.gov/ncidod/eid/vol7no6/meltzer.htm                                    )."). Although our estimate for smallpox represents a relatively modest transmission rate by comparison with some other infectious diseases, such as measles or chickenpox[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992)."), significant epidemics could result, particularly if there were delays in detecting the first cases or in setting up effective public health interventions.

## Methods

### Epidemic modelling

For epidemic modelling, transmissibility is often expressed as the basic reproductive number, _R_ 0, the mean number of secondary cases caused by each primary case in a population composed entirely of susceptible individuals[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992)."). The reproductive number, _R_, is an equivalent measure, but without reference to the proportion of susceptible hosts[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992)."). Smallpox infection results first in a non-infectious incubation period followed by a short prodrome with non-specific symptoms (assumed to be non-infectious[20](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR20 "Henderson, D. A. & Fenner, F. in Vaccines 2nd edn (eds Plotkin, S. A. & Mortimer, E. A.) 13–40 (Saunders, Philadelphia, 1994).")), which together constitute the latency period. Hosts then become overtly infectious and symptomatic, with a pustular rash and, thereafter, slowly recover and are immune, or die[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988)."), [17](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR17 "Dixon, C. W. Smallpox (Churchill, London, 1962)."). Whilst infectious, hosts transmit disease to susceptible individuals at a rate dependent on _R_ 0. Public health interventions interrupt transmission by removing infectious individuals into quarantine, or by reducing the susceptibility of contacts by vaccination[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992)."). These processes were modelled using an appropriately parameterized set of differential equations (1), which were solved using Euler's method with a time-step of 0.1 days.

![](https://media.springernature.com/lw600/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1038%2F414748a/MediaObjects/41586_2001_Article_BF414748a_Equ1_HTML.gif)

_S_ and _I_ represent the proportion of the population susceptible and infectious, respectively. _U_ represents the proportion dead and recovered, with the number dead dependent on the specific case fatality rates given in the data sets. The contacts of cases are divided into the following classes: _E_ _n_ the number of untraced latent individuals in the population, _E_ _i_ the number of traced latent contacts, and _C_ _i_ the number of traced uninfected contacts. The final class of contacts are those untraced and uninfected and so effectively remaining in _S_. _Q_ represents the proportion in quarantine and _V_ the proportion protected by vaccination. The average rate at which latent individuals become infectious[13](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR13 "Mack, T. M. Smallpox in Europe, 1950–1971. J. Infect. Dis. 125, 161–169 (1972)."), [21](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR21 "Gelfand, H. M. & Posch, J. The recent outbreak of smallpox in Meschede, West Germany. Am. J. Epidemiol. 94, 234–237 (1971).") is _α_ = (latency period)-1 = 0.0685 days-1 and the rate at which infectious individuals in the community recover or die[22](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR22 "Koplan, J. P., Azizullah, M. & Foster, S. O. Urban hospitals and rural village smallpox in Bangladesh. Trop. Geogr. Med. 30, 355–358 (1978).") is _γ_ = (infectious period)-1 = 0.116 days-1. Two states of quarantine are defined: the first for the traced contacts successfully vaccinated and released into the community at a rate _χ_ 1, and the second for the infectious cases, which enter _U_ at a rate _χ_ 2. Different vaccine efficacies are assumed for those uninfected, _ε_ 1, and infected, _ε_ 2. The proportion of contacts found through contact tracing is _ρ_ and the daily rate at which infectious individuals enter quarantine from the community is _θ_. The proportion of contacts infected is defined as _ϕ_. The rate at which potentially infected contacts occur is defined as _β_, as in equation (2), and _N_ is the size of the population in which the epidemic occurs.

![](https://media.springernature.com/lw418/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1038%2F414748a/MediaObjects/41586_2001_Article_BF414748a_Equ2_HTML.gif)

Additional assumptions are that no transmission occurs from those quarantined, dead or recovered and the background mortality rate was assumed to be negligible over the time periods examined.

For the Boston, Burford, Warrington and Chester data sets, _ρ_ = _θ_ = 0, which effectively reduces equations (1) above to a simple SEIR model[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992)."). Intervention parameters were only required when equations (1) was fitted to the data from Kosovo. Here, interventions were implemented 31 days after the onset of symptoms in the index case[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988).") with the associated parameters shown in [Table 2](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Tab2). The number of potentially infected contacts per case was determined as 50 (ref. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR1 "Fenner, F., Henderson, D. A., Arita, I., Jezek, Z. & Ladnyi, I. D. Smallpox and its Eradication (World Health Organization, Geneva, 1988).")). Values of _R_ 0 were derived for each outbreak by minimizing the mean square error between the mortality data and the predictions of mortality from the model, while applying the outbreak-specific case fatality rates to _U_ and adjusting _R_ 0 and time of onset of symptoms in the index case. In the case of Kosovo, equations (1) were fitted more simply to the reported number of cases rather than deaths. All the other parameters required for equations (1) were obtained independently from the published source(s) given in [Table 1](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#Tab1). For epidemics in London, _R_ 0 was roughly calculated from the interepidemic interval, _T_ = 2π\[ _L_( _D_ \+ _D_′)/( _R_ 0 \- 1)\]1/2, where _L_ is life expectancy between 1840 and 1870 adjusted for excess births over deaths, equal to 25 years, and _D_ \+ _D_′ is latent + infectious period, equal to 0.063 years[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR9 "Anderson, R. M. & May, R. M. Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control (Oxford Univ. Press, Oxford, 1992).").

### Estimation of current vaccination coverage

Given that smallpox vaccination ceased in industrialized countries in the mid to late 1970s (ref. [13](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR13 "Mack, T. M. Smallpox in Europe, 1950–1971. J. Infect. Dis. 125, 161–169 (1972).")), a crude estimate of the immunity of the contemporary UK population was calculated, on the basis of 50% having been vaccinated as infants up to 1972, and estimating that about 60% of these would be alive today from current population statistics. Of these only about 60% would still be protected by the vaccinations done on average 50 years previously, calculated by extrapolating from data on secondary attack rates, which increased from 4 to 12% over 10 years following vaccination[23](https://www.nature.com/articles/414748a#ref-CR23 "Mack, T. M., Thomas, D. B., Ali, A. & Khan, M. M. Epidemiology of smallpox in West Pakistan: I. Acquired immunity and distribution of disease. Am. J. Epidemiol. 95, 157–168 (1972)."). This suggests that the level of herd immunity may be about 18%, which will continue to decrease with time.
