_Acinetobacter baumannii_’s lifestyle includes soil-dwelling colonization of decaying plant material and airborne spread


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4.pdf)

## Abstract

_Acinetobacter baumannii_ is a Gram-negative nosocomial pathogen that is notorious for its rapid development of antibiotic resistance. However, its ecology and evolution outside hospital settings remain poorly defined. Here, we demonstrate that the natural lifestyle of _A. baumannii_ includes soil-dwelling and airborne dissemination, which helps explaining its adaptability and tolerance to desiccation, radiation and antibiotics, and thus its predisposition to establish within hospitals. Starting from white stork nestlings previously discovered as a reservoir, we studied food chains and associated environments and identified soil and decaying plants as habitats. We demonstrate that sterilized plant material is rapidly colonized by airborne _A. baumannii_. A set of 401 genomes were sequenced and compared to publicly available genomes, revealing numerous links between wildlife isolates and hospital strains, and disclosing intercontinental dispersal. Our pan-genome estimate of the species (~51,000 gene families) more than doubles that of previous studies. Our data further suggest massive radiation of the species early after its emergence, possibly fostered by human activity since the Neolithic. Now, it is possible to study the ecology and evolution of _A. baumannii_ in nature at an unprecedented temporal and spatial resolution and to elucidate the adaptive evolution of environmental bacteria towards multidrug-resistant opportunistic pathogens.

## Introduction

The Gram-negative _Acinetobacter baumannii_ is notorious for its potential to act as an opportunistic pathogen in hospitals, facilitated by an exceptional resistance to desiccation stress and intrinsic as well as acquired resistance to antibiotics and disinfectants. The genus _Acinetobacter_ currently comprises of around 100 described or tentative species[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR1 "Nemec, A. et al. Acinetobacter silvestris sp. nov. discovered in forest ecosystems in Czechia. International journal of systematic and evolutionary microbiology 72, 4 (2022)."), [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR2 "Nemec, A. et al. in Bergey’s Manual of Systematics of Archaea and Bacteria 1–78 (2015)."), the majority of which consist of environmental species, or commensals of human and animals that rarely cause any disease. Some species like _A. lwoffii_ and _A. johnsonii_, which are regularly found on human skin, mucosa and within the gut of humans and animals, appear to play an essential role in the development of immune tolerance[3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR3 "Ruokolainen, L. et al. Contrasting microbiotas between Finnish and Estonian infants: exposure to Acinetobacter may contribute to the allergy gap. Allergy 75, 2342–2351 (2020)."), [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR4 "Fyhrquist, N. et al. Acinetobacter species in the skin microbiota protect against allergic sensitization and inflammation. J. Allergy Clin. Immunol. 134, 1301–1309.e1311 (2014)."), [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR5 "Hanski, I. et al. Environmental biodiversity, human microbiota, and allergy are interrelated. Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. USA 109, 8334–8339 (2012)."), [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR6 "Ruokolainen, L. et al. Significant disparities in allergy prevalence and microbiota between the young people in Finnish and Russian Karelia. Clin. Exp. Allergy: J. Br. Soc. Allergy Clin. Immunol. 47, 665–674 (2017)."). In contrast, the species most frequently involved in hospital infections, e.g., _A. baumannii_, _A. pittii_, _A. nosocomialis_, and _A. seifertii_ do not represent commensals of humans and animals, and their natural habitats are still poorly described[7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR7 "Nemec, A. et al. Acinetobacter seifertii sp. nov., a member of the Acinetobacter calcoaceticus-Acinetobacter baumannii complex isolated from human clinical specimens. Int. J. Syst. Evolut. Microbiol. 65, 934–942 (2015)."), [8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR8 "Schleicher, X. et al. Molecular epidemiology of Acinetobacter baumannii and Acinetobacter nosocomialis in Germany over a 5-year period (2005-2009). Clin. Microbiol. Infect.: Publ. Eur. Soc. Clin. Microbiol. Infect. Dis. 19, 737–742 (2013)."), [9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR9 "Berlau, J., Aucken, H., Malnick, H. & Pitt, T. Distribution of Acinetobacter species on skin of healthy humans. Eur. J. Clin. Microbiol. Infect. Dis.: Publ. Eur. Soc. Clin. Microbiol. 18, 179–183 (1999)."). Although the zoonotic potential of _A. baumannii_ is evident from previous work[10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR10 "Ewers, C. et al. OXA-23 and ISAba1-OXA-66 class D β-lactamases in Acinetobacter baumannii isolates from companion animals. Int. J. Antimicrob. Agents 49, 37–44 (2017)."), [11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR11 "Klotz, P. et al. Seasonal occurrence and carbapenem susceptibility of bovine Acinetobacter baumannii in Germany. Front. Microbiol. 10, 272 (2019)."), [12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR12 "Wilharm, G. et al. Relatedness of wildlife and livestock avian isolates of the nosocomial pathogen Acinetobacter baumannii to lineages spread in hospitals worldwide. Environ. Microbiol. 19, 4349–4364 (2017)."), [13](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR13 "Lupo, A., Vogt, D., Seiffert, S. N., Endimiani, A. & Perreten, V. Antibiotic resistance and phylogenetic characterization of Acinetobacter baumannii strains isolated from commercial raw meat in Switzerland. J. Food Prot. 77, 1976–1981 (2014)."), [14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR14 "Rafei, R. et al. Extrahuman epidemiology of Acinetobacter baumannii in Lebanon. Appl. Environ. Microbiol. 81, 2359–2367 (2015)."), the pathogen is not generally accepted as zoonotic, e.g., not listed as zoonotic pathogen by public health agencies such as the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), Public Health England (PHE) or the European Center for Disease Prevention and Control (ECDC)[15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR15 "EFSA-ECDC. The European Union One Health 2020 Zoonoses Report. EFSA J.                    https://doi.org/10.2903/j.efsa.2021.6971                                     (2021)."), [16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR16 "OHZDP. One Health Zoonotic Disease Prioritization Workshop Report, United States. (2024)."), [17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR17 "Public Health England. List of zoonotic diseases.                    https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/list-of-zoonotic-diseases/list-of-zoonotic-diseases                                     (2019)."). As a hallmark, the killing of more than 400 individuals of a flock of sheep by a multidrug-resistant (MDR) and hypervirulent strain related to international clone 2 (IC2) illustrates the threat due to reverse zoonosis[18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR18 "Linz, B. et al. Virulent epidemic pneumonia in sheep caused by the human pathogen Acinetobacter baumannii. Front. Microbiol. 9, 2616 (2018)."). The relevancy to One Health is further underlined by the reservoir of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) genes found in environmental _Acinetobacter_ spp., as well as other bacteria, that can be mobilized into pathogenic _Acinetobacter_ spp. via horizontal gene transfer[19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR19 "Yoon, E. J. et al. Origin in Acinetobacter gyllenbergii and dissemination of aminoglycoside-modifying enzyme AAC(6’)-Ih. J. Antimicrob. Chemother. 71, 601–606 (2016)."), [20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR20 "Hernández-González, I. L., Mateo-Estrada, V. & Castillo-Ramirez, S. The promiscuous and highly mobile resistome of Acinetobacter baumannii. Microb. Genom.                    https://doi.org/10.1099/mgen.0.000762                                     (2022)."), [21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR21 "Ghaly, T. M., Paulsen, I. T., Sajjad, A., Tetu, S. G. & Gillings, M. R. A Novel family of Acinetobacter mega-plasmids are disseminating multi-drug resistance across the globe while acquiring location-specific accessory genes. Front. Microbiol. 11, 605952 (2020)."). Moreover, the release of MDR _A. baumannii_ from wastewater into the environment has been well-described[22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR22 "Higgins, P. G., Hrenovic, J., Seifert, H. & Dekic, S. Characterization of Acinetobacter baumannii from water and sludge line of secondary wastewater treatment plant. Water Res. 140, 261–267 (2018)."), [23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR23 "Jovcic, B., Novovic, K., Dekic, S. & Hrenovic, J. Colistin resistance in environmental isolates of Acinetobacter baumannii. Microb. Drug Resist. (Larchmt., N. Y.) 27, 328–336 (2021).").

Here, our intention was to improve the understanding of the ecology of _A. baumannii_ in natural habitats as we expect this knowledge to contribute to our understanding of the ecology of these bacteria in the hospital setting thus fostering better prevention and control measures. Moreover, from the hitherto available genome data it was impossible to retrace the evolution of the species because of the strong bias towards the study of MDR isolates and those of the predominating IC2 in particular[24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR24 "Castillo-Ramírez, S. Genomic epidemiology of Acinetobacter baumannii goes global. mBio 14, e0252023 (2023)."). Hence, starting from the association of diverse _A. baumannii_ with white stork nestlings as recently described[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR12 "Wilharm, G. et al. Relatedness of wildlife and livestock avian isolates of the nosocomial pathogen Acinetobacter baumannii to lineages spread in hospitals worldwide. Environ. Microbiol. 19, 4349–4364 (2017)."), we further explored the ecology of the bacteria. Preliminary data pointed to an only transient colonization of the nestlings and indicated the feed to be the true source. Following these potential paths, we came up with particular soil habitats being highly conducive to _A. baumannii_. Consequently, it was possible to establish a large collection of wildlife and environmental _A. baumannii_ isolates and to develop a genomics-based view on the ecology and evolution of the bacteria which is presented here.

## Results

### Update on the prevalence of _A. baumannii_ in white stork nestlings

Previously, we had presented data from 661 white stork nestlings sampled between 2013 and 2016 in Poland which were found to be colonized with _A. baumannii_ in the choana region at a prevalence of 25%[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR12 "Wilharm, G. et al. Relatedness of wildlife and livestock avian isolates of the nosocomial pathogen Acinetobacter baumannii to lineages spread in hospitals worldwide. Environ. Microbiol. 19, 4349–4364 (2017)."). We continued the systematic study of this environment and, based on 1319 white stork nestlings sampled between 2013 and 2018 across multiple regions of Poland, the average colonization rate was identified as 27.6% (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Fig1); and Suppl. Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). White stork nestlings were also sampled on a smaller scale in Germany in 2015 and the positive rate of choana samples reached 34.5% ( _n_ = 29). Choana samplings in Spain near Ciudad Real in 2015 revealed no positives from 57 white stork nestlings, but a positive rate of 7.8% ( _n_ = 64) was detected among white stork nestlings sampled in 2019 near Madrid (Suppl. Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Interestingly, this included for the first time the isolation of _A. nosocomialis_ ( _n_ = 2) from white stork samples, indicating differences in the ecology of bacteria and/or storks in Spain compared to Poland.

Cloacal sampling of white stork nestlings was conducted on 747 individuals in different regions of Poland throughout the study period and revealed an overall positive rate of 8%, however with great variability depending on the region and year of sampling (Suppl. Notably, significantly increased white blood cell counts in colonized nestlings argue for an infection by _A. baumannii_ rather than mere colonization (Suppl. Table [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1), Suppl. Note [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). The probability of isolating _A. baumannii_ from white stork nestlings decreases with the age of the chicks (Suppl. Figure [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)).

### Pellets from white storks

We had previously described that white stork spit pellets, consisting of indigestible remains of their food, are occasionally contaminated with _A. baumannii_ and might therefore be of interest for studying the ecology of both bacteria and storks[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR12 "Wilharm, G. et al. Relatedness of wildlife and livestock avian isolates of the nosocomial pathogen Acinetobacter baumannii to lineages spread in hospitals worldwide. Environ. Microbiol. 19, 4349–4364 (2017)."). We collected pellets from selected nests in Loburg and neighboring villages (Germany/state of Saxony-Anhalt) during March to August throughout the years 2015 and 2016. Interestingly, pellet samples proved positive for _A. baumannii_ not earlier than the end of May in 2015 and the end of April in 2016, respectively (Suppl. Figure [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). In 2015, positive samples were identified until migratory departure of the storks in August, with an overall rate of 14.8% positive pellets (13 out of 88), while in 2016 only a few positive samples were found from end of April until June (overall 7.1% positive pellets, 6 out of 85). Sporadically collected egg shells and feathers were also a source of _A. baumannii_ in few cases, and a stork chick found dead was also sampled positive in 2016. Pellets collected from stork colonies in Spain during the winter season of 2014/2015 were negative irrespective of their particular feeding grounds (landfills or nature, Suppl. Figure [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Overall, our data suggest that white storks arriving from their wintering grounds do not appear to be stably colonized by _A. baumannii_. Rather, they seem to acquire these bacteria after arrival in the summer quarters depending on the progression of the season, likely via the food chain.

It is worth mentioning, that pH values in pellets contaminated with _A. baumannii_ were found to be as low as pH 2 in some cases and it was proven that the bacteria were not only localized at the surface of the pellets but also inside (Suppl. Figure [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Consequently, although under laboratory conditions _A. baumannii_ cannot thrive at pH 2, it can survive such harsh conditions in a natural setting.

### Diversity of strains in individual nests

To challenge our hypothesis on an only transient carriage/colonization of parental white storks with _A. baumannii_ we analyzed the diversity of strains isolated from nestlings within individual nests. As a proxy of diversity, we made use of the intrinsic _bla_ OXA-51-like gene of _A. baumannii_ encoding the class D oxacillinase OXA-51, the protein variants of which are indexed and previously proven useful as phylogenetic marker[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR12 "Wilharm, G. et al. Relatedness of wildlife and livestock avian isolates of the nosocomial pathogen Acinetobacter baumannii to lineages spread in hospitals worldwide. Environ. Microbiol. 19, 4349–4364 (2017)."), [14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR14 "Rafei, R. et al. Extrahuman epidemiology of Acinetobacter baumannii in Lebanon. Appl. Environ. Microbiol. 81, 2359–2367 (2015)."), [25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR25 "Turton, J. F., Gabriel, S. N., Valderrey, C., Kaufmann, M. E. & Pitt, T. L. Use of sequence-based typing and multiplex PCR to identify clonal lineages of outbreak strains of Acinetobacter baumannii. Clin. Microbiol. Infect. 13, 807–815 (2007)."), [26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR26 "Zander, E., Nemec, A., Seifert, H. & Higgins, P. G. Association between β-lactamase-encoding bla(OXA-51) variants and DiversiLab rep-PCR-based typing of Acinetobacter baumannii isolates. J. Clin. Microbiol. 50, 1900–1904 (2012)."). We sequenced the _bla_ OXA-51-like gene of all isolates collected from white stork nestlings within a single year in a selected region of Poland and visualized the diversity on a map (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Fig2)). The diversity found within individual nests was striking, with up to 5 nestlings each carrying a distinguishable strain. These findings are inconsistent with a vertical transmission from stably colonized parental storks and support our hypothesis of a transient colonization/carriage via food intake from a diverse _A. baumannii_ population.

### Tracing the food sources

The predominant prey of white storks consists of small vertebrates, especially rodents and shrews, as well as arthropods and earthworms[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR27 "Böhning-Gaese, K. Zur Nahrungsökologie des Weißstorchs (Ciconia ciconia) in Oberschwaben: Beobachtungen an zwei Paaren. J. für Ornithologie 133, 61–71 (1992)."), [28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR28 "Kosicki, J. Z., Profus, P., Dolata, P. T. & Tobółka, M. Food composition and energy demand of the White Stork Ciconia ciconia breeding population. Literature survey and preliminary results from Poland. The White Stork in Poland: studies in biology, ecology and conservation. Bogucki Wyd. Nauk., Poznań, 169–183 (2006)."). In line with the literature, the dominant contribution of rodents and arthropods to the diet of white storks could be directly estimated from pellets of white storks collected in Poland and Germany (Suppl. Figure [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)), although analysis of the remains of earthworms from pellets, while possible[29](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR29 "Orłowski, G. et al. Using soil from pellets of White Storks Ciconia ciconia to assess the number of earthworms (Lumbricidae) consumed as primary and secondary prey. Ibis 158, 587–597 (2016)."), is time-consuming. To assess the potential role of beetles and other insects as sources of _A. baumannii_, we compared the content of pellets tested positive for _A. baumannii_ with that of pellets tested negative and found no difference in the fraction of insect matter (Suppl. Figure [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Moreover, dominant arthropods found in pellets such as the ground beetles _Zabrus tenebrioides_ and _Pterostichus melanarius_ (Suppl. Figure [7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)) collected from nature, albeit at low numbers ( _n_ = 13), were also found negative for _A. baumannii_. We also had the opportunity to sample grasshoppers collected in great quantities by an individual, deceased white stork, which was dissected and found gorged with grasshoppers, however all of the swallowed grasshoppers ( > 50) tested negative for _A. baumannii_. Moreover, pellets from Spanish storks, containing high amounts of crab and grasshopper remains (Suppl. Figure [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)), also tested negative. Altogether, we found no evidence of a contribution of arthropods captured by white storks to the acquisition of _A. baumannii_.

Next, we examined the potential role of rodents and shrews as a source of _A. baumannii_. We collected cat-captured rodents and shrews throughout Germany (states of Saxony-Anhalt, Hesse and Thuringia) in the period from 2014 to 2017 and took samples from the trachea and rectum. We found an average colonization rate of 2% for rodents ( _n_ = 154) and 4.7% for shrews ( _n_ = 64). Interestingly, all individuals colonized with _A. baumannii_ were captured between August and September (Suppl. Fig. [8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Next, we sampled wild, captive and laboratory rats ( _Rattus norvegicus_) from Germany[30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR30 "Raafat, D. et al. Molecular epidemiology of methicillin-susceptible and methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus in wild, captive and laboratory rats: effect of habitat on the nasal S. aureus population. Toxins (Basel)                    https://doi.org/10.3390/toxins12020080                                     (2020)."), [31](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR31 "Mehl, C. et al. Case report: tularaemia in a white-handed gibbon (Hylobates lar), Germany. Int. J. Vet. Sci. Med. 11, 121–125 (2023)."), [32](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR32 "Spahr, C. et al. Serological evidence of hepatitis E virus infection in zoo animals and identification of a rodent-borne strain in a Syrian brown bear. Vet. Microbiol 212, 87–92 (2017)."), as well as other rodents of the genera _Microtus_, _Myodes_, and _Apodemus_ captured in Germany[33](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR33 "Jeske, K. et al. Hantavirus-Leptospira coinfections in small mammals from central Germany. Epidemiol. Infect. 149, e97 (2021)."), [34](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR34 "Jeske, K. et al. Field vole-associated Traemmersee hantavirus from Germany represents a novel hantavirus species. Virus Genes 55, 848–853 (2019).") in the period 2007 to 2017. No rats and other rodents were found positive (nrats=491 and nother rodents = 532).

Earthworms were collected in Germany in the state of Saxony-Anhalt between August 2016 and September 2020. Out of 618 individual earthworms in total, we isolated _A. baumannii_ from 22 earthworms (3.6%) provisionally assigned to the genera _Lumbricus_, _Octolasium_, _Dendrobaena_ and _Eisenia_ (Suppl. Tab. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Of these positive samples, 19 were collected from May to September and only three isolates dated from March, April and November, respectively (1% positive of _n_ = 75 earthworms collected between October 21st and March 21st). The overall positive rate of _A. baumannii_ isolated from garden compost earthworms collected in Wernigerode (Germany) was 4.2% (14/331), and 4.3% (4/94) for a specific site at the bank of the river Holtemme (Germany), where we had recently also isolated _A. nosocomialis_ from soil[35](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR35 "Wilharm, G. Draft genome sequence of environmental isolate Acinetobacter nosocomialis U20-HoPe-S34-3 from Germany. Microbiol. Resour. Announc. 10, e0028621 (2021).").

### Other birds and their prey

We reasoned that studying other birds with an overlapping prey spectrum compared to white storks could help to indicate specificities of the relationship to _A. baumannii_. Consequently, we collected pellets, feathers and egg shells from a breeding colony of gray heron ( _Ardea cinerea_) in Germany. With the exception of one egg shell sample collected in June from which we could isolate _A. baumannii_ (Suppl. Fig. [9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)), all samples ( _n_ = 35) were negative. The detailed analysis of the pellets indicated their diet to consist of a significant portion of small mammals and insects (Suppl. Fig. [9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Further, we collected pellets ( _n_ = 101) from kestrel ( _Falco tinnunculus_) between March and October 2015 and 2016, and found three isolates (3%) of _A. baumannii_, each collected between August and September (Suppl. Fig. [10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Occasionally discovered owls’ pellets ( _n_ = 6), putatively from _Tyto alba_ and _Asio otus_, collected in August 2015 in Germany, were also positive for _A. baumannii_ in two cases. Moreover, we took cloacal samples from nestlings of the black stork ( _Ciconia nigra_), a species which, in contrast to white stork, is not synanthropic and is preferentially breeding and foraging in forests. Notwithstanding, anthropogenic waste was found in 26% of occupied nests[36](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR36 "Janic, B. et al. Plastic occurrence in nests of a large forest bird. Ecol. Indic.                    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ecolind.2023.110470                                     (2023)."). The diet of black stork nestlings in Poland is known to be dominated by fish and amphibians[37](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR37 "Kamiński, M. et al. Intra-Seasonal and brood-size dependent variation in the diet of black stork (Ciconia nigra) nestlings. Waterbirds 41, 268–275 (2018)."). Not a single one of 64 cloacal samples collected in Poland from black stork nestlings over a period of four years was found positive for _A. baumannii_. Taken together, gray heron, kestrel and black stork apparently differ from white stork regarding carriage of _A. baumannii_ although this might be influenced by differences in sampling depth and sampling types.

### Compost, soil, rhizosphere and the forest paradox

Following up on a possible role of earthworms in the transmission of _A. baumannii_ to white storks, we started collecting soil samples. Initially, we selected compost soil which is wet and rich in decomposing material, providing an environment from which earthworms could be easily collected. As we realized early on that compost soil represented an excellent source for _A. baumannii_, we utilized a boring rod to study the profile within one meter depth of the compost (Suppl. Fig. [11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Continued sampling of the soil originating from a single compost across a period of seven months yielded 86 isolates containing no less than 20 different variants of the OXA-51 protein family (60/91 samples positive, 66%). Concomitant sampling of earthworms within the same compost yielded 10 different variants of the OXA-51 protein from 7/44 (16%) positive earthworms. We observed a remarkable dynamic of isolated lineages and of the depth-depending colonization over time. The compost, which was continuously used for the deposition of vegetable waste and egg shells during this period, was not permanently colonized throughout all layers and there was a shift of the dominant lineages isolated over time. Continued sampling in subsequent years revealed that isolation rates declined (i) during winter and (ii) within 8-10 weeks after stopping to replenish the compost with fresh material.

Due to the anthropogenic influence on the compost and the ambiguity of the origin of _A. baumannii_ throughout this setting, we next attempted to identify soil habitats in more pristine environments. Searching for wet habitats rich in decomposing material where earthworms can thrive, we identified a site at the bank of the river Holtemme near Wernigerode, Germany, where we successfully isolated _A. baumannii_ from earthworms, soil samples and the rhizosphere of different plants ( _Urtica_ spp., _Impatiens_ spp., different grass species etc.). This site at the river bank with an area of only ~0.1 m2 turned out to be a hot spot for the isolation of _A. baumannii_ and yielded 22 isolates within 3 months representing 18 different variants of OXA-51 (Suppl. Fig. [12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Moreover, samples collected at this specific site yielded 10 isolates on a single day, each harboring a different OXA-51 variant. Noteworthy, extensive horizontal gene transfer (HGT) was detected between these co-colonizing lineages (Suppl. Fig. [13](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Strikingly, this site has remained a hot spot over the years even though the river bank has been remodeled several times due to flooding. None of the many additional sites we repeatedly sampled over the years along the river Holtemme showed any comparably high isolation rate.

Concomitantly, we had collected soil and earthworm samples from a forest area south of Wernigerode, representing both deciduous and coniferous forests of various compositions at an altitude between 300 and 450 m above sea level. Again, we chose wet habitats with decomposing plant material, mostly alongside creeks and ponds, however, of 269 samples only two were positive for _A. baumannii_ (0.7%) (Suppl. Fig. [14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). To prove the deduced principle that _A. baumannii_ preferentially colonize alongside creeks but not within forest areas, we selected a creek not previously sampled which has its source within the forest and which, after leaving the forest, flows through grassland before reaching the first village. We were unable to isolate _A. baumannii_ from any sample collected inside or along the edge of the forest although the creek’s banks were rich in decomposing material. The first positive sample was instead collected at a distance of approx. 300 m to the edge of the woods, but positive samples remained rare until the creek had passed the first village (Suppl. Fig. [15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). After passage of this village, we discovered two hot spots, which repeatedly collected positive samples (6/12 positive samples (50%) and 7/12 positive samples (58%), respectively). Collectively, forests possibly do not offer soil habitats supportive of _A. baumannii_, not even alongside creeks, whereas _A. baumannii_ apparently can thrive alongside creeks and rivers outside of forests even if they are lined with trees. Potentially, there are hot spots of _A. baumannii_ where they can occur in striking diversity outside forests.

### Diversity in terms of OXA-51 variants collected from Poland and Germany

Currently, there exist 381 assigned variants of the β-lactamase OXA-51 protein encoded by the intrinsic _bla_ OXA-51-like gene of _A. baumannii_ ([38](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR38 "Naas, T. et al. Beta-lactamase database (BLDB) - structure and function. J. Enzym. Inhib. Med Chem. 32, 917–919 (2017).") [http://www.bldb.eu/](http://www.bldb.eu/), accessed November 6th, 2024). Our collection of strains encompasses 209 variants, thus about 55% of the presently known worldwide diversity. Of the 209 variants encompassed by our collection, 122 represent previously undescribed variants which were made publicly available throughout the course of this study (Suppl. Data [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM3)). The dominant source of novel variants was white storks. Given that some of these OXA-variants have been shown to be useful as indicators of phylogenetic relationships, our findings suggested that our collection might represent a considerable portion of the worldwide known diversity of lineages. To substantiate this hypothesis, we performed extensive sequencing and phylogenomic analyses.

### Phylogenomics-based representation of the diversity of our collection

From our collection of 1,452 non-redundant _A. baumannii_ isolates collected outside the hospital context (Suppl. Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)), we chose at least one strain representing each OXA-variant for whole genome sequencing. Further, we selected few OXA-variants for the sequencing of larger sets of representative strains, in order to illustrate the diversity within these supposed lineages (e.g., OXA-104, -106, -126, 343, -374, -378, -431), and in addition, selected several strains representing each of the OXA-variants associated with the international clones (ICs) 1 to 8 (OXA-51/STPas15, OXA-64/ST25, OXA-65/ST79, OXA-66/ST2, OXA-68/ST10, OXA-69/ST1, OXA-71/ST3, OXA-90/ST78)[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR26 "Zander, E., Nemec, A., Seifert, H. & Higgins, P. G. Association between β-lactamase-encoding bla(OXA-51) variants and DiversiLab rep-PCR-based typing of Acinetobacter baumannii isolates. J. Clin. Microbiol. 50, 1900–1904 (2012)."). Moreover, due to the higher diversity of carbapenem-susceptible clinical isolates compared to carbapenem-resistant ones[8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR8 "Schleicher, X. et al. Molecular epidemiology of Acinetobacter baumannii and Acinetobacter nosocomialis in Germany over a 5-year period (2005-2009). Clin. Microbiol. Infect.: Publ. Eur. Soc. Clin. Microbiol. Infect. Dis. 19, 737–742 (2013)."), 15 carbapenem-susceptible human clinical isolates from Germany were selected according to their encoding OXA-variant, some previously described[39](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR39 "Wendel, A. F. et al. One-year molecular surveillance of carbapenem-susceptible A. baumannii on a German intensive care unit: diversity or clonality. Antimicrob. Resist. Infect. control 7, 145 (2018)."). Finally, the collection was supplemented by a diverse set of 21 isolates covering veterinarian und human clinical isolates from a previous study[40](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR40 "Müller, S. Molecular and functional typing of isolates of the Acinetobacter calcoaceticus-Acinetobacter baumannii complex with emphasis on multi-drug resistant Acinetobacter baumannii. Dissertation, Freie Universität Berlin, (2018)."). Altogether, our study provides 401 novel genomes, of which 229 originated from white stork nestling isolates, 77 from soil and plant root samples, 2 from air samples above compost, 59 from diverse animal materials including earthworms, rodents and shrews, pellets from diverse bird species, egg shells, feathers and nesting materials, 7 from veterinarian samples, and 27 from human clinical samples. For comparison to the previously available diversity within the species _A. baumannii_, a generic protocol selected 413 additional genomes available within public databases (Suppl. Data [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM3)).

The pan-genome of this collection includes 50,989 gene families, given a threshold of 90% amino acid sequence identity (Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Tab1)). The phylogenomic tree based on the core genome set of 1,728 conserved genes representing 1.328 megabasepairs (Mbp) of _A. baumannii_ genomic sequences is illustrated in Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Fig3). Especially remarkable is the share of deeply branching lineages, suggesting massive radiation early in evolution of the species. Most of the isolates differ by 18,000 to 23,000 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) between each other (Suppl. Data [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM3)). While only a few OXA-variants represented multiple times in this study are apparently monophyletic, such as OXA-431 or OXA-68, most OXA-variants show phylogenetic clustering (see interactive microreact project at ( [https://microreact.org/project/sNy69Jjwpj1qBKGFrkGSMz-acinetobacterproject04nov2024](https://microreact.org/project/sNy69Jjwpj1qBKGFrkGSMz-acinetobacterproject04nov2024)) and Suppl. Fig. [16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). In contrast, few OXA-variants show broad scattering, in particular the prototypic OXA-51 and some other OXA-variants (OXA-65, OXA-69, OXA-71) found associated with ICs[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR26 "Zander, E., Nemec, A., Seifert, H. & Higgins, P. G. Association between β-lactamase-encoding bla(OXA-51) variants and DiversiLab rep-PCR-based typing of Acinetobacter baumannii isolates. J. Clin. Microbiol. 50, 1900–1904 (2012).") (Suppl. Fig. [16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Another remarkable observation is that the collection of wildlife and environmental strains from Poland ( _n_ = 235) and Germany ( _n_ = 159) respectively, each covers a considerable portion of the worldwide known diversity (Fig. [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Fig4)), pointing to an efficient mechanism of global spread. Moreover, human, animal and environmental isolates likewise spread all-over the phylogenetic tree (Fig. [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Fig5)). Of particular interest are the many clades represented by environmental, animal and human clinical isolates, underpinning the relevancy of the One Health concept in regards to _A. baumannii_. This also includes representatives of ICs, namely IC8 (Suppl. Fig. [17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Additionally, IC4, IC5, IC6 and IC7 are now also represented by at least one isolate from wild animals, livestock or the environment. Distances between human clinical isolates and their related isolates from environmental or wildlife sources cover a broad range, approaching distances of 27 SNPs within the core genome in the case of a human clinical isolate from Canada related to a white stork isolate from Poland (both OXA-64), 35 SNPs between a white stork isolate from Poland and a human clinical isolate from Thailand (both OXA-433), or 48 SNPs between a human clinical isolate from Japan and another white stork isolate from Poland (both OXA-762) (Suppl. Data [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM3), Suppl. Tab. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). These results called for a molecular clock analysis to allow for temporal placement of the genetic and geographical distances. Accordingly, we performed BEAST analyses[41](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR41 "Holt, K. et al. Five decades of genome evolution in the globally distributed, extensively antibiotic-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii global clone 1. Microb. Genom. 2, e000052 (2016).") on the OXA-126 clade ( _n_ = 34), revealing a mutation rate of 1.1345 × 10-6 ( \+ /\- 3.2 × 10−7) per site per year corresponding to ~1.5 SNPs per year within the core genome (range 1.08-1.93 SNPs per year). However, given the weak temporal signal of our dataset covering only 9 years of sampling, it is important to note that the molecular clock rate reported for IC1 is only slightly different with 1.5 × 10-6 substitutions per site per year[41](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR41 "Holt, K. et al. Five decades of genome evolution in the globally distributed, extensively antibiotic-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii global clone 1. Microb. Genom. 2, e000052 (2016).") (Suppl. Data [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM3)). The most distant isolates within the OXA-126 clade, 13-291-1 C and 19-Pos71-1, differ by 12,354 SNPs corresponding to 8236 years (range ~6400–11,440 years), pointing to a strong and constant selection pressure stabilizing the OXA-126 variant within the natural context. Applying this molecular clock on the species level, the most recent common ancestor of all members of the species _A. baumannii_ likely existed only around 15,000 years ago. Accordingly, when applied to the closest relatives found between clinical and environmental/wildlife isolates in our study, those intercontinental pairs of isolates mentioned above share their most recent common ancestor (MRCA) only 18-32 years before present (and only 14-24 years if applying the IC1 rate). Further relationships of particular interest are listed in Suppl. Table [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1).

### Natural endowment with resistance genes and insertion sequence (IS) elements

Altogether, intercontinental spread between North America, Europe and Asia within a few years cannot be explained by the migratory activity of white storks which migrate between Europe and Africa. Rather, human activity, or natural mechanisms of airborne spread, need to be considered. Hence, we analyzed the genomes regarding their endowment with AMR genes and IS elements as indicators of anthropogenic selection pressure (Suppl. Data [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM3); Suppl. Fig. [18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)- [19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Globally, the newly sequenced isolates harbor an intrinsic _bla_ OXA-51-like gene (96% of the collection), a majority of which also harbor the _bla_ ADC gene in one of 43 variants (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Fig3)). Nickel resistance gene _nreB_ and efflux pump gene _amvA_ are also highly abundant. Moreover, most isolates also harbor the _ant(3”)-IIa_ aminoglycoside and the _abaF_ fosfomycin resistance genes, suggesting these also to be part of the intrinsic genomic endowment of the species. Only 12 isolates of our collection exhibit AMR gene counts above 3 (Suppl. Data [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM3)). Six of these are human clinical isolates representing STPas 2, 106, 1357, 2402, and 2409, four are animal isolates of veterinary and livestock origin representing STPas 23, 241 and 866. Only two isolates of environmental and wildlife origin reach a count of 4 AMR genes (STPas 23 and 309). This indicates the comparably pristine habitats they originate from. Similarly, while IS elements are rarely enriched in environmental and wildlife-associated isolates, clinically related strains harbor an increased amount of IS elements (Suppl. Data [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM3), Suppl. Fig. [19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Moreover, few of our environmental or wild animal isolates harbored an interrupted _comM_ gene, a configuration typically found in MDR lineages firmly established in the hospital[42](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR42 "Hamidian, M. & Hall, R. M. The AbaR antibiotic resistance islands found in Acinetobacter baumannii global clone 1 - Structure, origin and evolution. Drug Resist. Update. 41, 26–39 (2018)."). Altogether, this suggests that our environmental and wildlife-associated isolates show little signs of anthropogenic selection pressure and indeed originate from pristine environments.

These analyses revealed that genomes of clinical isolates are significantly enriched in several categories including replication, recombination and repair functions (Fig. [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Fig6)).

### Colonization of compost material and linkages to the fungal world

As our phylogenomic analyses suggested global spread of clonal lineages long before globalization of modern times, we considered various modes of dispersion independent of human activity. Our samplings in garden compost suggest that colonization of _A. baumannii_ is associated with decomposition of fresh plant material, as _A. baumannii_ vanishes after the feed of the compost is stopped. We hypothesized, this may point to attraction of _A. baumannii_ throughout the early steps of decomposition, during which fungi are key players. We thus speculated that _A. baumannii_ might start colonization together with fungal spores that could serve as a vehicle for local as well as global spread of _A. baumannii_. To prove an airborne spread of _A. baumannii_ in a local setting we mounted open tubes in a distance of 50 cm above an active compost with the opening positioned downwards or the tube positioned vertically for 24 h. Then the tubes were closed, transported to the laboratory, filled with liquid medium and processed to cultivate _A. baumannii_. As this ad hoc setting instantly yielded isolates of _A. baumannii_ from 4 out of 5 samples, we next designed experiments to study the colonization of sterilized fresh plant material deposited in the garden. The autoclaved plant material was deposited either directly in the garden in a distance of 5 to 10 m from the compost, or it was deposited in a sterilized plastic bag either open top and shielded from the soil or shielded from the top and accessible from the soil. We found that every setting ( _n_ = 4) was colonized within 2-3 weeks, indicating that colonization can occur not only via soil contact but also via the air.

Next, we tested if _A. baumannii_ is capable of attaching to fungal spores using _Aspergillus_ spp., which are known to play a key role during the first steps of plant decomposition and dominate within the compost setting[43](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR43 "He, P., Wei, S., Shao, L. & Lü, F. Aerosolization behavior of prokaryotes and fungi during composting of vegetable waste. Waste Manag. (N. Y.) 89, 103–113 (2019)."). We incubated spores from different _Aspergillus_ spp. isolates with different strains of _A. baumannii_ and observed a marked adherence of the bacteria to fungal spores (Fig. [7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Fig7)). Similarly, adherence to spores from different _Penicillium_ spp. isolates was observed. Interestingly, adherence was not an ad hoc phenomenon but required 2 to 3 h of incubation to manifest and resulted in an inhibition of spore germination, a process that typically became observable after 5-6 h of incubation in our setting. Taken together, these experiments demonstrate that _A. baumannii_ has the capacity to interact with fungal spores and to colonize new habitats via the air.

### The where and when of sampling

Finally, we challenged our current model of the prevalence of _A. baumannii_ in natural habitats. We chose to collect samples during late summer when positive rates at our hotspot sites peaked. We collected 20 samples from river banks within forests and 20 samples from river banks outside of forests across the Harz district, Germany. For both groups we chose sites we had never sampled previously and with indicators of nutrient-rich soil, such as deposition of decomposing plant material and growth of nettles ( _Urtica_ spp.), _Impatiens_ spp. or _Aegopodium podagraria_. While 1 out of 20 (5%) forest samples was positive for _A. baumannii_, 6 out of 20 (30%) samples were positive from river banks outside of forests. The positive rate of 30% in selected soil samples thus is in the same range as that yielded from choana sampling of white stork nestlings.

## Discussion

Here, we present a comprehensive study on the ecology of _A. baumannii_. Our findings outline that _A. baumannii_ can be effectively isolated from wet and nutrient-rich soil alongside waters, which is in line with previous studies[44](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR44 "Suresh, S., Aditya, V., Deekshit, V. K., Manipura, R. & Premanath, R. A rare occurrence of multidrug-resistant environmental Acinetobacter baumannii strains from the soil of Mangaluru, India. Arch. Microbiol. 204, 422 (2022)."), [45](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR45 "Govender, R., Amoah, I. D., Kumari, S., Bux, F. & Stenström, T. A. Detection of multidrug resistant environmental isolates of Acinetobacter and Stenotrophomonas maltophilia: a possible threat for community acquired infections? J. Environ. Sci. Today, representatives of this species can be isolated from specific natural habitats at an unprecedented scale, and we hope that this will stimulate the scientific community to collect and characterize _A. baumannii_ from natural habitats worldwide. Although our sample setting, essentially restricted to samples from Poland and Germany, represents a significant portion of the worldwide known diversity, evidently, our picture remains incomplete. In particular, we have not yet collected representatives of the international clones IC1 - IC3 from natural environments, including the clinically dominant IC2, suggesting that their relatives either prefer completely different habitats or are geographically restricted. We focus the discussion on the interpretation of our data in light of understanding the evolution behind this species and in particular adaptation to the hospital environment.

Seasonality of _Acinetobacter_ infections with a global peak in summer has been discussed[49](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR49 "Richet, H. Seasonality in Gram-negative and healthcare-associated infections. Clin. Microbiol. Infect.: Publ. Eur. Soc. Clin. Microbiol. Infect. Dis. 18, 934–940 (2012)."). However, seasonality has not been observed for MDR _Acinetobacter_ [50](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR50 "Fukuta, Y. et al. Lack of seasonality in the occurrence of multidrug-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii complex. Infect. Control Hospital Epidemiol. 33, 1051–1052 (2012)."), [51](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR51 "Liu, X., Qin, P., Wen, H., Wang, W. & Zhao, J. Seasonal meropenem resistance in Acinetobacter baumannii and influence of temperature-driven adaptation. BMC Microbiol. 24, 149 (2024)."), which show a high degree of clonality and are known to be transmitted from patient to patient and from hospital to hospital in contrast to the diverse group of non-MDR _Acinetobacter_ [8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR8 "Schleicher, X. et al. Molecular epidemiology of Acinetobacter baumannii and Acinetobacter nosocomialis in Germany over a 5-year period (2005-2009). Clin. Microbiol. Infect.: Publ. Eur. Soc. Clin. Microbiol. Infect. Dis. 19, 737–742 (2013)."), [39](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR39 "Wendel, A. F. et al. Our data on the occurrence of _A. baumannii_ in compost and soil samples as well as birds’ pellets and small mammals approve seasonality with a summer peak. In line, association of _A. baumannii_ with cattle also exhibits seasonality, peaking between May and August[11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR11 "Klotz, P. et al. Seasonal occurrence and carbapenem susceptibility of bovine Acinetobacter baumannii in Germany. Front. Microbiol. 10, 272 (2019)."). Noteworthy, aerosolization of bacteria and fungi also peaks in summer[52](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR52 "Xue, F. et al. Characterization of airborne bacteria and fungi at a land-sea transition site in Southern China. Sci. Total Environ. 849, 157786 (2022)."), as further discussed below. In conclusion, we provide further evidence that preferentially during the summer season, when _A. baumannii_ thrive in natural habitats, novel lineages of _A. baumannii_ enter the hospital environment worldwide causing increased infection rates with non-MDR appearance.

Here, we have demonstrated that _A. baumannii_ is capable of interacting with different fungal spores and that _A. baumannii_ colonizes decomposing plant material early on, when fungi play a dominant role in the decomposition. Notably, _A. baumannii_ is capable of growing on xylose, arabinose and ribose[53](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR53 "Alberti, L. et al. Int Microbiol 24, 545–558 (2021)."), [55](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR55 "Seiboth, B. & Metz, B. Fungal arabinan and L-arabinose metabolism. Appl Microbiol Biotechnol. 89, 1665–1673 (2011)."). _Aspergillus_ is a major cause of morbidity and mortality in a wide range of birds including specific lineages of poultry[56](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR56 "Thierry, S. et al. Dis. 36, 379–385 (2013).") and particularly so in white stork nestlings[57](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR57 "Olias, P., Gruber, A. D., Winfried, B., Hafez, H. Seminal work by Peleg et al. [61](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR61 "Peleg, A. Y. et al. Prokaryote-eukaryote interactions identified by using Caenorhabditis elegans. Proc. Farnesol was later demonstrated to disrupt cell membrane integrity in _A. baumannii_ and to impair biofilm formation and motility[63](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR63 "Kostoulias, X. et al. The virulence potential of _A. baumannii_ against _C. albicans_ has been further characterized by others[64](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR64 "Gaddy, J. A., Tomaras, A. Moreover, several isolates of _A. baumannii_ have been described to suppress phytopathogenic fungi[67](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR67 "Syed, S., Tollamadugu, N. & Lian, B. The microbiome analysis of household dust revealed a significant positive correlation of _Acinetobacter_ with several fungal genera such as _Alternaria_, _Aspergillus_ and _Fusarium_ [69](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR69 "Ding, L. Sporadically, _A. baumannii_ infection has been reported following _Aspergillus_ infection in humans[73](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR73 "Mahismita, P. & Dipti, G. Pseudomembranous Aspergillus tracheobronchitis with secondary Acinetobacter baumannii pneumonia. Indian J. Med. Res. 152, S136–s137 (2020)."), but metagenomics-based analyses suggest that fungal and bacterial co-infections of the lung are common, with _A. baumannii_ being one of the dominant pathogens[74](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR74 "Zhao, Z. et al. Prevalence of fungal and bacterial co-infection in pulmonary fungal infections: a metagenomic next generation sequencing-based study. Front. Cell. Infect. Microbiol. 11, 749905 (2021)."). Concerning our speculation that _A. baumannii_ could use _Aspergillus_ and other fungal spores for hitchhiking, it is important to emphasize that both _Acinetobacter_ and _Aspergillus_ readily aerosolize[43](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR43 "He, P., Wei, S., Shao, L. & Lü, F. Aerosolization behavior of prokaryotes and fungi during composting of vegetable waste. Waste Manag. (N. Y.) 89, 103–113 (2019)."). In the light of all this groundwork, our findings are far from surprising. It is however important to consider these possible linkages in the context of hygiene and nosocomial infections. For instance, mold growth on moist surfaces, a common problem in tropical regions, may also contribute to the spread of _Acinetobacter_ in the hospital. Municipal composting plants should be also assessed carefully[43](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR43 "He, P., Wei, S., Shao, L. & Lü, F. Aerosolization behavior of prokaryotes and fungi during composting of vegetable waste. Waste Manag. (N. Y.) 89, 103–113 (2019).").

Our data indicate that forests in Germany and Poland might not provide supportive habitats for _A. baumannii_. Particularly, we documented low isolation rates from forest soil samples and from samples collected in the vicinity of forests, and we found no association of _A. baumannii_ with sylvan black storks. Measured pH values from soils supportive of _A. baumannii_ were not significantly different from those of non-supportive soils (Suppl. Fig. [20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). We therefore hypothesize that _A. baumannii_ is kept away from forests either via direct inhibition of _A. baumannii_ by the fungi of the forests’ mycorrhizae or via suppression of those fungi supportive of _A. baumannii_ colonization. In line with the latter hypothesis, fungi of the division _Basidiomycota_ such as _Fistulina hepatica_ are known to produce antifungal compounds[75](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR75 "Lee, J. et al. Identification of Feldin, an antifungal polyyne from the beefsteak fungus Fistulina hepatica. Biomolecules                    https://doi.org/10.3390/biom10111502                                     (2020)."). If the former explanation should hold, forest microbiota should be intensively screened for potential antibiotic producers. Interestingly, a study from China on microbial emission levels depending on land use did not reveal a negative effect of forests on _Acinetobacter_ abundance[76](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR76 "Li, X., Chen, H. & Yao, M. Microbial emission levels and diversities from different land use types. Environ. Int. 143, 105988 (2020)."). Possibly, the situation in Europe differs from that in China or the general trend for the genus _Acinetobacter_ does not represent the situation for _A. baumannii_. In line with the latter explanation, several _Acinetobacter_ species such as _A. silvestris_ [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR1 "Nemec, A. et al. Acinetobacter silvestris sp. nov. discovered in forest ecosystems in Czechia. International journal of systematic and evolutionary microbiology 72, 4 (2022).") and _A. bohemicus_ [77](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR77 "Krizova, L., Maixnerova, M., Sedo, O. & Nemec, A. Acinetobacter bohemicus sp. nov. widespread in natural soil and water ecosystems in the Czech Republic. Syst. Appl. Microbiol. 37, 467–473 (2014).") have been isolated from forest soils. Microbial communities of forest soils are largely defined by pH[78](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR78 "Kaiser, K. et al. Driving forces of soil bacterial community structure, diversity, and function in temperate grasslands and forests. Sci. Rep. 6, 33696 (2016).") and dominant tree species[79](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR79 "Baldrian, P. et al. Active and total microbial communities in forest soil are largely different and highly stratified during decomposition. ISME J. 6, 248–258 (2012)."). However, to clarify relation of either of the two parameters with the lack of _A. baumannii_ in forests further data are required. It is evident that metagenomics data already available from forest soils need to be evaluated and complemented by new studies specifically involving _A. baumannii_ habitats, such as those uncovered within this study.

Our data suggest a massive radiation within the species _A. baumannii_ early after its emergence. The average distance of the numerous unrelated lineages within the species is ~20,000 SNPs per genome based on our core set of genes (Suppl. Data [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM3)). According to our molecular clock analyses and those of others[41](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR41 "Holt, K. et al. Five decades of genome evolution in the globally distributed, extensively antibiotic-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii global clone 1. Microb. Genom. 2, e000052 (2016)."), the substitution rates are in the range of 1.13 − 1.5 × 10-6 substitutions per site and per year. Based on our core genome alignment, this corresponds to 1.5 SNPs per core genome per year. Accordingly, most of the lineages are about 13,000 years apart from each other (range ~10,400-18,500 years, ~10,040 based on the rate for IC1). This is in agreement with global warming during the beginning of the Holocene and early Neolithic, when human activities started to change the environment, producing a plethora of novel types of habitats, especially due to deforestation and later the development of agriculture and livestock farming. In this context, deforestation is of specific importance given the adverse impact of forests on _A. baumannii_ as outlined above. In the European Alps, deforestation on small areas due to human activity has been dated back to about 10,000 years before present[80](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR80 "Garcés-Pastor, S. et al. High resolution ancient sedimentary DNA shows that alpine plant diversity is associated with human land use and climate change. Nat. Commun. 13, 6559 (2022)."). Moreover, livestock farming may have additionally contributed to this radiation, given the association of _A. baumannii_ with cattle, sheep, and poultry[11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR11 "Klotz, P. et al. Seasonal occurrence and carbapenem susceptibility of bovine Acinetobacter baumannii in Germany. Front. Microbiol. 10, 272 (2019)."), [12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR12 "Wilharm, G. et al. Relatedness of wildlife and livestock avian isolates of the nosocomial pathogen Acinetobacter baumannii to lineages spread in hospitals worldwide. Environ. Microbiol. 19, 4349–4364 (2017)."), [18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR18 "Linz, B. et al. Virulent epidemic pneumonia in sheep caused by the human pathogen Acinetobacter baumannii. Front. Microbiol. 9, 2616 (2018)."). As another piece of evidence, our data indicate that _A. baumannii_ prevalence in white stork samples is positively correlated with heterogeneous agricultural use (Suppl. Fig. [21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1), Suppl. Tables [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)- [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1), Suppl. Note [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). All in all, our data are in line with the hypothesis that human activity during the beginning of the Holocene and early Neolithic was a key driver of the adaptive radiation within the species.

Our local sample settings in Poland and Germany revealed a significant proportion of the worldwide known diversity of _A. baumannii_, with evolutionary distances to hitherto known genomes, mostly of clinical origin, ranging from very ancient to very recent. Thus, our data illustrate a massive global dispersal on the sub-species level since ancient times, which is consistent with the findings of Louca[81](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR81 "Louca, S. The rates of global bacterial and archaeal dispersal. ISME J. 16, 159–167 (2022).") that most prokaryotes are globally spread even at sub-species resolution. However, the velocity of spread even for human-associated prokaryotes exhibiting the highest diffusivity, was determined at only 580 kilometers per 100 years for individual lineages while for terrestrial bacteria it was only 370 km per 100 years. Intercontinental transmission rates of human-associated bacteria on a per-lineage basis were estimated at around 5.5 events per 106 years for the most probable transfer between North America and Europe, i.e., one intercontinental transfer event in every ~182,000 years[81](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR81 "Louca, S. The rates of global bacterial and archaeal dispersal. ISME J. 16, 159–167 (2022)."). Evidently, the massive intercontinental spread suggested by our data is exceeding estimates for those most transmissible species, namely human-associated ones[81](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR81 "Louca, S. The rates of global bacterial and archaeal dispersal. ISME J. 16, 159–167 (2022)."), by far. If nowadays human activity would account for the spread observed, a strong association of _A. baumannii_ with the human microbiota could possibly explain the data, however such an association has been refuted[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR9 "Berlau, J., Aucken, H., Malnick, H. & Pitt, T. Distribution of Acinetobacter species on skin of healthy humans. Eur. J. Clin. Microbiol. Infect. Dis.: Publ. Eur. Soc. Clin. Microbiol. 18, 179–183 (1999)."). Alternatively, a strong association of _A. baumannii_ with global flow of commodity, in particular livestock and meat which can be heavily contaminated with _A. baumannii_ [13](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR13 "Lupo, A., Vogt, D., Seiffert, S. N., Endimiani, A. & Perreten, V. Antibiotic resistance and phylogenetic characterization of Acinetobacter baumannii strains isolated from commercial raw meat in Switzerland. J. Food Prot. 77, 1976–1981 (2014)."), might explain the global spread. However, as outlined above, the origin of our environmental and wildlife isolates from pristine environments is evident from their genomic structure. All in all, there is no evidence that the observed global pattern of distribution of environmental _A. baumannii_ is caused by human activity. It is further not explainable by migratory patterns of white storks, which do not cross oceans[82](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR82 "Flack, A. et al. Costs of migratory decisions: a comparison across eight white stork populations. Sci. Adv. 2, e1500931 (2016)."). Based on our data and published work, we propose airborne spread as an original strategy of the species and possibly of other members of the genus _Acinetobacter_, possibly facilitated by hitchhiking on fungal spores. In the hospital setting and livestock production facilities, airborne spread of _A. baumannii_ has already been demonstrated[83](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR83 "Martin, E. & Jackel, U. Characterization of bacterial contaminants in the air of a duck hatchery by cultivation based and molecular methods. J. Environ. Monit. 13, 464–470 (2011)."), [84](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR84 "Yakupogullari, Y. et al. Is airborne transmission of Acinetobacter baumannii possible: a prospective molecular epidemiologic study in a tertiary care hospital. Am. J. Infect. Control 44, 1595–1599 (2016)."). The prerequisites for an effective long-distance atmospheric spread are tolerance to desiccation and radiation, as well as potential for aerosolization. The tolerance of _A. baumannii_ to desiccation is well documented[85](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR85 "Otter, J. A., Yezli, S., Salkeld, J. A. & French, G. L. Evidence that contaminated surfaces contribute to the transmission of hospital pathogens and an overview of strategies to address contaminated surfaces in hospital settings. Am. J. Infect. Control 41, S6–S11 (2013)."), [86](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR86 "Zeidler, S. & Müller, V. Coping with low water activities and osmotic stress in Acinetobacter baumannii: significance, current status and perspectives. Environ. Microbiol. 21, 2212–2230 (2019)."), [87](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR87 "Jawad, A., Seifert, H., Snelling, A. M., Heritage, J. & Hawkey, P. M. Survival of Acinetobacter baumannii on dry surfaces: comparison of outbreak and sporadic isolates. J. Clin. Microbiol. 36, 1938–1941 (1998)."), [88](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR88 "König, P. et al. The VBNC state: a fundamental survival strategy of Acinetobacter baumannii. mBio 14, e0213923 (2023)."). Further, resistance to radiation is not only described for _A. radioresistens_, but also for other _Acinetobacter_ species, including _A. baumannii_ [89](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR89 "Christensen, E. A., Gerner-Smidt, P. & Kristensen, H. Radiation resistance of clinical Acinetobacter spp.: a need for concern? J. Hospital Infect. 18, 85–92 (1991)."). Moreover, _Acinetobacter_ spp. are among the dominant airborne bacteria found in bioaerosols[76](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR76 "Li, X., Chen, H. & Yao, M. Microbial emission levels and diversities from different land use types. Environ. Int. 143, 105988 (2020)."), [90](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR90 "Mu, F. et al. Source identification of airborne bacteria in the mountainous area and the urban areas. Atmos. Res. 231, 104676 (2020)."). The aerosolization behavior of bacteria and fungi from vegetable waste compost has been studied in detail, revealing efficient aerosolization of both _Acinetobacter_ and various fungi including _Aspergillus_ [43](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR43 "He, P., Wei, S., Shao, L. & Lü, F. Aerosolization behavior of prokaryotes and fungi during composting of vegetable waste. Waste Manag. (N. Y.) 89, 103–113 (2019)."). In line with these findings, _Acinetobacter_ is abundant in rainwater[91](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR91 "Ahmed, W. et al. Amplicon-based taxonomic characterization of bacteria in urban and peri-urban roof-harvested rainwater stored in tanks. Sci. Total Environ. 576, 326–334 (2017)."), [92](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR92 "Bae, S., Maestre, J. P., Kinney, K. A. & Kirisits, M. J. An examination of the microbial community and occurrence of potential human pathogens in rainwater harvested from different roofing materials. Water Res. 159, 406–413 (2019)."). What is more, glacier microbiomes show the highest relative abundance of _A. baumannii_ and _A. junii_ compared to all other potential pathogens[93](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR93 "Liu, Y. et al. A database of glacier microbiomes for the Three Poles. Earth Syst. Sci. Data Discuss. 2023, 1–20 (2023)."). Finally, tropospheric sampling revealed the presence of _A. baumannii_ at a height of up to 3,000 meters above sea-level allowing long-distance transport of up to 2,000 kilometers[94](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR94 "Rodó, X. et al. Microbial richness and air chemistry in aerosols above the PBL confirm 2,000-km long-distance transport of potential human pathogens. Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. USA 121, e2404191121 (2024)."). Taken together, there is evidence illustrating that _A. baumannii_ is well adapted to survive atmospheric long-distance spread.

Previous analyses of the pan-genome of the species _A. baumannii_ ranged from 16,000–20,000 genes[95](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR95 "Mangas, E. L. et al. Pangenome of Acinetobacter baumannii uncovers two groups of genomes, one of them with genes involved in CRISPR/Cas defence systems associated with the absence of plasmids and exclusive genes for biofilm formation. Microb. Genom.                    https://doi.org/10.1099/mgen.0.000309                                     (2019)."), [96](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR96 "Galac, M. R. et al. A diverse panel of clinical Acinetobacter baumannii for research and development. Antimicrobial Agents And Chemother.                    https://doi.org/10.1128/aac.00840-20                                     (2020)."), [97](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR97 "Urhan, A. & Abeel, T. A comparative study of pan-genome methods for microbial organisms: Acinetobacter baumannii pan-genome reveals structural variation in antimicrobial resistance-carrying plasmids. Microb. Genom.                    https://doi.org/10.1099/mgen.0.000690                                     (2021)."). Here, we present a conservative estimate of 50,989 different genes, more than doubling previous appraisals (Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Tab1)). This drastic increase in the size of the pan-genome of the species illustrates the significant bias due to a previous focus on MDR isolates (the majority of which belong to a few widely disseminated clonal lineages) within the scientific community, but also indicates that compared to the broad diversity of lineages in nature, only few have managed to establish themselves within the hospital environment. Our data set will now facilitate identification of the critical factors of success owned by ICs. As a first clue, several functional categories appear enriched in clinical isolates (Fig. [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Fig6)). Even though a few established lineages dominate, there is a constant invasion of novel lineages into the hospital, particularly in summer. Given the enormous genomic diversity and fluidity illustrated here, combined with the capacity to recombine excessively[98](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR98 "Godeux, A. S. et al. Interbacterial Transfer of Carbapenem Resistance and Large Antibiotic Resistance Islands by Natural Transformation in Pathogenic Acinetobacter. mBio 13, e0263121 (2022)."), [99](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR99 "Wright, M. S. et al. New insights into dissemination and variation of the health care-associated pathogen Acinetobacter baumannii from genomic analysis. mBio 5, e00963–00913 (2014)."), a huge potential is evident for further development including the adaptation to the hospital environment and humans as hosts. This potential must not be underestimated given the recent development of a hypervirulent sub-lineage of IC2 isolated from sheep[18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR18 "Linz, B. et al. Virulent epidemic pneumonia in sheep caused by the human pathogen Acinetobacter baumannii. Front. Microbiol. 9, 2616 (2018)."). Note that as _A. baumannii_ is apparently a very recent species, with its radiation possibly starting only around 15,000 years ago, its adaptation to humans and livestock might be only in its beginnings. Moreover, we also need to consider the potential interspecies transfer of DNA from other nosocomial pathogens[100](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR100 "Traglia, G. M. et al. Interspecies DNA acquisition by a naturally competent Acinetobacter baumannii strain. Int. J. Antimicrobial Agents 53, 483–490 (2019).").

Given the broad distribution of human clinical isolates, animal and environmental isolates all-over the phylogenetic tree and the linkages between isolates of different sources disclosed, the capability to cause opportunistic infections appears as an ancient capability of the species rather than that of a few recent human-adapted lineages. In line with this, previous studies on avian isolates did not reveal significant differences in virulence compared to human clinical isolates[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR12 "Wilharm, G. et al. Relatedness of wildlife and livestock avian isolates of the nosocomial pathogen Acinetobacter baumannii to lineages spread in hospitals worldwide. Environ. Microbiol. 19, 4349–4364 (2017)."). Studies on wastewater discharge and river water indicate spreading of clinical isolates of _A. baumannii_ into the environment in Poland and elsewhere[23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR23 "Jovcic, B., Novovic, K., Dekic, S. & Hrenovic, J. Colistin resistance in environmental isolates of Acinetobacter baumannii. Microb. Drug Resist. (Larchmt., N. Y.) 27, 328–336 (2021)."), [101](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR101 "Hubeny, J. et al. Characterization of carbapenem resistance in environmental samples and Acinetobacter spp. isolates from wastewater and river water in Poland. Sci. Total Environ. 822, 153437 (2022)."), [102](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR102 "Serwecińska, L., Kiedrzyńska, E. & Kiedrzyński, M. A catchment-scale assessment of the sanitary condition of treated wastewater and river water based on fecal indicators and carbapenem-resistant Acinetobacter spp. Sci. Total Environ. 750, 142266 (2021)."). However, the IS _Aba_ 1/ _bla_ OXA-51-like genetic configuration observed in carbapenem-resistant _A. baumannii_ from wastewater and river water in Poland by Hubeny et al. [101](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR101 "Hubeny, J. et al. Characterization of carbapenem resistance in environmental samples and Acinetobacter spp. isolates from wastewater and river water in Poland. Sci. Total Environ. 822, 153437 (2022).") has not been observed in any of our environmental and wildlife isolates indicating their still pristine context. Interestingly, the same is true for the compost setting studied here indicating that the source of compost isolates is from nature rather than from the anthropogenic context. Thus, our sample and data collection can serve to represent the baseline of nativeness to study the evolution of lineages towards adaptation to the hospital environment and multidrug resistance. It will also help to decipher the role of livestock farming as a potential accelerant to the development of some lineages. We emphatically support the call to action recently expressed by others to implement a One Health perspective on _A. baumannii_ [103](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR103 "Castillo-Ramírez, S. Zoonotic Acinetobacter baumannii: the need for genomic epidemiology in a One Health context. The Lancet. Microbe                    https://doi.org/10.1016/s2666-5247(22)00255-5                                     (2022)."), [104](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR104 "Hernández-González, I. L. & Castillo-Ramírez, S. Antibiotic-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii is a One Health problem. Lancet Microbe 1, e279 (2020)."), [105](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR105 "Wareth, G., Neubauer, H. & Sprague, L. D. Acinetobacter baumannii - a neglected pathogen in veterinary and environmental health in Germany. Vet. Res. Commun. 43, 1–6 (2019)."). This should not only include pet, livestock and wildlife animals as well as environmental sampling, but also genome-based studying of non-MDR clinical isolates as these presumably only recently entered the hospital setting. Two recent publications describing isolates from various environmental sources show partial overlap with our dataset (STPas 44[106](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR106 "Mateo-Estrada, V. et al. Acinetobacter baumannii from grass: novel but non-resistant clones. Microb. Genom.                    https://doi.org/10.1099/mgen.0.001054                                     (2023)."); STsPas 2250, 2353, 1878[107](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR107 "Sykes, E. M. E. et al. Phylogenomic and phenotypic analyses highlight the diversity of antibiotic resistance and virulence in both human and non-human Acinetobacter baumannii. mSphere 9, e00741–00723 (2024).")) and illustrate the need to intensify One Health studies to identify the major sources and transmission routes.

Reliable isolation of _A. baumannii_ from clinical samples is challenging[108](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR108 "Marchaim, D. et al. Surveillance cultures and duration of carriage of multidrug-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii. J. Clin. Microbiol. 45, 1551–1555 (2007).") and so is from environmental material[109](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR109 "Yusuf, I., Skiebe, E. & Wilharm, G. Evaluation of CHROMagar Acinetobacter and MacConkey media for the recovery of Acinetobacter baumannii from soil samples. Lett. Appl. Microbiol.                    https://doi.org/10.1093/lambio/ovac051                                     (2022)."). Recently, evidence was presented that CHROMagar Acinetobacter might introduce a bias in isolation so that specific lineages might not be represented[109](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR109 "Yusuf, I., Skiebe, E. & Wilharm, G. Evaluation of CHROMagar Acinetobacter and MacConkey media for the recovery of Acinetobacter baumannii from soil samples. Lett. Appl. Microbiol.                    https://doi.org/10.1093/lambio/ovac051                                     (2022)."). Accordingly, alternative enrichment and isolation protocols should be run in parallel to complete our picture. Although we could demonstrate worldwide spread of lineages isolated in Poland and Germany, not all lineages necessarily spread worldwide. Ecology of _A. baumannii_ in other climates and geographic regions may differ considerably. The molecular clock analysis presented is impaired by a weak temporal signal and limited to a single clade. Further sampling and sequencing efforts are required to complement the data in order to better assess the evolutionary rates of _A. baumannii_ lineages in the environment as compared to clinical ones. This will allow us to define the early steps of evolution of the species and its interference with human evolution.

### Sample collection and processing

Agar gel medium transport swabs (COPAN 108 C, HAIN Lifescience, Germany) were used for sampling of white stork nestlings (choana and cloaca), and COPAN 110 C swabs were analogously used to sample dead rodents and shrew (tracheal and rectal sampling). Swabs were immediately transferred to Amies transport medium and stored at 4 °C until direct plating on CHROMagarTM Acinetobacter (CHROMagar, France). CHROMagar Acinetobacter was prepared according to the manufacturer’s description without addition of the CHROMagar MDR supplement CR102. Pellet and soil samples were preincubated in minimal salt medium supplemented with 0.2% acetate for 5 h at 37 °C prior to plating on CHROMagar Acinetobacter. Pellet samples for enrichment cultures were taken from the interior of pellets (~1 g) using sterile tweezers.

The compost was fed with plant remains from the garden, vegetable kitchen waste and egg shells which were deposited within a wooden frame (length/width/height: 0.8 m/0.8 m/0.5 m), the compost regime was as follows: feeding for twelve months beginning in spring (~March), afterwards rest period for 12 months without relocation and without stirring or turning, then spreading into garden, continuous sampling over the complete two years (~10 cm below surface (sampling with metal shovel and disinfection wipes); occasionally, sampling was applied with a boring rod of an effective length of 1 m (Bodenprobentechnik Peters, Germany); samples were taken every 10 cm.

Cat-captured rodents and shrews, deposited dead, were wrapped in aluminum foil or transferred into 50 ml tubes by the instructed collectors using gloves, stored at 4 °C and transported to the lab within 24 h. In a biosafety level 2 laboratory, tracheal and rectal swab samples were taken under the safety cabinet while wearing gloves. Carcasses were inactivated by autoclaving.

### Blood sample collection from white stork nestlings and processing

Two ml of blood was collected from each individual from the basilic vein. Due to the delicate walls of blood vessels in birds, it was decided to use a combination kit consisting of a 20 G intravenous cannula and a 10 ml syringe. The blood from the syringe was then poured into the K2-EDTA tubes to prevent the blood from clotting until laboratory analysis was conducted. Blood sample analysis included: red blood cell (RBC) number, hematocrit (PCV), and hemoglobin content (HGB). To determine the hematocrit, the blood was transferred to the capillaries, without anticoagulant, with a volume of 75 µl. The capillaries were centrifuged for 3 min at 12,000 g. The result was read on a hematocrit reader (MPW)[110](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR110 "Szutowicz, A. & Raszeja-Specht, A. Diagnostyka laboratoryjna TOM I. (Gdański Uniwersytet Medyczny, 2009)."), [111](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR111 "Turgeon, M. L. et al. Clinical hematology: theory and procedures. (Lippincott Williams & Wilkins, 2005)."). Drabkin’s cyanomethemoglobin method[112](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR112 "Drabkin, D. Crystallographic and optical properties of human hemoglobin. a proposal for the standarization of hemoglobin. Am. J. Med. 209, 268–270 (1945).") was used to determine hemoglobin in nestlings. For this purpose, 20 µl of blood was added to a test tube containing 5 ml of Drabkin’s reagent (Stamar), thoroughly shaken and allowed to stand for 20 min. After this time, the tubes were centrifuged at 3000 RPM for 5 min to separate the membranes and cell nuclei of the hemolyzed erythrocytes. Supernatant (hemoglobin bound to Drabkin’s reagent) was poured into polystyrene cuvettes (MedLab products). Then, using a UV-VIS spectrophotometer (λ = 540 nm) (Prove 300, Merck), the absorbance of the solution against cyanomethemoglobin was measured. The RBC count was determined using a Bürker hemocytometer, with Natt and Herrick staining being used because of the presence of nuclei in the erythrocytes[113](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR113 "Natt, M. P. & Herrick, C. A. A new blood diluent for counting the erythrocytes and leucocytes of the chicken. Poult. Sci. 31, 735–738 (1952)."). For this purpose, 20 µl of blood (dilution 1:200) was added to a test tube containing 4 ml of dye. The tubes were stoppered and continuously mixed with a roller mixer for 15 min. After this time, 20 µl of the mixture was placed in the hemocytometer chamber and allowed to stabilize for 2 min. Red blood cells were counted in 80 small squares at 400x magnification (MT5300, Meiji). The number of white blood cells was determined analogously to the number of red blood cells. The only difference was that blood cells were counted from the entire area of the Bürker counting chamber (magnification 400x (MT5300, Meiji)). Mean corpuscular hemoglobin (MCH) and mean corpuscular volume (MCV) was calculated based on RBC number, PCV, and HGB content values[114](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR114 "Campbell, T. W. Avian hematology and cytology. (2nd edn. Iowa State University Press, Ames) (1995).").

### Bacterial species identification

Species determination of isolates recovered from CHROMagar Acinetobacter was based on PCR detection of _bla_ OXA-51-like[115](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR115 "Turton, J. F. et al. Identification of Acinetobacter baumannii by detection of the blaOXA-51-like carbapenemase gene intrinsic to this species. J. Clin. Microbiol. 44, 2974–2976 (2006)."), partial 16S rRNA gene sequencing[116](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR116 "Weisburg, W. G., Barns, S. M., Pelletier, D. A. & Lane, D. J. 16S ribosomal DNA amplification for phylogenetic study. J. Bacteriol. 173, 697–703 (1991)."), and partial _rpoB_ sequencing using primers Ac696F and Ac1598R[117](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR117 "Nemec, A. et al. Acinetobacter beijerinckii sp. nov. and Acinetobacter gyllenbergii sp. nov., haemolytic organisms isolated from humans. Int. J. Syst. Evolut. Microbiol. 59, 118–124 (2009)."). To determine _bla_ OXA-51-like variation the coding region was fully sequenced[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR26 "Zander, E., Nemec, A., Seifert, H. & Higgins, P. G. Association between β-lactamase-encoding bla(OXA-51) variants and DiversiLab rep-PCR-based typing of Acinetobacter baumannii isolates. J. Clin. Microbiol. 50, 1900–1904 (2012)."). New OXA-51 protein variants were assigned and deposited at GenΒank (Suppl. Table [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)).

### Illumina sequencing

Libraries for Illumina short read sequencing were prepared from 1 ng of extracted DNA utilizing the Nextera XT DNA Library Prep Kit according to the manufacturer’s recommendations (Illumina Inc., USA). Sequencing was carried out in paired-end (2 × 300 base pairs) on a MiSeq benchtop instrument. Quality control included the removal of adapter sequences, minor contaminations and short contigs below 700 bp in length through an in-house pipeline. The whole genome shotgun project of 401 isolates has been deposited at GenBank under the BioProject accession PRJNA862736.

### Genomic reconstruction and gene-marker analysis

Short-read DNA fragments were successively utilized to re-construct high-quality genomes of the isolate collection using the de novo SPAdes assembler (v3.11.1)[118](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR118 "Bankevich, A. et al. SPAdes: a new genome assembly algorithm and its applications to single-cell sequencing. J. Comput. Biol. 19, 455–477 (2012)."). To assess the quality of the genome assemblies, wrapperstats.sh of BBMap (v38.22)[119](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR119 "Bushnell, B. in Conference: 9th Annual Genomics of Energy & Environment Meeting, Walnut Creek, CA, March 17-20, 2014 Medium: ED (United States, 2014).") was utilized. Genomic completeness was further assessed using checkM (v1.2.2)[120](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR120 "Parks, D. H., Imelfort, M., Skennerton, C. T., Hugenholtz, P. & Tyson, G. W. CheckM: assessing the quality of microbial genomes recovered from isolates, single cells, and metagenomes. Genome Res 25, 1043–1055 (2015)."). On average, completeness and contamination of the genomes were 99.9% and 0.92%, respectively. The overall completeness of the genomes ranged from 86% to 100%, with contamination detected by checkM ranging from 0% to 37%. Overall, 824 isolates exhibited high completeness ( > 98%) and low contamination ( < 5%), indicating high-quality genomes for further analysis. A full overview of assembly statistics is listed in the supplementary material (Suppl. Data [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM3)). Re-constructed genomes were then subjected to in silico MLST profiling using the mlst tool (v2.23.0) ( [https://github.com/tseemann/mlst](https://github.com/tseemann/mlst)) with both the ‘Oxford’ and ‘Pasteur’ schemes for _A. baumannii_. A total of 246 and 143 novel sequence types were identified for the Oxford and Pasteur MLST schemes respectively. A total of 346 novel isolates containing one or more novel allelic sequences were submitted to the isolate database of PubMLST ( [https://pubmlst.org/](https://pubmlst.org/)). Additional AMR gene profiling was conducted through the AMRFinderPlus pipeline (v3.11.26) ( [https://github.com/ncbi/amr](https://github.com/ncbi/amr)), utilizing the NCBI Antimicrobial Resistance Library for AMR (dated 2023-07-05) with default values of 80% for identity and coverage, respectively. Next, IS element characterization was performed via the ABRicate software (v1.0.1) ( [https://github.com/tseemann/abricate](https://github.com/tseemann/abricate)), utilizing a custom database based on the ISfinder collection[121](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR121 "Siguier, P., Perochon, J., Lestrade, L., Mahillon, J. & Chandler, M. ISfinder: the reference centre for bacterial insertion sequences. Nucleic Acids Res 34, D32–D36 (2006).") (dated 2023-07-05) with 90% identity and coverage. Finally, novel _bla_ ADC variants and the continuity of the _com_ M gene were further investigated through a BLAST-based custom Python script. A total of 32 novel _bla_ ADC variants were identified and subsequently submitted to NCBI.

### Functional Annotation

Genome assemblies were annotated using Bakta (v1.9.4)[122](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR122 "Schwengers, O. et al. Bakta: rapid and standardized annotation of bacterial genomes via alignment-free sequence identification. Microb. Genom.                    https://doi.org/10.1099/mgen.0.000685                                     (2021).") with default settings. The analysis included automated prediction of coding sequences (CDS), transfer RNA (tRNA), ribosomal RNA (rRNA), and pseudogenes. Functional annotations were derived from the UniProtKB and RefSeq database, as integrated within the Bakta pipeline. The CDS were extracted from the gbff output and translated using gbseqextractor (v0.0.5)[123](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR123 "Meng, G., Li, Y., Yang, C. & Liu, S. MitoZ: a toolkit for animal mitochondrial genome assembly, annotation and visualization. Nucleic Acids Res. 47, e63–e63 (2019).") which was then used as input for eggNOG-mapper (v2.1.12) ( [https://github.com/eggnogdb/eggnog-mapper](https://github.com/eggnogdb/eggnog-mapper)) for functional prediction against the eggNOG DB (v5.0.2). We then compared the identified counts of COG categories as defined by eggNOG associated with genes per sample between environmental and clinical isolates (Fig. [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#Fig6) and Suppl. Fig. [22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). Hereby, 91 out of 240 clinical samples were randomly subsampled to match the amount of environmental samples evaluated within this study. One-sided Wilcoxon tests were performed on each category to assess statistical differences between both groups.

### Phylogenetic characterization

Open reading frames (ORFs) predicted by Prokka (v1.13)[124](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR124 "Seemann, T. Prokka: rapid prokaryotic genome annotation. Bioinformatics 30, 2068–2069 (2014).") were subsequently used as input for Roary (v3.12.0)[125](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR125 "Page, A. J. et al. Roary: rapid large-scale prokaryote pan genome analysis. Bioinformatics 31, 3691–3693 (2015).") in order to conduct pan-genome analyses. Computed core genes were subsequently extracted, aligned and concatenated using default settings. The resulting alignment was then utilized to calculate a maximum likelihood-based phylogeny with RAxML (v.8.2.10)[126](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR126 "Stamatakis, A. RAxML version 8: a tool for phylogenetic analysis and post-analysis of large phylogenies. Bioinformatics 30, 1312–1313 (2014).") using 100 bootstraps under the assumption of the GTR-gamma DNA substitution model. ClonalFrameML (v1.11)[127](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR127 "Didelot, X. & Wilson, D. J. ClonalFrameML: efficient inference of recombination in whole bacterial genomes. PLoS Comput Biol. 11, e1004041 (2015).") was then used to correct for recombination events and phylogenetic groups were identified through Bayesian Analysis of Population Structure (BAPS). Here, we utilized BAPS with hierarchical clustering as implemented in the R package RhierBAPS (v1.0.1)[128](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR128 "Tonkin-Hill, G., Lees, J. A., Bentley, S. D., Frost, S. D. W. & Corander, J. RhierBAPS: an R implementation of the population clustering algorithm hierBAPS. Wellcome Open Res 3, 93 (2018)."). Grouping of the accessory genome was further assessed via t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE), in order to cluster the data through a range of values for perplexity ( _p_ = 5,10,20,50), the results of which were visualized using the microreact platform[129](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR129 "Argimón, S. et al. Microreact: visualizing and sharing data for genomic epidemiology and phylogeography. Microb. genomics 2, e000093 (2016)."). SNP distances between individual samples were computed through the snippy pipeline (v4.6.0) ( [https://github.com/tseemann/snippy](https://github.com/tseemann/snippy)).

### Temporal signal analysis

Temporal signal analysis was utilized to evaluate whether the samples evolve at a specific rate over an evolutionary time period. To assess this, a root-to-tip regression of genetic divergence was performed for the OXA-126 group against their sampling dates, using the previously generated maximum likelihood tree with a GTR-gamma DNA substitution model within TempEst (v1.5.3)[130](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR130 "Rambaut, A., Lam, T. T., Max Carvalho, L. & Pybus, O. G. Exploring the temporal structure of heterochronous sequences using TempEst (formerly Path-O-Gen). Virus Evol.                    https://doi.org/10.1093/ve/vew007                                     (2016)."). The best fitting tree contained the outgroup _A. baumannii_ 14-9D1. Upon the removal of visible outliers, the correlation coefficient was identified to be 0.25, with an R2 value of 0.0655, indicating an overall weak temporal signal. To compensate for this, the following molecular clock analysis was based heavily on previously defined parameters for IC-1[41](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR41 "Holt, K. et al. Five decades of genome evolution in the globally distributed, extensively antibiotic-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii global clone 1. Microb. Genom. 2, e000052 (2016).").

### Molecular clock analysis

In order to assess the molecular clock of _A. baumannii_, a xml file was configured using BEAUTi and subsequently utilized for BEAST (v2.5.0)[131](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR131 "Bouckaert, R. et al. BEAST 2.5: An advanced software platform for Bayesian evolutionary analysis. PLOS Comput. Biol. 15, e1006650 (2019).") analysis. BEAST was run on the OXA-126 group, containing detailed sampling dates of 30 isolates. The run was allowed to continue for 50 million iterations, sampling from the posterior every 1000st iteration. We inferred the temporal phylogeny for OXA-126 under a strict clock setting (i.e., normally distributed rate variation over the tree). The strict clock was set to utilize a log-normal prior on the clock rate, with a mean of -5 and standard deviation (sd) of 1.25[41](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR41 "Holt, K. et al. Five decades of genome evolution in the globally distributed, extensively antibiotic-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii global clone 1. Microb. Genom. 2, e000052 (2016).").

### Isolation of fungi and interaction studies with _A. baumannii_

Fungal isolates were obtained from environmental samples previously tested positive for _A. baumannii_ using the following protocol: Samples (e.g., 1 g of soil) were resuspended in 10 ml of minimal salt medium supplemented with 0.2% acetate (see above) and 100 µl suspension before being spread on Kimmig fungal agar ([132](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR132 "Kimmig, J. & Rieth, H. Antimykotika in experiment und Klinik. Arzneimittelforschung 3, 267–276 (1953)."), Becton Dickinson, Heidelberg, Germany) supplemented with 80 mg/l chloramphenicol (Kimmig agar). After 2-3 days of incubation at 27 °C, fungal colonies were selected and spread on Kimmig agar several times until considered pure cultures after macroscopic and microscopic inspection. The absence of bacterial contamination was verified via colony PCR using global 16S rRNA gene primers[116](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR116 "Weisburg, W. G., Barns, S. M., Pelletier, D. A. & Lane, D. J. 16S ribosomal DNA amplification for phylogenetic study. J. Bacteriol. 173, 697–703 (1991)."). Provisional species determination of fungal isolates was performed by sequencing of the rRNA genes and ITS (internal transcribed spacer)[133](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR133 "Bellemain, E. et al. ITS as an environmental DNA barcode for fungi: an in silico approach reveals potential PCR biases. BMC Microbiol. 10, 189 (2010)."), [134](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR134 "Toju, H., Tanabe, A. S., Yamamoto, S. & Sato, H. High-coverage ITS primers for the DNA-based identification of ascomycetes and basidiomycetes in environmental samples. PloS one 7, e40863 (2012)."). _Aspergillus_ spores were harvested after growth of fungi on Kimmig agar for 3 days at 34 °C. To this end, the plate was flooded with 5 ml of sterile phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) supplemented with 0.1% Triton X-100, followed by soft panning. Subsequently, the suspension was removed with a pipette while avoiding contact with the fungal colonies, aliquoted and frozen at -80 °C until further use. For seeding into 24-well plates, frozen spore suspensions were thawed, diluted by a factor of 50 into LB medium and 300 µl deposited into each well. The _A. baumannii_ strains were cultured in LB medium overnight at 37 °C, diluted 1:50 into fresh medium and further cultured until an optical density (OD) 600 nm of 1 was reached. For the spore adhesion assay, the bacteria were diluted 1:200 into LB medium and 300 µl were added to the spore seeding of a well. After 2-4 h at 37 °C, the medium was removed from the wells and replaced twice with fresh medium to filter any non-adherent bacteria before inverted microscopy was performed. For the spore germination inhibition assay, bacterial suspensions with an OD 600 nm of 1 generated as above were diluted 1:2000 into LB medium, of which 300 µl were added to the spore seeding of a well. Microscopic evaluation of spore germination inhibition was conducted after 5–7 h at 37 °C. For scanning electron microscopy (SEM), ThermanoxTM coverslips (ThermoFisher Scientific) were placed in 6-well plates, the incubation volume was reduced to 300 µl with seeding and incubation as above at 37 °C and fixed after 2 h in a solution of 2.5% glutaraldehyde, 1.0% paraformaldehyde in 50 mM HEPES buffer for 24 hrs. All samples were then washed in 50 mM HEPES, dehydrated in 30, 50, 70, 90, 95, 100% ethanol, critical point dried, mounted on aluminum stubs, sputter coated with a 10 nm layer of gold-palladium and finally examined in the SEM (ZEISS 1530 Gemini, Carl Zeiss Microscopy GmbH, Germany) operating at 3 kV using the in-lens electron detector.

### GIS-based analyses

All spatial data were analyzed using geographic information system (GIS) tools (Quantum GIS Software, 2010). Feeding grounds for all white stork nests were set as an area around the nest with a 3.5 and 4 km radius, respectively[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR27 "Böhning-Gaese, K. Zur Nahrungsökologie des Weißstorchs (Ciconia ciconia) in Oberschwaben: Beobachtungen an zwei Paaren. J. für Ornithologie 133, 61–71 (1992)."). Habitat structures of all feeding grounds were determined using CORINE Land Cover 2012 types, including arable land, pastures, heterogeneous agricultural areas, forests, total area of water reservoirs and total length of rivers.

A total of 295 nests were analysed (50 from the southern part of Lubsz voivodeship near Zielona Góra city, hereafter Zielona Góra; 95 from the southern part of Greater Poland voivodeship near Leszno city, hereafter Leszno; 150 from Opole voivodeship near Opole city, hereafter Opole). From these 295 nests during breeding seasons 2015–2018, we analysed 454 broods (mean number of broods per nest was 1.5). A total number of 1080 chicks was analysed. First, we tested for spatial autocorrelation with Moran’s local indicator[135](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR135 "Legendre, P. & Legendre, L. Numerical ecology. (Elsevier, New York. (Elsevier, Ed.). New York, 1998)."). As dataset we used geographic position of 295 nests and whether we isolated or not _A. baumannii_ in nests during four years of study. We found no spatial autocorrelation (Supplementary Table [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1), Suppl. Fig. [23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1)). The spatial autocorrelation was computed using SAM software[136](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR136 "Rangel, T. F., Diniz-Filho, J. A. F. & Bini, L. M. SAM: a comprehensive application for spatial analysis in macroecology. Ecography 33, 46–50 (2010).").

For each nest we obtained several environmental variables in a radius of 3.5 km and 4.0 km around the nest. We determined the landscape variable according to Corine land cover classification[137](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR137 "Agency, E.-E. E. Corine land cover,                    https://www.eea.europa.eu/en/datahub/                                     (2006).") as proportion to total buffer area: artificial surface (CLC\_1), arable land (CLC\_2.1), pastures (CLC\_2.3), heterogeneous agricultural area (CLC\_2.4), forests (CLC\_3.1), water reservoirs area (CLC\_5.1.2) and water courses length (CLC\_5.1.1). The GIS data were processed by Quantum GIS Software[138](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR138 "Team, Q. G. D. Quantum GIS geographic information system. Open Source Geospatial Foundation Project. (2012)."). Before analysis, the environmental variables were log-transformed in order to minimize effects of detached observations and skewed distributions.

We checked whether there is multicollinearity between those environmental variables by using variance inflation factor. We found that there is collinearity between arable land and forest areas. There is strong negative correlation between them (Pearson correlation for radius 3.5 km: −0.72, _p_ < 0.001 and for radius 4.0 km: −0.75, _p_ < 0.001), so we excluded forest variable from the analysis. It should be noted when interpreting the results that high area of arable land indicates low area of forest.

Precipitation data were obtained from the Institute of Meteorology and Water Management National Research Institute, Warsaw, Poland. Yearly sum of rainfall for each nest was calculated based on assignment to the closest meteorological station within a 3.5 km radius of the nest.

We compared the environmental variables within 3.5 km buffers around nests. We then compared regions using Kruskal-Wallis tests with results shown in Supplementary Table [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#MOESM1). Zielona Góra and Leszno had significantly more extensive agricultural use (mostly pasture) than Opole. On the other hand, Opole had the highest proportion of arable area, followed by Leszno and Zielona Góra. The proportion of forests was highest in buffers around nests in Zielona Góra.

Our dependent variable was the proportion of the number of positive chicks to total number of chicks in the broods. We used a generalized linear model with a binomial error distribution. Because the broods measurements were repeated in different years we used a mixed model with nest ID and year of study as random factors of the study. As independent variable we used above mentioned environmental variables, precipitation and mean age of fledglings in the broods (age was estimated according to size of beak) and region (as factor variable with three levels: Zielona Góra, Leszno, Opole). We started from the global models with the interaction of region factor with all other environmental covariates. The final model was selected using backward stepwise removal of nonsignificant effects of interactions ( _P_ < 0.05)[139](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR139 "Zuur, A. F., Ieno, E. N., Walker, N. J., Saveliev, A. A. & Smith, G. M. Mixed effects models and extensions in ecology with R. Vol. 574 (Springer, 2009).").

For the statistical analyses we used R software[140](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR140 "Team, R. C. R: A language and environment for statistical computing. R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria.                    http://www<span type=\"Italic\" name=\"Emphasis\" class=\"Italic\">.R-project.org/                                     (2016).") and for mixed modeling approach we used lme4 package[141](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR141 "Bates, D. lme4: Linear mixed-effects models using Eigen and S4. R. Package Version 1, 1 (2016)."). Graphics were made using the packages ggplot2 v. 3.4.4[142](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR142 "Wickham, H. ggplot2 - Elegant Graphics for Data Analysis. Springer Cham (2016).") and ggeffects v. 1.3.1[143](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-70072-4#ref-CR143 "Lüdecke, D. ggeffects: Tidy data frames of marginal effects from regression models. J. Open Source Softw. 3, 772 (2018).").
