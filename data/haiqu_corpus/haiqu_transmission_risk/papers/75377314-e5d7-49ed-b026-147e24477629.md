## Abstract

Successful replication within an infected host and successful transmission between hosts are key to the continued spread of most pathogens. Competing selection pressures exerted at these different scales can lead to evolutionary trade-offs between the determinants of fitness within and between hosts. Here, we examine such a trade-off in the context of influenza A viruses and the differential pressures exerted by temperature-dependent virus persistence. For a panel of avian influenza A virus strains, we find evidence for a trade-off between the persistence at high versus low temperatures. Combining a within-host model of influenza infection dynamics with a between-host transmission model, we study how such a trade-off affects virus fitness on the host population level. We show that conclusions regarding overall fitness are affected by the type of link assumed between the within- and between-host levels and the main route of transmission (direct or environmental). The relative importance of virulence and immune response mediated virus clearance are also found to influence the fitness impacts of virus persistence at low versus high temperatures. Based on our results, we predict that if transmission occurs mainly directly and scales linearly with virus load, and virulence or immune responses are negligible, the evolutionary pressure for influenza viruses to evolve toward good persistence at high within-host temperatures dominates. For all other scenarios, influenza viruses with good environmental persistence at low temperatures seem to be favored.

## Author Summary

It has recently been suggested that for avian influenza viruses, prolonged persistence in the environment plays an important role in the transmission between birds. In such situations, influenza virus strains may face a trade-off: they need to persist well in the environment at low temperatures, but they also need to do well inside an infected bird at higher temperatures. Here, we analyze how potential trade-offs on these two scales interact to determine overall fitness of the virus. We find that the link between infection dynamics within a host and virus shedding and transmission is crucial in determining the relative advantage of good low-temperature versus high-temperature persistence. We also find that the role of virus-induced mortality, the immune response and the route of transmission affect the balance between optimal low-temperature and high-temperature persistence.

## Introduction

Influenza A viruses infect both humans and animals, causing frequent outbreaks [\[1\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Cox1), [\[2\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Nicholson1). In humans, the infection can be life-threatening for individuals with weak immune systems, leading to an estimated annual worldwide mortality burden of ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e001)[\[3\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Taubenberger1), [\[4\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Thompson1). Due to its zoonotic nature, and frequent spillover from wild and livestock populations, eradication of the virus is virtually impossible [\[1\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Cox1), [\[5\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Webster1). Further, the danger that a novel influenza strain with high virulence and pandemic potential will start to spread in the human population is always present [\[6\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Horimoto1)– [\[8\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Palese1). The 2009 H1N1 pandemic demonstrated that the emergence of novel pandemic strains is still largely unpredictable. Improvement of our surveillance, prediction and control capabilities requires that we obtain a better understanding of the whole transmission cycle of the virus and the mechanisms governing the complex processes of infection and spread.

One useful approach for studying the whole infection and transmission process is through the use of multiscale studies, wich have seen increased general development and use in recent years (see e.g. [\[9\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Mideo1), [\[10\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Alizon1) for reviews and [\[11\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Reperant1) for a recent application to influenza). A multiscale approach allows one to address the question of how different selection pressures on the within- and between-host levels interact to impact overall fitness. This is important if we want to better understand and predict the infection and transmission dynamics and evolution of the virus.

Here, we use such a multiscale framework and focus on one specific aspect, namely evolutionary pressures shaped by temperature-dependent virus persistence. The importance of temperature on influenza virus fitness is well established. For instance, the attenuated live influenza vaccine is cold-adapted, which leads to reduced fitness in human hosts, making it safe for vaccination purposes [\[12\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Belshe1), [\[13\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Cox2). Temperature has also been shown to impact within-host dynamics and transmission in laboratory studies [\[14\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Lowen1), [\[15\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Scull1). Recent theoretical and experimental evidence suggests that persistence in the environment is an important factor of transmission for avian influenza [\[16\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Breban1)– [\[24\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Stallknecht1). Transmission through an environmental stage (e.g. long-lasting droplets, fomites) seems to also play a role for influenza transmission in humans [\[25\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Atkinson1)– [\[29\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Tang1). Since temperatures in the environment and within a host can be markedly different, it is possible that the virus faces a trade-off: It can either optimize its ability to persist within a host, or optimize its ability to persist outside a host. It is well known that the decay rate of most viruses depends on temperature, with faster virion decay occurring at higher temperature [\[30\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Stallknecht2)– [\[32\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Brown1). Interestingly, recent data [\[33\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Brown2) suggest that temperature-dependent decay rates differ between influenza strains. Some strains are very stable at environmental temperatures (![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e002)) but rapidly decay at higher within-host temperatures (![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e003)), while others persist less well at low temperatures but also have a less rapid decay as temperature increases [\[33\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Brown2). These data suggest that some virus strains might optimize persistence within a host, while others might optimize persistence outside a host, with a possible trade-off between the two. This in turn can affect both within-host and between-host dynamics. The dynamics on these two levels interact to determine overall fitness. (Note that the data presented in [\[33\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Brown2) – which we will analyze below – is for different HA-NA serotypes. However, the phenomenon of temperature-dependent decay we discuss is not specific to distinct serotypes. We will therefore use the generic term “strain” throughout this study).

To analyze the impact that such a temperature-dependent trade-off can have on virus fitness, we build a multi-scale model that embeds a within-host infection process within a population transmission framework. A number of theoretical studies have previously considered trade-offs between environmental persistence and within-host performance, see e.g. [\[34\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Bonhoeffer1)– [\[38\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Caraco1). Those studies considered generic trade-offs and models without direct relation to a specific pathogen or fitting to data. A few notable studies that involved data looked at environmental survival and virulence of human pathogens [\[39\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Walther1) and environmental survival and growth in phages [\[40\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Paepe1). Here, we focus on avian influenza A and combine experimental data with models to explicitly consider temperature-dependent virus decay as the mediator of trade-offs. We find that for direct transmission scenarios, viruses with long within-host persistence perform overall best. For environmental transmission scenarios, the balance was shifted toward viruses with good environmental persistence. This was especially true if shedding or infection rates were assumed to be proportional to the logarithm of the virus load. We further show that the addition of an immune response or pathogen virulence reduced the importance of differences in the within-host decay rate between strains, and lead to an increased importance of good environmental persistence.

This simple model can describe most data for influenza virus infections rather well [\[41\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Smith1), [\[42\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Beauchemin1). After an initial rise in virus load, uninfected target cells become depleted, leading to a subsequent virus decline and resolution of the infection. This so-called target-cell limited model is basically equivalent to a simple epidemic model, which produces a single infectious disease outbreak in a susceptible population. However, it is also known that influenza infections stimulate an immune response, which likely plays some role in viral clearance, though the exact contributions of various components of the immune response to virus clearance are still not fully understood. We consider an alternative model with an immune response in the supplementary materials.

### The between-host model

To describe influenza transmission dynamics on the between-host level, we use a framework that takes into account both direct and environmental transmission routes, as has been recently advocated [\[16\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Breban1), [\[17\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Rohani1). Similar models – not specific to influenza – that explicitly include an environmental stage have been designed and analyzed previously [\[35\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Gandon1), [\[36\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Day1), [\[38\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Caraco1), [\[43\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Bonhoeffer2)– [\[46\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Li1).

### Defining fitness and connecting the two scales

Our main quantity of interest is fitness of the virus at the host population level. One way to quantify fitness is through the basic reproductive number, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e060), which is defined as the expected number of new infections caused by one infected host in a fully susceptible population [\[47\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Anderson1)– [\[49\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Heffernan1). For our model, one can split ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e061) into two components, namely direct transmission from host to host (![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e062)), and indirect transmission through the environmental route (![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e063)), such that ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e064)[\[17\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Rohani1), [\[36\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Day1). For direct transmission, we have![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e065)(8)where ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e066) is the susceptible population at time 0, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e067) is fraction of hosts that are still infectious at time ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e068) after infection started, and ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e069) denotes the rate at which an infectious individual at infection age ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e070) infects new individuals. If we assume that all infected hosts are infectious for a fixed duration, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e071), and non-infectious afterwards, we can write![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e072)(9)Mathematically, this corresponds to choosing the proportion of host infectious after time ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e073), ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e074), as a Heaviside function, and the recovery rate, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e075), in the between-host model equations as a Dirac delta-function. While the infectious period could end either due to resolution of the infection (recovery) or host death, for the low pathogenic influenza strains we consider here, mortality is negligible [\[50\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Alexander1)– [\[52\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Majumdar1). Therefore, for the main part of this study, the end of the infectious period should be interpreted biologically as recovery. In the supplementary materials we briefly consider virus-associated mortality (i.e. virulence) and how it might alter the results presented in the main part of the manuscript.

We can define the duration of infectiousness ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e076) in terms of the within-host model, as the time from the start until the end of the infection, which we define as the time virus levels drop below a given level, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e077) (in our simulations chosen to be one virion). ## Results

Both the within- and between-host models contain a term for virus decay, namely ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e117) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e118). It is obvious that to maximize fitness, the virus should minimize both ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e119) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e120), i.e. it should be able to persist well both outside the host (in the air or on fomites for humans, in water for avian species) and inside the host. However, as we show below, there seem to be trade-offs between the ability to persist at low versus high temperatures. While higher temperatures lead to faster decay for all strains, some strains are better at persisting in the environment at low temperatures (low ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e121)), but this comes at the cost of rapid decay inside a host at higher temperatures (high ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e122)). In contrast, other strains seem to persist less well at low temperatures, but as temperature increases, their decay rate increases less rapidly, making them more stable at higher temperatures. Given this potential trade-off between ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e123) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e124), we analyze how within- and between-host levels interact to determine overall virus fitness on the host population level as measured by ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e125). Within-host decay, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e126), affects within-host viral dynamics and thereby, through the link-functions ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e127), both the direct and environmental fitness components ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e128) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e129) ( [equations 13](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989.e086) and [18](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989.e108)). The between-host decay term, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e130), only affects the environmental fitness component, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e131). Therefore, we expect that depending on transmission route and link functions, the impact of good low- versus high-temperature persistence on fitness can change. We will show how this plays out in the following.

The analysis of this dataset can be taken as suggestion for the presence of a trade-off between stability at low and high temperatures – at least for the panel of strains we investigated here. Since this is a small sample of strains, we do not want to over-emphasize the finding. However it seemed real and interesting enough to ask the questio: “How would such a potential trade-off lead to interactions on the within-host and between-host levels and affect overall virus fitness?”. We address this question in the remainder of the paper.

As a potentially interesting side question – not further considered in the remainder of this paper – we wondered whether there are systematic differences between strains belonging to different groups. Based on amino acid differences, strains with different HA types can be clustered into two groups, as indicated in [Table 4](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi-1002989-t004) (see e.g. [\[66\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Throsby1)– [\[68\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Corti1)). We were curious to see if systematic differences in the decay behavior between the two groups could be observed. However, statistical tests applied to both the absolute and rank-transformed values of ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e165) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e166) did not identify significant differences between groups, suggesting that – based on the available data – differences in HA sequences between the two groups do not express themselves phenotypically as differences in temperature-dependent decay characteristics.

### Fitting the within-host model

To simulate a within-host infection, we need to specify parameter values for the within-host model. While parameter estimates are available for influenza infections in humans and mice [\[41\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Smith1), [\[42\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Beauchemin1), they have not been previously estimated for ducks. We therefore fitted the model to recent data from influenza infections with H3N8 in mallards ( _Anas platyrhynchos_) [\[69\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Brown3). This virus strain was not used in the decay experiments shown in [table 4](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi-1002989-t004), therefore, we do not have a direct estimate for the within-host clearance rate, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e167). The straightforward approach would be to obtain ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e168) together with the other parameters by fitting to the data, but this approach is problematic. As has been shown previously, it is impossible to use the within-host model ( [equations 1](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989.e011)– [3](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989.e013)) to accurately estimate both ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e169) and death rate of infected cells, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e170), from virus titer data alone [\[42\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Beauchemin1), . Because of this, we instead set ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e171) per day, which is the mean value of ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e172) for the 12 strains reported in [table 4](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi-1002989-t004). We also tried to fit ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e173), and as expected, the fit did not improve and ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e174) could not be properly estimated. To perform the fit, we assume that the infection was started by a ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e175) (![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e176) is the viral dose that results in a 50% chance of infecting an embryonated egg, assumed to correspond to 1 infectious virion) and that the initial number of uninfected target cells is ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e177)[\[71\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Uni1) (while this estimate is for chickens rather than ducks, the exact value is not qualitatively important: changes in the target cell numbers only re-scale the model parameter ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e178) and otherwise produce the same dynamics). In [figure 5](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi-1002989-g005), we show the best fit to the data, with parameter values presented in [Table 1](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi-1002989-t001). We want to point out that while these parameter estimates are useful and accurate enough for the purpose of our study, they come with caveats. Most importantly, estimates are based on the validity of the model used. A model that does not include an immune response is likely an over-simplification, albeit a necessary one since adding additional immune response components and trying to fit such a model to virus load data only would lead to over-fitting. See e.g. [\[41\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Smith1), [\[42\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Beauchemin1) and references therein for further discussions of this and related points concerning fitting influenza data to models.

### Determining between-host fitness

For each strain listed in [table 4](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi-1002989-t004), we can use ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e181) and the parameters determined in the previous section and simulate the within-host infection dynamics. This allows us to numerically determine the duration of infection, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e182), and the total virus load, which in turn specifies the different link functions, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e183). We also have estimates for ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e184) for each strain. To determine fitness as measured by ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e185), we also need to know the population size ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e186), and several constants of proportionality, namely ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e187) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e188) describing the linkage between within-host virus load and shedding and infection rates, and the environmental transmission rate, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e189). Those quantities are not well known and will likely differ for different environments. Therefore, absolute values of ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e190) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e191) are hard to estimate. However, for any strain ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e192) we can consider its fitness relative to some reference strain, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e193). If we make the assumption that for a given scenario, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e194), ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e195), ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e196) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e197) do not differ between strains; and consider the two extreme cases of either only direct (![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e198)) or only environmental (![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e199)) transmission, relative fitness for strain ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e200) and link-function ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e201) (![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e202)), relative to some reference strain, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e203), is given by![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e204)(19)for direct transmission and![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e205)(20)for indirect, environmental transmission. As expected, if we consider only direct transmission ( [equation 19](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989.e204)), the ability of the virus to persist at low temperatures (low ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e206)) does not impact its fitness and therefore the strain that optimizes persistence at high temperatures (low ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e207)) and therefore optimizes within-host dynamics (large ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e208)) performs best. In the presence of environmental transmission ( [equation 20](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989.e205)), fitness is influenced by persistence both inside the host (low ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e209), leading to high ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e210)) and in the environment (low ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e211)).

The two cases, environmental only and direct only transmission represent extremes in terms of potential trade-offs. For direct transmission alone, there is no trade-off; optimizing within-host fitness is always the best strategy. The environmental transmission only scenario represents the case where the importance of the environmental stage is as large as it can possibly be. Mixture of the two transmission routes leads to values with intermediate importance of environmental persistence. While it is certainly possible to consider the general case with both direct and environmental transmission and compute absolute and relative fitness values, this would require making rather arbitrary assumptions about values for some of the unknown parameters of proportionality. Since considering such a general mixed transmission scenario would not add much beyond the results for the two simpler extreme cases, we focus on these two extreme cases in the following.

In [figure 6](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi-1002989-g006), we show the relative fitness of the 12 different strains, for exclusively direct or environmental transmission scenarios. We plot relative fitness for the three different link-functions between within-host virus load and transmission/shedding described above (![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e212), ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e213) and ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e214) given by [equations (12)](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989.e085), [(15)](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989.e089) and [(16)](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989.e095)). Strains are sorted according to within-host performance (i.e. with increasing values of ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e215)). We arbitrarily chose H1N1 as the reference strain, which therefore always has a fitness of 1. As expected, for direct transmission ( [figure 6A](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi-1002989-g006)), better within host persistence at high temperatures leads overall to higher fitness. Results differ little between the link function based on the simple linear assumption, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e216), and the additional inclusion of total discharge, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e217). However, assuming that the amount of shedding is proportional to the logarithm of virus load, ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e218), reduces the relative importance of within-host dynamics. Put another way, since ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e219) “counts” ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e220) instead of ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e221), the fitness impacts of differences in within-host virus load between strains are diminished and, consequently, the relative fitness advantage of strains with high within-host persistence is reduced. This therefore increases the relative fitness of the strains with high ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e222). In fact, for the three strains with the lowest within-host fitness (H5N2, H11N6 and H6N4), the somewhat reduced within-host fitness due to higher ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e223) leads to lower virus load but a longer duration of infection, and because virus load factors into shedding only in a logarithmic fashion, a longer duration of infection leads to a slightly increased fitness despite higher ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e224). See also the next section for another appearance of this phenomenon. Note that it is unclear how biologically reasonable sustained within-host virus load (i.e. a long duration of infection) is. In most immunocompentent hosts, the immune response usually clears influenza relatively rapidly [\[72\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Baccam1)– [\[77\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Pawelek1). In the supplementary materials, we investigate an extended within-host model which includes an antibody-mediated immune response.

For this strain and link function ![](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article/file?type=thumbnail&id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989.e230), the reduction in relative importance of the high-temperature within-host dynamics compared to the low-temperature between-host persistence strongly increases this strain's relative fitness (see top left corner of [figure 6B](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi-1002989-g006)).

## Discussion

Trade-offs between different traits or phenotypes acting at different scales are likely common and have been explored previously (see e.g. [\[9\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Mideo1), [\[10\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Alizon1), [\[40\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Paepe1), [\[53\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Lange1), [\[58\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Handel2), [\[78\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Coombs1)– [\[88\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Volkov1) for some recent work). In this study, we focused on trade-offs in temperature-dependent virus decay and analyzed how the interaction of within- and between-host scales determines overall fitness. Taking a panel of influenza A strains, we found evidence that a trade-off exists between the ability to persist at low temperatures versus high temperatures. Of course, the negative correlation found in the data should by no means be taken as proof of the existence of such a trade-off. Further, more detailed studies are needed to investigate this potential trade-off more carefully. If the finding holds up, it would also be very interesting to elucidate the mechanism responsible for this trade-off. As it currently stands, we consider the observed pattern as an interesting suggestion that made it worthwhile to investigate how – given such a trade-off – the within-host and between-host scales interact to impact overall fitness. By linking the within-host dynamics to the population level, we were able to estimate population level-fitness as measured by the reproductive number for both the cases where transmission is through direct contact between birds and where transmission occurs through an environmental stage (i.e. virus persistence in water). We found that if direct transmission is dominant, viruses that persist well at high temperatures and therefore perform well within a host also had the best between-host fitness. This trend was most pronounced if transmission or shedding was directly proportional to the total within-host virus load. For the environmental transmission scenario, the balance was somewhat shifted toward viruses with good environmental, low-temperature, persistence. This was especially true if shedding and infection rate were assumed to be proportional to the logarithm of the virus load.

In the supplementary materials, we also explored the impacts of taking into account an immune response. We found a somewhat diminished importance of differences in the between-host decay rate between strains. This, in turn, leads to greater emphasis on the fitness contribution of environmental persistence. Along similar lines, a brief analysis of a model including virulence suggests that if high within-host fitness leads to host death and thereby interruption of transmission, the balance would be further tipped toward strains that have good environmental persistence.

Both more detailed within-host models including further aspects of the immune response and more detailed virulence models are worthwhile avenues for further studies. So are models with more detailed links of the within-host and between-host scales. However, to go beyond qualitative results, the right kind of data would need to be available to allow proper specification and parameterization of such more complex models.

In addition, it will be worthwhile to follow up with studies that look at virus fitness beyond the reproductive number. Specifically, given the epidemic behavior of influenza, a model that would explicitly simulate multiple rounds of seasonal between-host outbreaks (along the lines of [\[16\]](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1002989#pcbi.1002989-Breban1)) and track persistence and extinction of strains with different temperature-dependent persistence strategies might be insightful. Similarly, a more detailed model of environmental persistence, e.g. through inclusion of seasonal variation and other dynamical features, and its effect on fitness as measured by the reproductive number or some other suitable quantity might be of interest.

Another fruitful topic for future studies is to investigate additional potential trade-offs. It is known that temperature has an effect on other phenotypes, such as virus binding efficiency or the performance of polymerase. This could be included in a model by making other model parameters temperature-dependent. Provided the right kind of data were available, one could then study how temperature impacts these additional parameters and thereby overall fitness.

In summary, our results show that differences in fitness can at times be substantial and strongly depend on transmission route and how within-host and between-host models are linked. Based on our findings, we predict that if shedding and infection rates are proportional to virus load, virulence is negligible, and within-host virus clearance is primarily determined by temperature-dependent virus decay, there is strong evolutionary pressure for influenza viruses to increase persistence at high temperatures. Conversely, if virus shedding and direct transmission rates scale with the logarithm of virus load, if virulence plays an important role, or if within-host virus clearance is essentially via the immune response or other non-temperature dependent mechanisms, influenza viruses with good environmental persistence at low temperatures should be favored.
