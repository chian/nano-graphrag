Adaptive resource aware and privacy preserving federated edge learning framework for real time internet of medical things applications


[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w.pdf)

[Download PDF](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w.pdf)

## Abstract

The Internet of Medical Things requires frameworks that ensure secure processing, computational efficiency, and scalability for continuous healthcare data streams. Existing solutions remain limited in their ability to support real-time anomaly detection, resource-constrained optimization, and multi-tier aggregation in large-scale deployments. This work proposes an Adaptive Federated Edge Learning framework specifically designed for healthcare systems enabled by the Internet of Medical Things. The framework incorporates Adaptive Modular Learning Units that allocate computations according to device-specific resource budgets and update models through federated gradient descent. Dynamic Data Encoding transforms heterogeneous medical data into a unified and decorrelated feature representation with alignment and quality weighting to preserve consistency across distributed sources. A Hierarchical Federated Aggregation mechanism performs device, regional, and global parameter updates using data-size weighting and delay-aware factors to maintain training stability and scalability in heterogeneous network environments. Privacy-Preserving Secure Enclaves provide encrypted model training and aggregation with differential privacy noise injection, ensuring that sensitive clinical data remain protected throughout the learning cycle. Real-time anomaly detection is achieved through a streaming pipeline based on sliding windows, dimensionality reduction, covariance analysis, and adaptive thresholding with context-aware clustering. Experimental evaluation using clinical datasets and simulated Internet of Medical Things environments demonstrated that the framework achieved 96.3% accuracy in controlled conditions and sustained 110 ms latency in streaming anomaly detection.

## Introduction

The Internet of Medical Things integrates networked medical devices, clinical applications, and middleware to acquire, transport, and process physiological signals and clinical records with strict constraints on latency, privacy, and reliability[1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR1 "Othman, S. IEEE Systems Journal 17(2), 2422–2433 (2023)."), [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR3 "Kumar, M. & Chand, S. A provable secure and lightweight smart-healthcare cyber-physical system with public verifiability. IEEE Systems Journal 16(4), 5501–5508 (2022)."). Federated learning provides a distributed training paradigm in which local models update shared parameters without centralizing raw clinical data, thereby aligning with regulatory requirements and reducing exposure of sensitive information[4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR4 "Yang, C., Xu, X., Bilal, M., Wen, Y. & Huang, T. Deep-deterministic-policy-gradient-based task offloading with optimized K-means in edge-computing-enabled IoMT cyber-physical systems. A secured remote patient monitoring framework for IoMT ecosystems. Scientific Reports 15(1), 22882 (2025)."). Edge, fog, and cloud computing form a multi-tier execution substrate that assigns sensing, inference, aggregation, and archival functions to appropriate hardware tiers according to bandwidth, compute capacity, and availability profiles[6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR6 "Xiao, L., Zou, B. & Kui, X. A secure lossless redundancy elimination scheme with semantic awareness for cloud-assisted health systems. IEEE Systems Journal 17(3), 4615–4626 (2023)."). Contemporary deployments must support continuous anomaly detection, adaptive resource allocation on heterogeneous devices, and scalable coordination across geographically dispersed sites[7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR7 "Zahoor, S. & Mir, R. Heterogeneity in sensing modalities, sampling rates, and data quality introduces feature misalignment and covariate shift, which degrade model convergence and increase false alarms[9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR9 "Mahmud, S., Keller, M., Ahmed, S. & Karam, R. FAMID: False alarms mitigation in IoMT devices. IFIP International Internet of Things Conference, 199–217 (2023)."). Intermittent connectivity and variable propagation delay complicate global optimization and increase staleness of model updates. Privacy risks arise from gradient inversion, linkage to auxiliary datasets, and enclave or transport compromises. These factors motivate architectures that combine resource-aware local training, universal feature representations for heterogeneous streams, hierarchical aggregation with delay sensitivity, and privacy-preserving computation[10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR10 "Patni, S. & Lee, J. IEEE Access 13, 118890–118911 (2025)."). The objective is to achieve stable convergence, bounded end-to-end latency, and defensible privacy guarantees while operating under device-level power and memory limits and network-level throughput constraints[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR2 "Samuel, O. et al. An anonymous IoT-based e-health monitoring system using blockchain technology. ### Motivation and problem statement

Healthcare delivery supported by the Internet of Medical Things requires analytical frameworks that can process heterogeneous clinical data streams under strict latency and privacy constraints[12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR12 "Amzil, A., Abid, M., Hanini, M., Zaaloul, A. & El Kafhali, S. Stochastic analysis of fog computing and machine learning for scalable low-latency healthcare monitoring. Cluster Computing 27(5), 6097–6117 (2024)."). Current approaches face limitations when applied to distributed deployments with thousands of interconnected medical devices producing data of varying modalities and sampling frequencies[13](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR13 "Ding, X. et al. A feasibility study of multi-mode intelligent fusion medical data transmission technology of industrial Internet of Things combined with medical Internet of Things. Internet of Things 21, 100689 (2023)."). Centralized machine learning solutions cannot satisfy the dual requirements of scalability and compliance with privacy regulations because they rely on raw data aggregation in cloud infrastructures, introducing communication overhead, latency, and risk of disclosure[14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR14 "Han, T., Zhang, L., Pirbhulal, S., Wu, W. & de Albuquerque, V. H. C. A novel cluster head selection technique for edge-computing based IoMT systems. Computer Networks 158, 114–122 (2019)."). Conventional federated learning has addressed part of this issue by keeping data local; however, it does not natively incorporate resource awareness, and the training process often exceeds the memory and computational budgets of edge devices. Additionally, heterogeneity in feature distributions across patients and devices produces alignment inconsistencies, limiting global model convergence and increasing false detection rates in anomaly monitoring tasks[15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR15 "Zachos, G., Mantas, G., Porfyrakis, K., Bastos, J. M. C. S. & Rodriguez, J. Anomaly-based intrusion detection for IoMT networks: Design, implementation, dataset generation, and ML algorithms evaluation. IEEE Access 13, 41994–42028 (2025)."). Communication efficiency is another critical challenge because repeated parameter exchanges between devices and the central server saturate bandwidth and degrade system responsiveness[16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR16 "Islam, U. et al. A hybrid fog-edge computing architecture for real-time health monitoring in IoMT systems with optimized latency and threat resilience. Scientific Reports 15(1), 25655 (2025)."). These technical barriers indicate that conventional learning paradigms remain insufficient for large-scale IoMT healthcare deployments where real-time detection and continuous adaptation are essential.

The problem is further compounded by architectural and security considerations in multi-tier deployments[17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR17 "Awan, K. A., Ud Din, I., Almogren, A., Khattak, H. A. & Rodrigues, J. J. P. C. EdgeTrust: A lightweight data-centric trust management approach for IoT-based healthcare 4.0. Electronics 12(1), 140 (2022)."). Edge and fog devices exhibit non-uniform availability, variable communication delays, and heterogeneous reliability, producing staleness in global updates and lowering predictive accuracy when standard aggregation rules are applied. Existing anomaly detection schemes integrated into IoMT platforms typically rely on static thresholds or shallow models that cannot adapt to the dynamics of real-time patient monitoring, limiting their applicability in emergency-critical scenarios[18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR18 "Panja, S., Chattopadhyay, A. K., Nag, A. & Singh, J. P. Fuzzy-logic-based IoMT framework for COVID-19 patient monitoring. Computers & Industrial Engineering 176, 108941 (2023)."). Privacy mechanisms such as homomorphic encryption or differential privacy, when applied in isolation, increase computational and communication overhead and are often infeasible on low-power devices[19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR19 "Sakhri, A., Maimour, M., Doghmane, N., Rondeau, E. & Harize, S. An energy-efficient IoMT three-tier architecture for continuous monitoring of endangered bird species. Pervasive and Mobile Computing, 102093 (2025)."). Moreover, adversarial inference attacks on gradients and potential vulnerabilities in execution environments expose sensitive health information to reconstruction risks[21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR21 "Garg, N., Wazid, M., Singh, J., Singh, D. P. & Das, A. K. Security in IoMT-driven smart healthcare: A comprehensive review and open challenges. Security and Privacy 5(5), e235 (2022)."). The absence of unified frameworks that simultaneously address resource-aware learning, universal feature encoding, hierarchical aggregation, and privacy-preserving execution creates a critical gap in the design of IoMT-enabled healthcare analytics[22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR22 "Syed, L. et al. Smart healthcare framework for ambient assisted living using IoMT and big data analytics techniques. Future Generation Computer Systems 101, 136–151 (2019)."). Addressing this gap requires an integrated system that sustains model accuracy, ensures low-latency anomaly detection, and guarantees protection of sensitive clinical data across distributed healthcare networks.

### Research objectives and practical implications

The primary objective of this research is to design and validate an adaptive federated edge learning framework that integrates resource-aware local training, dynamic data encoding, hierarchical aggregation, and privacy-preserving computation for Internet of Medical Things healthcare systems[23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR23 "Ma, X., Hu, J., Liang, S. & Wu, Y. Federated learning and resource-aware graph neural network for intrusion detection in 6G-IoT driven healthcare system. IEEE Internet of Things Journal (in press) 1–1 (2025)."). The framework aims to establish a unified approach that enables real-time anomaly detection while operating within the constraints of computational budgets, memory capacities, and bandwidth limitations inherent to distributed devices. A central research goal is to construct Adaptive Modular Learning Units that allocate model complexity according to device-specific resource profiles while maintaining convergence across heterogeneous environments[24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR24 "Annappa, B. et al. Fedcure: A heterogeneity-aware personalized federated learning framework for intelligent healthcare applications in IoMT environments. IEEE Access 12, 15867–15883 (2024)."). Another objective is to formulate Dynamic Data Encoding techniques that align heterogeneous feature distributions and reduce inter-device variability to improve global model stability. At the aggregation layer, the research targets the development of Hierarchical Federated Aggregation mechanisms that incorporate delay-aware weighting to mitigate the effects of asynchronous updates while sustaining scalability[25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR25 "Fan, J., Qin, J., Liu, L. & Tian, H. Associative tasks computing offloading scheme in Internet of Medical Things with deep reinforcement learning. China Communications 21(4), 38–52 (2024)."). Finally, the research seeks to implement Privacy-Preserving Secure Enclaves that allow encrypted computations and integrate differential privacy noise injection to ensure that sensitive clinical information is protected throughout the training and inference pipeline[26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR26 "Hassan, M. U., Rehmani, M. H. & Chen, J. Differential privacy techniques for cyber physical systems: A survey. IEEE Communications Surveys & Tutorials 22(1), 746–789 (2020)."). These objectives are directed toward advancing the technical state of federated learning in healthcare by addressing challenges of heterogeneity, latency, and security in a unified manner[27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR27 "Jiao, Y., Razaq, M. M., Peng, L. & Ho, P.-H. Enhancing IoMT security using large multimodal models. IEEE Network (in press) 1–10 (2025).").

The practical implications of this research extend to multiple facets of healthcare delivery and system deployment. In clinical monitoring scenarios, the framework enables continuous assessment of patient data streams with real-time anomaly detection, thereby supporting prompt decision-making in intensive care units, remote patient management, and emergency response[28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR28 "Dahan, F. et al. A smart IoMT-based architecture for e-healthcare patient monitoring system using artificial intelligence algorithms. Frontiers in Physiology 14, 1125952 (2023)."). From a deployment perspective, the proposed system reduces dependency on centralized infrastructures by distributing computation across edge and fog devices, which enhances scalability and resilience in large hospital networks and telemedicine platforms. The integration of delay-aware hierarchical aggregation provides robust model convergence even under heterogeneous network conditions, ensuring that predictive accuracy is maintained in geographically distributed healthcare facilities[29](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR29 "Luna-delRisco, M., et al. Adoption of Internet of Medical Things (IoMT) as an opportunity for improving public health in Latin America. Proceedings of the 13th Iberian Conference on Information Systems and Technologies (CISTI), 1–5 (2018)."). Privacy-preserving computation directly supports compliance with data protection regulations, minimizing risks of data leakage and ensuring patient confidentiality during collaborative model training[30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR30 "Ghubaish, A. et al. Recent advances in the Internet-of-Medical-Things (IoMT) systems security. IEEE Internet of Things Journal 8(11), 8707–8718 (2021)."). Efficient resource allocation reduces operational costs associated with high-bandwidth transmissions and centralized processing while enabling low-power devices to contribute to federated learning without exceeding energy budgets.

### Overview of the proposed approach

The proposed framework introduces an integrated architecture for adaptive federated edge learning tailored to the operational requirements of Internet of Medical Things healthcare systems. At the device level, Adaptive Modular Learning Units allocate model complexity in proportion to the available computational and memory resources, enabling lightweight execution of inference and training tasks without exceeding device-level budgets. These modules utilize resource-aware objectives to maintain convergence while ensuring that local updates remain computationally feasible. Dynamic Data Encoding is employed to transform heterogeneous sensor streams, electronic health records, and imaging features into a unified representation. This transformation incorporates normalization, feature alignment, and decorrelation procedures to reduce inter-device variability and mitigate covariate shift, thereby stabilizing global training across distributed participants. At the intermediate layer, Hierarchical Federated Aggregation performs multi-tier parameter updates from edge devices to fog nodes and subsequently to cloud servers. The aggregation process incorporates data-size weighting and delay-aware factors to adjust the contribution of updates from regions with variable connectivity and propagation delays, which preserves stability in asynchronous environments. Privacy-Preserving Secure Enclaves form the protective layer of the architecture, ensuring encrypted execution of computations and augmenting privacy protection with differential privacy noise addition during aggregation. Collectively, these components operate as a unified system to address the limitations of conventional federated learning by balancing accuracy, efficiency, and security in real-time healthcare analytics.

The approach also incorporates a real-time anomaly detection pipeline designed to operate in parallel with the federated learning cycle. This module applies sliding windows to continuous patient data streams, followed by dimensionality reduction and covariance-based scoring to identify anomalies. Adaptive thresholding ensures that detection remains sensitive to variations in patient-specific baselines, while clustering-based contextual analysis distinguishes true anomalies from statistical outliers. Integration with the federated learning process ensures that local anomaly detection benefits from continuously updated global models, which improves precision in detecting clinically significant events. By embedding this anomaly detection mechanism within the broader framework, the system supports immediate identification of abnormal physiological patterns while simultaneously contributing to global learning. The hierarchical design allows anomaly information to propagate through local and regional levels without overloading network links, maintaining low end-to-end latency even in large-scale deployments.

### Contribution of the research work

This research establishes an integrated framework for adaptive federated edge learning designed for Internet of Medical Things healthcare systems under strict requirements of scalability, privacy preservation, and real-time responsiveness. The proposed architecture combines resource-aware local training, universal feature encoding, hierarchical aggregation, and secure computation into a unified pipeline. The work advances beyond existing federated learning methods by addressing device-level constraints, heterogeneous data alignment, asynchronous communication, and encrypted execution simultaneously. The technical contributions of this work are as follows:

- Adaptive Modular Learning Units (AMLUs) that enable resource-aware federated training on heterogeneous edge devices. AMLUs quantify device-specific resource budgets in terms of available computational cycles and memory, and dynamically select model complexity profiles that remain within operational limits. This mechanism ensures that devices with constrained resources contribute meaningful updates without experiencing computational overload. The AMLU formulation integrates resource ratios into the objective function of local training, which stabilizes convergence and reduces divergence caused by heterogeneous device capabilities.

- Dynamic Data Encoding (DDE) framework that produces a universal feature space for heterogeneous medical data. The encoding process normalizes and projects device-specific data into a decorrelated representation while applying alignment losses to reduce distributional discrepancies. A quality-weighted fusion mechanism further integrates representations by assigning higher weights to reliable encoders based on statistical quality measures. This design mitigates covariate shift across devices and improves the robustness of global model aggregation.

- Hierarchical Federated Aggregation (HFA) scheme with privacy-preserving secure enclaves (PPSE). The HFA organizes aggregation into device, regional, and global tiers, incorporating data-size weighting and delay-aware factors to handle asynchronous updates. PPSE supports encrypted training and aggregation, while differential privacy noise is injected into aggregated updates to protect sensitive clinical data from inference attacks. This dual design ensures both scalability and defensible privacy guarantees across distributed healthcare networks.

## Proposed framework: adaptive federated edge learning framework

The proposed Adaptive Federated Edge Learning (AFEL) framework is designed to address fundamental challenges in Internet of Medical Things deployments, including heterogeneous data distributions, constrained computational resources, asynchronous connectivity, and strict privacy requirements. As illustrated in Figure [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Fig1), the framework integrates Adaptive Modular Learning Units (AMLUs), Dynamic Data Encoding (DDE), Hierarchical Federated Aggregation (HFA), Privacy-Preserving Secure Enclaves (PPSE), and Real-Time Anomaly Detection (RTAD) into a layered workflow. Each component targets a distinct limitation while contributing to a cohesive architecture that supports end-to-end healthcare analytics. AMLUs implement resource-aware training by adjusting model depth and complexity according to device-specific budgets, which prevents overload on low-capacity nodes while preserving local contribution to global optimization. DDE transforms heterogeneous input streams from wearable sensors, imaging systems, and electronic health records into a universal, decorrelated feature space, thereby minimizing covariate shift across devices. This transformation stabilizes convergence during aggregation and improves anomaly detection accuracy. The workflow, as shown in Figure [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Fig1), establishes an end-to-end system where heterogeneous inputs are standardized, processed under resource-aware conditions, aggregated hierarchically, secured at the computational level, and analyzed in real time for anomaly detection, enabling reliable and privacy-preserving healthcare analytics in Internet of Medical Things systems.

Proposed system architecture illustrating the hierarchical data flow across Input, Processing, Aggregation, Security, and Output layers for healthcare systems.

The upper layers of the framework coordinate secure aggregation and anomaly detection in real time. HFA performs multi-tier aggregation at edge, fog, and cloud levels using data-size weighting and delay-aware coefficients, which mitigates staleness of model updates while maintaining scalability across large deployments. PPSE enforces privacy at the computation layer by executing training and aggregation within hardware-secured enclaves and injecting calibrated differential privacy noise, preventing gradient inversion and inference attacks on sensitive medical data. RTAD operates as a continuous monitoring pipeline, applying sliding windows, dimensionality reduction, covariance-based scoring, and adaptive thresholds to detect anomalies in patient data streams with low latency. This module supports immediate detection of clinically critical events while leveraging continuously updated global models from the federated process.

### AFEL modules working

The Adaptive Federated Edge Learning (AFEL) framework is underpinned by modular components that collaborate to ensure efficiency, scalability, and privacy in IoMT-enabled healthcare systems.

#### Adaptive Modular Learning Units (AMLUs)

AMLUs implement resource-aware local learning on heterogeneous edge nodes. Each AMLU maintains a module-specific resource metric, a resource-constrained training objective, a resource-normalized activation, a resource-proportional fusion rule, and a federated gradient update. The design targets strict device budgets, bounded latency, and stable convergence under heterogeneous feature distributions. The module stack partitions computation across \\(m\\) hierarchical sub-modules while preserving a common supervision signal.

Let the local dataset be \\({\\textbf{X}}=\\{{\\textbf{x}}\_1,\\dots ,{\\textbf{x}}\_n\\}\\), \\({\\textbf{x}}\_i\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^d\\), with target vector \\({\\textbf{y}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^n\\). Computation is partitioned into \\(m\\) sub-modules \\(\\{M\_1,\\dots ,M\_m\\}\\). Equation [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ1) defines the per-module resource score \\(R\_j\\in {\\mathbb {R}}\_{>0}\\) as

$$\\begin{aligned} R\_j=\\frac{C\_j}{T\_j}, \\end{aligned}$$

(1)


where \\(C\_j\\) denotes available computational capacity for module \\(j\\) (e.g., effective FLOPs/s) and \\(T\_j\\) denotes expected processing time per inference/training step for module \\(j\\) (s). The AMLU training objective in Equation [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ2) is

$$\\begin{aligned} \\min \_{{\\textbf{w}}} \\;\\; \\sum \_{j=1}^{m} \\frac{\\Vert {\\textbf{X}}\_j {\\textbf{w}}\_j - {\\textbf{y}} \\Vert \_2^{2}}{R\_j} + \\lambda \\Vert {\\textbf{w}}\_j \\Vert \_{1}, \\end{aligned}$$

(2)


subject to the resource budget in Equation [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ3),

$$\\begin{aligned} \\sum \_{j=1}^{m} R\_j \\le R\_{\\text {max}}, \\end{aligned}$$

(3)


where \\({\\textbf{X}}\_j\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times d}\\) is the module-specific design matrix (e.g., transformed features for \\(M\_j\\)), \\({\\textbf{w}}\_j\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d}\\) is the parameter vector of \\(M\_j\\), \\({\\textbf{w}}=\\{{\\textbf{w}}\_1,\\dots ,{\\textbf{w}}\_m\\}\\), \\(\\Vert \\cdot \\Vert \_2\\) is the Euclidean norm, \\(\\Vert \\cdot \\Vert \_1\\) is the \\(\\ell \_1\\) norm, \\(\\lambda>0\\) is the sparsity regularizer, and \\(R\_{\\text {max}}>0\\) is the allowable aggregate resource budget. Equation [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ2) weights the data-fitting term by \\(1/R\_j\\) to prioritize modules with higher resource scores, while Equation [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ3) constrains the sum of resource allocations. Equation [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ4) defines the resource-normalized activation for module \\(j\\):

$$\\begin{aligned} f\_j({\\textbf{x}}\_i)=\\sigma \\!\\left( \\frac{{\\textbf{x}}\_i^{\\top }{\\textbf{w}}\_j}{R\_j+\\epsilon }\\right) , \\end{aligned}$$

(4)


where \\(\\sigma :{\\mathbb {R}}\\!\\rightarrow \\!{\\mathbb {R}}\\) is a differentiable activation (e.g., logistic sigmoid), \\({\\textbf{x}}\_i^{\\top }{\\textbf{w}}\_j\\) is the inner product, and \\(\\epsilon>0\\) is a small constant for numerical stability. Equation [5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ5) specifies the fusion of sub-module outputs as a convex combination:

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{o}}=\\sum \_{j=1}^{m}\\alpha \_j f\_j({\\textbf{x}}\_i), \\qquad \\alpha \_j=\\frac{R\_j}{\\sum \_{k=1}^{m} R\_k}, \\end{aligned}$$

(5)


where \\({\\textbf{o}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}\\) is the fused scalar output (extendable to vector outputs per class), \\(\\alpha \_j\\in \[0,1\]\\) is the resource-proportional weight, and \\(\\sum \_{j=1}^{m}\\alpha \_j=1\\). Parameter updates follow Equation [6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ6):

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{w}}\_j^{(t+1)}={\\textbf{w}}\_j^{(t)}-\\eta \\,\\nabla \_{{\\textbf{w}}\_j}{\\mathscr {L}}({\\textbf{X}},{\\textbf{y}},{\\textbf{w}}), \\end{aligned}$$

(6)


where \\(t\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\\) is the iteration index, \\(\\eta>0\\) is the learning rate, and \\({\\mathscr {L}}(\\cdot )\\) is a differentiable local loss (consistent with Equation [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ2)). Gradients are computed on device and subsequently integrated within the federated protocol; the notation \\(\\nabla \_{{\\textbf{w}}\_j}\\) denotes the partial gradient with respect to \\({\\textbf{w}}\_j\\).

#### Hierarchical Federated Aggregation (HFA)

HFA implements a multi-tier aggregation protocol that coordinates device, regional, and global model updates under heterogeneous connectivity and workload conditions. The objective is to reduce communication overhead, control staleness, and preserve convergence properties while operating at scale. Hierarchical Federated Aggregation (HFA) Protocol

The on-device update rule in Equation [7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ7) performs a first-order step for edge device \\(i\\in \\{1,\\dots ,N\\}\\):

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{W}}\_i^{(t+1)} = {\\textbf{W}}\_i^{(t)} - \\eta \\,\\nabla \_{{\\textbf{W}}\_i} {\\mathscr {L}}\_i\\!\\left( {\\textbf{W}}\_i^{(t)}\\right) , \\end{aligned}$$

(7)


where \\({\\textbf{W}}\_i^{(t)}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\_w}\\) denotes the parameter vector (or vectorized parameter tensor) at iteration \\(t\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\\), \\(N\\) is the number of edge devices, \\(\\eta>0\\) is the learning rate, \\({\\mathscr {L}}\_i:{\\mathbb {R}}^{d\_w}\\!\\rightarrow \\!{\\mathbb {R}}\\) is a differentiable local loss for device \\(i\\), and \\(\\nabla \_{{\\textbf{W}}\_i}\\) is the gradient operator with respect to \\({\\textbf{W}}\_i\\). The device-level partial update in Equation [8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ8) records the parameter increment:

$$\\begin{aligned} \\Delta {\\textbf{W}}\_i^{\\text {local}} = {\\textbf{W}}\_i^{(t+1)} - {\\textbf{W}}\_i^{(t)}, \\end{aligned}$$

(8)


where \\(\\Delta {\\textbf{W}}\_i^{\\text {local}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\_w}\\) is the per-iteration update vector computed locally. Local aggregation across the \\(N\\) devices uses data-size weights as in Equation [9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ9):

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{W}}\_{\\text {local}}^{(t+1)} = \\frac{\\sum \_{i=1}^{N} n\_i \\,\\Delta {\\textbf{W}}\_i^{\\text {local}}}{\\sum \_{i=1}^{N} n\_i}, \\end{aligned}$$

(9)


where \\(n\_i\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\_{>0}\\) denotes the number of training samples (or an equivalent sample-weight proxy) on device \\(i\\); \\({\\textbf{W}}\_{\\text {local}}^{(t+1)}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\_w}\\) is the locally aggregated update. The denominator \\(\\sum \_{i=1}^{N} n\_i>0\\). Regional aggregation groups devices by region \\(r\\in \\{1,\\dots ,R\\}\\) with index set \\({\\mathscr {N}}\_r\\subseteq \\{1,\\dots ,N\\}\\). Equation [10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ10) forms the regional aggregate at the fog node:

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{W}}\_r^{(t+1)} = \\frac{\\sum \_{i \\in {\\mathscr {N}}\_r} n\_i \\,{\\textbf{W}}\_{\\text {local}, i}^{(t+1)}}{\\sum \_{i \\in {\\mathscr {N}}\_r} n\_i}, \\end{aligned}$$

(10)


where \\({\\textbf{W}}\_{\\text {local}, i}^{(t+1)}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\_w}\\) denotes the device-\\(i\\) contribution to the local aggregate (by construction, \\({\\textbf{W}}\_{\\text {local}, i}^{(t+1)} \\equiv \\Delta {\\textbf{W}}\_i^{\\text {local}}\\)), \\({\\textbf{W}}\_r^{(t+1)}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\_w}\\) is the regional parameter update, and \\(\\sum \_{i \\in {\\mathscr {N}}\_r} n\_i>0\\). Global aggregation at the cloud fuses regional updates using regional data-size weights as in Equation [11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ11):

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{W}}\_{\\text {global}}^{(t+1)} = \\frac{\\sum \_{r=1}^{R} n\_r \\,{\\textbf{W}}\_r^{(t+1)}}{\\sum \_{r=1}^{R} n\_r}, \\end{aligned}$$

(11)


where \\(R\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\_{>0}\\) is the number of regions, \\(n\_r=\\sum \_{i \\in {\\mathscr {N}}\_r} n\_i\\) is the regional sample count, and \\({\\textbf{W}}\_{\\text {global}}^{(t+1)}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\_w}\\) is the global update; \\(\\sum \_{r=1}^{R} n\_r>0\\). To discount stale contributions, Equation [12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ12) defines a delay-aware coefficient:

$$\\begin{aligned} \\beta \_r = \\frac{1}{1 + \\lambda \\tau \_r}, \\end{aligned}$$

(12)


where \\(\\beta \_r\\in (0,1\]\\) is dimensionless, \\(\\tau \_r\\ge 0\\) is the end-to-end communication and processing delay for region \\(r\\) (s), and \\(\\lambda \\ge 0\\) is a tunable scaling parameter controlling the decay rate. The delay-weighted global fusion in Equation [13](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ13) integrates \\(\\beta \_r\\) into the aggregation:

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{W}}\_{\\text {global}}^{(t+1)} = \\frac{\\sum \_{r=1}^{R} \\beta \_r n\_r \\,{\\textbf{W}}\_r^{(t+1)}}{\\sum \_{r=1}^{R} \\beta \_r n\_r}, \\end{aligned}$$

(13)


where the denominator \\(\\sum \_{r=1}^{R} \\beta \_r n\_r>0\\) and each term \\(\\beta \_r n\_r\\) serves as a joint delay–data weight. Regional and global objectives are defined in Equations [14](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ14) and [15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ15). The regional loss aggregates device losses:

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {L}}\_r({\\textbf{W}}\_r) = \\sum \_{i \\in {\\mathscr {N}}\_r} \\frac{n\_i}{\\sum \_{j \\in {\\mathscr {N}}\_r} n\_j} \\,{\\mathscr {L}}\_i({\\textbf{W}}\_i), \\end{aligned}$$

(14)


where \\({\\mathscr {L}}\_r:{\\mathbb {R}}^{d\_w}\\!\\rightarrow \\!{\\mathbb {R}}\\) is the regional objective. The global loss averages regional objectives:

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {L}}\_{\\text {global}} = \\sum \_{r=1}^{R} \\frac{n\_r}{\\sum \_{k=1}^{R} n\_k} \\,{\\mathscr {L}}\_r({\\textbf{W}}\_r), \\end{aligned}$$

(15)


where \\({\\mathscr {L}}\_{\\text {global}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}\\) is the scalar objective minimized by the protocol.

Dynamic Data Encoding (DDE) Workflow

Let \\(S\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\_{>0}\\) denote the number of Internet of Medical Things devices and \\({\\textbf{D}}=\\{{\\textbf{d}}\_s\\}\_{s=1}^{S}\\) the set of device-specific data matrices with \\({\\textbf{d}}\_s\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\_s\\times p\_s}\\), where \\(n\_s\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\_{>0}\\) is the sample count and \\(p\_s\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\_{>0}\\) is the feature dimension for device \\(s\\). Equation [16](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ16) defines per-device standardization,

$$\\begin{aligned} \\tilde{{\\textbf{d}}}\_s = \\frac{{\\textbf{d}}\_s - \\mu \_s}{\\sigma \_s}, \\end{aligned}$$

(16)


where \\(\\tilde{{\\textbf{d}}}\_s\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\_s\\times p\_s}\\) is the standardized matrix, \\(\\mu \_s\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{1\\times p\_s}\\) is the feature-wise mean row vector, and \\(\\sigma \_s\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{1\\times p\_s}\\) is the feature-wise standard-deviation row vector (elementwise division). Equation [17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ17) applies a device-specific affine map \\(\\phi \_s:{\\mathbb {R}}^{p\_s}\\rightarrow {\\mathbb {R}}^{q}\\) to embed the standardized data into a universal \\(q\\)-dimensional space,

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{e}}\_s = \\phi \_s(\\tilde{{\\textbf{d}}}\_s) = \\tilde{{\\textbf{d}}}\_s {\\textbf{W}}\_s + {\\textbf{b}}\_s, \\end{aligned}$$

(17)


where \\({\\textbf{e}}\_s\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\_s\\times q}\\) is the encoded matrix for device \\(s\\), \\({\\textbf{W}}\_s\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{p\_s\\times q}\\) is a learnable weight matrix, \\({\\textbf{b}}\_s\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{1\\times q}\\) is a learnable bias row vector broadcast across rows, and \\(q\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\_{>0}\\) is the universal embedding dimension. Cross-device consistency is encouraged by the pairwise alignment loss in Equation [18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ18),

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {L}}\_{\\text {align}} = \\sum \_{s=1}^{S}\\sum \_{t=1}^{S} \\big \\Vert {\\textbf{e}}\_s - {\\textbf{e}}\_t \\big \\Vert \_{F}^{2}, \\end{aligned}$$

(18)


where \\(\\Vert \\cdot \\Vert \_{F}\\) denotes the Frobenius norm and \\(\\{{\\textbf{e}}\_s\\}\_{s=1}^{S}\\) are the device encodings. (In practice, row counts are harmonized to a common \\(n^{\\star }\\) by resampling or indexing so that \\({\\textbf{e}}\_s,{\\textbf{e}}\_t\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n^{\\star }\\times q}\\) for valid subtraction.) To reduce redundancy and enforce whitening, Equation [19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ19) penalizes deviations of second-order statistics from the identity,

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {L}}\_{\\text {decor}} = \\sum \_{s=1}^{S} \\big \\Vert \\mathbf {\\Sigma }\_s - {\\textbf{I}} \\big \\Vert \_{F}^{2}, \\end{aligned}$$

(19)


where \\(\\mathbf {\\Sigma }\_s={\\textbf{e}}\_s^{\\top }{\\textbf{e}}\_s\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{q\\times q}\\) is the unnormalized second-moment matrix of \\({\\textbf{e}}\_s\\) and \\({\\textbf{I}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{q\\times q}\\) is the identity matrix. The device encodings are combined by the quality-weighted fusion in Equation [20](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ20),

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{E}}\_{\\text {global}} = \\sum \_{s=1}^{S} \\alpha \_s {\\textbf{e}}\_s, \\end{aligned}$$

(20)


where \\({\\textbf{E}}\_{\\text {global}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n^{\\star }\\times q}\\) is the fused encoding (after row harmonization) and \\(\\alpha \_s\\in \[0,1\]\\) are device weights that sum to one. Equation [21](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ21) specifies \\(\\alpha \_s\\) via quality scores \\(Q\_s\\),

$$\\begin{aligned} \\alpha \_s = \\frac{Q\_s}{\\sum \_{t=1}^{S} Q\_t}, \\quad Q\_s = \\frac{1}{\\big \\Vert \\mathbf {\\Sigma }\_s - {\\textbf{I}} \\big \\Vert \_{F} + \\epsilon }, \\end{aligned}$$

(21)


where \\(Q\_s\\in {\\mathbb {R}}\_{>0}\\) is the inverse-whitening-deviation score for device \\(s\\), \\(\\epsilon>0\\) ensures numerical stability, and \\(\\sum \_{t=1}^{S}Q\_t>0\\) guarantees a valid normalization. The total objective in Equation [22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ22) couples alignment, decorrelation, and Tikhonov regularization of the fused representation,

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {L}}\_{\\text {total}} = {\\mathscr {L}}\_{\\text {align}} + \\lambda \_{1}\\,{\\mathscr {L}}\_{\\text {decor}} + \\lambda \_{2}\\,\\big \\Vert {\\textbf{E}}\_{\\text {global}} \\big \\Vert \_{F}^{2}, \\end{aligned}$$

(22)


where \\(\\lambda \_{1}\\ge 0\\) and \\(\\lambda \_{2}\\ge 0\\) are hyperparameters that control the trade-off between cross-device alignment, feature decorrelation, and magnitude regularization of \\({\\textbf{E}}\_{\\text {global}}\\). The Frobenius term \\(\\Vert {\\textbf{E}}\_{\\text {global}}\\Vert \_{F}^{2}\\) equals the sum of squared entries of the fused encoding and stabilizes optimization.

#### Privacy-Preserving Secure Enclaves (PPSE)

PPSE formalizes computation over protected representations inside hardware-backed trusted execution environments. The mechanism uses an affine masking transform for inputs, enclave-confined linear algebra for inference and training, encrypted aggregation of updates, and calibrated differential privacy noise for release.

Privacy-Preserving Secure Enclaves (PPSE) Training and Aggregation

Let \\({\\textbf{X}}=\\{{\\textbf{x}}\_i\\}\_{i=1}^{n}\\) with \\({\\textbf{x}}\_i\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d}\\) denote the input samples (\\(n\\) samples, \\(d\\) features). Equation [23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ23) defines the enclave-side affine masking transform

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{x}}\_i)={\\textbf{x}}\_i^{\\top }{\\textbf{K}}+{\\textbf{r}}\_i^{\\top }, \\end{aligned}$$

(23)


where \\({\\textbf{K}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\\times d}\\) is a full-rank secret key matrix known only inside the enclave, and \\({\\textbf{r}}\_i\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d}\\) is a per-sample random mask (zero-mean, enclave-generated). Stackwise, \\({\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{X}})={\\textbf{X}}{\\textbf{K}}+{\\textbf{R}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times d}\\) with \\({\\textbf{R}}=\[{\\textbf{r}}\_1,\\dots ,{\\textbf{r}}\_n\]^{\\top }\\). Given model parameters \\({\\textbf{W}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\\times o}\\) (\\(o\\) outputs), Equation [24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ24) defines masked-domain inference:

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{y}})={\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{X}})\\,{\\textbf{W}}, \\end{aligned}$$

(24)


where \\({\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{y}})\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times o}\\) are masked outputs. Expanding Equation [24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ24) yields Equation [25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ25):

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{y}})=({\\textbf{X}}{\\textbf{K}}+{\\textbf{R}})\\,{\\textbf{W}}, \\end{aligned}$$

(25)


where \\({\\textbf{X}}{\\textbf{K}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times d}\\) and \\({\\textbf{R}}{\\textbf{W}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times o}\\). Equation [26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ26) recovers plaintext predictions using the enclave-held inverse \\({\\textbf{K}}^{-1}\\):

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {D}}({\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{y}}))=\\big ({\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{y}})-{\\textbf{R}}{\\textbf{W}}\\big )\\,{\\textbf{K}}^{-1}, \\end{aligned}$$

(26)


where \\({\\mathscr {D}}(\\cdot )\\) denotes the de-masking map and \\({\\textbf{K}}^{-1}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\\times d}\\) exists by full rank. Let \\(N\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\_{>0}\\) be the number of collaborating nodes and \\({\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{y}}\_i^{(t)})\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\_w}\\) be the masked update vector from node \\(i\\) at iteration \\(t\\) (\\(d\_w\\) parameters). Equation [27](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ27) defines additive aggregation in the masked domain:

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{y}}\_{\\text {agg}}^{(t)})=\\sum \_{i=1}^{N}{\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{y}}\_i^{(t)}), \\end{aligned}$$

(27)


where the sum is elementwise over parameter coordinates. Equation [28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ28) applies enclave de-masking:

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{y}}\_{\\text {agg}}^{(t)}={\\mathscr {D}}\\!\\left( {\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{y}}\_{\\text {agg}}^{(t)})\\right) , \\end{aligned}$$

(28)


yielding the plaintext aggregate update \\({\\textbf{y}}\_{\\text {agg}}^{(t)}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\_w}\\) only within the enclave boundary. Let \\({\\mathscr {L}}:{\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times o}\\times {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times o}\\rightarrow {\\mathbb {R}}\\) be a differentiable loss, \\({\\textbf{Y}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times o}\\) be labels (e.g., one-hot or continuous), and \\(\\eta>0\\) be the learning rate. Using masked operands, Equation [29](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ29) states the gradient in the masked domain:

$$\\begin{aligned} \\nabla {\\mathscr {L}}\\big ({\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{X}}),{\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{W}})\\big ) ={\\mathscr {E}}\\!\\left( {\\textbf{X}}^{\\top }\\big ({\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{X}})\\,{\\textbf{W}}-{\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{Y}})\\big )\\right) , \\end{aligned}$$

(29)


where \\({\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{W}})\\) and \\({\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{Y}})\\) denote masked parameters and masked labels, respectively; \\({\\textbf{X}}^{\\top }\\big (\\cdot \\big )\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\\times o}\\) matches \\({\\textbf{W}}\\)’s shape. Equation [30](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ30) applies the masked-domain update:

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {E}}\\!\\left( {\\textbf{W}}^{(t+1)}\\right) ={\\mathscr {E}}\\!\\left( {\\textbf{W}}^{(t)}\\right) -\\eta \\,\\nabla {\\mathscr {L}}\\big ({\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{X}}),{\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{W}})\\big ), \\end{aligned}$$

(30)


where \\({\\textbf{W}}^{(t)}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\\times o}\\) is the parameter matrix at iteration \\(t\\). To enforce record-level privacy during aggregation, Equation [31](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ31) injects calibrated Gaussian noise in the masked domain:

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{y}}\_{\\text {agg}}^{(t)}) \\leftarrow {\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{y}}\_{\\text {agg}}^{(t)}) + \\Delta {\\textbf{N}}, \\qquad \\Delta {\\textbf{N}}\\sim {\\mathscr {N}}\\!\\left( {\\textbf{0}},\\,\\sigma ^{2}{\\textbf{I}}\\right) , \\end{aligned}$$

(31)


where \\(\\Delta {\\textbf{N}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\_w}\\), \\(\\sigma ^{2}>0\\) is the noise variance, and \\({\\textbf{I}}\\) is the \\(d\_w\\times d\_w\\) identity matrix. The enclave-anchored training objective in Equation [32](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ32) minimizes the expected plaintext loss under masking:

$$\\begin{aligned} \\min \_{{\\textbf{W}}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\\times o}} \\;\\; {\\mathbb {E}}\_{{\\mathscr {E}}}\\!\\left\[ \\,{\\mathscr {L}}\\!\\big ({\\mathscr {D}}({\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{X}})\\,{\\textbf{W}}),\\,{\\textbf{Y}}\\big )\\right\] , \\end{aligned}$$

(32)


subject to the masking constraint in Equation [33](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ33):

$$\\begin{aligned} \\big \\Vert {\\mathscr {E}}({\\textbf{x}}\_i)-{\\textbf{x}}\_i^{\\top }\\big \\Vert \_{2}\\le \\epsilon , \\quad \\forall i\\in \\{1,\\dots ,n\\}, \\end{aligned}$$

(33)


where \\(\\epsilon>0\\) bounds per-sample deviation between masked and plaintext representations (measured in \\({\\mathbb {R}}^{1\\times d}\\)). Constraint \\(\\epsilon\\) sets a limit on masking distortion to preserve numerical stability of downstream linear algebra while maintaining confidentiality within the enclave. #### Real-Time Anomaly Detection (RTAD)

RTAD provides continuous assessment of streaming clinical signals under strict latency constraints. The pipeline comprises (i) temporal windowing, (ii) feature projection, (iii) covariance-based scoring, (iv) adaptive thresholding, (v) density-aware clustering, (vi) priority assignment, and (vii) stability optimization.

Let the data stream at time \\(t\\in {\\mathbb {R}}\_{\\ge 0}\\) be \\({\\textbf{S}}(t)=\\{{\\textbf{s}}\_{i}(t)\\}\_{i=1}^{n}\\) with \\({\\textbf{s}}\_{i}(t)\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d}\\) (\\(n\\) samples, \\(d\\) features). Equation [34](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ34) defines the temporal window

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {W}}\_{t}=\\{{\\textbf{s}}\_{i}(\\tau )\\mid \\tau \\in \[t-\\Delta t,\\,t\]\\}, \\end{aligned}$$

(34)


where \\(\\Delta t>0\\) is the window length (s). The windowed matrix in Equation [35](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ35) stacks observations:

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{M}}\_{t}=\\begin{bmatrix} {\\textbf{s}}\_{1}(t-\\Delta t) & \\cdots & {\\textbf{s}}\_{n}(t-\\Delta t)\\\ \\vdots & \\ddots & \\vdots \\\ {\\textbf{s}}\_{1}(t) & \\cdots & {\\textbf{s}}\_{n}(t) \\end{bmatrix}\\!, \\end{aligned}$$

(35)


with \\({\\textbf{M}}\_{t}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{w\\times n d}\\) or, equivalently, reshaped to \\({\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times d}\\) after temporal aggregation; \\(w\\) is the number of sampled instants in \\(\[t-\\Delta t,t\]\\). Dimensionality reduction uses a linear map \\(\\Phi :{\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times d}\\rightarrow {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times k}\\) with \\(k\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\_{>0}\\). Equation [36](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ36) defines the (unnormalized) covariance proxy,

$$\\begin{aligned} \\mathbf {\\Sigma }\_{t}={\\textbf{M}}\_{t}^{\\top }{\\textbf{M}}\_{t}, \\end{aligned}$$

(36)


where \\(\\mathbf {\\Sigma }\_{t}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{d\\times d}\\). The projection in Equation [37](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ37) applies \\({\\textbf{U}}\_{t}\\),

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\textbf{F}}\_{t}=\\Phi ({\\textbf{M}}\_{t})={\\textbf{U}}\_{t}{\\textbf{M}}\_{t}, \\end{aligned}$$

(37)


where \\({\\textbf{U}}\_{t}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{k\\times d}\\) contains the top-\\(k\\) eigenvectors of a covariance estimate compatible with \\(\\mathbf {\\Sigma }\_{t}\\), and \\({\\textbf{F}}\_{t}=\[{\\textbf{f}}\_{1}(t),\\dots ,{\\textbf{f}}\_{n}(t)\]^{\\top }\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{n\\times k}\\) are projected features. Anomaly scoring uses a quadratic form under the inverse covariance metric. Equation [38](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ38) defines

$$\\begin{aligned} A\_{i}(t)=\\sqrt{({\\textbf{f}}\_{i}(t)-\\varvec{\\mu }\_{t})^{\\top }{\\textbf{C}}\_{t}^{-1}({\\textbf{f}}\_{i}(t)-\\varvec{\\mu }\_{t})}, \\end{aligned}$$

(38)


where \\({\\textbf{f}}\_{i}(t)\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{k}\\) is the \\(i\\)-th projected vector, \\(\\varvec{\\mu }\_{t}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{k}\\) is the sample mean of rows of \\({\\textbf{F}}\_{t}\\), and \\({\\textbf{C}}\_{t}\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{k\\times k}\\) is a positive-definite covariance estimate (e.g., shrinkage of \\(\\operatorname {cov}({\\textbf{F}}\_{t})\\)). Adaptive thresholding in Equation [39](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ39) sets

$$\\begin{aligned} \\tau (t)=\\mu \_{A}(t)+\\alpha \\,\\sigma \_{A}(t), \\end{aligned}$$

(39)


where \\(\\mu \_{A}(t)\\in {\\mathbb {R}}\\) and \\(\\sigma \_{A}(t)\\in {\\mathbb {R}}\_{>0}\\) are the mean and standard deviation of \\(\\{A\_{i}(t)\\}\_{i=1}^{n}\\), and \\(\\alpha>0\\) controls sensitivity. Context modeling uses clustering. Equation [40](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ40) defines cluster \\(C\_{j}(t)\\),

$$\\begin{aligned} C\_{j}(t)=\\{{\\textbf{f}}\_{i}(t)\\mid \\Vert {\\textbf{f}}\_{i}(t)-{\\textbf{c}}\_{j}(t)\\Vert \_{2}<\\epsilon \_{j}\\}, \\end{aligned}$$

(40)


where \\({\\textbf{c}}\_{j}(t)\\in {\\mathbb {R}}^{k}\\) is the centroid and \\(\\epsilon \_{j}>0\\) is the inclusion radius. Equation [41](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ41) updates \\(\\epsilon \_{j}\\) from intra-cluster dispersion,

$$\\begin{aligned} \\epsilon \_{j}=\\frac{\\sum \_{{\\textbf{f}}\_{i}\\in C\_{j}(t)} \\Vert {\\textbf{f}}\_{i}(t)-{\\textbf{c}}\_{j}(t)\\Vert \_{2}}{\|C\_{j}(t)\|}, \\end{aligned}$$

(41)


where \\(\|C\_{j}(t)\|\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\_{\\ge 0}\\) is the cluster cardinality. Cluster density in Equation [42](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ42) is

$$\\begin{aligned} D\_{j}(t)=\\frac{\|C\_{j}(t)\|}{\\operatorname {Volume}(C\_{j}(t))}, \\end{aligned}$$

(42)


where \\(\\operatorname {Volume}(C\_{j}(t))>0\\) denotes a \\(k\\)-dimensional volume proxy (e.g., hypersphere with radius \\(\\epsilon \_{j}\\)). Priority assignment uses ordinal levels. Equation [43](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ43) maps decisions to \\(\\{1,2,3\\}\\),

$$\\begin{aligned} {\\mathscr {R}}(t)={\\left\\{ \\begin{array}{ll} 3, & A\_{i}(t)>\\beta \\,\\tau (t),\\\ 2, & \\tau (t)<A\_{i}(t)\\le \\beta \\,\\tau (t),\\\ 1, & \\text {otherwise}, \\end{array}\\right. } \\end{aligned}$$

(43)


where \\(\\beta>1\\) scales the high-priority threshold and \\({\\mathscr {R}}(t)\\in \\{1,2,3\\}\\) is the assigned priority for the current assessment. Temporal stability is quantified by entropy in Equation [44](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ44),

$$\\begin{aligned} H(t)=-\\sum \_{k=1}^{K} p\_{k}(t)\\,\\log p\_{k}(t), \\end{aligned}$$

(44)


where \\(K\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\_{>0}\\) is the number of active clusters and \\(p\_{k}(t)\\in \[0,1\]\\) is the proportion of detected anomalies in \\(C\_{k}(t)\\) with \\(\\sum \_{k}p\_{k}(t)=1\\). The stability objective in Equation [45](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ45) balances entropy and score variation,

$$\\begin{aligned} \\max \\;\\; \\sum \_{t=1}^{T} H(t)-\\gamma \\sum \_{t=1}^{T}\\big \|A(t)-A(t-1)\\big \|, \\end{aligned}$$

(45)


where \\(T\\in {\\mathbb {N}}\_{>0}\\) is the horizon, \\(\\gamma>0\\) is a smoothness coefficient, and \\(A(t)=\\frac{1}{n}\\sum \_{i=1}^{n}A\_{i}(t)\\). The RTAD loss in Equation [46](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Equ46) integrates pointwise and contextual penalties,

$$\\begin{aligned} \\min \_{\\Phi ,\\,\\tau ,\\,C} \\;\\; {\\mathscr {L}}\_{\\text {RTAD}}&=\\sum \_{t=1}^{T}\\Bigg \[\\sum \_{i=1}^{n}\\big (A\_{i}(t)-\\tau (t)\\big )^{2} +\\lambda \\sum \_{j=1}^{K}\\big (D\_{j}(t)-\\delta \\big )^{2}\\Bigg \], \\end{aligned}$$

(46)


where \\(\\lambda \\ge 0\\) controls the contextual penalty, \\(\\delta>0\\) is the density threshold, and the optimization variables are the projection \\(\\Phi\\), threshold function \\(\\tau (\\cdot )\\), and cluster assignment \\(C=\\{C\_{j}(t)\\}\\).

## Experimental simulation & results

The experimental evaluation of the proposed framework was carried out using a controlled testbed configured to replicate IoMT deployment scenarios. Edge-layer emulation was performed on Raspberry Pi 4 Model B units, each equipped with a quad-core ARM Cortex-A72 processor and 4 GB RAM, while the fog layer was implemented on a Dell Precision 5820 Tower workstation incorporating an Intel Xeon W-2245 CPU, 32 GB RAM, and an NVIDIA Quadro RTX 4000 GPU. The iFogSim simulation platform was employed to model IoMT traffic flows, latency behavior, and computational workload distribution across hierarchical layers. The evaluation compared the proposed framework with state-of-the-art baselines including IoMT-CVA[36](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#ref-CR36 "Liu, J. et al. XSRU-IoMT: Explainable simple recurrent units for threat detection in Internet of Medical Things networks. Processed data streams were partitioned into training, validation, and testing sets with stratification to preserve class distribution.

In controlled conditions with static data and minimal noise, the proposed approach achieved the highest accuracy of 96.3%, outperforming IoMT-CVA (92.1%) and AI-SecIoMT (91.8%), as illustrated by Figure [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-23398-w#Fig2). ## Discussion

The experimental outcomes confirm that the proposed Adaptive Federated Edge Learning (AFEL) framework achieves substantial improvements in accuracy, latency, and scalability when compared with state-of-the-art baselines. The integration of Adaptive Modular Learning Units (AMLUs) allowed heterogeneous edge devices to contribute updates without exceeding their computational budgets, ensuring that even constrained nodes maintained active participation in the training cycle. This design directly reduced model divergence caused by resource imbalance, which is a persistent challenge in conventional federated learning deployments. The Dynamic Data Encoding (DDE) module further stabilized global convergence by normalizing, aligning, and decorrelating heterogeneous features into a unified representation. This preprocessing step was essential in mitigating covariate shift across distributed data sources, thereby enhancing robustness against false anomaly detections. Hierarchical Federated Aggregation (HFA) improved system scalability by reducing update staleness through delay-aware weighting while simultaneously lowering communication overhead. The ability of the framework to sustain 96.3% accuracy under controlled conditions and 95.8% in real-time anomaly detection illustrates its suitability for critical healthcare tasks that demand stable performance despite noisy environments and asynchronous connectivity.

Beyond performance gains, the Privacy-Preserving Secure Enclaves (PPSE) ensured that sensitive clinical data remained protected throughout training and aggregation. By confining computations to enclave environments and applying calibrated differential privacy noise, the framework achieved strong resilience against gradient inversion, linkage attacks, and inference threats that compromise patient confidentiality. This dual-layer privacy design directly addresses regulatory and ethical requirements in clinical data handling, making AFEL highly suitable for real-world adoption. Resource utilization analysis further demonstrated that the framework significantly reduced CPU and memory consumption compared with baselines, thereby extending operational viability for low-power IoMT devices. Scalability tests confirmed consistent throughput across deployments ranging from 50 to 1,000 devices, highlighting the framework’s capacity to adapt to hospital-scale and telemedicine-scale environments.

## Conclusion

Internet of Medical Things (IoMT) deployments require architectures that achieve efficiency, scalability, and reliability under heterogeneous devices and stringent privacy constraints. This research presents an Adaptive Federated Edge Learning framework whose core advances are a hierarchical federated aggregation mechanism with delay-aware weighting and resource-aware modular learning units. Resource utilization analysis indicated 38% CPU usage under minimal processing, outperforming XSRU-IoMT (50%) and Neuro-IoMT (63%). Throughput remained consistent across scale, sustaining 95 Mbps in small networks and 85 Mbps in large networks, exceeding IoMT-CVA and AI-SecIoMT by 10–20%. End-to-end latency averaged 110 ms for real-time anomaly detection, while controlled evaluations maintained 96.3% detection accuracy. Future work will extend cross-device communication efficiency through adaptive scheduling and congestion-aware update policies, incorporate multi-modal medical data streams (physiological waveforms, imaging, and electronic records) with modality-specific encoders within the DDE layer, and strengthen enclave operations via remote attestation and formal privacy accounting.
